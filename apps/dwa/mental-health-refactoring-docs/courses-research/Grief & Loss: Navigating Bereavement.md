## 1. Evidence base for “Grief & Loss: Navigating Bereavement”

### 1.1 Modern grief models

- The Dual Process Model (Stroebe & Schut) describes healthy grieving as moving back and forth (oscillating) between “loss‑oriented” coping (feeling and facing the grief) and “restoration‑oriented” coping (attending to daily life, roles, and new tasks). [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10848151/)
- Loss‑oriented coping includes yearning, intense sadness, telling stories about the person, rituals, and remembering, while restoration‑oriented coping involves adapting to new roles, returning to work, managing practical tasks, and gradually re-engaging with life. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- Continuing bonds theory emphasizes that many people heal not by “letting go” completely, but by developing a new kind of ongoing inner connection to the deceased or to what was lost (e.g., memories, values, rituals), which can be adaptive and comforting. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- Meaning‑making models highlight how people try to make sense of their loss and their changed world; finding meaning can involve personal growth, new commitments, spiritual perspectives, or ways of honoring what was lost, and can support long‑term adjustment. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)

### 1.2 Normal grief vs. Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)

- Grief typically involves intense waves of sadness, yearning, and other symptoms that gradually soften over time, with people slowly regaining capacity for daily life, even while still feeling the loss. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)
- Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD), now recognized in DSM‑5‑TR, describes a small minority of bereaved people who experience intense, persistent grief that lasts beyond culturally expected periods (e.g., at least 12 months after death in adults) and substantially impairs functioning. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9159802/)
- PGD symptoms include persistent yearning or longing, intense emotional pain, difficulty accepting the death, feeling life is meaningless without the deceased, significant identity disruption, and marked functional impairment. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)
- Prevalence estimates suggest PGD affects a modest proportion of bereaved adults (around 3–4% in some community samples), though risk increases under certain circumstances (e.g., traumatic or unexpected losses). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9159802/)

### 1.3 Grief interventions and support research

- Reviews of grief interventions show that general support and psychoeducation can be helpful for many bereaved people, especially when they validate experiences and provide coping tools, although not everyone needs formal therapy. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)
- Cognitive‑behavioral and meaning‑oriented therapies show the strongest evidence for treating clinically significant or prolonged grief reactions, including structured approaches specifically designed for PGD. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- Group‑based and internet‑based grief programs (often CBT‑informed) can reduce grief intensity, depression, and related distress, though completion rates and effects vary. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- Postvention programs for suicide bereavement can improve some grief and mental health outcomes, but evidence for reducing suicidal behavior is mixed, and more high‑quality research is needed. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)

### 1.4 Complicated/prolonged grief risk factors

- Risk for PGD or more complicated grief is higher when the death is sudden, traumatic, or perceived as very unexpected, and when the bereaved person feels unprepared for the death. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)
- Other long‑term risk factors include prior mental health conditions (e.g., history of depression or bipolar disorder), strong emotional dependency or very close kinship to the deceased, insecure attachment styles, and prior losses or adversity. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolonged_grief_disorder)
- Caregivers, especially those who cared for a partner or who were already experiencing depression, may be at elevated risk for prolonged grief responses. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)

### 1.5 Self-care during bereavement

- Self‑care during grief includes attending to basic needs like sleep, nutrition, hydration, movement, and medical care, which can be disrupted by intense emotional pain. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- Gentle, flexible routines and small restorative activities (e.g., walking, connecting with others, creative expression) are associated with better longer‑term adjustment and reflect the “restoration‑oriented” side of the Dual Process Model. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- Psychoeducational and supportive interventions that encourage self‑compassion, normalize grief variability, and promote practical coping skills can reduce distress and support resilience, especially when tailored to the person’s culture and type of loss. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)

## 2. Patient‑friendly content for 8 modules

Below are MDX‑ready scaffolds: learning goals, key teaching points, and tone guidance.

### Module 1: Understanding grief (there’s no “right way”)

**Learning goals**  
- Understand grief as a natural response to many kinds of loss.  
- Learn that grief is not a linear set of stages and has no fixed timeline.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Grief is a whole‑person response (emotional, physical, social, spiritual) to losing someone or something important—this can include death, divorce, job loss, health changes, immigration, or identity changes. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- There is no “correct” way to grieve; people may cry, feel numb, feel angry, feel relief, or experience many emotions at once, and all of these can be valid. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- Modern models like the Dual Process Model describe grief as moving between feeling the loss and stepping into daily life, rather than passing through neat, predictable stages. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10848151/)
- “Your grief is valid” means your reactions are shaped by your relationship, culture, history, and circumstances; comparison (“I should be over it”) often increases suffering.  

***

### Module 2: Common grief experiences (physical, emotional, cognitive, behavioral)

**Learning goals**  
- Normalize the wide range of grief responses.  
- Help learners recognize their own experiences in compassionate language.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Physical experiences can include fatigue, changes in sleep and appetite, tightness in chest, stomach discomfort, or feeling “heavy” or “restless”; these are common after loss but should be discussed with healthcare providers if concerning. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)
- Emotional experiences may involve sadness, anger, guilt, relief, confusion, numbness, longing, or moments of peace, and these feelings can come in waves that change over time. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- Cognitive experiences can include trouble concentrating, disbelief, mental “fog,” intrusive images or memories, or questioning faith and assumptions about the world. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)
- Behavioral changes may include social withdrawal, restlessness, crying, changes in routines, or increased use of distractions; some behaviors are protective, while others may increase risk if they become extreme or long‑lasting (e.g., heavy substance use). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)

***

### Module 3: Grief myths vs. reality (stages aren’t linear, no timeline)

**Learning goals**  
- Gently challenge common grief myths.  
- Reduce shame and self‑criticism around how someone is grieving.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Myth: “You should follow the five stages of grief in order.” Reality: Emotions rarely unfold in a tidy sequence; people move back and forth, repeat feelings, and sometimes feel multiple emotions at once. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10848151/)
- Myth: “You should be over it by now.” Reality: There is no universal timetable; many people feel grief in some form for years, though the intensity and frequency of waves often change over time. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)
- Myth: “Crying is the only healthy way to grieve.” Reality: Some people cry often, others rarely; cultural, personal, and gender norms all shape how people express grief, and internal grieving is still real grieving. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- Myth: “Moving forward means forgetting or ‘letting go’ completely.” Reality: Continuing bonds and meaningful remembrance can be part of healthy adjustment. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)

***

### Module 4: Coping with grief waves and triggers

**Learning goals**  
- Explain grief “waves” and common triggers.  
- Offer gentle, practical coping strategies.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Grief often comes in waves—periods of intense emotion followed by quieter times; triggers like anniversaries, songs, places, smells, or unexpected reminders can bring strong waves, even years later. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- Understanding the Dual Process Model can reduce guilt about having moments of distraction or even joy; both grief‑focused and life‑focused moments are part of healing. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10848151/)
- Skills for riding waves can include grounding techniques (breathing, noticing senses), naming feelings, safe expression (crying, journaling, art), and reaching out to trusted people. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- Many people find it helpful to plan ahead for known triggers (birthdays, anniversaries, holidays) with simple rituals or support plans. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)

***

### Module 5: Self‑care during loss (basic needs, gentle routines)

**Learning goals**  
- Emphasize compassionate, realistic self‑care.  
- Help learners build small routines that support their nervous system.  

**Core teaching points**  
- In acute grief, basic needs often suffer; eating small, frequent meals, staying hydrated, taking medications as prescribed, and trying for regular sleep are foundational forms of self‑care. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)
- Gentle movement (e.g., short walks, stretching), time in nature, and low‑pressure social contact can support physical and emotional regulation, but should be framed as invitations, not “shoulds.” [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- Self‑compassion practices—speaking to oneself as one would to a dear friend, allowing rest, and recognizing limits—are linked to better adjustment and less self‑criticism in bereavement. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)
- Encouraging pacing (doing a little, resting a little) aligns with restoration‑oriented coping and can help prevent overwhelm. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)

***

### Module 6: Continuing bonds (maintaining connection with who/what was lost)

**Learning goals**  
- Introduce the idea that ongoing connection can be healthy.  
- Offer concrete ways to honor and remember.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Continuing bonds theory suggests that healing often involves creating an inner, ongoing relationship with the person or part of life that was lost, rather than cutting off all connection. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- Examples of continuing bonds include talking to the person in one’s mind or prayers, keeping meaningful objects, engaging in rituals, carrying forward their values, or doing acts of kindness in their memory. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- For non‑death losses (health, identity, job), continuing bonds can look like honoring past versions of self, acknowledging skills and lessons learned, or integrating the loss into one’s story in a respectful way. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- It is important to distinguish comforting, flexible connections from patterns that keep someone completely stuck (e.g., being unable to engage in any new activities or roles for many months); this is where professional support can help. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)

***

### Module 7: Support systems and asking for help

**Learning goals**  
- Normalize needing support during grief.  
- Help learners map and use different types of support.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Grief can be isolating, and people around the bereaved may not know what to say; having a variety of supports (friends, family, spiritual communities, peer groups, professionals) can help meet different needs. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- Asking for specific help (e.g., “Can you bring a meal on Tuesday?” “Can you sit with me tonight?”) often leads to better support than general requests. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)
- Grief support groups, whether in‑person or online, allow people to share with others who “get it,” and research suggests they can reduce grief intensity and improve social functioning for many participants. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- Professional help (grief‑informed therapists, counselors, or faith leaders) can be particularly important when grief feels overwhelming, prolonged, or intertwined with trauma or prior mental health conditions. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)

***

### Module 8: Finding meaning and moving forward (not “moving on”)

**Learning goals**  
- Reframe “moving on” to “moving forward with.”  
- Introduce meaning‑making in accessible language.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Many people describe “rebuilding” life after loss, which can involve integrating grief into a new sense of self, roles, and priorities, rather than returning to life “as if nothing happened.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- Meaning‑making can include personal growth, new or deepened relationships, advocacy or volunteering, creative work, spiritual or philosophical shifts, and quiet, private changes in what feels important. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- Moving forward does not erase love or pain; instead, it reflects the capacity to carry grief alongside moments of connection, purpose, and even joy. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- For some people, especially those experiencing PGD, structured professional treatment focused on grief and meaning‑making can be an important part of moving forward. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)

## 3. Interactive elements (education & reflection)

These are non‑diagnostic, reflective tools that can be implemented as MDX components or HTML5 widgets.

### 3.1 Grief experience self‑reflection (NOT assessment)

**Purpose**  
- Invite users to notice and name their grief experiences without scoring or labels.

**Content ideas**  
Prompt with checkboxes or free‑text:  
- “Since my loss, I have noticed in my body…” (e.g., fatigue, tight chest, restlessness).  
- “Emotionally, I have felt…” (sadness, anger, relief, numbness, guilt, gratitude, other). [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)
- “In my thoughts, I have experienced…” (memories, disbelief, worry, mental fog, questions about ‘Why?’). [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2022.880380/full)
- “In my behavior, I have…” (withdrawn, reached out, kept busy, struggled with routines). [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)

End with gentle normalization text: “There is no right or wrong set of answers. This reflection is one way to honor what you’re going through and to prepare for conversations with trusted people or professionals.”

***

### 3.2 Self‑care checklist for grieving

**Purpose**  
- Translate self‑care research into small, doable actions.

**Content ideas**  
List items with toggles or checkboxes, prefaced with “If it feels possible, you might consider…”:  
- “I had at least one nourishing food or snack today.” [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK613190/)
- “I drank water or other hydrating fluids several times today.”  
- “I took prescribed medications or attended medical appointments I need.”  
- “I moved my body in a gentle way (e.g., short walk, stretching).” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- “I had at least one supportive contact (message, call, presence), even if brief.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- “I allowed myself a moment of rest without judging myself.”  

Include reminder text: “This is not a test. Some days, just getting through is enough.”

***

### 3.3 Grief triggers awareness worksheet

**Purpose**  
- Help learners identify triggers and plan coping.

**Content fields**  
Columns:  
- “Trigger (date, place, situation, reminder).”  
- “What feelings show up?”  
- “How does my body react?”  
- “What has helped me before, or what might I try?”  

Example entry:  
- Trigger: “Their birthday approaching.”  
- Feelings: “Sadness, longing, irritability.”  
- Body: “Tired, tight chest.”  
- Coping: “Plan a small ritual, ask a friend to check in that day, allow myself to cry.” [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)

***

### 3.4 Memory and connection activities

**Purpose**  
- Offer structured ways to engage in continuing bonds and meaning‑making.

**Activity ideas**  
- “Memory pages”: Guided prompts such as “One funny memory,” “Something they taught me,” “A place that reminds me of them.” [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- “Values handoff”: Reflection on “What values or qualities from this person/period do I want to carry forward?” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)
- “Letter writing”: Write a letter to the person or to the part of life that was lost; learners may choose to keep, share, or ritualize this in their own way. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- For non‑death losses: “Honoring what was” (listing skills, experiences, or aspects of identity that remain part of the person’s story).  

***

### 3.5 Support system mapper

**Purpose**  
- Help learners visualize and diversify sources of support.

**Content structure**  
- Circles or columns labeled: “Inner circle” (closest people), “Everyday support” (colleagues, neighbors, community), “Professional/Spiritual support” (therapists, support groups, faith leaders), “Online/Helpline resources.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- Prompts:  
  - “Who feels emotionally safe to talk to?”  
  - “Who is practical help (rides, meals, childcare)?”  
  - “Who can support my spiritual or cultural rituals?”  
- Action prompt: “Choose one small outreach you might make this week (e.g., send a text, look up a support group, schedule an appointment).”

## 4. Safety protocols & key educational messages

These points should appear in course intros, footers, and relevant modules.

### 4.1 Grief and suicidal thoughts (988 integration)

- Intense grief can sometimes include thoughts like “What’s the point?” or “I wish I could be with them,” which may or may not reflect suicidal intent; this distinction is important and always deserves care and attention. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- Educational text: “If you are thinking about ending your life, feel unable to stay safe, or are making plans to harm yourself, this is an emergency. In the United States, you can call or text 988 or use chat at 988lifeline.org for immediate support; in other countries, please contact your local emergency number or crisis line.” [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)
- Clarify that crisis services are for moments of intense distress, not only when someone is “sure” they will act, and that reaching out is a sign of care, not weakness.  

### 4.2 Prolonged Grief Disorder signs (when to seek help)

- Educational markers (not for self‑diagnosis) may include: grief that remains very intense and distressing most days well beyond a year after a death, persistent yearning, feeling life is meaningless without the deceased, severe trouble functioning at work or home, and feeling “stuck” rather than slowly changing over time. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9159802/)
- Encourage seeking professional help if grief feels unmanageable, is worsening, or interferes with functioning, regardless of how long it has been. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)
- Note that only trained clinicians can diagnose PGD or other conditions, and that getting help is about accessing support, not being judged.  

### 4.3 Anniversary reactions awareness

- Anniversary reactions are temporary increases in grief symptoms around meaningful dates (death date, birthday, holidays, hospitalizations, or other loss‑related events), and they are common even years later. [cascadecounselingutah](https://www.cascadecounselingutah.com/blog/understanding-the-dual-process-model-of-grief-a-balanced-approach-to-healing/)
- Normalize that these reactions do not mean someone is “back at the beginning”; they often reflect love and memory, not a lack of progress. [bluenotepsychotherapy](https://www.bluenotepsychotherapy.com/blog-1-1/grief-models-part-4-the-dual-process-model)
- Encourage planning gentle rituals, extra support, or lighter schedules around significant dates when possible.  

### 4.4 Complicated grief risk factors

- Explain that some factors can increase vulnerability: sudden or violent losses, deaths involving children, prior trauma, limited social support, pre‑existing mental health conditions, and very high dependency on the person who died. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolonged_grief_disorder)
- Also note contextual factors like stigma (e.g., suicide, overdose), multiple losses in a short time, or systemic barriers (e.g., financial stress, discrimination) that can compound grief. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6354344/)
- Emphasize that risk factors are not destiny; they simply highlight who might benefit from extra monitoring and support.  

### 4.5 Distinguishing grief from depression (education only)

- Grief and depression can overlap, but some differences include: in grief, painful feelings often come in waves and are closely tied to thoughts about the loss, while in major depression, mood is more persistently low and less tied to specific reminders. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)
- In grief, self‑esteem is usually preserved (“I miss them so much”), whereas in depression, people often experience strong self‑critical thoughts (“I’m worthless,” “Everything is my fault”). [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/prolonged-grief-disorder)
- Both grief and depression are serious; if someone feels numb, hopeless most of the time, or has persistent suicidal thoughts, a professional evaluation is important, regardless of labels. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11979903/)

- People grieve many kinds of loss: death, divorce or breakup, job loss,
