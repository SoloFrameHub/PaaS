When and How to Adjust Your Sleep Window

One of the most common questions I get in my practice is, "Do I have to stick to this short sleep window forever?" The answer is no! The initial window is a temporary tool to rebuild sleep pressure. Once your sleep becomes more efficient, we can gradually expand it.

The Guideline: We aim for a Sleep Efficiency of about 85-90%. If your efficiency is consistently higher than 90%, it's a sign your body is ready for a bit more time in bed. If it's below 80-85%, we hold steady.

Navigating Common Sleep Disruptions

Here is some gentle guidance for maintaining your resilience when life gets in the way. The key in all these scenarios is to get back to your core routine as soon as possible.

Travel & Jet Lag

Be patient with your body. Adjust to the new time zone as quickly as possible by adopting local meal times and getting morning sunlight. If you have a bad night, don't nap for hours. Instead, stick to your routine and trust your body to recalibrate.

High-Stress Periods

During stressful times, your wind-down routine becomes even more critical. Don't skip it! Double down on the practices that calm you, like your "Worry Time" journal or gentle stretching. Protect your sleep schedule fiercely; it's your best defense against burnout.

When You're Sick

Listen to your body. When you're ill, your body needs more rest for immune function. This is a time to be flexible with your sleep window. Allow yourself to rest as much as you need, and then gently return to your schedule once you're feeling better.

Social Events & Late Nights

It's important to live your life! Enjoy the late night. The next day, simply get up at your regular wake-up time (even if it's tough) and avoid napping. This gets you back on track in a single day. One late night does not erase your progress.

What if I Just Can't Sleep? The Power of "Non-Sleep Deep Rest"

"Sometimes, despite our best efforts, sleep just doesn't come. In these moments, I invite you to change the goal. Instead of striving for sleep, strive for rest. Lying quietly in the dark, focusing on your breath, is still profoundly restorative for your nervous system. This is often called Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR). By giving yourself permission to just rest, you take the pressure off, which paradoxically makes sleep more likely to arrive."
