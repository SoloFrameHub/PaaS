## 1. Scope of the lesson (placeholders)

In the published literature, chronic loneliness is associated with:

- Elevated risk for depression, anxiety, and other mood and anxiety disorders.
- Higher all‑cause mortality and increased risk for cardiovascular disease, inflammation, and poorer immune function.

You can draft a psychoeducational section that:

- Distinguishes situational loneliness from chronic loneliness.
- Presents loneliness as a signal (like hunger) that encourages reconnection, rather than a character flaw.
- Emphasizes that social connection can be improved with skills practice and graded exposure‑style activities.

## 2. Guided Next.js flows (structure only)

Think of each guided flow as a small, client‑side wizard that **stores no PHI by default** and clearly labels responses as educational reflections, not clinical records.

Example component shells you can adapt:

- `components/loneliness/SocialGoalPlanner.tsx`
Steps:

1. Identify a low‑intensity social contact goal (e.g., “Send a quick text to a friend this week”).
2. Rate current confidence (1–10) and anticipated discomfort.
3. Plan concrete steps and timing (“I will do this on Tuesday at 7 p.m.”).
4. Record after the attempt: what happened, what they learned, whether the goal was “good enough.”
- `components/loneliness/SocialExperimentWizard.tsx`
Steps:

1. Name a negative belief (“If I talk to people, they will find me boring”).
2. Define a brief social experiment (“Say hello to one cashier and see what happens”).
3. Predict feared outcome vs real outcome.
4. Reflect on the evidence for and against the belief after the experiment.

For each component, define TypeScript types that avoid identifiers:

```ts
// components/loneliness/types.ts
export type SocialGoalStatus = "planned" | "completed" | "abandoned";

export interface SocialGoalEntry {
  id: string;
  createdAtISO: string;
  description: string;          // e.g., "Sent a kind text"
  domain?: "texting" | "calling" | "inPerson";
  predictedDiscomfort: number;  // 0–10
  actualDiscomfort?: number;    // 0–10
  status: SocialGoalStatus;
  reflections?: string;         // user‑written; warn about PHI
}
```

## 3. Journaling components for connection and values

To promote self‑reflection without collecting identifiable data:

- Use types such as:

```ts
export interface SocialConnectionEntry {
  id: string;
  dateISO: string;
  contactType: "online" | "phone" | "inPerson" | "none";
  contactEffort: "no effort" | "minimal" | "moderate" | "high";
  feltCloseness: number;  // e.g., 0–10
  barriers?: string;
  insights?: string;
}
```

- Turn these into guided questions:
    - “Where on a 0–10 scale did you feel connected today?”
    - “What small thing might increase your sense of connection tomorrow?”
    - “What values matter most to you in relationships (e.g., kindness, curiosity, honesty)? How could one tiny step today align with that value?”

Clarify in the UI:

- “You are not required to name specific people; you can answer in general terms.”

## 4. Accessibility and UX for social anxiety / neurodivergence

Guidelines to implement as reusable patterns across your loneliness module:

- Predictable navigation:
    - Fixed‑order steps in every wizard (e.g., “Step 1 of 5”).
    - Always‑visible “Back” and “Next” / “Save and continue” links so users feel in control.
- Clear labels and predictable form structure:

```
- Each form section has a clear `<legend>`; inputs have `<label>` tied by `htmlFor`.  
```

    - Progress is visible both visually and via `aria-current="step"`.
- Reduced anxiety‑inducing friction:
    - “Save and exit” at every screen so users can pause without losing progress.
    - No mandatory sharing; if you later build clinician‑sharing, make it explicit opt‑in external to the platform (e.g., “You can download this as a PDF and choose whether to share it with your therapist.”).
- Plain‑language feedback:
    - Avoid labeling findings as “diagnostic” or “clinical”; instead, frame everything as “reflections” or “patterns to notice.”
    - Provide gentle, non‑judgmental messages when users skip optional questions: “It’s okay to leave this blank or come back later.”

***

## 5. PHI, anonymity, and clinician‑sharing language

Since you are working in a HIPAA‑inspired context:

- By default:
    - Store journal entries, goals, and experiments locally (e.g., `localStorage` or IndexedDB) without linking to accounts or IPs.
    - Never prompt users to name people, institutions, or personal details. Add visible banners:
        - “Please avoid entering names, email addresses, or other identifiable information in these fields.”
- If you later allow export or clinician sharing:
    - Make download and sharing **explicit opt‑in**, with a short statement explaining:
        - “Downloading this file gives you a copy of your reflections, which may contain personal information you entered.”
        - “You control whether to send this file to a clinician or keep it private.”
    - Treat any exported file as potentially PHI and instruct users to store it securely.
