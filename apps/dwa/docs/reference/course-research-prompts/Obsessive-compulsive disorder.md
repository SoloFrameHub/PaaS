Obsessive-compulsive disorder is a common, highly **treatable** condition, and this “OCD Toolkit” can help people understand their symptoms, feel less alone, and get ready to engage in effective professional care, especially ERP-based treatment. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)

## 1. Evidence base for the OCD Toolkit

### 1.1 ERP as gold-standard treatment

- Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) is a structured form of cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) where people intentionally face feared thoughts, images, or situations (exposure) while resisting rituals and avoidance (response prevention). [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/about-ocd/treatment/erp/)
- Multiple clinical trials and meta-analyses show ERP significantly reduces OCD severity and is recognized as a first-line, evidence-based treatment by major professional organizations. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)
- ERP works by breaking the learned link between obsessions, anxiety, and compulsions, allowing anxiety to decline naturally and teaching the brain that feared outcomes are unlikely or manageable. [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)
- Reviews and real-world data indicate that a large majority of patients experience meaningful, often long-lasting improvement when they complete a full course of ERP. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2211364921000646)

### 1.2 OCD subtypes and presentations

- While DSM-5 does not formally subtype OCD, research repeatedly finds common symptom dimensions: contamination/cleaning, symmetry/ordering, harm/checking, taboo or “unacceptable” thoughts (e.g., sexual, aggressive, religious), and sometimes hoarding. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
- Contamination OCD often involves intense fear of germs, illness, or feeling “dirty,” with repetitive decontamination rituals such as excessive washing or cleaning. [treatmyocd](https://www.treatmyocd.com/what-is-ocd/info/ocd-subtypes/what-are-the-4-main-types-of-ocd-what-experts-say/b?smo=1)
- Symmetry or “just right” OCD includes obsessions about things needing to feel even, exact, or complete, paired with arranging, ordering, repeating, or counting rituals. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- Harm OCD involves intrusive fears of accidentally or intentionally harming self or others, often leading to checking, reassurance seeking, or avoidance of perceived risk. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
- Taboo/unacceptable thought OCD centers on intrusive sexual, violent, or blasphemous images and ideas that feel deeply at odds with the person’s values, often accompanied by mental rituals or avoidance. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)

### 1.3 Cognitive therapy for OCD

- Cognitive models of OCD emphasize the role of how people interpret intrusive thoughts—for example, believing that having a disturbing thought means they are dangerous or immoral (“thought-action fusion”). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)
- Cognitive therapy for OCD targets beliefs about responsibility, overestimation of threat, intolerance of uncertainty, and perfectionism to reduce distress and reliance on compulsions. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)
- Meta-analytic work suggests that adding cognitive elements to ERP can further enhance treatment response for some patients, though ERP-based CBT remains the backbone of psychotherapy for OCD. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S016517812200453X)

### 1.4 Self-help OCD intervention research

- Guided self-help and digital programs using ERP and CBT principles can produce moderate symptom improvements, especially when accompanied by some clinician support, although effects are generally smaller than full therapist-delivered ERP. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2211364921000646)
- Telehealth ERP programs have shown significant average reductions in OCD severity over about 12 weeks, suggesting that remote, structured, skills-based interventions can be effective when properly designed and supported. [treatmyocd](https://www.treatmyocd.com/what-is-ocd/what-is-erp)
- Self-help tools are best framed as supplements to, not replacements for, individualized ERP with a trained clinician, particularly for severe or complex presentations. [treatmyocd](https://www.treatmyocd.com/what-is-ocd/what-is-erp)

### 1.5 Medication options (SSRIs for OCD)

- Serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs and clomipramine) are evidence-based pharmacological treatments for OCD and are endorsed as first-line options in major treatment guidelines. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/ocd-treatment-guide/understanding-medication/)
- Treatment of OCD typically requires higher SSRI doses and longer trials than depression; guidelines recommend at least 8–12 weeks, with 6 or more weeks at moderate to high target doses. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5310107/)
- Meta-analyses indicate that efficacy increases up to approximately a fluoxetine-equivalent dose around 40 mg, though some guideline tables support higher ranges (e.g., 60–80 mg equivalents) while balancing side effects and tolerability. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2021.717999/full)
- Medication plus ERP is often considered when symptoms are severe, when ERP alone is not sufficient, or when access to specialized psychotherapy is limited. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/ocd-treatment-guide/understanding-medication/)

## 2. Patient-friendly content for 8 modules

Below is MDX-ready content scaffolding; each module includes plain-language explanations, validation, and key takeaways.

### Module 1: Understanding OCD

**Learning goals (patient-facing)**  
- Understand what OCD is and is not.  
- Learn the difference between obsessions and compulsions.  
- See why OCD is sometimes called “the doubt disease.”  

**Core teaching points**  
- OCD is a mental health condition where a person experiences intrusive, unwanted thoughts, images, or urges (obsessions) and feels driven to do repetitive behaviors or mental acts (compulsions) to reduce distress or prevent something bad. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)
- Obsessions are not chosen; they show up automatically, often about topics that matter deeply to the person (e.g., safety, morality, health). [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
- Compulsions can be visible (washing, checking, re-arranging, asking for reassurance) or invisible (mental reviewing, silent praying, neutralizing images). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- OCD is sometimes called “the doubt disease” because it attacks the feeling of certainty; even everyday situations can feel loaded with “what if?” questions. [harbormentalhealth](https://harbormentalhealth.com/2021/12/19/obsessive-compulsive-disorder-ocd-2/)
- Many people without OCD have random weird thoughts, but in OCD those thoughts feel sticky, scary, and “urgent,” leading to a cycle that takes over time and energy. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)

**Normalizing & hope language**  
- Emphasize: “You are not your thoughts; OCD targets what you care about most.”  
- Highlight: “OCD is highly treatable with the right tools and professional support.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)

**Suggested MDX snippet (outline style)**  
- `## What is OCD?`  
- `## Obsessions vs. compulsions`  
- `## Why OCD latches onto doubt`  
- `## How OCD shows up in everyday life`  

***

### Module 2: Common OCD themes

**Learning goals**  
- Recognize common OCD themes.  
- Understand that OCD can attach to many topics, including taboo ones.  
- Reduce shame through psychoeducation.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Researchers observe several common OCD dimensions: contamination/cleaning, symmetry and “just right,” harm and responsibility, taboo/unacceptable thoughts, and sometimes hoarding or collecting. [harbormentalhealth](https://harbormentalhealth.com/2021/12/19/obsessive-compulsive-disorder-ocd-2/)
- Contamination OCD can involve fears of germs, illness, chemicals, bodily fluids, or feeling morally “contaminated,” leading to cleaning, washing, or avoidance rituals. [treatmyocd](https://www.treatmyocd.com/what-is-ocd/info/ocd-subtypes/what-are-the-4-main-types-of-ocd-what-experts-say/b?smo=1)
- Symmetry/“just right” OCD may involve arranging items, repeating actions, or doing things until they feel exact or “even enough.” [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
- Harm OCD involves fears of accidentally or intentionally harming self or others (e.g., “What if I push someone in front of a train?”), often followed by checking or avoiding. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- Taboo-thought OCD brings distressing sexual, violent, or religious images or urges that clash with the person’s values; people with this form of OCD are not more likely to act on these thoughts, but feel extreme guilt and fear. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)

**Normalizing & hope language**  
- Emphasize: “Having an intrusive thought, even a disturbing one, does not mean you want it or will act on it.”  
- Clarify: “Therapists trained in OCD understand these themes and will not judge you for talking about them.”  

***

### Module 3: The OCD cycle

**Learning goals**  
- Learn the standard OCD cycle (trigger → obsession → anxiety → compulsion → relief → stronger OCD).  
- Begin to see patterns in one’s own experience.  

**Core teaching points**  
- A trigger can be anything: a situation, a sensation, a memory, a word, or even an internal image. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/about-ocd/treatment/erp/)
- The trigger activates an obsession—an intrusive thought or feeling (“What if I left the stove on?” “What if I offended God?”), which produces anxiety, guilt, disgust, or a sense of incompleteness. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)
- Compulsions are attempts to feel better or get certainty, such as checking, washing, asking for reassurance, or mentally reviewing scenarios. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- Compulsions bring short-term relief, but they teach the brain that the obsession was dangerous and that rituals are necessary, which strengthens OCD over time. [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)

**Visual explanation**  
- Text-based diagram:  
  - Trigger → Obsession → Distress → Compulsion → Temporary relief → More doubt next time.  

***

### Module 4: Why compulsions make OCD worse

**Learning goals**  
- Understand how compulsions backfire in the long term.  
- Build motivation to consider ERP-based change with a professional.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Compulsions are like “short-term painkillers” for anxiety; they work briefly but keep the underlying fear alive and often make it grow. [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)
- When you perform a ritual, your brain learns: “I only stayed safe because I did the compulsion,” so it demands more rituals in the future. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/about-ocd/treatment/erp/)
- Avoidance is also a compulsion—skipping situations, people, or objects to prevent anxiety keeps you from discovering that you can actually handle your fears. [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)
- Understanding this paradox (“the more I ritualize, the more stuck I get”) is a key step toward engaging in ERP with guidance.  

**Motivational framing**  
- “You are not weak for doing compulsions; they are your brain’s attempt to protect you. ERP teaches your brain a new, more effective way to feel safe.”  

***

### Module 5: Introduction to ERP concepts

**Learning goals**  
- Learn what ERP is in accessible language.  
- Understand the basic idea of facing fears without rituals.  
- Clarify that this course is not a substitute for ERP with a trained clinician.  

**Core teaching points**  
- ERP involves gradually and intentionally facing feared triggers (exposure) while resisting rituals, safety behaviors, or avoidance (response prevention). [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/about-ocd/treatment/erp/)
- Over time, as you stay in the situation without rituals, your anxiety naturally rises and then falls, and your brain learns that you can handle it and that feared outcomes are less likely than OCD suggests. [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)
- ERP is collaborative and paced; therapists usually create a hierarchy from easier to harder situations and work through them step by step. [my.clevelandclinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/erp-therapy)
- Research shows ERP is highly effective for many people with OCD and is considered a first-line treatment by expert organizations. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2211364921000646)

**Important boundary**  
- Repeatedly state: “This module introduces ERP concepts for education only. Actual ERP should be designed and guided by a qualified therapist who understands OCD.”  

***

### Module 6: Managing intrusive thoughts

**Learning goals**  
- Normalize intrusive thoughts.  
- Teach that thoughts do not equal actions or intentions.  
- Offer basic cognitive and mindfulness-based strategies (for education/preview).  

**Core teaching points**  
- Intrusive thoughts are random mental events; studies show that almost everyone occasionally has strange, unwanted thoughts, including about harm, sex, or blasphemy. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
- In OCD, the problem is not the thought itself but how it is interpreted—e.g., “If I think it, I must secretly want it” (thought-action fusion). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)
- A helpful stance is “Maybe, maybe not”—acknowledging uncertainty instead of chasing 100% certainty, which OCD never allows. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)
- Mindfulness-informed approaches encourage noticing a thought (“There’s my harm OCD thought again”) without arguing with it or trying to neutralize it, and then returning attention to chosen actions. [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)

**Key reassurance (Harm OCD)**  
- People with Harm OCD are not more likely to commit violence; their distress, avoidance, and help-seeking reflect strong values and a fear of doing harm, not a wish to do it. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)

***

### Module 7: Building an OCD‑fighting lifestyle

**Learning goals**  
- Introduce supportive lifestyle habits that can complement professional treatment.  
- Emphasize realistic, gentle changes rather than perfectionism.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Regular sleep, balanced nutrition, movement, and consistent routines can help regulate mood and make ERP work easier, though they do not replace treatment. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/ocd-treatment-guide/understanding-medication/)
- Stress management skills (breathing exercises, values-based activities, social support) can reduce overall vulnerability to OCD flare-ups. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)
- Reducing rituals gradually with professional guidance and limiting “OCD accommodation” from family (e.g., endless reassurance) are important long-term strategies. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/about-ocd/treatment/erp/)
- Relapse prevention often includes keeping a list of ERP gains, continuing occasional exposures, and having a plan for early warning signs. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S016517812200453X)

***

### Module 8: Professional treatment and finding an OCD specialist

**Learning goals**  
- Explain treatment options and how to seek help.  
- Show what to expect in ERP and medication visits.  
- Direct users to credible OCD specialist resources.  

**Core teaching points**  
- Evidence-based treatments include ERP-focused CBT, SSRIs or clomipramine, and sometimes a combination, especially for more severe OCD. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5310107/)
- An adequate SSRI trial usually lasts at least 8–12 weeks at moderate to high doses specific for OCD, and many people continue medication beyond acute treatment based on shared decision-making with their prescriber. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2021.717999/full)
- The International OCD Foundation (IOCDF) maintains directories of clinicians and programs trained in OCD and ERP, including intensive and specialty services. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/ocd-treatment-guide/understanding-medication/)
- Telehealth ERP programs and OCD specialty clinics can expand access when local providers are limited. [my.clevelandclinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/erp-therapy)

**Empowerment message**  
- Encourage patients to ask clinicians directly, “What experience do you have treating OCD with ERP?” and to seek second opinions if they feel their OCD is not understood.

## 3. Interactive elements (MDX/HTML5-ready concepts)

Below are conceptual designs suitable for LearnWorlds or MDX components; you can wrap these in custom HTML5/JS/React-style components as allowed by your platform.

### 3.1 OCD symptoms self-check (psychoeducational, not diagnostic)

**Purpose**  
- Help users recognize possible OCD patterns and decide whether to seek a professional evaluation.  

**Structure (content)**  
- Intro text: “This self-check is for education only and does not provide a diagnosis. For any concerns, please consult a licensed mental health professional.”  
- Items (Likert scale: Never / Sometimes / Often / Almost always):  
  - “I experience unwanted, repetitive thoughts, images, or urges that feel intrusive and hard to control.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)
  - “I feel driven to repeat certain behaviors (e.g., washing, checking, arranging) to reduce anxiety or prevent something bad.” [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
  - “These thoughts or rituals take a lot of my time or get in the way of my daily life.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6343408/)
  - “I feel intense guilt, disgust, or fear about thoughts that go against my values (e.g., harm, sex, religion).” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- Feedback logic (non-clinical):  
  - Low endorsement → “Your answers do not strongly suggest OCD, but only a professional can provide clarity.”  
  - High endorsement → “Your answers suggest experiences that are common in OCD; it may be helpful to seek an evaluation from a clinician familiar with OCD and ERP.”

### 3.2 Obsession–compulsion mapping worksheet

**Purpose**  
- Help users visualize their own OCD cycle.  

**Content fields**  
- Column 1: “Trigger” (situation, internal feeling, image).  
- Column 2: “Obsession” (thought/urge/image).  
- Column 3: “Feelings” (anxiety, disgust, guilt, doubt).  
- Column 4: “Compulsion/ritual” (behavior or mental act).  
- Column 5: “Short-term effect” (relief, certainty, feeling “just right”).  
- Column 6: “Long-term effect” (more doubt, more rituals, less freedom).  

**Example row**  
- Trigger: “Leaving the house.”  
- Obsession: “What if I left the stove on and the house burns down?” [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
- Feelings: “Intense anxiety, tight chest, racing heart.”  
- Compulsion: “Check stove repeatedly, ask partner if it’s off.”  
- Short-term effect: “Relief for a few minutes.”  
- Long-term effect: “Next time, I feel even less sure unless I check again and again.” [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)

***

### 3.3 Trigger hierarchy introduction

**Purpose**  
- Educate users about hierarchies without guiding them through ERP independently.  

**Content**  
- Brief explanation: “In ERP, therapists often help you list feared situations from easiest to hardest; this is called a ‘hierarchy.’” [my.clevelandclinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/erp-therapy)
- Interactive list builder:  
  - Step 1: “List 5–10 OCD triggers.”  
  - Step 2: “Rate each 0–10 for anxiety.”  
  - Step 3: “Sort from lowest to highest anxiety.”  
- Safety text:  
  - “Do not attempt ERP alone for severe or complex OCD; use this exercise as a way to understand your OCD better and to prepare for work with a specialist.”  

***

### 3.4 Intrusive thought normalizing tool

**Purpose**  
- Reduce shame and “thought–action fusion” via psychoeducation and reflection.  

**Content ideas**  
- Step 1: Define intrusive thoughts, with examples that are clearly labeled as common in OCD, not endorsed by the platform. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- Step 2: Two-column reflection interface:  
  - Column A: “What my OCD says this thought means about me.”  
  - Column B: “What a compassionate, science-based perspective might say.”  
- Pre-filled examples:  
  - Thought: “I had a violent image about hurting someone I love.”  
  - OCD says: “I am dangerous and might snap.”  
  - Compassionate perspective: “People with OCD have these thoughts precisely because they care deeply; the thought is a symptom, not a desire.” [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)

***

### 3.5 Treatment readiness assessment

**Purpose**  
- Help users consider beliefs, motivation, and practical barriers to beginning ERP or medication.  

**Content (agreement scale)**  
- “I understand that recovery from OCD usually involves facing some of my fears.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)
- “I am willing to try reducing rituals with guidance, even if it feels uncomfortable.” [compasshealthcenter](https://compasshealthcenter.net/blog/how-exposure-response-prevention-erp-treats-ocd-at-compass-health-center/)
- “I feel able to commit time and energy to regular sessions or structured work.”  
- “I have concerns about treatment (e.g., cost, access, fear of judgment) that I would like to discuss with a provider.”  
- Automated feedback:  
  - High readiness: Encouraging message to schedule an ERP consultation.  
  - Mixed readiness: Suggest journaling questions and a list of questions to ask a potential therapist.  
  - Low readiness: Normalize fear, suggest more education and possibly a one-time consult to discuss options.

## 4. Safety protocols & messaging

These points should be woven throughout the course (e.g., in intro, footers, and relevant modules) in user-facing language.

### 4.1 Education vs. treatment

- Clearly state: “This course provides education about OCD and is not a substitute for an evaluation, diagnosis, or treatment by a licensed professional.”  
- Encourage users to seek individualized care, especially if symptoms are severe, worsening, or interfering with safety, work, school, or relationships. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935308/)

### 4.2 OCD requires professional treatment

- Emphasize that evidence-based treatment for OCD typically involves ERP with a trained therapist, with or without medication. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/ocd-treatment-guide/understanding-medication/)
- Clarify that self-guided work is best viewed as preparation or enhancement, not a full replacement, for care with an OCD specialist. [treatmyocd](https://www.treatmyocd.com/what-is-ocd/what-is-erp)

### 4.3 Harm OCD reassurance (thoughts ≠ actions)

- Explain that Harm OCD is characterized by intrusive, ego-dystonic thoughts (in conflict with a person’s values), not by actual violent intent. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3992249/)
- Emphasize: “Having a thought is not the same as wanting to act on it; intrusive thoughts are symptoms, not confessions.”  
- Reassure users that OCD specialists are familiar with these themes and that disclosure is an important step in receiving effective help.  

### 4.4 Suicidal OCD awareness

- Differentiate between:  
  - Suicidal OCD (ego-dystonic, fear-based intrusive thoughts about suicide, with distress and avoidance). [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/ocd/types-of-ocd)
  - Active suicidal intent (plans, desire to die, preparing for self-harm), which always requires urgent professional help.  
- Include clear crisis language, tailored to anonymous international audiences, for example:  
  - “If you are thinking about ending your life or feel you might act on suicidal thoughts, this is an emergency. Contact your local emergency number, go to the nearest emergency department, or reach out to a crisis line in your country immediately.”  

### 4.5 Finding IOCDF-trained specialists

- Direct users to the International OCD Foundation website, which offers an OCD therapist and program directory and additional educational materials. [iocdf](https://iocdf.org/ocd-treatment-guide/understanding-medication/)
- Encourage users to search specifically for clinicians with experience in ERP or OCD specialty programs, including telehealth options when local care is limited. [treatmyocd](https://www.treatmyocd.com/what-is-ocd/what-is-erp)
