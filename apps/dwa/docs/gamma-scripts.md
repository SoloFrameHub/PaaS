## 1. OCD Toolkit

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Understanding OCD: Breaking the Obsession-Compulsion Cycle"

Slide 1 — Title
"Understanding OCD: Breaking the Obsession-Compulsion Cycle"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson evidence-based course on the most misunderstood — and most treatable — anxiety disorder"

Slide 2 — The Truth About OCD
OCD is not about being neat or organized. Real OCD is a serious condition that can consume hours of a person's day and cause tremendous suffering. It's sometimes called "The Doubt Disease" because it targets the things you value most — your safety, your loved ones, your morality — and floods you with unbearable uncertainty.

Slide 3 — What You'll Learn
- What OCD actually is and how it differs from normal worry
- Common OCD themes: contamination, harm, symmetry, taboo thoughts
- The OCD cycle: trigger, obsession, distress, compulsion, temporary relief
- Why compulsions backfire and strengthen OCD over time
- Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP): the gold-standard treatment
- How to find an OCD specialist and what to expect

Slide 4 — The OCD Cycle
Trigger leads to Obsession (intrusive thought/image/urge) which leads to Distress (anxiety, guilt, disgust) which leads to Compulsion (ritual to reduce distress) which leads to Temporary Relief — but the cycle restarts stronger. Every compulsion teaches your brain the obsession was a real threat.

Slide 5 — The Core Message
"You are not your thoughts." Intrusive thoughts are normal — everyone has them. The difference with OCD is not the content of the thought, but the meaning attached to it and the compulsive response it triggers. This course will help you understand that distinction and prepare you for the most effective treatment available.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons covering: What OCD Is, Common Themes, The OCD Cycle, Why Compulsions Backfire, Introduction to ERP, Normalizing Intrusive Thoughts, Lifestyle Support, and Finding Professional Help. Duration: 8-10 hours. Evidence-based. Self-paced.

## 2. Panic Disorder

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Panic Attacks: Understanding & Overcoming the Fear of Fear"

Slide 1 — Title
"Panic Attacks: Understanding & Overcoming the Fear of Fear"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson CBT-based course for understanding panic physiology and breaking the panic cycle"

Slide 2 — What Happens During Panic
Your heart pounds. You can't breathe. You feel dizzy, your hands tingle, a wave of dread washes over you. You think: "I'm having a heart attack" or "I'm losing control." You are not dying. You are not going crazy. Your body's alarm system is firing when there is no real danger. A panic attack is your fight-or-flight response activated at the wrong time.

Slide 3 — The Panic Cycle
A panic attack creates the "fear of fear" cycle: Physical sensation (heart racing) leads to Catastrophic misinterpretation ("I'm having a heart attack") which leads to More anxiety which leads to More physical sensations. This cycle turns individual panic attacks into panic disorder. Breaking this cycle is the core of treatment.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- The physiology of panic: amygdala, stress hormones, and the fight-or-flight response
- Why panic sensations feel dangerous but are completely harmless
- How to break the fear-of-fear cycle using cognitive restructuring
- Interoceptive exposure: reducing fear of your own body sensations
- Breathing and grounding techniques for during an attack
- How to reduce safety behaviors and avoidance
- Lifestyle triggers: caffeine, sleep, alcohol
- Building a long-term maintenance plan

Slide 5 — The Science of Recovery
Panic attacks typically peak within 10 minutes and resolve within 20-30 minutes. They cannot harm you physically. CBT for panic disorder has a 70-90% success rate. The techniques in this course are the same ones used in clinical settings worldwide.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Covers: Panic Physiology, The Panic Cycle, Challenging Catastrophic Thoughts, Interoceptive Exposure, Breathing & Grounding, Reducing Avoidance, Lifestyle Factors, and Long-Term Management. Evidence-based. Self-paced.

## 3. Social Anxiety

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Social Anxiety: From Avoidance to Confidence"

Slide 1 — Title
"Social Anxiety: From Avoidance to Confidence"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson CBT course for overcoming the fear of judgment and building social confidence"

Slide 2 — More Than Shyness
Social anxiety is not introversion and it's not shyness. It's a persistent, intense fear of being judged, embarrassed, or humiliated in social situations — and it's the third most common mental health condition. The fear can be so powerful that people restructure their entire lives around avoiding it. But CBT research shows 47-74% of people with social anxiety show significant improvement with the right techniques.

Slide 3 — The Social Anxiety Cycle
Before a social situation: anticipatory anxiety and mental rehearsal. During: intense self-focus, monitoring for signs of failure, safety behaviors (avoiding eye contact, staying quiet). After: post-event rumination, replaying every moment, concluding it went badly. This three-phase cycle maintains and strengthens the fear.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- The difference between introversion, shyness, and social anxiety disorder
- The spotlight effect and mind-reading: cognitive distortions that fuel fear
- Cognitive restructuring to challenge fears of judgment
- Graduated exposure to social situations (step by step)
- Attention training: shifting focus outward instead of inward
- Social skills practice: conversation, assertiveness, active listening
- Long-term maintenance and relapse prevention

Slide 5 — The Path Forward
Social anxiety shrinks your world. Every avoided event, every declined invitation, every silent meeting makes the fear grow stronger. This course reverses that process — gradually, compassionately, with evidence-based tools. Your world can expand again.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Covers: Understanding Social Anxiety, The Social Anxiety Cycle, Cognitive Patterns, Challenging Fears, Graduated Exposure, Attention Shifting, Building Social Confidence, and Long-Term Management. Evidence-based. Self-paced.

## 4. Anger Management

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Understanding & Managing Anger: From Reactivity to Response"

Slide 1 — Title
"Understanding & Managing Anger: From Reactivity to Response"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson CBT course for understanding anger patterns and building healthier expression"

Slide 2 — Anger Is Not the Enemy
Everyone gets angry. Anger is one of the most basic human emotions — as natural as fear, sadness, or joy. It tells you something feels wrong, unfair, or threatening. Anger protects boundaries, detects injustice, and motivates change. The goal of this course is not to eliminate anger — it's to understand it, catch it earlier, and respond in ways that actually serve you.

Slide 3 — When Anger Becomes a Problem
Problem anger isn't defined by feeling angry — it's defined by intensity (explosive reactions), frequency (constant irritability), duration (holding grudges for days), and impact (damaged relationships, regret, consequences). Underneath anger often lives hurt, fear, shame, or depression.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- Distinguish healthy anger from problem anger
- Identify your personal triggers, body warning signs, and escalation patterns
- Arousal-reducing techniques: breathing, relaxation, strategic time-outs
- Challenge anger-fueling thought patterns: "shoulds," mind-reading, catastrophizing
- The difference between suppression, aggression, and healthy expression
- Assertive communication using I-statements and active listening
- Recognizing emotions underneath anger
- Repairing relationships after conflict

Slide 5 — The Myth of Venting
Research has consistently shown that "letting it out" — punching pillows, screaming, venting — actually increases anger, not decreases it. This course teaches what actually works: understanding the cycle, cooling down the body, challenging the thoughts, and expressing needs assertively.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. CBT-based strategies that reduce anger intensity and protect relationships. Self-paced. Evidence-based.

## 5. Bipolar Disorder

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Bipolar Disorder: Building Stability Through Understanding"

Slide 1 — Title
"Bipolar Disorder: Building Stability Through Understanding"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson psychoeducation course on managing bipolar I, II, and cyclothymia with evidence-based strategies"

Slide 2 — Beyond "Mood Swings"
Bipolar disorder is far more than everyday mood swings. It's a serious, chronic mental health condition involving distinct episodes of mania (or hypomania) and depression that can profoundly disrupt your life. Understanding the condition is the first and most powerful step toward stability. People with bipolar disorder spend significantly more time in depression than in mania — which is why this course covers both sides.

Slide 3 — The Bipolar Spectrum
Bipolar I: full manic episodes (7+ days, significant impairment, possible psychosis). Bipolar II: hypomanic episodes (less severe, shorter) plus major depressive episodes. Cyclothymia: chronic fluctuation between mild highs and lows. Mixed Features: simultaneous symptoms of mania and depression. Each type requires its own management approach.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- The differences between Bipolar I, II, cyclothymia, and mixed features
- How to track mood, sleep, energy, and irritability for early warning signs
- CBT skills to challenge extreme beliefs during mood episodes
- Daily rhythm stabilization using IPSRT (Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy)
- Managing depressive episodes with behavioral activation
- Recognizing early mania/hypomania and using harm reduction
- Understanding medication adherence as the cornerstone of stability
- Building a personalized relapse prevention and crisis plan

Slide 5 — The Power of Routine
Research shows that stabilizing daily routines — consistent sleep/wake times, regular meals, structured activity — is one of the most effective non-medication strategies for preventing mood episodes. This course teaches you how to build and protect those rhythms.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Grounded in CBT, IPSRT, and structured self-monitoring. This course is psychoeducation — it works alongside professional treatment, not as a replacement. Self-paced.

## 6. Depression Action

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Depression: From Understanding to Action"

Slide 1 — Title
"Depression: From Understanding to Action"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson evidence-based course for breaking the depression cycle using behavioral activation and cognitive skills"

Slide 2 — More Than Sadness
Everyone feels sad sometimes. But clinical depression is different — it's persistent, pervasive, and affects your ability to function. Depression isn't a choice, a weakness, or something you can "snap out of." It's a real condition with real neurological underpinnings. And here's the good news: behavioral activation — the core technique in this course — rivals medication effectiveness for mild-to-moderate depression.

Slide 3 — The Depression Trap
Depression creates a vicious cycle: low mood leads to withdrawal and inactivity, which leads to loss of pleasure and accomplishment, which leads to lower mood. This "depression trap" is self-reinforcing — but it can be broken. Behavioral activation works by reversing the cycle: taking small actions first, and letting mood follow.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- Distinguish normal sadness from clinical depression
- Understand the depression trap and how withdrawal maintains low mood
- Apply behavioral activation to break the inactivity cycle
- Reconnect with personal values and meaningful activities
- Challenge negative thought patterns using cognitive techniques
- Use exercise as an evidence-based mood treatment
- Implement lifestyle changes (sleep, nutrition) that support recovery
- Know when and how to seek professional help

Slide 5 — You Are Not Broken
Depression lies to you. It tells you nothing will help, that you're too tired to try, that you don't deserve to feel better. This course teaches you to act despite those thoughts — not because the thoughts are wrong, but because waiting to "feel like it" means waiting forever. Action comes first. Motivation follows.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Covers: Understanding Depression, The Depression Trap, Behavioral Activation, Values & Meaning, Cognitive Skills, Exercise as Medicine, Lifestyle Changes, and Seeking Help. Evidence-based. Self-paced.

## 7. Grief & Loss

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Navigating Grief: Understanding Loss & Finding Your Way"

Slide 1 — Title
"Navigating Grief: Understanding Loss & Finding Your Way"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson compassionate course on grief as a natural response — with no fixed timeline and no 'right' way"

Slide 2 — Your Grief Is Valid
If you're here, you're likely going through one of the hardest experiences a person can face. Before anything else: your grief is valid. However it looks, however it feels, however long it takes — it is a real and natural response to real loss. This course won't tell you how to grieve. There is no correct way. It offers information, gentle tools, and compassionate company.

Slide 3 — Grief Is Bigger Than You Think
Grief isn't just about death. People grieve the loss of relationships, health, abilities, homes, careers, safety, dreams, and identity. Grief is a whole-person experience — it affects your body (exhaustion, pain, appetite changes), your mind (confusion, disbelief), your emotions (sadness, anger, guilt, relief), and your behavior (withdrawal, restlessness).

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- Grief as a natural, whole-person response to many kinds of loss
- Common physical, emotional, cognitive, and behavioral grief experiences
- Challenging harmful myths (there are no fixed stages or timelines)
- Coping with grief waves and planning for triggers like anniversaries
- Compassionate self-care that supports your nervous system
- Continuing bonds: maintaining connection with who or what was lost
- Building a support system (personal, professional, community)
- Finding meaning without pressure to "move on" or forget

Slide 5 — Beyond the Five Stages
The "five stages of grief" model is outdated and often harmful. Modern grief research uses the Dual Process Model — you naturally oscillate between confronting grief (loss-oriented) and re-engaging with life (restoration-oriented). Both movements are healthy. Both are necessary.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Grounded in the Dual Process Model. Compassionate, evidence-based psychoeducation. No timelines. No "shoulds." Self-paced.

## 8. Low Self-Esteem

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Rebuilding Self-Worth: From Inner Critic to Inner Companion"

Slide 1 — Title
"Rebuilding Self-Worth: From Inner Critic to Inner Companion"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson CBT and compassion-focused course on understanding and changing the patterns behind low self-esteem"

Slide 2 — A Learned Pattern, Not a Fixed Trait
How do you feel about yourself deep down — not when things go well, but when you make a mistake, when no one is watching? For many people, the answer is "not great." Low self-esteem touches relationships, work, health, and the simple capacity to enjoy being alive. But here's what most people don't realize: self-esteem is a learned pattern of thinking, and because it is learned, it can be changed.

Slide 3 — The Self-Esteem Trap
CBT reveals a self-reinforcing cycle: Negative core beliefs ("I'm not good enough") lead to conditional rules ("I must be perfect or I'm worthless") which lead to safety behaviors (avoiding challenges, seeking reassurance) which lead to missed opportunities which confirm the original belief. This cycle can be interrupted at every stage.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- The CBT model: core beliefs, conditional assumptions, and safety behaviors
- Recognizing your inner critic and its patterns
- Challenging self-critical thoughts with structured thought records
- How core beliefs form through early experiences
- Self-compassion as an alternative to self-criticism (CFT techniques)
- Behavioral experiments to test negative self-beliefs
- Identifying personal strengths, values, and evidence of competence
- Building lasting self-worth and preventing relapse

Slide 5 — Self-Compassion Is Not Weakness
Many people resist self-compassion because they believe self-criticism motivates them. Research shows the opposite: self-compassion increases motivation, resilience, and willingness to try again after failure. Being kind to yourself is not letting yourself off the hook — it's giving yourself the support you need to grow.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Uses CBT, Schema Therapy, and Compassion-Focused Therapy. Evidence-based. Self-paced.

## 9. Managing Perfectionism

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Breaking Free from Perfectionism: When High Standards Become a Trap"

Slide 1 — Title
"Breaking Free from Perfectionism: When High Standards Become a Trap"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson CBT course on the transdiagnostic pattern behind anxiety, depression, OCD, and burnout"

Slide 2 — Not All Perfectionism Is Harmful
Have you ever spent hours reworking something already good enough — or avoided starting because you were afraid it wouldn't be perfect? Here's the nuance most people miss: not all perfectionism is harmful. Healthy high standards push you to grow. Maladaptive perfectionism keeps you stuck in a cycle of fear, procrastination, and self-criticism.

Slide 3 — The Perfectionism Trap
The trap: Set impossibly high standards. Either avoid the task (procrastination) or achieve it but dismiss the success ("Anyone could do that"). Conclusion: "I'm not good enough." Repeat. Perfectionism is self-reinforcing because success is never satisfying and failure confirms the worst fear.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- Adaptive high standards vs. maladaptive perfectionistic concerns
- Your personal perfectionism patterns: rigid rules, self-criticism, procrastination
- Cognitive restructuring to challenge perfectionistic thinking
- Behavioral experiments: deliberately testing "good enough"
- Exposure to imperfection and reducing checking behaviors
- Deeper schemas: "I am only worthwhile if I achieve"
- Self-compassionate alternatives to the inner perfectionist
- Building long-term flexibility and preventing relapse

Slide 5 — A Transdiagnostic Pattern
Perfectionism isn't a standalone problem — it's a pattern that fuels anxiety, depression, OCD, eating disorders, social anxiety, and burnout simultaneously. Treating perfectionism often improves multiple conditions at once. This makes it one of the highest-leverage targets in mental health.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. CBT and schema-based techniques. Evidence-based. Self-paced.

## 10. Stress & Burnout

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Stress & Burnout: Understanding, Recovery & Prevention"

Slide 1 — Title
"Stress & Burnout: Understanding, Recovery & Prevention"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson evidence-based course on chronic stress, burnout recognition, boundary-setting, and sustainable recovery"

Slide 2 — When Stress Doesn't Stop
Everyone experiences stress. It's normal and even helpful in short bursts. But when stress becomes chronic — when recovery never quite happens — it shifts into something more pervasive. Burnout is not laziness or weakness. The WHO defines it by three dimensions: emotional exhaustion, cynicism and mental distance, and reduced professional efficacy. It's about the mismatch between demands and resources.

Slide 3 — The Burnout Spectrum
You don't go from fine to burned out overnight. The spectrum: Engaged (challenged but energized) leads to Overextended (working harder, recovering less) leads to Disengaged (going through the motions) leads to Ineffective (doubting your competence) leads to Burnout (complete depletion across all three dimensions).

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- Distinguish normal stress, chronic stress, and burnout
- How chronic stress affects your brain, body, and immune system
- Identify personal triggers and early warning signs
- Set effective boundaries at work and in personal life
- Evidence-based stress relief: breathing, mindfulness, and movement
- The four recovery experiences that restore energy
- Sustainable lifestyle changes: sleep, movement, nutrition, connection
- How to rebuild after burnout and prevent relapse

Slide 5 — Recovery Is Not Optional
Burnout doesn't resolve with a vacation. It requires structural changes — to your boundaries, your routines, and often your environment. This course gives you the tools to both recover from burnout and build a life that prevents it from returning.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Research-backed strategies for stress management and burnout recovery. Self-paced. Evidence-based.

## 11. Trauma Recovery

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "Trauma Recovery: Understanding Your Brain's Protective Response"

Slide 1 — Title
"Trauma Recovery: Understanding Your Brain's Protective Response"
Subtitle: "An 8-lesson psychoeducation course on trauma, PTSD, grounding skills, and preparing for healing"

Slide 2 — What Happened TO You, Not What's Wrong WITH You
Trauma responses — hypervigilance, flashbacks, emotional numbness, difficulty sleeping — are not weaknesses or character flaws. They are your nervous system's survival mechanisms doing exactly what they were designed to do. The symptoms that feel like they're working against you are actually evidence that your brain is trying to keep you safe.

Slide 3 — How Trauma Changes the Brain
Three key brain changes: The amygdala (alarm system) becomes hyperactive, firing at non-threatening stimuli. The hippocampus (memory organizer) struggles to file traumatic memories properly, leaving them feeling "present" rather than "past." The prefrontal cortex (rational thinking) goes partially offline during activation.

Slide 4 — What You'll Learn
- What trauma is and how PTSD develops
- How trauma changes the brain: amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex
- The four stress responses: fight, flight, freeze, and fawn
- Grounding techniques for flashbacks, dissociation, and overwhelm
- Identifying personal triggers and building a coping plan
- Self-care and stabilization practices for daily life
- Building a safety plan and support system
- Evidence-based treatment options and when to seek help

Slide 5 — This Course Is a Foundation
This course is psychoeducation and skill-building — not therapy. It will not ask you to process traumatic memories. Instead, it builds the understanding, stability, and skills that make professional healing work more effective. Think of it as preparing the ground.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
8 lessons. 8-10 hours. Trauma-informed, evidence-based. Go at your own pace. Take breaks. Use grounding techniques. Your safety comes first. Self-paced.

## N3. Dietary Patterns for Mental Health (replaces dietary-patterns.pdf)

**Gamma prompt:**

Create a presentation titled "What You Eat Is Shaping Your Mood: The Science of Dietary Patterns"

Slide 1 — Title
"What You Eat Is Shaping Your Mood: The Science of Dietary Patterns"
Subtitle: "A 12-lesson evidence-based course on how ultra-processed foods, Mediterranean eating, psychobiotics, and global dietary patterns drive depression risk — and what to do about it"

Slide 2 — A 385,000-Person Wake-Up Call
If you eat like most people in a Western country, more than half your calories come from ultra-processed foods. In 2024, a landmark umbrella review published in The BMJ — pooling data from 385,000 participants — found that high consumption of ultra-processed foods is associated with 53% higher odds of depression and anxiety. This is not one quirky study. It's the convergent signal of a decade of nutritional psychiatry research. What you eat is actively shaping your mental health — and most people have never been told.

Slide 3 — What You'll Learn
- Why ultra-processed foods raise depression and anxiety risk by up to 53% — and the dose-response relationship that means every small change counts
- The three biological mechanisms linking diet to brain function: gut barrier damage, neuroinflammation, and neurotransmitter depletion
- The Mediterranean diet: clinical trial evidence showing up to 32% reduction in depression scores in 12 weeks
- How to calculate your Dietary Inflammatory Index (DII) and understand its direct link to mood
- Plant-based diets: the real mental health benefits and the nutrient risks you need to know
- Fermented foods vs. psychobiotic supplements — what the evidence actually supports and what it doesn't
- How dietary acculturation affects mental health in immigrant and diaspora communities
- How to build a personalized, culturally respectful, evidence-based dietary pattern

Slide 4 — From Correlation to Causation
Nutritional psychiatry has matured far beyond observational studies. The 2017 SMILES trial — a randomized controlled trial — showed a Mediterranean-style dietary intervention reduced depression scores by 32% in 12 weeks, with a number-needed-to-treat of 4.1. For comparison, many antidepressants have an NNT of 7-8. The dose-response relationship in UPF research (every 10% increase in UPF intake raises risk measurably) adds to the causal case. This course teaches you to read and apply this research — not as a diet prescription, but as a toolkit for making informed choices.

Slide 5 — Food Is Not Medicine. But the Pattern Matters.
This course is not about clean eating, restriction, or guilt. It's about understanding that the pattern of what you eat — over weeks and months — is one of the most powerful modifiable factors affecting your risk of depression and anxiety. Every meal is a small vote for your brain's chemistry. This course will show you what the evidence actually says, help you assess your own dietary pattern, and give you practical tools for change that fit your real life, your culture, and your budget.

Slide 6 — Course Structure
12 lessons. 10-12 hours. Covers: Ultra-Processed Food Risk, Mechanisms of Harm, Mediterranean Diet Evidence, Anti-Inflammatory Eating & the DII, Plant-Based Diets, Fiber & Prebiotics, Fermented Foods, Psychobiotic Supplements, Global Dietary Comparisons, Dietary Acculturation, Dietary Pattern Assessment, and Building Your Evidence-Based Pattern. Self-paced. Evidence-based.
