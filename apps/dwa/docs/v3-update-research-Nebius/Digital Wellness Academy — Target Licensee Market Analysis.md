# Digital Wellness Academy — Target Licensee Market Analysis

## Executive Summary

SoloFrameHub's Digital Wellness Academy platform is best positioned to target **mid-market independent and PE-backed psychiatric group practices** — organizations with 10 to 100+ providers and 3 to 50+ locations that are large enough to have a digital education budget but not large enough to build proprietary technology in-house. The U.S. outpatient psychiatry market reached $15.88 billion in 2024 and is growing at a 10.63% CAGR toward $39.22 billion by 2033, creating a deep and expanding pool of well-capitalized practices that need exactly what the platform offers. Only 6% of mental health facilities have adopted certified health information technology, which represents both the challenge and the opportunity: most practices are not yet digitally equipped, and the ones growing fastest are actively looking for vendor solutions.[^1][^2]

The platform should avoid the top three national-scale organizations (LifeStance, Talkiatry, Refresh/Optum) which are building proprietary infrastructure, and instead concentrate on the tier below: independently operated or PE-backed multi-location groups that have revenue but no tech team.

***

## Market Landscape

### Size of the Opportunity

There are 15,421 mental health treatment facilities in the U.S. as of 2024, of which approximately 8,270 are outpatient settings — the core venue for the platform's target licensees. Private equity has entered behavioral health aggressively: 60% of all PE deals since 2018 have involved behavioral health organizations, and PE now owns more than 25% of behavioral health facilities in states like North Carolina and Colorado. This PE activity is critical context — PE-backed practices have capital budgets, operational pressure to differentiate, and a mandate to scale — making them ideal technology buyers.[^3][^4]

Scaled outpatient mental health platforms are currently trading at 10–14× EBITDA multiples, with multi-state groups of 15–20+ locations commanding premium valuations. This means these organizations have strong financial incentives to invest in operational technology that improves outcomes, patient engagement, and clinician retention.[^5]

### The Technology Gap

Despite the market scale, HIT adoption in behavioral health remains dramatically behind other healthcare sectors. Only 6% of mental health facilities have adopted certified health IT, compared to 29% of broader behavioral health organizations. A 2024 ASPE study confirmed that digital strategies support integrated care models but face significant adoption and interoperability barriers. This gap is the platform's opening — there is no dominant player in behavioral health patient education at the practice licensing level, and the market is actively seeking solutions.[^6][^1]

Ninety-two percent of patients now expect digital interactions with healthcare providers, yet most psychiatric group practices cannot meet this expectation because they lack the tools. The platform bridges exactly this gap.[^7]

## Licensee Tiers

### Tier 1 — National Scale (Avoid for Direct Licensing)

These organizations are building or have built proprietary technology platforms. Direct platform licensing is unlikely, though co-marketing partnerships or white-label arrangements may be possible at a later stage.

| Organization | Scale | Status | Why Not a Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LifeStance Health** | 8,040 clinicians, 550+ centers, 33 states, $1.42B revenue (2025)[^8] | Public (NASDAQ: LFST) | Own tech stack, internally resourced |
| **Talkiatry** | 800+ psychiatrists, 3M visits, $210M Series D (Feb 2026)[^9][^10] | VC-backed, building own platform | Proprietary matching + care platform[^10] |
| **Refresh Mental Health** | 3,000 clinicians, 300 locations[^11] | Acquired by Optum/UnitedHealth (2022)[^12] | Corporate procurement; UHG controls tech decisions |

LifeStance's 2025 revenue grew 14% year-over-year to $1.424 billion, with 8,040 clinicians treating over 1 million unique patients through 9 million visits. Talkiatry raised an oversubscribed $210 million Series D in February 2026 to continue building its proprietary technology platform and expand its Mindshare Partner Program. Both organizations are investing in internal technology infrastructure that makes them unlikely near-term licensing candidates.[^8][^9][^13]

### Tier 2 — Mid-Market Sweet Spot (Primary Target)

These organizations are large enough to have technology budgets and operational pressure to differentiate, but have not built or announced proprietary patient education platforms. They are independently operated or PE-backed, giving individual leadership the authority to make a platform licensing decision.

#### Mindpath Health
**Profile:** 450+ clinicians, 80+ clinic locations, operating across Arizona, California, Florida, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Texas. Independently operated (not publicly traded or PE-owned as of current records) with a stated emphasis on technology and clinical research as part of its care model. Serves 70,000+ patients. Offers in-person and telehealth, with a full spectrum of psychiatry, therapy, and interventional services (TMS, esketamine).[^14][^15][^16]

**Why they fit:** Independent governance means the CMO or clinical leadership can make a platform licensing decision without navigating PE board approval or corporate procurement. At 450+ clinicians across 80+ locations, the license fee and revenue split create meaningful recurring revenue. Their stated mission of "technology and clinical research" advancement signals receptivity to digital tools. The multi-state footprint in TX and FL directly overlaps with SoloFrameHub's home market geography.[^14]

**Pitch angle:** Provider-assigned patient education between appointments reduces clinician burden, extends the therapeutic value of every session, and improves outcomes metrics — which directly supports their outcomes-based care narrative and referral retention.

#### Mindful Therapy Group
**Profile:** $100M–$250M revenue range, operating across Washington, Oregon, and Arizona with locations in Seattle, Spokane, Tacoma, Bellevue, Everett, Vancouver, and Goodyear AZ. Offers therapy, medication management, psychological testing, and group therapy. Accepts Medicaid in Washington. In-person and telehealth.[^17][^18][^19]

**Why they fit:** Revenue scale and multi-state footprint indicate operational maturity and budget. Group therapy is already a core service offering, which means the platform's group therapy cohort module is an immediate clinical fit — not a new capability they'd need to sell internally. No proprietary tech platform evident.

**Pitch angle:** The group therapy cohort system directly enhances how they run their existing group programs — structured curriculum, digital facilitator guides, and patient progress tracking between group sessions.

#### PsychPlus (Houston, TX)
**Profile:** Multi-location psychiatric practice based in Houston with clinics across Clear Lake, Heights, Katy, Kingwood, River Oaks, South Cypress, Sugar Land, Willowbrook, and The Woodlands. Services include psychiatry, therapy, Spravato (esketamine), TMS, neuropsychological testing, and Intensive Outpatient Programs (IOP). Walk-in availability at several locations.[^20]

**Why they fit:** Houston-based, directly in SoloFrameHub's home market. Multiple locations with intensive outpatient programming creates immediate demand for structured patient education curriculum. IOP programs in particular — which typically run 3-5 days per week for multiple weeks — are an ideal use case for cohort-based digital curriculum tied to each IOP track (e.g., DBT skills, trauma recovery).

**Pitch angle:** IOP track curriculum on the platform gives clinicians a structured digital backbone for their programs, extends therapeutic value outside session hours, and provides measurable patient progress data.

#### Ellie Mental Health
**Profile:** Franchise model, 200+ clinics opened as of December 2024, with territories available in most states. Franchise fee of $60,000 per clinic, total investment range $392K–$680K. Revenue grew 544% from 2018–2021. Developing its own patient app. The franchisor provides shared services including insurance credentialing, billing, scheduling, and proprietary software to franchisees.[^21][^22][^23]

**Why they fit:** A single licensing deal with the Ellie Mental Health franchisor (Mendota Heights, MN) would instantly distribute the platform across 200+ clinics without clinic-by-clinic sales. The franchisor controls technology and shared services decisions — one conversation, one contract. Their existing app development suggests openness to digital tools, and a white-labeled integration as "Ellie's patient education platform" powered by Digital Wellness Academy is a compelling partnership structure.[^22]

**Pitch angle:** White-label the platform as the official Ellie patient education curriculum. Position it as a franchisee benefit that differentiates Ellie from other therapy franchise models.

### Tier 3 — Independent Multi-Location Practices (Volume Target)

This tier encompasses the largest addressable market: independent psychiatric or therapy group practices with 10–50 providers across 3–15 locations. These are the practices that appear on fastest-growing lists, are backed by regional PE, and are actively investing in operational infrastructure.

Examples from the 2025 Inc. 5000 behavioral health fastest-growing list:[^17]
- **Tree of Life Counseling Center** (Freehold, NJ) — $5M–$10M revenue, 348% growth
- **AB Psych Consulting** (Fontana, CA) — $1M–$5M revenue, 461% growth, TMS services
- **Longbranch Recovery & Wellness** (Covington, LA) — $5M–$10M revenue, hybrid in-person/telehealth

These practices share a common profile: growing fast, adding providers, taking on payer contracts, but without dedicated technology staff. The platform's $5,500 license fee is a straightforward operational expense for an organization generating $5M+ in revenue, not a capital decision requiring board approval.

## What Practices Are Actually Buying in 2025

Black Book Research's 2025 behavioral health technology vendor survey, drawing from 1,308 behavioral health professionals, identified the top trends driving technology purchasing decisions:[^24]

- **Integration of behavioral and physical health:** Six in ten behavioral health IT professionals are prioritizing integrated solutions[^24]
- **Telehealth expansion:** 80% of clinicians plan to expand telehealth capabilities in 2025[^24]
- **Value-based care analytics:** 22% of providers investing in analytics to assess treatment efficacy and align with payer requirements[^24]
- **AI-powered clinical note-taking:** Clinicians are actively adopting these tools and "fighting to be first in line"[^25]

The platform addresses the first three directly: it is built as an integrated clinical support tool (not just consumer education), it extends patient engagement across telehealth and in-person delivery, and it captures assessment data that becomes outcomes evidence for payer negotiations.

Research also shows digital mental health tools are most effective when integrated into clinical workflows rather than deployed as standalone consumer apps. This is exactly the platform's architecture — provider-assigned, clinically integrated, not just a consumer download.[^26][^27]

## What Practices Need From a Platform Vendor

Healthcare organizations evaluating white-label and licensed platforms consistently prioritize these criteria:[^28][^29][^30]

| Criterion | Platform Response |
|---|---|
| HIPAA compliance with BAA | PostgreSQL RLS + GCP BAA per tenant |
| Configurable to clinical workflows | Provider portal with assignment engine, not generic |
| Scalable across providers and locations | Multi-tenant architecture, role-based access |
| Not displacing existing EHR | Complement to EHR, not replacement |
| Vendor support and discovery process | SoloFrameHub direct engagement model |
| Outcomes evidence | PHQ-9/GAD-7 trend data per patient |
| White-label capability | Tenant branding, custom domain |

A key insight from procurement research: practices are not buying platforms — they are buying vendor relationships. The decision-maker is typically the CMO or Practice Director, not IT. The pitch needs to be clinically framed, not technically framed.[^29]

## Sales & Outreach Strategy

### Decision-Maker Profile

The licensing decision is made by one of three people depending on practice size and structure:

1. **Chief Medical Officer (CMO)** — largest group practices; clinically oriented, focused on outcomes and provider experience
2. **Practice Administrator / CEO** — PE-backed groups; business-oriented, focused on operational efficiency and ROI
3. **Owner-Physician** — independent multi-location practices; both clinical and financial decision-maker

### Outreach Sequence

1. **LinkedIn direct outreach** to CMOs and Practice Administrators at target Tier 2 and Tier 3 organizations — personalized message referencing their specific service mix (e.g., group therapy, IOP programs)
2. **Conference presence** — Behavioral Health Tech (BHT) Conference is the highest-concentration venue for technology-receptive behavioral health operators; also OPEN MINDS Senior Executive Strategy Summit[^31]
3. **Clinical credentialing as lead generation** — CEU-eligible courses attract individual providers; when multiple providers from the same practice engage, flag the practice for a licensing conversation
4. **RPS as reference case** — Real Psychiatric Services as a live licensed deployment provides proof of concept; even though RPS is in testing phase, the license structure and BAA execution are real

### Pricing Rationale

The $5,500 annual license fee represents approximately 0.1% of revenue for a $5M practice — a rounding error operationally. The 60/40 revenue split on subscription revenue then creates meaningful upside for both SoloFrameHub and the practice as patient adoption grows. For comparison, most behavioral health EHR platforms charge $50–$150 per provider per month, which a practice with 20 providers would pay at $12,000–$36,000 annually for EHR alone. The Digital Wellness Academy license at $5,500 is positioned as a complementary clinical tool, not a replacement — it sits alongside the EHR at a fraction of that cost.[^32][^28]
