# Movement Medicine: Exercise as Mental Health Treatment
## Evidence Review Brief for Course Development

> **Document Purpose:** This is a research brief for expert course authors and licensed clinical reviewers at the Digital Wellness Academy. It is not consumer-facing copy. All evidence gradings and language recommendations are intended to guide responsible, scientifically accurate psychoeducation.

***

## Executive Summary

A robust and growing body of evidence supports the use of physical activity as an evidence-based adjunct for several common mental health conditions, particularly depression and anxiety in adults. The claim that "exercise can be as effective as medication" is partially supported by current meta-analytic evidence — but only under specific, carefully qualified conditions. Unrestricted use of that phrase in course marketing constitutes an overstatement of the science and carries ethical and liability implications. This brief provides the evidence foundation needed to design responsible, evidence-graded course content that educates without overstating, motivates without misleading, and promotes safety through appropriate clinical referral protocols.

***

## Section 1: Defining the Safe Educational Scope

### What Psychoeducation Can Responsibly Teach

A responsibly designed evidence-based psychoeducational course on exercise and mental health can appropriately cover:

- **Wellness education:** How regular physical activity promotes general psychological wellbeing, mood regulation, sleep quality, stress resilience, and cognitive vitality in the general population.
- **Adjunctive self-management:** How exercise, used alongside professional mental healthcare, can contribute to symptom reduction in mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety; how learners can safely implement evidence-based exercise habits.
- **Neurobiological literacy:** The mechanisms through which exercise influences brain chemistry (BDNF, serotonin, HPA axis), without implying that learners can "self-prescribe" for diagnosed disorders.
- **Behavioral tools:** How to use motivational strategies, habit formation, behavioral activation frameworks, and goal-setting to increase consistent movement.
- **Population-appropriate guidance:** Age-, condition-, and severity-specific guidance on when exercise is beneficial, when it should complement professional care, and when professional referral is necessary.

### What the Course Must Not Claim

The following statements fall outside the responsible scope of a psychoeducational course and should not appear in course marketing or module text:

- That exercise can replace, substitute for, or be chosen *over* medication or psychotherapy by the learner based on this course alone.
- That exercise "cures" depression or anxiety.
- That completing this course constitutes treatment for any diagnosed mental health condition.
- That learners who fail to benefit from exercise have failed personally or are exercising incorrectly.
- That exercise protocols taught in this course are therapeutic prescriptions equivalent to those issued by licensed clinicians.

### Three-Tier Framework for Course Language

| Tier | Definition | Example Language Permitted |
|------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Wellness Education** | Information about general psychological benefits of physical activity in healthy or sub-threshold populations | "Regular movement is associated with improved mood, reduced stress, and better sleep quality." |
| **Adjunctive Self-Management** | Evidence-based strategies that support, complement, or enhance ongoing professional mental health care | "Exercise is recommended as an evidence-based adjunct to standard depression treatment, with strong guidelines support." |
| **Treatment-Level Claim** | Assertions that exercise alone treats, resolves, or is equivalent to clinical intervention for diagnosed disorders | ❌ NOT permitted without clinical supervision context: "Exercise is just as effective as antidepressants for your depression." |

## Section 2: Evidence Review by Mental Health Domain

### 2.1 Depression

**Evidence Grade: STRONG**

Depression is the condition for which the evidence base for exercise is deepest, most consistent, and most clinically actionable.

The landmark 2024 *BMJ* systematic review and network meta-analysis by Noetel et al. — covering 218 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and 14,170 participants — found that walking/jogging (Hedges' g = −0.62), yoga (g = −0.55), strength training (g = −0.49), mixed aerobic exercise (g = −0.43), and tai chi/qigong (g = −0.42) all produced moderate reductions in depression compared with active control conditions. Exercise effects were proportional to prescribed intensity. Yoga and strength training showed the best tolerability profiles. A 2025 systematic review with meta-analysis (Banyard et al., 32 RCTs, n=3,243) reported a pooled SMD of −0.97 for depression with large magnitude of effect.[1][2][3]

The January 2026 Cochrane review (Clegg et al.) examined 73 RCTs with approximately 5,000 adults with depression, finding a moderate clinical effect for exercise vs. no treatment (SMD = −0.62, 95% CI −0.81 to −0.42); when compared to psychological therapy, the effect was similar based on moderate-certainty evidence from ten trials; when compared to antidepressant medication, the effect was also similar but based on low-certainty evidence.[4][5][6]

A 2022 network meta-analysis comparing exercise, antidepressants, and their combination in adults with non-severe depression found no statistically significant difference in symptom reduction across all three treatment types, though antidepressants showed fewer dropouts than exercise.[7]

International lifestyle psychiatry clinical guidelines (Marx et al., 2022) awarded physical activity and exercise the highest evidence Grade (Grade 2) for MDD among all lifestyle interventions assessed, recommending a target of 150–300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity aligned with WHO 2020 guidelines.[8][9]

**Exercise modality for depression:**
- All major types (aerobic, resistance, mind-body, mixed) show comparable symptom reductions[10]
- A dose-response analysis identified a minimum effective dose of approximately 320 MET-min/week; optimal results at 860 MET-min/week with a U-shaped relationship[10]
- Supervised group exercise three times weekly for 30–40 minutes is the most consistently evidence-supported protocol[11]

**Key limitation:** The Cochrane review notes that only 1 of 73 studies met Cochrane criteria for low risk of bias; confidence in evidence graded low to very low per CINeMA. Long-term follow-up data remain scarce.[1][4]

***

### 2.2 Anxiety Disorders

**Evidence Grade: MODERATE (with important caveats)**

Exercise produces significant reductions in anxiety symptoms across multiple populations; however, the evidence for anxiety disorders as primary diagnostic targets is less robust than for depression.

A 2023 meta-analysis of RCTs in college students (SMD = −0.55, 95% CI −0.76 to −0.35) found that aerobic exercise and yoga both significantly relieved anxiety symptoms. A 2025 systematic review (Banyard et al.) reported a pooled SMD of −0.66 for anxiety outcomes in clinically diagnosed populations.[12][2]

A critical systematic review from Duke University (Stonerock, Gupta, Blumenthal, 2023) — covering RCTs with anxiety as the *a priori* primary outcome — raised significant concerns about study quality and urged caution about overstating exercise as an anxiety treatment. The review noted that many positive anxiety trials used anxiety as a secondary rather than primary outcome, inflating apparent effect sizes.[13]

A 2024 *Frontiers in Psychiatry* study in children and adolescents with mental health disorders found parent-reported but not youth self-reported reductions in anxiety after a structured physical activity intervention. This discrepancy warrants careful framing for adolescent-targeted modules.[14]

**Physiological mechanism for anxiety:** Regular aerobic exercise is associated with lower sympathetic nervous system reactivity and blunted HPA axis response to psychosocial stressors; 30 minutes of aerobic exercise has been shown to significantly blunt cortisol response to subsequent stress tasks.[15][16]

**⚠️ Marketing Language Red Flag:** Presenting exercise as a primary treatment for anxiety disorders (panic disorder, GAD, social anxiety disorder, OCD, PTSD) at the same level of confidence as for depression is not supported by current evidence. Generalized anxiety symptom reduction is well-supported; disorder-specific treatment-level efficacy requires specialist evaluation.

***

### 2.3 Stress Symptoms

**Evidence Grade: STRONG (for general stress response)**

Evidence is robust that regular physical activity reduces subjective stress, lowers physiological markers of stress reactivity, and blunts HPA axis responses. The cross-stressor adaptation hypothesis — that aerobically fit individuals exhibit diminished physiological responses to non-exercise stressors — is well-established in the literature. Studies consistently show reduced cortisol reactivity, lower ACTH responses, and reduced sympathetic activation in trained individuals.[16][17][15]

Behavioral mechanisms also contribute: physical activity serves as a healthy behavioral coping strategy, increases feelings of self-efficacy and mastery, and provides a structured break from rumination and worry.[18]

***

### 2.4 Sleep Problems

**Evidence Grade: STRONG**

Exercise is one of the most consistently effective non-pharmacological interventions for sleep quality. A 2025 meta-analysis of 81 RCTs (n=6,193 participants) reported a significant reduction in Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) scores following exercise [MD = −1.77, 95% CI = −2.28 to −1.25]. A 2026 meta-analysis of 12 RCTs focusing specifically on moderate-intensity exercise in adults with insomnia yielded a pooled Hedges' g = −0.65 (95% CI −0.88 to −0.42), qualifying as a clinically meaningful moderate effect.[19][20]

**Modality specifics:**
- Both aerobic and mind-body (yoga, tai chi) exercise improve subjective sleep quality[21][19]
- Resistance training appears most effective for older adults with insomnia[22]
- A network meta-analysis found mind-body exercise combined with treatment as usual and moderate aerobic exercise to be the highest-ranked interventions for sleep disorders[21]

***

### 2.5 Cognitive Function and Concentration

**Evidence Grade: STRONG for older adults; MODERATE for working-age adults**

Exercise — especially aerobic training — robustly enhances cognitive function via BDNF upregulation and hippocampal neurogenesis. A foundational meta-analysis (Szuhany et al.) across 29 studies (N=1,111) demonstrated a moderate effect size (Hedges' g = 0.46) for BDNF increases following a single exercise session, with regular training further amplifying resting BDNF levels. A 2025 review confirms that aerobic exercise boosts BDNF in the hippocampus, promoting neurogenesis and synaptogenesis linked to memory and attention improvement.[23][24][25]

In older adults, combined aerobic plus resistance training showed significant positive effects on overall cognition and executive function in multiple meta-analyses. A 2025 meta-analysis specifically examining resistance exercise in older adults found selective improvements in overall cognition, working memory, verbal learning, and spatial memory.[26][27][28]

For children and adolescents, a dose-dependent relationship was found between physical fitness and reduced risk of depression, anxiety, and ADHD in a large Taiwanese national dataset study.[29]

***

### 2.6 Burnout and Emotional Wellbeing

**Evidence Grade: MODERATE (limited high-quality studies)**

A systematic review by Naczenski et al. synthesizing 10 studies found strong evidence (SIC = −0.86) for physical activity's effect on reducing exhaustion in intervention studies, and moderately strong evidence in longitudinal studies for a negative relationship between physical activity and burnout. Critically, the reviewers note that only one included study qualified as high-quality, calling for cautious interpretation.[30][31][32][33]

A 2024 systematic review of healthcare workers (21 studies) found physical activity consistently associated with reduced emotional exhaustion and depersonalization — the two primary burnout domains — across a variety of inferential methods.[34]

**⚠️ Course Note:** Burnout is not a DSM/ICD psychiatric diagnosis; it is an occupational phenomenon. Module language should frame exercise as a burnout-prevention and resilience tool, not a treatment for occupational burnout meeting clinical criteria.

## Section 3: Critical Evaluation of the Core Marketing Claim

### The Claim: "Physical activity can be as effective as medication for anxiety and depression"

#### When This Claim Is Supported

This claim is *partially* supported under the following specific conditions:

1. **For mild-to-moderate major depression** in adult populations without severe comorbidities, some studies and a 2022 network meta-analysis show no statistically significant difference in depressive symptom reduction between exercise and antidepressant medication.[35][7]
2. **For short-term outcomes only** — the evidence base almost entirely reflects end-of-treatment scores; long-term follow-up data are largely absent.[4]
3. **For exercise programs that are supervised, structured, and adherence-supported**, not self-directed informal activity.[11]
4. **For depression specifically** — not generalized to all anxiety disorders with equivalent evidence strength.[13]

#### When This Claim Is Overstated

1. **For severe depression:** Structured exercise programs are explicitly *not* recommended as sole treatment for severe depression per RACGP HANDI clinical guidelines. Suicidal ideation, inability to function, and severe anhedonia require psychiatric evaluation and are contraindications to exercise-only management.[36][11]
2. **Based on evidence quality:** The Cochrane 2026 review explicitly grades the exercise-vs.-medication comparison as *low certainty* and notes it is based on "a few small trials". Low-certainty evidence does not license categorical equivalence claims.[6][4]
3. **For all anxiety disorders:** Evidence for treatment-level equivalence between exercise and anxiolytics or CBT for specific anxiety disorder diagnoses is not established to the same degree as for depression.[13]
4. **For long-term maintenance:** There is insufficient data on whether exercise maintains antidepressant-level effects over 12+ months.[4]
5. **For medication discontinuation:** No course content should suggest or imply that learners should reduce, alter, or discontinue psychiatric medication based on initiating an exercise program.

#### Recommended Rewrites for Scientific Accuracy

| Original Marketing Phrase | Evidence-Accurate Rewrite |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| "Exercise can be as effective as medication for depression and anxiety" | "For mild-to-moderate depression, structured exercise has shown comparable short-term symptom reduction to antidepressant medication in some studies, though evidence certainty remains low." |
| "Physical activity is a proven treatment for anxiety and depression" | "Physical activity is an evidence-based adjunct recommended in clinical guidelines for depression and stress, with growing evidence for anxiety symptoms." |
| "Use exercise instead of medication" | "Exercise may complement medication and therapy; decisions about changing medication should always involve a prescribing clinician." |
| "Movement is medicine for your mental health" | "Movement supports mental health — and for many people with mild symptoms, it forms an important part of a broader wellness strategy." |

## Section 4: Exercise Variables That Matter Most

### Modality

| Variable | Evidence Summary | Grade |
|----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Aerobic exercise** | Most studied; strong reductions in depression, anxiety, stress; BDNF upregulation | Strong |
| **Resistance training** | Significant depression reduction; cognitive benefits in older adults; good tolerability | Strong |
| **Combined (aerobic + resistance)** | Best for cognition; more effective than aerobic alone for depression per some RCTs | Strong |
| **Yoga / mind-body** | Among the most effective and best-tolerated for depression; good for sleep | Strong |
| **Tai chi / qigong** | Moderate depression reduction; good acceptability; sleep and cognitive benefits | Moderate |
| **HIIT** | Evidence for mood benefits; adherence is a major challenge | Moderate |

For depression, the 2024 *BMJ* analysis found walking/jogging the most evidence-dense modality, and yoga and strength training the most acceptable. For sleep, mind-body exercise and moderate aerobic exercise are the highest-ranked options. For cognition in older adults, combined exercise programs consistently outperform single-modality.[27][1][21]

### Intensity

- Effect sizes for depression are **proportional to exercise intensity** — higher intensity predicts greater benefit[1]
- However, lower-intensity activity still produces meaningful benefits and is more accessible for deconditioned or symptomatic individuals[37]
- WHO guidelines recommend 150–300 minutes/week of moderate-intensity or 75–150 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity activity as the target range[8]
- Optimal dose for depression: ~860 MET-min/week; minimum clinically meaningful dose: ~320 MET-min/week[10]

### Frequency and Duration

- Supervised group sessions of **30–40 minutes, 3 times per week** is the most consistently supported protocol for depression[11]
- Minimum intervention period for meaningful effects appears to be **9–12 weeks**[11]
- **Short-term interventions** (under 3 months) show the largest initial effects for depression in older adults, though medium- and long-term programs also benefit[38]
- A structured, periodized program produces 28% greater reductions in depressive symptoms compared to unstructured activity[39]

### Supervision and Group vs. Solo

- **Supervised group exercise** consistently outperforms unsupervised individual exercise for depression outcomes and adherence[11]
- Exercising in a "true group" with cohesion-building elements is superior to standard classes or home-based programs across multiple outcome categories[40]
- Social support is a strong predictor of depressive symptom reduction independent of exercise dose[41][42]
- However, for digital delivery settings, online exercise platforms incorporating group interaction elements can compensate for absence of in-person supervision[43]

### Adherence

Adherence is perhaps the most underemphasized clinical variable. Without adherence, no protocol produces benefit:

- Individuals with mental health disorders face unique barriers: low motivation, negative self-perception, fatigue, stigma, and environmental constraints[44]
- Strategies with evidence include motivational interviewing, graded exposure to exercise, cognitive reappraisal of exercise barriers, social support integration, and self-efficacy building[45][44]
- Better mental and physical health at baseline predicts higher adherence[46]
- Depression-related anergia and hopelessness can directly impede exercise initiation — this is a clinical issue requiring therapeutic attention, not willpower coaching[47]

### Outdoor / Green Exercise vs. Indoor

**Evidence Grade: Mixed/Emerging**

Green exercise (outdoor activity in natural or green urban environments) may offer additive psychological benefits compared to indoor exercise at equivalent intensity, but evidence is heterogeneous and often mixed. A 2025 systematic review of urban green exercise found promising effects from 15 RCTs but with limited evidence quality. The most effective nature-based programs were 12–13 weeks in duration. Green exercise's primary advantage appears to be reduced perceived exertion and increased motivation, which may improve adherence rather than providing distinct neurobiological benefits.[48][49][50][51][52]

## Section 5: Neurobiological and Behavioral Mechanisms

### Neurobiological Pathways

- **BDNF and neuroplasticity:** Physical exercise — especially aerobic activities — reliably increases brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) in the hippocampus, promoting neurogenesis and synaptogenesis. Depression is associated with reduced BDNF and decreased hippocampal neuroplasticity; exercise reverses this. A meta-analysis of 29 studies found Hedges' g = 0.46 for BDNF increase from a single session, with chronic training amplifying resting BDNF further.[24][25][23]
- **Monoamine systems:** Exercise increases serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine release, mirroring some mechanisms of antidepressant action.[53][23]
- **Endorphin/endocannabinoid release:** Acute exercise generates endorphin and endocannabinoid surges producing transient mood elevation; this "runner's high" effect is real but primarily accounts for acute rather than chronic mental health benefits.[23]

### HPA Axis and Stress Physiology

- Regular aerobic exercise is associated with lower sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis reactivity to subsequent stressors[15]
- Voluntary exercise modulates corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF) release and ACTH secretion, reducing overall stress sensitivity[17]
- 30 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise has been shown to blunt salivary cortisol and alpha-amylase responses to psychosocial laboratory stress tasks[16]

### Behavioral Activation

- Exercise and formal behavioral activation (BA) therapy produce comparable reductions in depressive symptoms, both significantly better than waitlist controls, in RCT evidence[54][18]
- Both mechanisms work through re-engagement with rewarding activities, challenging avoidance patterns, and reinstating routines disrupted by depression[54]
- Physical activity requires significantly less therapist time investment than BA, making it an efficient low-intensity adjunct[54]

### Self-Efficacy

- Exercise self-efficacy (belief in ability to exercise) is a significant independent predictor of depressive symptoms in older adults, above and beyond exercise behavior itself[42][41]
- Increasing self-efficacy through graduated challenges and mastery experiences should be a primary course pedagogy goal, not simply providing exercise "prescriptions"[45]

### Sleep Regulation

- Exercise improves sleep onset latency, sleep efficiency, and subjective sleep quality through multiple pathways including body temperature regulation, adenosine homeostasis, and anxiety reduction[20][19]
- Sleep improvement may itself be a mediating mechanism for exercise's antidepressant effects — the bidirectionality of sleep and mood should be taught explicitly[23]

### Social Connection

- Group exercise contexts provide social contact, belonging, and accountability that independently predict mental health improvement[40][41]
- Isolation exacerbates depression; group movement simultaneously addresses physical inactivity and social withdrawal[41]
- Online platforms incorporating community features (group challenges, live classes, peer support) can partially replicate in-person group benefit[43]

## Section 6: Population-Specific Evidence

### Adults (18–64 Years)

**Strongest evidence base.** Most RCTs target this population. Exercise effects on depression (BMJ 2024, Cochrane 2026), anxiety symptoms, stress, and sleep quality are well-supported. Optimal protocols are achievable for most adults without medical comorbidities.[20][1][4]

### Adolescents (10–19 Years)

**Evidence Grade: Moderate for healthy adolescents; Mixed for clinical populations**

A 2024 review of physical activity in healthy adolescents (Ruiz-Ranz et al.) found positive effects on anxiety, depression, and emotional wellbeing, but noted that interventions without appropriate exercise program design showed no change in psychological variables — protocol quality matters. A 2025 meta-analysis (Fu et al., 30 studies) found physical activity interventions significantly more effective than control for mental health in typically developing children and adolescents. A large national dataset study found dose-dependent relationships between fitness levels and reduced risk of depression, anxiety, and ADHD diagnoses.[55][56][57][29]

**Adolescent-specific cautions:**
- Youth self-report of benefit is less consistent than parent-report — learner engagement may not map to self-perceived improvement[14]
- Competitive sports contexts can increase rather than reduce anxiety in performance-anxious adolescents
- Exercise dependence and disordered eating risk must be addressed in adolescent modules
- Parental and school involvement is a key implementation factor[58]

### Older Adults (65+)

**Evidence Grade: Strong**

Meta-analyses consistently show benefits for depression, anxiety, and cognitive function in older adults. A 2024 review of 20 RCTs found significant depression improvements from resistance, aerobic, and group exercise programs. Combined aerobic plus resistance training produces the best cognitive outcomes in this population. Resistance training specifically enhances working memory, verbal learning, and spatial memory. Exercise also significantly improves sleep quality in older adults with insomnia, with resistance exercise showing the strongest effect.[59][28][60][22][27]

**Older adult-specific adaptations:**
- Fall risk assessment before high-impact programs
- Cardiovascular screening for vigorous activity
- Social isolation is a major risk factor; group-based formats particularly valuable
- Multiple chronic conditions are common; individualized programming is essential

### People with Chronic Illness

**Evidence Grade: Moderate**

Evidence supports exercise for mental health improvements in populations with cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cancer, and other chronic conditions, alongside well-established physical benefits. Cancer mortality is reduced 7–17% with increased PA; depression and anxiety symptoms are improved across chronic disease populations with daily PA. However, medical clearance, individualized programming, and coordination with treating clinicians are essential preconditions for exercise recommendations in this population.[61][62]

### Mild vs. Moderate vs. Severe Symptoms

| Symptom Severity | Exercise Role | Course Guidance |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Sub-threshold / Mild** | Primary prevention, wellness support, mood enhancement | Exercise is a powerful standalone tool; teach evidence-based protocols |
| **Mild–Moderate depression or anxiety** | Evidence-based adjunct; may be comparably effective to first-line treatments for some | Present as a valuable self-management strategy alongside professional care |
| **Moderate-Severe depression** | Important adjunct but NOT sufficient as standalone | Exercise should accompany, not replace, clinical assessment and treatment |
| **Severe depression / active suicidality** | Exercise may be initiated once stabilized; NOT a standalone treatment | Always refer; explicitly state exercise is not appropriate as primary care here |

## Section 7: Evidence for Digital Delivery

**Evidence Grade: Moderate (growing rapidly)**

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (Bhundoo, Pillay, Wilke; 18 RCTs, moderate-to-high methodological quality) found that online exercise (OE) was superior to no exercise for depression (SMD = 1.08), mental wellbeing (SMD = 0.79), self-efficacy (SMD = 1.1), and mood/emotion (SMD = 0.47). Critically, online exercise was **non-inferior to face-to-face exercise** for all tested outcomes. Evidence certainty was graded low to moderate.[43]

A 2025 systematic review of digital lifestyle interventions (Brinsley et al.) covering exercise and other lifestyle domains found significant improvements in depression, anxiety, stress, and wellbeing outcomes across digital delivery formats. A 2025 study of digital physical activity interventions for workers found significant improvements in mental health symptoms via digital health technologies.[63][64]

The NHS NICE IPAcT toolkit (2024) documents integration of remote physical activity interventions into NHS Talking Therapies, providing a clinical model for online psychoeducational exercise delivery.[65]

**What works in digital delivery:**

- Structured, sequenced programs with clear progression (analogous to in-person supervised programs)[43]
- Platforms incorporating social interaction (live classes, group challenges, community forums) show stronger mental health effects[43]
- Goal-setting with motivational messaging improves physical activity levels[43]
- Hybrid models (online content + live coaching touchpoints) likely outperform fully asynchronous content for adherence
- Self-efficacy enhancement as an explicit course design goal[18]

**Digital delivery limitations:**

- Adherence and dropout remain significant challenges without synchronous accountability
- Evidence primarily from healthy adults; limited clinical population data
- Low certainty of evidence requires cautious claims
- Cannot substitute for clinical monitoring in symptomatic populations

## Section 8: Course Development Recommendations

### Strongest Evidence-Backed Modules

1. **Exercise and Depression:** Core module; substantial, multi-source evidence. Present both neurobiological and behavioral mechanisms. Use BMJ 2024 and Cochrane 2026 as anchor references. Include dose, modality, and adherence guidance. *(Strong evidence)*[1][4]

2. **Exercise and Sleep:** Highly evidence-supported; practical and actionable. Excellent for low-barrier engagement since sleep is nearly universally relevant. *(Strong evidence)*[19][20]

3. **Exercise and Stress Physiology:** Well-mechanistically grounded; directly relevant to occupational wellness users. HPA axis, cortisol reactivity, and behavioral coping framing work well for non-clinical learners. *(Strong evidence)*[15][16]

4. **Exercise, BDNF, and Brain Health:** Compelling neuroscience that supports motivation and health literacy. Appropriate framing: "exercise is the brain's fertilizer" (BDNF as "Miracle-Gro" metaphor used in NPR/Cochrane coverage). *(Strong evidence)*[25][53][24][23]

5. **The Adherence Challenge:** Critical module. Why good evidence doesn't produce results if barriers are not addressed. Addresses stigma, motivation, anergia, and self-efficacy. *(Strong evidence, underrepresented in courses)*[44][45]

6. **Population Modules:** Separate, adapted content for adolescents, older adults, and those with chronic illness. *(Moderate–Strong evidence depending on condition)*

### Weak or Overstated Areas to Avoid

| Area | Why to Avoid / Reframe |
|------|----------------------|
| "Exercise treats anxiety disorders" as a category-level claim | Anxiety disorder evidence quality is lower than depression; disorder-specific efficacy varies widely; misleads clinical population [13] |
| Green/outdoor exercise as a distinct therapeutic "prescription" | Evidence is mixed and heterogeneous; additive benefit modest and uncertain [49][50] |
| Exercise for PTSD as primary treatment | Very limited RCT evidence for PTSD specifically; risk of triggering [66] |
| Burnout "treatment" framing | Burnout is not a clinical diagnosis; evidence is promising but low quality [30] |
| Long-term maintenance equivalence to medication | Long-term follow-up data are largely absent from the exercise literature [4] |
| Exercise as prevention for serious mental illness | Prevention data is correlational; should not be framed as causal clinical prevention [67] |

### Evidence-Based Language Recommendations

- ✅ "Evidence-based adjunct to mental health care"
- ✅ "Supports symptom management in mild-to-moderate depression"
- ✅ "Comparable short-term effects to antidepressants for mild-to-moderate depression — based on current, albeit low-certainty, evidence"
- ✅ "WHO-recommended amounts of physical activity are associated with improved mental wellbeing"
- ✅ "Exercise cannot replace professional assessment and treatment for diagnosed conditions"
- ✅ "Start where you are — even small increases in movement have measurable mental health benefits"
- ❌ "Exercise is the cure for depression"
- ❌ "You don't need medication if you exercise consistently"
- ❌ "This program will treat your anxiety"
- ❌ "Exercise is as effective as any antidepressant" (without qualifiers on severity, certainty, and population)

## Section 9: Red Flags and Clinical Referral Guidance

This section must appear in course onboarding, module introductions, and completion resources.

### When Exercise Is NOT Sufficient as Standalone Care

Learners presenting with any of the following should receive explicit prompts to seek professional evaluation before or alongside using this course:

| Indicator | Reason for Referral |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Passive or active suicidal ideation | Exercise is not an acute safety intervention; requires immediate clinical assessment |
| Severe depression (significant functional impairment, unable to work/care for self) | Exercise not recommended as sole treatment per RACGP, NICE[11][36] |
| Manic or hypomanic symptoms | Exercise can exacerbate activation; mood stabilization takes clinical priority |
| Active psychosis | Disorganized cognition impairs safe exercise engagement; clinical stabilization first |
| Eating disorder with exercise component | Compulsive exercise is a clinical feature of some eating disorders; this course could cause harm[11] |
| PTSD with significant somatic symptoms | Interoceptive awareness during exercise can trigger trauma responses; specialist guidance needed |
| Chronic suicidal ideation | Requires specialist mental health management, not a psychoeducational course |
| Severe anxiety with panic disorder | Exercise-induced physiological arousal may trigger panic; specialist-guided introduction needed |

### Recommended Course Safety Language

> *"This course provides evidence-based education about the mental health benefits of physical activity. It is not a substitute for professional mental health assessment or treatment. If you are experiencing significant symptoms that interfere with daily functioning, thoughts of self-harm, or have concerns about your mental health, please contact a licensed mental health professional or your primary care provider. If you are in crisis, please contact a crisis line in your country."*

### Mandatory Referral Prompts

Courses should include validated, low-burden symptom screening items (e.g., PHQ-2, GAD-2) at onboarding — not for diagnostic purposes, but to route highly symptomatic learners toward professional resources. Learners scoring above screening thresholds should receive a non-stigmatizing, supportive message directing them to professional care alongside (not instead of) course participation.
