**Course Focus:** Evidence-based psychoeducation on the mental health benefits of collective physical activity, team sports, and group-based participation — with critical safety framing on recreational vs. competitive contexts.

## Module 1: Foundations (Lessons 1-4)

### Lesson 1: Why Collective Activity — The Social Cure
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the "social cure" framework: how meaningful group membership produces mental health benefits through belonging, identity, support, and purpose
- Distinguish between physical activity benefits (general) vs. collective activity benefits (social + physical)
- Recognize that team sport provides mental health benefits *regardless of physical activity volume* (isolating the social component)
- Learn the critical recreational vs. competitive sport distinction

**Key Concepts:**
- Social identity approach (Tajfel and Turner, Haslam et al.)
- Team sport > individual sport for adult mental health (29-study systematic review)
- Social cure mechanism: belonging → identity → support → purpose → mental health
- Recreational sport shows benefits; competitive/elite sport shows mental health *risks*

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG  
**Research Foundation:** Eather et al. 2023 systematic review (29 studies, adults 18-84), social cure framework, community belonging OR = 5.778

**Interactive Components (Target: 5-6):**
- Opening Callout (reflection): "Connection Is Not Just Nice to Have"
- InsightGrid: Social cure research statistics (4 items)
- FlipCard set: "Myths about team sports and mental health" (3-4 cards)
- InteractiveScenario: "Choosing between solo gym vs. group class"
- Checkin (scale): "How connected do you currently feel to groups or communities?"
- Checkin (reflect): "What's one group you've been part of that made you feel like you belonged?"

### Lesson 2: The Evidence — What Research Shows
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the dose-response relationship: higher frequency of team sport participation → greater mental health benefit
- Learn the specific outcomes: team sport reduces loneliness by 30%, improves life satisfaction, reduces depression/anxiety
- Recognize age-specific evidence (adults vs. youth)
- Identify the mechanistic distinction: exercise dose vs. social context as active ingredients

**Key Concepts:**
- Team sport reduces loneliness risk by 30% (RR = 0.70) in youth — individual sport does NOT
- Adults in team sport show better mental health than individual sport participants
- Continued participation (not sporadic) drives benefits
- Elite/competitive sport shows reversed pattern (higher distress, loneliness)

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE-STRONG  
**Research Foundation:** Longitudinal study on sport trajectories + loneliness (n=762 youth), meta-analysis on adult sport participation

**Interactive Components (Target: 5-6):**
- InsightGrid: Key research findings (loneliness reduction, depression, anxiety, life satisfaction)

### Lesson 3: The Critical Distinction — Recreational vs. Competitive Sport
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand that recreational and competitive sport have *opposite* mental health risk profiles
- Learn the documented harms of competitive/elite sport: increased mental health disorders, loneliness, maltreatment exposure
- Recognize the 70-80% youth dropout rate by age 15 driven by psychological factors (stress, diminished enjoyment)
- Identify safe participation contexts: recreational, community-based, psychologically safe environments

**Key Concepts:**
- Recreational sport: mental health benefits  
- Competitive/elite sport: mental health risks (16.9% current mental disorders in youth elite athletes)
- Over half of U.S. college athletes report being "very lonely" despite team membership
- Performance pressure, toxic cultures, and maltreatment reverse the benefits

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE-STRONG for harm evidence  
**Research Foundation:** 2026 Public Health Post review (194 studies, n=88,516), youth elite athlete prevalence studies, dropout research

**Interactive Components (Target: 6-7):**
- Callout (warning): "Not All Team Environments Are Safe or Beneficial"

- InteractiveScenario: "Your child is invited to join a competitive travel team — what do you consider?"

- Checkin (reflect): "Have you experienced performance pressure in a group activity? How did it affect you?"

### Lesson 4: Mechanisms — How Collective Activity Works
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the six mechanisms: social support, identity & belonging, accountability & adherence, enjoyment & intrinsic motivation, routine & temporal structure, meaning & purpose
- Learn that social identity must be *positive and meaningful* to produce health benefits (not stigmatized or coercive)
- Recognize that enjoyment mediates the relationship between participation and wellbeing
- Identify why group exercise participants show stronger adherence than solo exercisers

**Key Concepts:**
- Social support correlates: wellbeing (r = 0.31), reduced anxiety (r = −0.22), reduced depression (r = −0.27)
- Group membership → sense of belonging → meaning/purpose → mental health (27.4% of wellbeing variance explained)
- Adherence advantage: group exercisers arrive on time, drop out less, resist schedule disruptions
- Enjoyment is both mechanism and marker of whether activity will benefit mental health

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG for mechanisms  
**Research Foundation:** Meta-analysis of social support in athletes (40 studies, n=14,462), social cure longitudinal mediation studies

**Interactive Components (Target: 6):**
- InsightGrid: Six mechanisms at a glance
- SlideNavigation: "From group membership to mental health" (4 slides showing causal pathway)
- BodyMap: "Where do you feel connection when you're part of a group?" (energy, warmth, openness)
- EnhancedAccordion: Deep dive into each mechanism (6 accordion items)
- Checkin (scale): "Which mechanism resonates most with your experience?"
- Checkin (reflect): "Think of a group you love being part of. Which mechanisms are at play?"

### Lesson 7: Volunteering for Mental Health
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand age-moderated volunteering benefits: strongest for adults 40+, less clear for young adults
- Learn the curvilinear dose-response: moderate volunteering intensity is most beneficial (not too little, not too much)
- Recognize volunteering outcomes: improved depression, life satisfaction, psychological wellbeing, self-esteem, self-efficacy, social support, sense of community
- Identify organizational vs. individual volunteering differences

**Key Concepts:**
- Volunteering → mental wellbeing relationship emerges above age 40, continues into old age
- Umbrella review (11 databases): strong/very strong evidence for depression, life satisfaction, wellbeing
- Curvilinear effect: moderate intensity optimal (excessive volunteering shows diminishing returns)
- Population-based longitudinal study: 66,343 person-years (British Household Panel Survey)

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE (age-moderated, curvilinear effects)  
**Research Foundation:** 66,343 person-years longitudinal study, 2023 umbrella review on volunteering outcomes

**Interactive Components (Target: 5-6):**

### Lesson 8: Clubs, Classes, and Community Groups
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the social cure literature: it's the *social identity* formed from group membership (not the specific activity) that drives mental health benefits
- Learn that group membership quality matters more than activity type
- Recognize the Groups4Health intervention for building and maintaining group memberships
- Identify stigmatized vs. meaningful group memberships (social curse vs. social cure)

**Key Concepts:**
- Social identity → meaning, support, agency → mental health benefits
- Stigmatized or non-meaningful group memberships do NOT produce benefits (may reverse)
- Groups4Health social identity intervention shows mental health improvements in young adults with affective disturbances
- Umbrella review (Boelens et al. 2022): small positive impact for organized sport in youth; only 2 systematic reviews on non-sport activities (mixed results)

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE (groups matter, type matters less than quality)  
**Research Foundation:** Social cure framework (Haslam, Jetten, Cruwys et al.), Groups4Health intervention studies

**Interactive Components (Target: 6):**
- InsightGrid: Social identity approach key concepts
- SlideNavigation: "From group membership to mental health" (4 slides: join group → form identity → gain support → experience meaning → improve mental health)

### Lesson 9: Social Prescribing and Community Pathways
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand social prescribing: connecting individuals to community activities for health outcomes
- Learn the NHS and public health implementation of collective activity-mental health evidence
- Recognize the longitudinal mediation pathway: group membership → belonging → support → reduced loneliness → quality of life
- Identify that populations with low socioeconomic status may require up to 2 years of sustained participation for benefits

**Key Concepts:**
- Social prescribing represents policy-level implementation of social cure evidence
- Longitudinal study (n=630): group membership increases → quality-of-life improvements (serially mediated through belonging, support, reduced loneliness)
- Sustained engagement is critical (not one-off participation)
- NHS social prescribing link workers connect patients to community resources

**Evidence Grade:** EMERGING-MODERATE (policy-supported with growing evidence base)  
**Research Foundation:** Social prescribing pathway longitudinal study (n=630), NHS evidence reviews

### Lesson 15: Protecting Mental Health in Sport — Recognizing Toxic Environments
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the documented prevalence of toxic leadership and maltreatment in sport: 24% of UK world-class athletes reported witnessing unacceptable behaviors, 27.5% of US Olympic athletes reported misconduct
- Learn the signs of toxic team culture: psychological maltreatment, bullying, abusive coaching, suppression of emotional expression, fear-based motivation
- Recognize bullying in sport as understudied but highly prevalent
- Identify when to leave a team environment (safety-critical decision)

**Key Concepts:**
- Toxic sport culture produces performance anxiety, fear of failure, eating disorders, self-harming behaviors, reduced wellbeing
- All types of maltreatment (psychological, physical, sexual, neglect) significantly correlate with mental health harm
- Bullying in sport: mental health impairments, physical health impairments, decreased performance and dropout, culture of aggression and silence
- Transgender and gender diverse adolescents: identity-based bullying in sport is among highest predictors of depression/anxiety

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG EVIDENCE OF HARM  
**Research Foundation:** 2025 scoping review of toxic leadership in high-performance sport, Canadian national team maltreatment study, TGD adolescent study (n=10,454)

**Interactive Components (Target: 6-7):**

### Lesson 16: Exercise Compulsion and Knowing Your Limits
**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand exercise addiction: association with anxiety, depression, mood disorders, eating disorders
- Learn the risk factors: body image dissatisfaction, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive traits, childhood trauma
- Recognize that team environments can reinforce escalating training norms (social contagion of compulsive exercise)
- Identify when to seek professional support for exercise compulsion

**Key Concepts:**
- Exercise addiction risk associated with significantly higher depression scores, ADHD, childhood trauma (2022 University of Basel study)
- Systematic review: those at risk for exercise addiction show eating disorders, depression, anxiety, borderline personality disorder symptoms more frequently
- Team norms can normalize excessive training (peer pressure, identity tied to performance)
- Difference between beneficial exercise motivation and compulsive patterns

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE-STRONG (emerging systematic evidence)  
**Research Foundation:** 2025/2026 Journal of Affective Disorders meta-analysis, systematic review on exercise addiction, 2022 University of Basel study

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the common barriers to collective activity: time, motivation, cost, transportation, social anxiety, past negative experiences
- Learn problem-solving strategies for each barrier type
- Recognize that addressing barriers requires both individual problem-solving and structural/policy solutions

**Key Concepts:**
- Barrier categories: logistical (time, cost, transportation), psychological (social anxiety, fear of judgment, past trauma), structural (accessibility, discrimination, geographic)

- Structural solutions: subsidized programs, inclusive leadership, accessible facilities, transportation support
