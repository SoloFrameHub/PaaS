

# Evidence Review for Course Development: Creative Expression & Art Therapy for Emotional Wellness

## 1. Scope and role definition

### 1.1 What a psychoeducational course can appropriately teach

A non-clinical, psychoeducational course can legitimately and safely teach:

- **Basic models linking creativity and wellbeing**  
  - How engaging in creative activities (drawing, collage, coloring, music, movement, writing) can support positive emotion, autonomy, competence, and relatedness in everyday life. [who](https://www.who.int/europe/publications/i/item/9789289054553)
- **Evidence-informed micro-practices** (skills, not therapy):  
  - Reflective journaling (e.g., daily check-in, gratitude lists, structured prompts). [fmch.bmj](https://fmch.bmj.com/content/10/1/e001154)
  - Expressive writing in *low-intensity forms* (brief, time-limited writing about stress, emotions, or values, with safety caveats). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2736499/)
  - Visual art for *emotion labeling and awareness* (e.g., color maps of mood, drawing emotions as shapes, simple symbolic representations without interpretation by the instructor). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5629828/)
  - Creative routines that support regulation and pleasure (coloring, doodling, crafts, photography, gentle movement, music listening/playing). [who](https://www.who.int/initiatives/arts-and-health)
  - Meaning-making tools: values clarification, “what matters” lists, storying difficult experiences with a focus on context and coping (not trauma processing). [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.00584/full)
- **Psychoeducation about boundaries and mental health**  
  - Normalization that creative work can evoke emotions; guidance on pacing, self-monitoring, and stopping/grounding.  
  - Clear explanation of when to seek **licensed art therapy or psychotherapy** (e.g., trauma, self-harm, severe mood disturbance).  
  - Basic trauma‑informed principles: choice, safety, no forced disclosure, no interpretation of participants’ artwork, and respect for cultural and personal meanings. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35577560/)

These can be framed as **skills for emotional awareness, regulation, and wellbeing**, not as substitutes for treatment. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/news-room/apa-blogs/creative-arts-enhancing-mental-health)

### 1.2 What should be reserved for licensed art therapy or psychotherapy

Based on art therapy scope-of-practice discussions, clinical trial designs, and trauma-informed guidance, the following belong in **licensed art therapy or psychotherapy** contexts, not a generic education course: [journals.sagepub](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/15248380221097442)

- **Formal art therapy**:  
  - Assessment, diagnosis-informed treatment planning, and outcome monitoring using creative media.  
  - Systematic interpretation of symbolic material, transference, and relational dynamics.  
  - Integrative work on trauma memories, dissociation, complex grief, eating disorders, psychosis, or self-harm via art.  
- **Trauma-focused creative processing**:  
  - Direct processing of detailed trauma narratives (e.g., expressive writing about most traumatic events) in people with PTSD or serious comorbidities. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18230238/)
  - Exposure-style assignments that deliberately activate traumatic memories.  
- **High-risk populations and content:**  
  - Persistent suicidal ideation, active self-harm, psychotic disorders, acute mania, severe eating disorders, and complex PTSD should be managed in treatment, not unmoderated self-help. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK588208/)
- **Interpretive authority over participants’ work**:  
  - Course facilitators should *not* interpret participants’ images or texts as diagnostic or analytic (“this means you have unresolved anger toward your mother”). That crosses into clinical territory and can be harmful without training. [rachelsikorski](http://rachelsikorski.com/art-therapy/)

Course language must make this boundary explicit: these modules are **not art therapy**, do **not** provide diagnosis or treatment, and are not appropriate in crisis; they are educational tools that some people find supportive alongside or between clinical care. [henryford](https://www.henryford.com/visitors/caregivers/care-connections/education/art-therapy-vs-art-education)

## 2. Evidence overview by practice type

### 2.1 Expressive writing

#### 2.1.1 Foundational and recent evidence

Expressive writing (EW) typically involves writing about one’s deepest thoughts and feelings about stressful or traumatic experiences for several short sessions. [sparq.stanford](https://sparq.stanford.edu/sites/g/files/sbiybj19021/files/media/file/baikie_wilhelm_2005_-_emotional_and_physical_health_benefits_of_expressive_writing.pdf)

- Early meta-analyses suggested **small but reliable effects** of EW on health and psychological outcomes, particularly in non-clinical or mildly distressed samples. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3830620/)
- More recent work emphasizes **heterogeneity and modest effect sizes** and highlights that benefits depend heavily on instructions, engagement, and population. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10300201/)
- A 2006 meta-analysis on stressful or traumatic experiences found small overall health benefits, which varied by outcome and context. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19742069/)
- A network meta-analysis in adult trauma survivors comparing EW to psychotherapy, other writing, and controls found **limited efficacy** of standard EW for PTSD symptoms; structured trauma-focused therapies generally outperform simple EW. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9772920/)
- Trials that incorporate **emotion acceptance, cognitive reappraisal, or self-compassion framing** show better outcomes than unguided “venting” or purely negative recounting. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1192595/full)

#### 2.1.2 Trauma and PTSD applications

- Evidence is **mixed** for EW as a PTSD treatment: some trials show symptom reduction; others find minimal or no benefit compared with control writing or therapy. [ovid](https://www.ovid.com/journals/regep/abstract/10.1177/1089268019831645~a-meta-analysis-of-expressive-writing-on-posttraumatic?redirectionsource=fulltextview)
- Meta-analytic work indicates that **PTSD symptoms, posttraumatic growth, and quality of life** may see small improvements, but effect sizes are often modest and inconsistent. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2023.1204053/full)
- In some high-risk individuals or when instructions emphasize reliving trauma without containment, EW can temporarily **increase distress** or physiological arousal. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2736499/)

**Course implication:** EW should be taught as a *light*, optional self-help practice for stress and emotion processing in relatively stable participants, with explicit warnings against using the course to process major trauma without clinical support. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10702225/)

### 2.2 Journaling (reflective, therapeutic, gratitude, positive self-talk)

A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis on journaling for mental illness found that journaling (variously defined, including expressive, gratitude, and cognitive approaches) produced **small-to-moderate improvements** in depression and anxiety across heterogeneous studies. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8935176/)

- The meta-analysis reported beneficial effects on depressive symptoms and anxiety/somatoform symptoms across contexts (including online and clinical samples), though heterogeneity and small sample sizes limit firm conclusions. [fmch.bmj](https://fmch.bmj.com/content/10/1/e001154)
- A VA/primary-care guidance document on therapeutic journaling highlights benefits for stress, mood, and coping, emphasizing that structured prompts (e.g., gratitude, self-compassion, cognitive reframing) are often more helpful than unstructured “venting.” [va](https://www.va.gov/WHOLEHEALTHLIBRARY/docs/Therapeutic-Journaling.pdf)
- A recent trial of **positive self-talk journaling** demonstrated improvements in psychological wellbeing and self-compassion among adults with mild-to-moderate distress. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12699903/)
- Mindfulness-based journaling and gratitude journaling show effects comparable to other positive psychology interventions: modest improvements in wellbeing and depression. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10393216/)

**Course implication:** journaling is one of the most teachable and evidence-supported tools, particularly when framed as **structured reflective practice** (e.g., gratitude lists, strengths, values, self-compassion) rather than unrestricted emotional dumping. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10393216/)

### 2.3 Art-based interventions and creative arts therapies

#### 2.3.1 Licensed art therapy and creative arts therapies

“Art therapy” refers to **clinical use of art within a therapeutic relationship by a trained, credentialed art therapist**, distinct from art education or general creative activities. [lrl.mn](https://www.lrl.mn.gov/docs/2026/mandated/260360.pdf)

- A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found that **art therapy interventions** significantly alleviated depressive symptoms across populations (effect sizes typically in the small-to-moderate range), although heterogeneity and risk of bias were concerns. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12141542/)
- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of **active visual art therapy** reported small but significant improvements in psychological outcomes (depression, anxiety, stress, quality of life) across various populations; results were stronger when art therapy was delivered by trained art therapists in structured group or individual formats. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39264631/)
- A 2026 meta-analysis of **creative arts therapy for anxiety, depression, and quality of life** across populations suggested overall positive effects of arts-based therapies as adjunctive treatments, though results varied by modality, population, and methodological rigor. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13000673/)
- In psychosis and schizophrenia, some trials of adjunctive art therapy show improvements in positive symptoms, negative symptoms, self-efficacy, and social functioning, but evidence is mixed and limited; a recent RCT using visual art therapy found symptom improvements but authors highlight the need for careful integration with psychiatric care. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6940592/)

For **eating disorders and body image**:  

- A 2024 systematic review of art therapy in eating disorders found **promising but limited** evidence; many trials were small and methodologically weak, but art therapy appears to support emotional expression and identity work as an adjunct to standard treatment. [jpsychopathol](https://www.jpsychopathol.it/article/view/466)

These results support art therapy as a **legitimate clinical modality**, but they depend crucially on **trained therapists, treatment context, and client selection**. [ciiat](https://ciiat.org/faqs/tarts/afl/what-is-the-difference-between-art-therapy-and-therapeutic-arts/)

#### 2.3.2 Group arts interventions and arts-in-health programs

WHO and regional reports on arts and health summarize a broad literature where arts participation (visual arts, music, dance, drama, writing) is associated with improved wellbeing, social connection, and some mental health outcomes. [cultureforhealth](https://www.cultureforhealth.eu/app/uploads/2023/02/Final_C4H_SummaryReport_small.pdf)

- A 2024/2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of **group arts interventions for depression and anxiety among older adults** found small-to-moderate improvements in depressive and anxiety symptoms, though studies varied widely in design and quality. [nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44220-024-00368-1)
- Arts-in-health and community arts programs often show improvements in mood, loneliness, self-esteem, and social participation. [doaj](https://doaj.org/article/138e98f77f1149c4b85a2dd4ef472812)

**Course implication:** group arts practices (drawing, collage, simple creative tasks) can be presented as **general wellbeing practices**, but clinical claims should be reserved for settings that emulate art therapy conditions (trained facilitators, mental health oversight).

### 2.4 Creative expression, emotion processing and regulation

Research on **emotion labeling and creative engagement** supports several mechanisms relevant to the course:

- **Affect labeling** (putting feelings into words) attenuates amygdala activation and supports regulation, serving as a plausible mechanism for expressive writing and journaling. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9799301/)
- Creative activities are associated with **greater day-to-day positive affect and wellbeing**, partly mediated by autonomy, competence, and social relatedness during creative episodes. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0092656624001004)
- Emerging work shows that drawing can serve as a “window to emotion” and that technology-assisted analysis of drawings can capture emotional content, underscoring that visual expression can externalize and organize feelings. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11109233/)

**Course implication:** teaching visual and written **emotion labeling** (e.g., “draw your stress as a weather pattern,” “name three feelings and color them”) fits the evidence base and respects scope boundaries. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5629828/)

## 3. Distinguishing practice categories

### 3.1 Licensed art therapy evidence

- Delivered by **licensed art therapists / creative arts therapists**.  
- Involves therapeutic alliance, treatment planning, and integration with mental health care.  
- Evidence: RCTs and systematic reviews supporting benefits in depression, anxiety, trauma-related distress, psychosis, and chronic illness when used as adjuncts. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7763866/)
- Uses art media *and* therapist-guided reflection; includes careful handling of transference, trauma content, and symbolic meaning. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/news-room/apa-blogs/healing-through-art)

### 3.2 Expressive arts practices (clinical or quasi-clinical)

- Structured uses of art, writing, music, drama within therapeutic or facilitated-group contexts, often guided by mental health or arts-in-health professionals.  
- Evidence: arts-in-health and group arts interventions meta-analyses and WHO reports; positive but heterogeneous mental health and wellbeing outcomes. [qmro.qmul.ac](https://qmro.qmul.ac.uk/xmlui/handle/123456789/104092?show=full)
- May overlap with therapy, especially when focused on trauma, severe depression, or complex conditions—then require professional oversight. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35577560/)

### 3.3 Creative self-help

- Unguided or lightly guided use of journaling, drawing, coloring, crafts, music, or movement by individuals on their own.  
- Evidence:  
  - Journaling and gratitude: modest improvements in mood and wellbeing. [pnas](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2425193122)
  - Expressive writing: small overall effects, with meaningful variation by instructions, engagement, and baseline distress. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19742069/)
  - Everyday creativity: associated with higher subjective wellbeing, but largely correlational. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8305859/)
- Best framed as **self-care tools**, not treatments.

### 3.4 General wellbeing practices

- Arts participation, hobby crafts, “adult coloring,” writing for pleasure, and community arts events fall into this category.  
- WHO and other public-health reports highlight these as components of **social participation and cultural engagement** that support mental and physical health at a population level. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK553773/)
- These can be included without therapy claims, e.g., “creative routines that support enjoyment, connection, and stress relief.”

## 4. Evaluating “healing psychological wounds”

### 4.1 When the claim is partly appropriate

The phrase “healing psychological wounds” may be **loosely appropriate** when:

- Referring to **licensed art therapy** or psychotherapy contexts addressing trauma, depression, grief, or other clinical issues, supported by structured interventions and supervision. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.571124/full)
- Describing **adjunctive** roles of expressive writing or art therapy in broader treatment plans—for example, symptom reduction in depression or trauma-related distress within clinical trials. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9772920/)
- Framed metaphorically and not as a guaranteed outcome, e.g., “supporting healing processes” in people receiving care.

### 4.2 When the claim is too strong or misleading

For a **standalone psychoeducational course**, “healing psychological wounds” is too strong because:

- Evidence for creative self-help (journaling, expressive writing, at-home art) shows **modest and variable effects**, not consistent healing of clinically significant trauma or psychiatric conditions. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10702225/)
- EW for PTSD and trauma yields mixed results; more intensive trauma-focused therapies are substantially more effective. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9772920/)
- Arts-in-health programs support wellbeing and social connection but do not reliably “heal” major psychiatric wounds on their own. [who](https://www.who.int/europe/publications/i/item/9789289054553)

Using this phrasing in marketing risks implying **therapeutic efficacy** and **scope overreach** and could mislead high-risk users into relying on a course instead of appropriate treatment.

### 4.3 Evidence-consistent rewording

Better, safer formulations include:

- “Using art, writing, and creative expression to **support emotional awareness, regulation, and wellbeing**.” [who](https://www.who.int/initiatives/arts-and-health)
- “Learning creative tools that can **help you process everyday stress and emotions** and complement therapy when needed.” [who](https://www.who.int/europe/publications/i/item/9789289054553)
- “Exploring creative practices that may **increase insight, meaning-making, and emotional expression**, which some people find healing when used alongside professional care.” [sparq.stanford](https://sparq.stanford.edu/sites/g/files/sbiybj19021/files/media/file/baikie_wilhelm_2005_-_emotional_and_physical_health_benefits_of_expressive_writing.pdf)

Avoid phrases like “heal deep psychological wounds,” “treat trauma,” or “cure depression and anxiety” in a non-clinical course.

## 5. Strongest teachable methods

### 5.1 Reflective journaling

Evidence:

- Systematic review/meta-analysis: journaling interventions (diverse formats) improved depression and anxiety outcomes, with low-to-moderate effect sizes. [fmch.bmj](https://fmch.bmj.com/content/10/1/e001154)
- VA and primary care materials highlight journaling for stress management, cognitive restructuring, and self-reflection, emphasizing structure and moderation. [fammed.wisc](https://www.fammed.wisc.edu/files/webfm-uploads/documents/outreach/im/tool-therapeutic-journaling.pdf)
- Positive psychology approaches (gratitude, strengths, self-compassion journaling) show benefits for life satisfaction and depressive symptoms. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6437090/)

Teachable components:

- Daily or weekly prompts: “What felt meaningful today?”, “What did I handle well?”, “Where did I feel supported?”.  
- Gratitude entries, strengths spotting, self-compassion letters.  
- Boundaries: recommended time limits (e.g., 10–20 min), optional sharing, clear stop rules if distress rises.

### 5.2 Expressive writing (low-intensity, non-trauma-focused)

Evidence:

- EW provides small benefits on emotion, health, and stress, especially when framed around **emotion acceptance and cognitive reappraisal** rather than pure reliving. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3830620/)
- More recent meta-analytic and experimental findings emphasize **mixed effects**, small average benefits, and the importance of writer engagement and instructions. [journals.plos](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0308928)

Teachable components for a course:

- Short, time-limited writing exercises on current stressors, interpersonal conflicts, or meaningful events, with prompts encouraging:  
  - describing facts and feelings,  
  - acknowledging impact,  
  - reflecting on coping, values, and future directions.  
- Explicit recommendation **not** to use the course for detailed trauma writing without clinical support.  
- Emphasis on self-monitoring and pausing if distress rises beyond a manageable level.

### 5.3 Visual art for emotion labeling and self-awareness

Evidence:

- Neuroscience on **affect labeling** shows that naming feelings (through words or symbolic representation) can diminish limbic activation and support regulation. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9799301/)
- Visual representations (color, line, shape) can externalize emotional states and are used in both art therapy and arts-in-health contexts. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11393726/)
- Arts-in-health and group art interventions show mental-health benefits in nonclinical and clinical samples. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40084230/)

Teachable components:

- Emotion color wheels: map feelings to colors and intensities.  
- “Draw your mood as a landscape/weather” for nonverbal labeling.  
- Over-time emotion tracking with simple icons or colors.  

Facilitators should **avoid interpretation**; participants are encouraged to reflect on their own meaning (“What does this image say to you?”).

### 5.4 Meaning-making and self-expression

Evidence:

- Writing and art that integrates experience with values and identity may support meaning-making, which is associated with better mental health and quality of life. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.00584/full)
- Positive expressive writing (combining emotional disclosure with positive reframing, strengths, or gratitude) shows benefits for subjective health and wellbeing. [journals.plos](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0308928)

Teachable components:

- Story prompts: “A challenge I have grown through,” “A time I surprised myself,” “People and values I want to carry forward.”  
- Combined art + words: images plus short texts reflecting values, strengths, or hopes.  

This should be presented as **meaning exploration**, not trauma reprocessing.

### 5.5 Gentle creative routines

Evidence:

- Everyday creative activities are associated with higher momentary and long-term subjective wellbeing. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0092656624001004)
- WHO and CultureForHealth reports emphasize arts participation as a protective factor for mental health. [cultureforhealth](https://www.cultureforhealth.eu/app/uploads/2023/02/Final_C4H_SummaryReport_small.pdf)

Teachable components:

- Low-barrier routines: coloring, sketching, movement to music, small daily creative tasks.  
- Emphasis on **enjoyment, curiosity, and flexibility**, not performance or productivity.

## 6. Caution areas and red flags

### 6.1 Trauma activation

Risks:

- Detailed expressive work about traumatic events can temporarily worsen distress and is **not consistently effective** as a stand-alone treatment for PTSD. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18230238/)
- Trauma-aware arts-health guidance emphasizes safety, empowerment, and avoiding forced disclosure. [journals.sagepub](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/15248380221097442)

Course implications:

- Avoid prompts that explicitly ask for **graphic trauma details**.  
- Include warnings: if participants notice flashbacks, panic, dissociation, or sustained worsening, they should stop the exercise and seek professional help.  
- Use grounding and resourcing prompts (senses, safe imagery, present-moment focus) and keep difficult-material prompts optional.

### 6.2 Severe depression and suicidality

Risks:

- Journaling and expressive writing can intensify rumination in some individuals with severe depression, especially when unstructured. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2736499/)
- Self-harm treatment and suicide-prevention guidelines emphasize rapid linkage to clinical care, assessment, and safety planning rather than self-guided creative work alone. [ashe](https://www.ashe.org/patientsafety)

Course implications:

- All modules should include clear messaging: **this course is not a crisis service**.  
- Encourage participants with persistent low mood, hopelessness, or suicidal thoughts to seek professional evaluation.  
- Provide crisis resources (e.g., crisis lines, local emergency contacts) and advise discontinuing exercises if they trigger self-harm urges or persistent worsening.

### 6.3 Psychosis and complex conditions

Risks:

- Art therapy for psychotic disorders must be carefully structured and integrated with psychiatric care; results are mixed but promising in clinical contexts. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12359299/)
- Unguided expressive work in psychosis may intersect with delusional content or exacerbate disorganization.

Course implications:

- State explicitly that individuals with **psychosis, schizophrenia-spectrum, or bipolar with psychotic features** should use creative practices under the guidance of their treatment team.  
- A purely psychoeducational course should not advertise benefits for psychosis and should avoid interpreting content that may be psychotic in nature.

### 6.4 Eating disorders and body image

Risks:

- Art therapy is used clinically in eating disorders to explore body image and identity, but this work can evoke intense shame, self-criticism, or focus on body parts. [psychiatryinvestigation](https://psychiatryinvestigation.org/upload/pdf/0502006013.pdf)
- Without containment and clinical skill, body-focused creative exercises may backfire.

Course implications:

- Avoid body-focused assignments like “draw your body” or “draw what you hate about your body.”  
- If body image is addressed, keep it **values- and function-focused** (what the body allows, sensations, self-compassion) and encourage people with active eating disorders to work with clinicians.

### 6.5 Self-harm content

Risks:

- Creative work may express self-harm urges or plans; if facilitators are not clinicians, they may not know how to respond.  
- Guidelines on self-harm emphasize systematic risk assessment and safety planning, which exceed the remit of a generic course. [icanotes](https://www.icanotes.com/2025/10/13/self-harm-treatment-plan-interventions-goals/)

Course implications:

- Provide **clear guidance**: if someone uses the exercises to describe or plan self-harm, they should reach out to a professional or crisis service immediately. Facilitators should not act as covert therapists.  
- Do not encourage “art as harm substitution” in high-risk individuals without a clinical context.

### 6.6 Interpreting symbolic material without training

Risks:

- Interpreting art as diagnostic (“this black color means depression / abuse / hidden rage”) is outside the competence of non-therapists and can be harmful or culturally insensitive. [rachelsikorski](http://rachelsikorski.com/art-therapy/)
- Trauma-informed arts-health principles warn against imposing meaning on participants’ creative work; meaning-making should be participant-led. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35577560/)

Course implications:

- Train facilitators to **ask open reflection questions** (“what meaning does this have for you?”) and avoid interpretation or advice-giving based on imagery.  
- Emphasize that creative work is personal data, not a test.

## 7. Strongest evidence-backed modules

Based on the above, the best candidates for core modules in a psychoeducational course are:

1. **Foundations of creativity and emotional wellbeing**  
   - Content: science of creativity, emotion labeling, and basic art-and-health evidence. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8305859/)
   - Focus: normalize creativity as a human capacity; clarify boundaries between education and therapy.

2. **Reflective journaling for mood and self-understanding**  
   - Content: structured journaling types (gratitude, strengths, self-compassion, problem-solving). [va](https://www.va.gov/WHOLEHEALTHLIBRARY/docs/Therapeutic-Journaling.pdf)
   - Skills: prompts, time limits, noticing rumination, integrating with daily routines.

3. **Low-intensity expressive writing for stress and emotion processing**  
   - Content: brief writing exercises on everyday stressors, guided by emotion-acceptance and cognitive reappraisal instructions. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10300201/)
   - Safety: explicit “do not use this for major trauma without a therapist,” stop rules, grounding.

4. **Visual emotion mapping and self-awareness**  
   - Content: simple art tasks to label and externalize emotions; use of color, line, and shape for mood tracking. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11109233/)
   - Skills: connecting images to self-chosen language and coping steps, without facilitator interpretation.

5. **Creative meaning-making and values-based expression**  
   - Content: writing and art exercises focusing on values, strengths, small wins, social connection, and hope. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.00584/full)
   - Emphasis: integrating experiences into a coherent, compassionate narrative without processing trauma in detail.

6. **Building gentle creative routines**  
   - Content: designing sustainable creative habits for pleasure, connection, and regulation. [who](https://www.who.int/initiatives/arts-and-health)
   - Skills: habit design, low-pressure creativity, aligning with energy and access.

7. **Safety, boundaries, and when to seek help (mandatory module)**  
   - Content: screening questions, examples of red flags, guidance on therapy vs. education, and crisis resources. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/news-room/apa-blogs/creative-arts-enhancing-mental-health)

## 8. Claims to narrow and safer wording

### 8.1 Claims to narrow or avoid

Marketing statements that likely **overstate the science** for a generic course:

- “Heal deep psychological wounds through art and writing.”  
- “Treat trauma, anxiety, and depression with creative expression alone.”  
- “Art therapy tools for self-healing” (if the course does not involve licensed art therapists).  
- “Resolve PTSD or eating disorders through expressive writing or drawing.”  

These claims misrepresent both the **strength of evidence** and **scope of practice**. [ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK588208/)

### 8.2 Safer evidence-based wording

Examples of **course-level** wording consistent with the evidence:

- “Learn evidence-informed creative practices—like reflective journaling, expressive writing, and simple art exercises—to support emotional awareness, regulation, and everyday wellbeing.” [who](https://www.who.int/europe/publications/i/item/9789289054553)
- “Explore how writing and art can help you notice, name, and express emotions in ways that many people find clarifying and calming.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10300201/)
- “This course is educational and not a substitute for therapy. It can complement work with a mental health professional if you choose.” [henryford](https://www.henryford.com/visitors/caregivers/care-connections/education/art-therapy-vs-art-education)
- “We will not interpret your art or writing. You choose what your creative work means and how much you want to share.” [ciiat](https://ciiat.org/faqs/tarts/afl/what-is-the-difference-between-art-therapy-and-therapeutic-arts/)

## 9. Red-flag and referral guidance

The course should include clear, repeated guidance for participants and facilitators. Examples:

### 9.1 Participant-facing red flags (self-screening)

Participants should be advised to seek professional help if they:

- Experience **frequent or intense suicidal thoughts, self-harm urges, or plans**, especially linked to course exercises. [ashe](https://www.ashe.org/patientsafety)
- Notice **worsening depression, hopelessness, or inability to function** over several weeks. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3830620/)
- Have **flashbacks, dissociation, panic attacks, or overwhelming distress** when engaging in creative exercises, especially involving past trauma. [journals.sagepub](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/15248380221097442)
- Have a history of **psychosis, bipolar disorder with psychotic features, or severe eating disorder** and are not currently under clinical care. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2215036616301468)

The course can provide scripted language: “If you notice any of these signs, it is important to pause the course, reduce or stop the exercises, and contact a licensed mental health professional or crisis service.”

### 9.2 Facilitator-facing red flags

Course materials should train facilitators to:

- Avoid giving clinical advice or diagnoses.  
- Immediately suggest clinical help when participants express:  
  - suicidal ideation or self-harm intent;  
  - severe, persistent distress;  
  - psychotic experiences;  
  - active eating disorder behaviors.  
- Maintain **strict boundaries**: no private therapy-like messages, no interpretation of art/writing, no promises of healing. [pdresources](https://www.pdresources.org/product/ethics-boundaries-in-psychotherapy/)

## 10. Summary: Evidence-based course positioning

- **Strongest evidence** supports modules on: structured journaling, low-intensity expressive writing, visual emotion labeling, creative routines, and meaning-oriented reflection, all framed as tools for **emotional awareness, regulation, and wellbeing**, not as standalone treatments. [journals.plos](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0308928)
- **Licensed art therapy and creative arts therapies** have stronger clinical evidence for depression, anxiety, trauma-related distress, psychosis, and eating disorders, but they require trained therapists and clinical contexts and should not be implied in a generic “course.” [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6940592/)
- The phrase “healing psychological wounds” is **too strong** for a psychoeducational course. It should be softened to language about **supporting emotional wellbeing, expression, and meaning**, clearly distinguishing the course from clinical treatment. [ovid](https://www.ovid.com/journals/regep/abstract/10.1177/1089268019831645~a-meta-analysis-of-expressive-writing-on-posttraumatic?redirectionsource=fulltextview)
- A high-integrity course explicitly embeds **safety, trauma-informed principles, and referral guidance**, and positions creative practices as **complements** to, not replacements for, professional care. [psychiatry](https://www.psychiatry.org/news-room/apa-blogs/creative-arts-enhancing-mental-health)

This framing preserves the genuine benefits of creative expression while respecting clinical boundaries and the current evidence base.
