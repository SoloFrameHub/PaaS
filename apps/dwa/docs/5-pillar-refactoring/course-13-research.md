# Mental Health First Aid & Community Support: High-Rigor Research Brief for Course Development

**Prepared for:** Digital Wellness Academy — Real Psychiatric Services  
**Audience:** Licensed course development review team  
**Classification:** Expert-level; for internal curriculum authoring only  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Research Standard:** Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, RCTs, Cochrane reviews, WHO/APA/NICE/SAMHSA/AFSP/SPRC guidelines; time horizon 2020–present, landmark older evidence retained where still foundational

***

## Executive Summary

Mental Health First Aid (MHFA) and its cognate programs — gatekeeper training, community mental health literacy education, and stigma-reduction campaigns — have a coherent and meaningful, though importantly limited, evidence base. The evidence is **strong** for improving trainee knowledge, reducing stigma, and increasing helping intentions. It is **moderate** for short-term increase in actual helping behaviors. It is **weak-to-absent** for downstream patient-level or clinical outcomes, including individual mental health, mental health service utilization, or suicide rates. The 2023 Cochrane systematic review — the highest-rigor synthesis available — could not draw conclusions about MHFA's effect on recipient mental health, citing very low–certainty evidence, high risk of bias across all studies, and absence of any service-usage or adverse-effect data.[1][2]

**Course developers must calibrate language accordingly.** Marketing copy that implies MHFA training "saves lives," "prevents suicide," or "improves mental health outcomes" for recipients is unsupported by current high-quality evidence. The most defensible claims are those about trainee-level outcomes (literacy, stigma, confidence, intent to help) with honest acknowledgment that translation to patient-level benefit remains to be established.

## Part I: Defining the Safe Scope of a Non-Clinician MHFA Course

### What Learners Can Do (Appropriate Scope)

A non-clinician MHFA learner, upon course completion, is equipped to function as an informed, caring bridge — not a treatment provider. Their appropriate role, grounded in the internationally validated ALGEE action plan (Approach/assess, Listen, Give information, Encourage professional help, Encourage self-help strategies), includes:[3][4][5]

- **Noticing and approaching**: Recognizing behavioral, emotional, and verbal signs that a person may be experiencing mental health difficulties or distress, and finding a safe, private moment to express concern
- **Non-judgmental active listening**: Offering attentive, empathic, non-directive support, validating the person's experience without minimizing or catastrophizing
- **Providing psychoeducation**: Sharing basic, accurate information about mental health conditions to reduce shame and normalize help-seeking
- **Encouraging professional referral**: Helping the person identify appropriate professional resources (GPs, therapists, psychiatrists, crisis lines) and, if appropriate, offering to assist with access
- **Encouraging self-help**: Discussing known self-care and support strategies (peer support, community resources, lifestyle factors)
- **Crisis recognition and immediate escalation**: Identifying signs of acute crisis (suicidal ideation with plan/intent, active self-harm, psychotic episode, acute intoxication with medical risk, threat to others) and facilitating emergency response without delay
- **Safe messaging**: Using language about suicide and self-harm that adheres to evidence-based safe messaging guidelines from AFSP, SPRC, and WHO[6][7][8]
- **Maintaining their own wellbeing**: Recognizing and managing vicarious distress, knowing when and how to step back and seek their own support[9][10]

### What Learners Must Never Do (Hard Scope Boundaries)

These prohibitions are non-negotiable and must be written into the course with explicit, repeated safety language:

- **Never diagnose**: Non-clinicians are not equipped to assign DSM or ICD diagnostic labels; doing so may cause harm through misattribution or stigmatization
- **Never provide therapy**: No counseling, psychotherapy, cognitive restructuring, trauma processing, or clinical intervention of any kind
- **Never prescribe, recommend, or critique medications**: Medication advice — including whether to start, stop, or adjust psychotropic medications — belongs exclusively to prescribing clinicians
- **Never perform a clinical suicide risk assessment**: The standardized, structured clinical risk assessment (e.g., Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale, structured professional judgment tools) is a clinical competency. MHFA learners assess for *indicators* only, then escalate
- **Never promise confidentiality you cannot keep**: If a person's safety or the safety of others is at risk, or if mandatory reporting obligations apply (see below), that information must be shared appropriately — learners must be transparent about this from the outset
- **Never attempt to manage a severe mental health crisis alone**: Psychosis with behavioral disorganization, active suicidal crisis with means access, manic rage, severe dissociation, acute intoxication with medical risk — all require emergency professional response
- **Never engage therapeutically with an active abuser** in a domestic violence situation; this is dangerous and may worsen outcomes for the victim
- **Never position themselves as a substitute for professional care** in any way, including through extended "supportive" roles that function de facto as case management

### Where Crisis Intervention Must Defer to Professionals or Emergency Services

The following situations require immediate escalation beyond MHFA scope. The course must specify these clearly and repeatedly:

| Situation | Escalation Pathway |
|---|---|
| Active suicidal ideation with plan, intent, or access to means | Call 911 or accompany to emergency services; call 988 (US Suicide & Crisis Lifeline); do not leave person alone |
| Active self-harm occurring or wound requiring medical care | Call 911; provide basic physical first aid while waiting |
| Acute psychosis with behavioral disorganization, aggression, or threat | Call 911; do not physically intervene; create safe distance |
| Acute manic episode with agitation, impulsivity, or threat to self/others | Call 911 or contact person's emergency contact/treatment team |
| Acute intoxication with medical deterioration (overdose, loss of consciousness) | Call 911 immediately; this is a medical emergency |
| Disclosed or suspected child abuse or neglect | Follow mandatory reporting obligations (varies by state/jurisdiction); contact child protective services[11] |
| Disclosed domestic violence with immediate safety risk | Do not confront the abuser; help the person contact the National DV Hotline (1-800-799-SAFE) or 911 if in immediate danger[12] |
| Disclosed elder abuse | Contact adult protective services; mandatory reporting applies in many jurisdictions[11] |
| Severe dissociation, stupor, or unresponsive state | Call 911; do not attempt to "bring person back" through intense stimulation |
| Any situation where the learner feels unsafe | The learner's physical safety takes precedence; remove self from situation and call emergency services |

## Part II: Comprehensive Evidence Review

### 2.1 Mental Health First Aid Training

#### Trainee-Level Outcomes (Moderate-to-Strong Evidence)

The most comprehensive synthesis of MHFA evidence is the 2018 Morgan et al. systematic review and meta-analysis (PLoS ONE), covering 18 trials and 5,936 participants. It found:[13][14]

- **Knowledge gain**: Effect sizes Cohen's d = 0.31–0.72 (small-to-moderate), consistent across most studies
- **Recognition of mental disorders**: d = 0.22–0.52
- **Beliefs about effective treatments**: d = 0.19–0.45
- **Stigma reduction** (social distance, personal stigma): d = 0.08–0.14 (small but consistent)
- **Confidence in helping**: d = 0.21–0.58
- **Intentions to provide first aid**: d = 0.26–0.75
- **Actual helping behaviors at follow-up**: d = 0.23 at 6 months (small, statistically significant); quality of helping behaviors was unclear

These effects persisted to 6 months post-training, but evidence beyond 12 months was insufficient to draw conclusions.[13]

A complementary meta-analysis (Maslowski, 2018, University of Minnesota; 15 studies) confirmed small-to-moderate effect sizes for trainees, with study quality inversely associated with effect size — a critical finding suggesting that less methodologically rigorous studies inflate estimates.[15][16]

A 2023 Swiss RCT with undergraduate students demonstrated that MHFA significantly improved basic mental health knowledge and reduced stigma in the short and longer terms.[17]

#### Patient/Recipient-Level Outcomes (Very Weak Evidence — KEY CAVEAT)

This is where the evidence base is critically and consequentially thin. The 2023 Cochrane systematic review (Richardson et al.) represents the highest-quality synthesis available. Across 21 RCTs involving 22,604 participants, the review found:[2][1]

- **Individual mental health outcomes at 6–12 months**: OR 0.88 (95% CI 0.61–1.28), **very low certainty** — conclusions cannot be drawn
- **Mental health service usage**: Not measured at the primary timepoint in any included study
- **Adverse effects**: No published data

The review downgraded evidence for high risk of bias (all studies), serious unexplained heterogeneity (I² = 62%), and serious imprecision (wide CIs crossing the null).[1]

**⚠️ Marketing flag**: Claims such as "MHFA training improves the mental health of people in your community" or "reduces mental illness" are not supported by current evidence. The correct framing is: *"MHFA training improves trainee knowledge, reduces stigma, and increases willingness to help — the pathway from this to improved recipient mental health is plausible but not yet demonstrated."*

The most methodologically robust RCT on recipient outcomes (Morgan et al., 2019 BMC Psychiatry) randomized 384 parents to receive Youth MHFA or a physical first aid control, then tracked adolescent mental health at 1 and 2 years. There was **no significant difference** between groups in adolescent mental health outcomes at either timepoint. Parent knowledge improved, but this did not translate to measurable benefit in the young people.[18]

A 2021 evaluation of MHFA for the suicidal person course found improved beliefs, reduced stigma, and increased confidence among trainees, but noted that translating these improvements into actual behavior change remains the critical unresolved question.[19]

### 2.2 Gatekeeper Training

Gatekeeper training programs (e.g., QPR — Question, Persuade, Refer; LivingWorks ASIST; safeTALK) train lay people to identify suicide warning signs and connect at-risk individuals to professional help.[20][21]

#### Short-Term Evidence (Moderate)

- Immediate post-training improvements in **suicide-related knowledge, self-efficacy, and attitudes** are consistently demonstrated across studies[22][23][24]
- QPR training significantly improves attitudes, perceived behavioral control, and intention to intervene (Cohen's d = 0.49 for self-efficacy immediately post-training)[25]
- The Austrian national SUPRA evaluation (28 trainings, 427 participants) found substantial improvement in knowledge, self-efficacy, and attitudes immediately post-training, comparable to or larger than established programs; effects mostly upheld at 6-month follow-up[23]
- Online R&R (Recognise & Respond) gatekeeper training demonstrated non-inferiority to QPR online for confidence gains, with both groups maintaining improvements at 3-month follow-up[26]

#### Long-Term and Behavioral Evidence (Weak)

Holmes et al.'s systematic review of long-term gatekeeper training efficacy found:[22]
- Knowledge and self-efficacy showed the strongest endurance, but knowledge decayed over time
- Gatekeeper attitudes at follow-up had **returned to baseline in 57% of studies** (mean follow-up ~4.4 months)
- **Behavioral intention and actual behavior both show weak training effects**, with poor translation of training into real intervention behavior

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of gatekeeper training (PMC, 2025) similarly found that while immediate effects were supported, RCTs have not produced definitive results. ASIST implementation on the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline improved helpers' adherence to crisis intervention protocols.[27][28]

School-based suicide prevention meta-analysis (Kiran et al., reviewed by APA 2024): Universal gatekeeper interventions produced large increases in suicide prevention skills (SMD = 1.04); universal interventions were associated with a nearly twofold reduction in odds of suicidal thoughts and behaviors (OR = 1.85), but evidence quality was low in most individual studies.[29]

**Evidence summary for gatekeeper training**: Moderate evidence for trainee outcomes; weak evidence for behavioral translation; insufficient high-quality evidence for population-level suicide reduction attributable specifically to gatekeeper training.

### 2.3 Suicide Prevention Education

Meta-analysis of school-based suicide prevention programs (2021, n=27 studies): programs had the largest impact on suicide **awareness** (g = 0.72) and **helping skills** (g = 0.43); minimal effects on suicide behavior (g = 0.23), psychological wellness, or psychological distress. The scoping review of school-aged adolescent suicide prevention programs (PMC 2025) found consistent improvements in mental wellbeing, self-efficacy, and help-seeking, with reductions in suicidal behaviors — but cautions that evidence quality varied substantially.[30][31]

AFSP and SPRC safe messaging guidelines are consensus-based and have substantial indirect evidence from contagion research. The core evidence-based prohibitions:[7][8][6]
- Do not describe method or location
- Do not glorify, romanticize, or normalize suicide
- Do not present suicide as a solution to suffering or inevitable
- Do not simplify causes
- Do emphasize that help is available, recovery is possible, and suicide is preventable

### 2.4 Stigma Reduction

Contact-based education — combining educational information with first- or second-hand accounts from people with lived experience — is the most evidence-supported approach to stigma reduction. Meta-analytic effect sizes for contact-based education range from d = 0.28 to d = 0.47 for reducing social distance and personal stigma.[32][33]

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis of anti-stigma interventions in youth (10–19 years) confirmed that both education and contact approaches reduce stigma effectively in the short term. A 2025 JAMA Network Open review reaffirmed short-term benefits but highlighted **limited long-term effects**, calling for sustained, ongoing anti-stigma efforts rather than single-contact interventions.[34][35]

**⚠️ Marketing flag**: Stigma reduction from a single training course does not persist reliably. Claims of "lasting stigma reduction" require repeated exposure and community-level reinforcement. Single-dose training is likely insufficient for durable attitude change.

### 2.5 Community Mental Health Literacy

Mental health literacy (MHL) — the knowledge and beliefs about mental disorders that aid recognition, management, or prevention — is positively associated with help-seeking behavior. A Swiss longitudinal community study found MHL significantly predicted active help-seeking for mental health problems. MHL improves help-seeking indirectly through reduced perceived stigma and increased perceived social support.[36][37]

WHO's mhGAP programme (2023 third edition) provides the global evidence-based framework for training non-specialist health workers in mental health care in low-resource settings, including updated recommendations on depression, psychosis, suicidal ideation, substance use, and other priority conditions across 100+ countries.[38][39][40]

### 2.6 Help-Seeking Facilitation

MHL training increases intention and willingness to seek help, but the path from intention to actual help-seeking is mediated by structural access barriers, stigma, and individual factors. Effective help-seeking facilitation in a community course must include:[41][42]
- Concrete, accessible referral pathways (not just abstract encouragement)
- Addressing specific barriers for the target population (e.g., cultural stigma, cost, wait times)
- Recognition that different groups face differential access barriers (e.g., Black males at HBCUs face intersecting barriers of masculinity norms, minority stress, and institutional distrust)[41]

### 2.7 Crisis Recognition

The MHFA Australia Delphi methodology has produced expert consensus guidelines for recognizing and responding to psychosis, suicide risk, bipolar/mania, self-harm, and substance use — grounded in consensus of clinicians, consumers, and carers. These represent best available expert guidance, even where RCT evidence is lacking for specific recognition training.[43][44][45][46]

## Part IV: Best-Practice Content Areas

### 4.1 Approaching Someone in Distress

Evidence-based recommendations from MHFA Australia's ALGEE framework and trauma-informed care guidelines:[47][48][49][5][50]

- **Choose the right time and place**: Private, safe, quiet, away from observation pressure — never approach in public settings or in front of others without the person's consent
- **Use non-judgmental, open language**: "I've noticed you seem to be going through a lot lately — I wanted to check in." Avoid diagnostic framing ("You seem depressed") or pressure ("You need to get help now")
- **Lead with care, not diagnosis**: Express genuine concern for the person as a whole, not as a problem to be fixed
- **Ask permission**: "Is it okay if I ask how you're doing?" — respects autonomy and is consistent with trauma-informed care principles (safety, choice, collaboration)[51]
- **Be aware of cultural context**: Directness norms, eye contact, physical space, gender dynamics, and prior experiences with authority or healthcare vary significantly across communities
- **Shift from "what's wrong with you?" to "what happened to you?"**: This fundamental reorientation, central to trauma-informed practice, creates safety and reduces shame[49]
- **Be prepared for rejection**: The person may not want to talk; respect this. Leave the door open without pressure. This is not a failure of the approach.
- **Stay regulated yourself**: A calm, present affect is itself therapeutic — attunement and co-regulation have neurobiological bases in social engagement theory

### 4.2 Active Listening

Active listening was pioneered in the therapeutic tradition of Carl Rogers and Richard Farson; its evidence base in clinical settings is robust, though most research involves trained counselors rather than laypeople. The core components with evidence:[52]

- **Reflecting and paraphrasing**: Restating what the person said in slightly different words to communicate understanding ("It sounds like you've been feeling completely overwhelmed")
- **Emotional validation**: Naming the emotion without judgment ("That makes sense — what you're going through sounds exhausting")
- **Open questions**: Inviting elaboration rather than yes/no answers ("Can you tell me more about what's been happening?")
- **Tolerate silences**: Pauses are often productive; resist filling them with reassurance or advice
- **Non-verbal attunement**: Face the person, maintain culturally appropriate eye contact, open body posture, lean slightly forward; these signals of presence are neurologically processed as safety cues[53]
- **Avoid advice-giving prematurely**: A study of 115 participants found that those who received active listening responses felt more *understood* than those who received advice; satisfaction with conversation did not differ significantly between groups[52]
- **Avoid minimizing**: "Things could be worse," "at least you have X," "just think positive" all communicate that the person's distress is not valid
- **Burnout and emotional labor for the listener**: Mental health counselors who practice active listening are at risk for burnout through high emotional labor. This applies to community helpers; build in explicit helper self-care content.[54]

Research on nurse-led active listening for spouses of people with depression found that depressive scores in the partner (patient) improved significantly post-intervention, suggesting that even structured listening by a non-specialist can have measurable benefits — though this requires substantially more contact than a single interaction.[55]

### 4.3 Encouraging Professional Help

- Frame professional help as strength, not weakness or failure
- Provide concrete, specific pathways rather than generic encouragement: "You could talk to your GP, a counselor through [specific program], or call 988 to talk to someone trained in this today"
- Offer to help with practical barriers: "Would it help if I sat with you while you looked up what's available?" or "I can help you figure out how to make the first appointment"
- Address anticipated barriers explicitly: cost, stigma, wait times, past negative experiences with the system
- Respect autonomy: the person has the right to refuse help; maintain the relationship and keep the door open
- When there is imminent risk, professional escalation is not optional: the course must be explicit that in crisis situations, encouraging professional help is superseded by facilitating emergency services

### 4.4 Encouraging Self-Help and Support Strategies

Evidence-based self-help and support strategies appropriate to recommend:[4][56]

- **Social connection**: Peer support networks, community groups, trusted relationships
- **Physical health basics**: Sleep, nutrition, physical activity — each has its own substantial evidence base for mood regulation; mention without prescription
- **Structured self-management resources**: Apps, guided self-help programs, bibliotherapy with credible sources
- **Peer support programs**: Lived-experience peers; these are distinct from informal support and have a growing evidence base
- **Community resources**: Faith communities (for those who engage with them), cultural support networks, crisis lines
- **Reducing isolation**: Encourage continued social engagement even when withdrawal feels natural

**Important boundary**: Recommending specific products, supplements, alternative treatments, or specific coping behaviors (including exercise prescriptions) is outside scope. Frame all suggestions as *possibilities to explore* rather than prescriptions.

### 4.5 Safety Boundaries for Helpers

Course content must include explicit module on helper wellbeing and boundary maintenance. Key research-grounded content:[10][9]

- **Vicarious trauma (secondary traumatic stress)**: Estimated to affect 40–85% of helping professionals at some point; community helpers are also at risk[9]
- **Signs to recognize in oneself**: Intrusive thoughts about the person helped, avoidance, hypervigilance, sleep disruption, emotional numbness, cynicism
- **Boundary setting**: Helpers are not on call 24/7; they are not responsible for the outcome of the person's choices; maintaining their own wellbeing protects the quality of help they can offer
- **Not my responsibility to fix**: Empowerment, not rescue — the helper's role is to open doors, not to carry the person through them
- **When to stop**: If the helper is becoming distressed, re-traumatized, or unsafe, they must disengage and refer

### 4.6 Crisis Escalation Protocol

The course must teach a clear, memorable escalation decision tree. Best-practice protocol, synthesized from MHFA Australia, SAMHSA, and AFSP guidance:

1. **Assess for immediate risk**: Is there a threat to life (self or others)? Is there a weapon or means present? Is the person unable to ensure their own safety?
2. **If yes → IMMEDIATE escalation**: Call 911; call 988; do not leave person alone if safe to stay; do not attempt to manage alone
3. **If no immediate threat → MHFA support**: ALGEE protocol, professional referral, safety planning conversation
4. **Key question to always ask (in appropriate context)**: "Are you having thoughts of hurting or killing yourself?" — Asking does NOT increase suicide risk; evidence consistently shows it can provide relief[45]
5. **Communicate your concern to the person**: "I'm going to call for help because I'm worried about your safety — I'm doing this because I care about you"
6. **After the crisis**: The helper should debrief, seek support, and not carry this alone

## Part V: Specific Caution Areas and Course Guidance

### 5.1 Suicide and Self-Harm

**Evidence base**: High-quality Delphi consensus guidelines exist for suicidal ideation and non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) in the MHFA framework; NICE guideline NG225 (2022) provides comprehensive clinical guidance on self-harm assessment and management, including role of non-clinicians.[46][57][45]

**What the course should teach**:
- Warning signs: verbal cues (direct or indirect statements about wanting to die, hopelessness, being a burden), behavioral changes (giving away possessions, withdrawal, sudden calm after agitation), circumstantial factors (recent loss, access to lethal means)
- Safe messaging: Never detail methods; never glorify; always provide resources; emphasize hope and preventability[6][7]
- Direct, calm inquiry: "Are you thinking about suicide?" — Naming it directly is appropriate and reduces not increases risk
- NSSI recognition: cutting, burning, scratching — distinguish from suicide attempt; both require professional evaluation
- Not all self-harm is suicidal: NSSI is a coping mechanism; approach with curiosity and non-judgment, not alarm[58]

**Caution areas**:
- Never attempt to extract a "promise not to harm yourself" — this is clinically discredited and potentially harmful
- Never remove or hide means without consent and without professional involvement; this may escalate crisis
- Never leave an acutely suicidal person alone if it is safe to stay; if leaving is necessary, call 911
- Safe storage of lethal means (especially firearms) is a public health intervention with evidence; the course may mention this but not implement it
- Course content on suicide must follow AFSP/SPRC safe messaging standards throughout[7]

### 5.2 Psychosis

**Evidence base**: Two Delphi consensus guideline studies (MHFA Australia: 2007 original, 2021 updated) providing 89 endorsed items for psychosis first aid. No RCTs for specific psychosis first aid behaviors.[44][43]

**What the course should teach**:
- Early signs: social withdrawal, unusual beliefs or perceptions, disorganized speech or behavior, significant personality change, decline in self-care
- Approach calmly, in a quiet, low-stimulation environment; introduce yourself; speak clearly and simply
- Do not argue with delusional content; do not collude with it either — focus on the person's feelings ("That sounds frightening")
- If there is an advance directive or known treatment team, try to contact them[59]
- Encourage professional help urgently; psychosis requires medical evaluation

**Caution areas**:[60][61]
- **Severe psychotic state**: Do NOT approach without backup; maintain safe distance; primary goal is safety
- Do NOT make sudden movements, raised voices, or physical contact without permission
- Do NOT challenge hallucinations or delusions directly — this can escalate agitation
- Do NOT assume substance use explains all symptoms — psychosis may be primary or substance-induced
- Do NOT ignore warning signs as "just a phase" or normal developmental variation
- **When to call 911**: When person is a danger to self or others, or cannot ensure basic safety; do not delay

### 5.3 Mania

**Evidence base**: MHFA Australia guidance on bipolar disorder; clinical literature on urgent bipolar management.[62][63][64]

**What the course should teach**:
- Signs of mania: elevated/expansive/irritable mood, decreased sleep without fatigue, grandiosity, impulsivity (financial, sexual, interpersonal), pressured speech, distractibility, risky behavior
- Approach as a calm presence; do not match the person's energy or argue with grandiose beliefs
- Recognize that the person may not perceive themselves as ill during a manic episode — engagement must be gentle

**Caution areas**:[62]
- Do NOT tell someone to "calm down" or dismiss the episode as character
- Do NOT adopt an overprotective or patronizing stance — this can provoke agitation
- Manic states carry elevated suicide risk, especially in mixed states; assess for suicidal ideation even when presentation seems elevated/euphoric[63]
- Do NOT make financial decisions on the person's behalf; do NOT give them money
- If behavior is a danger to self or others, or person is highly agitated or threatening: call 911

### 5.4 Intoxication and Substance Use

**Evidence base**: MHFA Australia substance use guidelines; SAMHSA guidance.[65]

**What the course should teach**:
- Signs of intoxication: slurred speech, unsteady gait, confusion, altered responsiveness
- Overdose recognition: unresponsive, slow or stopped breathing, blue lips — medical emergency requiring immediate 911 call
- Maintain safe physical distance; assess for potential aggression; be non-confrontational
- Do not expect the person to acknowledge a problem during acute intoxication
- Withdrawal from some substances (alcohol, benzodiazepines) is medically dangerous — do NOT encourage abrupt cessation without medical supervision

**Caution areas**:[65]
- Intoxication can mask pain from injury — be alert to medical comorbidity
- Aggressive behavior is more likely with stimulant intoxication and alcohol
- Do not leave alone if overdose risk is present; call 911
- Substance use may co-occur with suicidal crisis — heightens risk; treat as compounded emergency

### 5.5 Domestic Violence

**Evidence base**: MHFA guidance and DV literature; mandatory reporting obligations.[12][66][11]

**What the course should teach**:
- Recognition: unexplained or recurring injuries; fearfulness around a partner; isolation from family/friends; partner controlling behavior; flinching, jumpiness, changed behavior around the abusive person
- Safety first: never confront an abuser; this puts the victim at greater risk
- Create a private, safe space to ask; validate the person's experience; do not pressure disclosure
- Provide information about resources: National DV Hotline 1-800-799-SAFE (7233), local shelters, safety planning

**Caution areas**:
- Do NOT offer opinions on whether the person should leave — this is complex, dangerous, and ultimately their decision
- Do NOT promise to keep information confidential if there is a safety emergency
- Mandatory reporting may apply where children are present and endangered[11]
- Do NOT facilitate contact with the abuser, even if the person requests it, in a way that compromises safety
- Safety planning (identifying routes of escape, safe people, important documents) should be briefly outlined but not conducted by a non-clinician without DV specialist support

### 5.6 Child Safety

**Evidence base**: Mandatory reporting statutory framework; child welfare standards.[67][11]

**What the course should teach**:
- Recognizing signs of physical abuse, neglect, emotional abuse, or sexual abuse in children
- Non-clinicians in community settings may have mandatory reporting obligations depending on jurisdiction and role
- If a child discloses abuse: believe them; do not promise to keep secrets; tell them they were right to tell you; report to child protective services
- Do not conduct an investigation or detailed interview — this is for trained professionals

**Caution areas**:
- Failure to report suspected child abuse may carry legal consequences for mandatory reporters[11]
- Child safety supersedes confidentiality; the course must be clear about this hierarchy
- Do NOT confront a suspected abuser
- Assess whether a child is currently in a safe environment; if not, 911 may be needed

### 5.7 Severe Depression

**What the course should teach**:
- Recognition: persistent sadness, hopelessness, changes in sleep/appetite, loss of interest, cognitive slowing, social withdrawal, fatigue
- Suicide risk: moderate-to-high in severe depression; always assess for suicidal ideation
- Approach with patience; depression slows engagement; allow silences; do not demand that the person "snap out of it"
- Practical help may be welcome (accompanying to appointments, helping with daily tasks temporarily)[56]
- Encourage professional evaluation promptly; severe depression is treatable

**Caution areas**:[62]
- Do NOT minimize with toxic positivity ("count your blessings," "think positive")
- Enabling total social withdrawal can worsen depression; gently encourage connection without pressure
- Sudden improvement in mood in a severely depressed person may indicate resolution of ambivalence about suicide — reassess risk
- Do NOT attempt to provide therapy; listen and refer

### 5.8 Panic and Trauma Reactions

**Evidence base**: Trauma-informed care literature; psychological first aid (PFA) framework.[48][68][69][47][51]

**What the course should teach**:
- Panic attack recognition: acute onset, intense fear, physical symptoms (heart racing, shortness of breath, dizziness, chest pain), fear of dying or losing control; typically peaks in 10 minutes
- Stay calm; reassure that panic attacks, while frightening, are not medically dangerous
- Guide slow, controlled breathing (in through nose, out through mouth) without pressure; do not instruct hyperventilating person to breathe into a bag
- Acute trauma reactions (dissociation, flashbacks, hyperarousal): create physical safety; speak calmly; ground with simple sensory prompts ("Can you feel your feet on the floor?")[70]
- Do not require the person to narrate or re-tell their trauma experience

**Caution areas**:
- If panic symptoms include first-episode chest pain or irregular heartbeat in a person over 40 or with cardiac risk: call 911 to rule out medical emergency
- Trauma reactions may produce altered consciousness — do not leave person alone until stabilized
- Psychological first aid (PFA) — though widely endorsed by WHO, Red Cross, and SAMHSA — has limited empirical evidence for long-term effectiveness; its evidence base is primarily expert consensus and short-term feasibility studies[68]

## Part VI: Synthesis — Strongest Evidence Modules, Claims to Soften, and Required Safety Language

### 6.1 Strongest Evidence-Backed Modules (Build With Confidence)

These modules rest on the most robust evidence and can be presented with appropriate confidence:

1. **Mental Health Literacy Foundations**: Recognition of common conditions (depression, anxiety, psychosis, bipolar disorder, substance use disorders); knowledge of warning signs and risk factors. *Evidence: strong, consistent, replicated across populations*[14][17][13]

2. **Stigma Awareness and Reduction**: Contact-based education; first-person narratives; social distance concepts. *Evidence: moderate, effect sizes d = 0.28–0.47; note short-term nature*[32][34]

3. **ALGEE Action Plan**: The structured 5-step approach framework. *Evidence: programmatic consensus; individual elements supported by clinical literature*[71][3][4]

4. **Safe Messaging for Suicide**: Do's and don'ts for discussing suicide in community settings. *Evidence: strong expert consensus; AFSP/SPRC guidelines; indirect evidence from contagion research*[8][6][7]

5. **Crisis Recognition and Escalation**: Recognizing acute crisis; escalating to 988 and 911; when not to manage alone. *Evidence: consensus guidelines; expert Delphi consensus*[4][45][60]

6. **Active Listening Skills**: Reflecting, validating, open questions, tolerating silence. *Evidence: moderate; primarily clinical literature; experimental evidence for trainee-level outcomes*[55][52]

7. **Helper Self-Care and Scope Awareness**: Vicarious trauma, boundary maintenance, when to step back. *Evidence: substantial occupational health literature*[10][9]

8. **Help-Seeking Facilitation**: Concrete referral pathways; barrier reduction. *Evidence: moderate; MHL-help-seeking association well-established*[37][42][36]

### 6.2 Claims to Soften or Reframe

The following specific claims, common in MHFA marketing and course descriptions, require modification for intellectual honesty and regulatory safety:

| Claim to Avoid | Evidence-Supported Alternative |
|---|---|
| "MHFA saves lives" | "MHFA equips people with skills that may contribute to life-saving referrals — the evidence for population-level suicide reduction is emerging" |
| "Reduces mental illness in the community" | "Helps identify and support people who may be experiencing mental health difficulties" |
| "Proven to improve recipient mental health" | "Has demonstrated benefits for trained helpers; effects on those helped are plausible but not yet established by high-quality research"[1] |
| "You'll know what to do in any mental health crisis" | "You'll have a structured approach and the confidence to take helpful first steps and connect people to professional care" |
| "Replaces the need for professional services" | [Should never appear in any form; this framing is dangerous] |
| "Reduces suicide rates" | "Gatekeeper training is one component of comprehensive suicide prevention; evidence for population-level impact is promising but requires large-scale systemic approaches"[22] |
| "Long-lasting attitude change" | "Attitudes improve post-training; sustained change requires ongoing reinforcement and practice"[35] |

### 6.3 Strict Safety Language — Required in All Course Materials

The following language (or equivalent) must appear explicitly in all course materials, not buried in fine print:

**General scope disclaimer** (required in course introduction):
> *This course provides education to support people in taking initial, helpful action when someone may be experiencing a mental health difficulty. It does not provide clinical training, certify participants as mental health clinicians, or authorize participants to conduct assessments, diagnoses, or treatment of any kind. Completion of this course does not create a therapeutic relationship or professional duty of care.*

**Crisis boundary statement** (required in all crisis-related modules):
> *In situations where someone's life may be at risk — including active suicidal crisis, acute psychosis with danger, overdose, or any situation involving immediate threat of harm — the appropriate response is always to call emergency services (911 in the US) and/or the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline. Attempting to manage such situations independently exceeds the safe scope of this training.*

**Confidentiality boundary statement** (required):
> *Mental Health First Aid is not therapy and is not bound by clinical confidentiality. If someone's safety or the safety of others is at immediate risk, that concern must override privacy. Mandatory reporting obligations for suspected child abuse, elder abuse, or (in some jurisdictions) intimate partner violence apply regardless of any expectation of privacy.*

**Safe messaging advisory** (required for all suicide-related content):
> *All content in this course that discusses suicide, self-harm, and mental health crises follows the evidence-based safe messaging guidelines established by the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention (AFSP) and the Suicide Prevention Resource Center (SPRC). If you are experiencing thoughts of suicide, please call or text 988 now.*

**Scope and referral boundary** (required):
> *If you are currently supporting someone in distress, this course is not a substitute for that person receiving professional mental health care. Please encourage professional help and use the crisis resources provided in this course.*

### 6.4 Recommended Crisis Boundary Wording for Specific Situations

**For the module introduction to crisis recognition, use this language**:
> *"This section teaches you to recognize the signs that a situation has moved beyond what Mental Health First Aid can address, and what to do in those moments. Recognizing these boundaries is not a failure — it is the most important skill in this training. Knowing when to call for help, and doing so quickly, is the action that matters most."*

**For the suicide module, use this language**:
> *"Asking someone directly if they are thinking about suicide is not only appropriate in the right context — it is one of the most important things you can do. Research consistently shows that asking does not plant the idea or increase risk. What you do after they answer matters: if someone says they are, take it seriously, stay with them if it is safe to do so, and connect them to professional help immediately."*

**For handoff and referral, use this language**:
> *"Your role is to be the bridge, not the destination. Just as someone who is trained in CPR begins chest compressions and calls 911 — not because CPR isn't valuable, but because it is a life-saving bridge to the care that person actually needs — your role in mental health first aid is to provide immediate, compassionate support while ensuring the person reaches appropriate professional care."*

## Part VII: Additional Development Recommendations

### Evidence Gaps That Require Intellectual Honesty in the Course

1. **Adverse effects of MHFA training are unstudied**: The 2023 Cochrane review found no published data on adverse effects — including whether training re-traumatizes some participants or places inappropriate burden on learners. The course should acknowledge this, include participant opt-out pathways, and build in support for distressed learners.[1]

2. **Cultural generalizability is limited**: Most MHFA evidence comes from Western, high-income, English-language populations. Adaptation for diverse cultural, linguistic, and socioeconomic contexts requires deliberate work beyond what the existing evidence base can support.

3. **Training decay is real and requires planning**: Gatekeeper attitudes return to baseline in 57% of studies within ~4 months. Any course that implies its effects are durable without reinforcement misrepresents the evidence. Recommend booster content at 6-month intervals.[22]

4. **Online versus in-person delivery**: Online gatekeeper training has demonstrated non-inferiority to in-person QPR for confidence gains, but the majority of the MHFA training literature involves in-person delivery. The interactive, relational components of MHFA may be harder to replicate digitally; this should be noted.[26]

5. **Psychological first aid (PFA)** — widely used in disaster and acute crisis contexts and aligned with MHFA principles — has been endorsed by WHO, SAMHSA, and Red Cross, but lacks the rigorous evidence base of clinical interventions. Its promulgation has outpaced its evaluation. Include with appropriate caveats.[68]
