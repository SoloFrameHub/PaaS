Stabilization is the first phase of trauma recovery — and it's the phase you can work on right now, with or without a therapist. It means creating enough safety, routine, and self-care in your daily life that your nervous system can begin to calm down.

This isn't about "fixing" yourself. It's about creating the conditions where healing can happen.

## 1. Why Stabilization Comes First

Trauma therapy follows a three-phase model supported by research:

**Phase 1: Stabilization and Safety** — Where you are now
- Establishing safety in daily life
- Reducing overwhelming symptoms
- Learning coping strategies
- Addressing basic needs
- Building a foundation for deeper work

**Phase 2: Trauma Processing** — With professional guidance
- Working through traumatic memories
- Making sense of what happened
- Requires professional support

**Phase 3: Integration and Reconnection** — Long-term
- Applying insights to present life
- Rebuilding relationships and meaning
- Moving forward

**You are working on Phase 1 in this course.** This is not the "easy" phase or the "before you get to the real work" phase. Stabilization IS the work. Research shows it produces measurable improvements in PTSD symptoms on its own.

## 2. The Foundations of Stabilization

### Safety

Safety is the bedrock of trauma recovery. This means:

**Physical safety:**
- Are you in a safe living environment?
- Are you free from ongoing abuse or threat?
- If not, please reach out to resources (National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233)

**Emotional safety:**
- Do you have at least one person you feel safe with?
- Can you set boundaries with people who are harmful to you?
- Are you able to limit contact with people who trigger you?

### Sleep

Trauma and sleep have a difficult relationship. Your alarm system stays active at night, nightmares may disturb your rest, and hypervigilance makes it hard to relax.

**Sleep stabilization strategies:**
- Maintain consistent sleep and wake times — even on weekends
- Create a calming bedtime routine
- Make your bedroom feel safe (nightlight, locked doors, whatever you need)
- Limit screens before bed
- Use grounding if you wake from a nightmare
- Avoid using alcohol as a sleep aid — it disrupts sleep quality

### Routine

Predictability helps your nervous system feel safer. When your days have structure, there are fewer surprises for your alarm system to react to.

- Build a simple daily routine — wake time, meals, activities, bedtime
- Include at least one positive activity each day
- Don't over-schedule — leave room for rest and flexibility
- Consistency matters more than perfection

### Basic Needs

Trauma often disrupts the most basic self-care. Gently reconnect with:

- **Nutrition:** Regular meals, even if small. Nourishing food when possible.
- **Hydration:** Drinking enough water throughout the day
- **Movement:** Gentle physical activity — walking, stretching, yoga
- **Hygiene:** Basic personal care (shower, clean clothes, brushing teeth)

If any of these feel overwhelming, start with one small step. Brushing your teeth counts. Making a piece of toast counts. Every act of self-care is a statement that you matter.

## 3. Boundaries: Protecting Your Recovery

Boundaries are essential during trauma recovery. They protect your energy, your safety, and your healing process.

<ScenarioCard title="When Someone Pushes Past Your Boundaries">
A family member keeps asking detailed questions about what happened to you. They say they're asking because they care, but each conversation leaves you feeling drained and activated. You've hinted that you don't want to talk about it, but they keep bringing it up.
**What you might want to say but feel unable to:** "Please stop asking me about this."
**What a clear, compassionate boundary sounds like:** "I know you care, and I appreciate that. I'm not ready to talk about what happened, and when I am, I'll let you know. For now, the most helpful thing you can do is just spend time with me without bringing it up."
**Remember:** You do not owe anyone your story. You choose who knows, what they know, and when they learn it. A boundary is not rejection — it is self-protection.
</ScenarioCard>

## 4. Self-Care Across Domains

Self-care for trauma recovery goes beyond bubble baths. It spans multiple areas of life:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Physical** | Gentle movement, adequate sleep, nourishing food, reducing caffeine and alcohol, warm baths, time outdoors |
| **Emotional** | Journaling, creative expression, allowing feelings without judgment, setting emotional boundaries |
| **Social** | Connecting with safe people, asking for support, attending a support group, spending time with animals |
| **Spiritual** | Meditation (if it feels safe), time in nature, meaningful activities, practices that give you a sense of purpose |
| **Practical** | Managing responsibilities in small steps, organizing your space, reducing clutter, maintaining a routine |

<SlideNavigation persistKey="trauma-lesson6-selfcare-domains">
<Slide title="Physical Self-Care">Gentle movement, adequate sleep, and nourishing food form the physical foundation of recovery. You do not need to run marathons or follow strict diets — a short walk outside, a warm meal, or simply stretching for a few minutes all count. Reducing caffeine and alcohol can also help your nervous system settle. Listen to what your body needs today, not what you think it should need.</Slide>
<Slide title="Emotional Self-Care">Journaling, creative expression, and allowing yourself to feel without judgment are powerful emotional supports. You might draw, write, play music, or simply sit with what you are feeling. The goal is not to fix emotions but to acknowledge them. Setting emotional boundaries — choosing not to absorb other people's distress — is also a form of emotional self-care.</Slide>
<Slide title="Social Self-Care">Connecting with safe people, even briefly, helps counteract the isolation that trauma often creates. This could mean texting a friend, attending a support group, or spending time with a pet. You do not have to share your story to benefit from social connection — just being around someone who feels safe can be restorative.</Slide>
<Slide title="Spiritual Self-Care">This is about meaning and purpose, however you define it. Time in nature, meditation (if it feels safe for you), meaningful activities, or practices that connect you to something larger than your pain. If traditional spiritual practices feel complicated right now, simply noticing beauty — a sunset, a kind gesture — can nourish this part of you.</Slide>
<Slide title="Practical Self-Care">Managing responsibilities in small, manageable steps reduces the overwhelm that feeds trauma symptoms. Organizing one drawer, paying one bill, or tidying one corner of your space can create a sense of control and accomplishment. Routine maintenance of your environment is a quiet but powerful form of self-care.</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

### Self-Care Is Not Selfish
If you find yourself feeling guilty about self-care, remember: **you cannot heal from a depleted state.** Taking care of your basic needs isn't optional — it's the foundation that everything else rests on.
## 5. Navigating Difficult Days
Some days will be harder than others. This is not a sign of failure — it's the nature of recovery.
### When You're Having a Hard Day
- **Lower your expectations** — survival mode is enough
- **Do one small thing** — get out of bed, eat something, take a shower
- **Use grounding** — when the past feels more real than the present
- **Reach out** — to a safe person, a crisis line, or a therapist
- **Remind yourself** — "Bad days don't erase good days. This will pass."
### Things That Can Make Days Harder
- Poor sleep the night before
- Anniversary dates or seasonal triggers
- Conflict with others
- Seeing something in the news related to your trauma
- Physical illness or fatigue
- Stress from work, finances, or relationships
- Overexertion — pushing too hard without rest
### Pacing
**Healing is not linear.** You may have a great week followed by a terrible day. That terrible day doesn't mean you've gone backwards — it means your nervous system is still learning to regulate.
Go slowly. Rest when needed. There is no deadline for healing.
## 6. What Self-Care Is NOT
Self-care for trauma is NOT:
- **Numbing:** Using substances, excessive screen time, or overwork to avoid feeling
- **Avoidance disguised as self-care:** "I'm taking care of myself by never leaving the house"
- **Punishment-based:** "I'll eat properly and exercise because I should" (this creates more stress)
- **Performance:** "I need to meditate for 30 minutes every day perfectly"
Self-care IS:
- **Gentle:** Kind and compassionate toward yourself
- **Flexible:** Adapting to what you need on any given day
- **Honest:** Acknowledging when you're struggling
- **Basic:** Sometimes self-care is brushing your teeth and drinking a glass of water

## 7. Key Takeaways
1. **Stabilization is Phase 1 of trauma recovery** — and it IS the work, not preparation for the work
2. **Safety, sleep, routine, and basic needs** are the foundations
3. **Boundaries protect your healing** — you do not owe anyone your story or your energy
4. **Self-care spans physical, emotional, social, spiritual, and practical** domains
5. **Healing is not linear** — bad days don't erase progress
6. **Go slowly and pace yourself** — there is no deadline
7. **Self-care is not selfish** — it's the foundation of recovery
