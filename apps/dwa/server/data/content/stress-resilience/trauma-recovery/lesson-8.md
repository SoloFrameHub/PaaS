This course has given you psychoeducation about trauma, grounding techniques, trigger management, stabilization practices, and safety planning. These are real, evidence-based skills that make a meaningful difference.

But for many people with PTSD, full recovery involves professional trauma therapy — working with a trained therapist who can guide you through processing traumatic memories safely. In this final lesson, you'll learn what professional treatment looks like, which approaches have the strongest evidence, and how to find the right help.

## 1. When to Seek Professional Help

Consider professional evaluation if:

- **Symptoms have lasted more than one month** and are not improving
- **Symptoms interfere with daily life** — work, relationships, basic functioning
- **You're using substances** (alcohol, drugs) to cope with symptoms
- **You have thoughts of self-harm** or suicide
- **You feel unable to function** — can't work, can't care for yourself, can't leave the house
- **You want support with trauma processing** — moving beyond coping into healing
- **Flashbacks or dissociation are severe or frequent**
- **You feel stuck** despite using self-help strategies consistently

**Seeking help is not failure. It's using the most effective tools available.**

## 2. Evidence-Based Trauma Treatments

Research, including the latest APA and VA/DoD guidelines, recommends specific therapies as first-line treatments for PTSD:

### Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

- **What it is:** A structured 12-session therapy that focuses on the thoughts and beliefs you developed about the trauma
- **How it works:** Identifies "stuck points" — unhelpful beliefs like "It was my fault" or "I can never be safe" — and helps you examine and modify them
- **Key strength:** You do NOT need to retell the trauma in detail. It focuses on thoughts, not reliving
- **Evidence:** Strong research support; first-line recommendation by APA and VA/DoD
- **Best for:** People whose trauma has changed their beliefs about themselves, others, or the world

### Prolonged Exposure (PE)

- **What it is:** Gradually approaching trauma-related memories and situations in a safe therapeutic environment
- **How it works:** You retell the trauma narrative repeatedly until it loses its emotional charge, and you gradually re-enter avoided situations
- **Key strength:** Gold-standard treatment with decades of evidence
- **Evidence:** Strong research support; first-line recommendation
- **Note:** Can be challenging; has higher dropout rates than some other therapies. A skilled therapist paces this carefully.

### Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)

- **What it is:** A therapy that uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, tapping, or audio tones) while you briefly hold traumatic memories in mind
- **How it works:** The bilateral stimulation appears to help the brain reprocess traumatic memories so they lose their emotional intensity
- **Key strength:** Does NOT require detailed retelling of the trauma
- **Evidence:** Effective, especially for single-incident trauma. Conditional recommendation by APA, recommended by WHO and VA/DoD
- **Best for:** People who want to avoid detailed verbal recounting of trauma

### Written Exposure Therapy

- **What it is:** Writing about the traumatic experience in a structured format over 5 sessions
- **How it works:** The writing process helps organize and process traumatic memories
- **Key strength:** Shorter treatment (5 sessions vs. 12-16), improved retention compared to PE
- **Evidence:** Strong emerging evidence; recommended in 2023 VA/DoD guidelines

### Key Finding

The latest guidelines recommend **specific manualized psychotherapies over medication** as first-line treatment for PTSD. Therapy is the primary treatment — medication can be a helpful addition, not a replacement.

### What Won't Happen

- You won't be forced to talk about anything you're not ready for
- A good therapist will not push you faster than you can handle
- You won't be "re-traumatized" by a competent, trained therapist
- You won't have to start with the worst memory — treatment is paced

### Your Rights as a Client

- You can ask questions about the therapist's training and approach
- You can say "I'm not ready for that" at any time
- You can take breaks during sessions
- You can switch therapists if the fit isn't right
- You are in control of your treatment

## 4. Medication Options

While therapy is the first-line treatment, medication can help manage symptoms:

**SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors):**
- Sertraline (Zoloft) and paroxetine (Paxil) are FDA-approved for PTSD
- Can reduce the intensity of symptoms while you engage in therapy
- Typically take 4-6 weeks for full effect

**Other medications that may help:**
- Prazosin for trauma-related nightmares
- SNRIs as an alternative to SSRIs

**Important:** Benzodiazepines are generally NOT recommended for PTSD — they can interfere with trauma processing and carry dependence risks. If you've been prescribed benzodiazepines, discuss this with your prescriber.

## 5. Finding a Trauma-Informed Therapist

### What to Look For

- **Training** in one of the evidence-based trauma treatments (CPT, PE, EMDR)
- **Experience** specifically with trauma and PTSD
- **A trauma-informed approach** — emphasizing safety, choice, and empowerment
- **Someone you feel comfortable with** — the therapeutic relationship matters

### Where to Search

- Ask your primary care doctor for a referral
- **Psychology Today therapist finder** — filter by "PTSD" and "trauma"
- **ABCT (Association for Behavioral and Cognitive Therapies)** directory
- **EMDR International Association** therapist directory
- **VA resources** for veterans
- **Community mental health centers** — often offer sliding-scale fees
- **Telehealth options** — effective and can expand your geographic range

### Questions to Ask a Potential Therapist

- "What is your training in trauma treatment?"
- "Which evidence-based approaches do you use?"
- "How do you handle it if I become overwhelmed during a session?"
- "What does the first few sessions look like?"
- "Do you have experience with [your type of trauma]?"

### It's Okay to Interview Multiple Therapists

Finding the right therapist is like finding the right fit for any important relationship. If the first person doesn't feel right, try another. The therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of treatment outcome.

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Professional trauma therapy is the most effective treatment** for PTSD
2. **CPT, PE, EMDR, and Written Exposure Therapy** all have strong evidence
3. **Therapy is recommended over medication** as first-line treatment
4. **You control the pace** — a good therapist will not push you faster than you can handle
5. **Finding the right therapist matters** — interview multiple if needed
6. **Seeking help is strength** — you deserve support in your healing
