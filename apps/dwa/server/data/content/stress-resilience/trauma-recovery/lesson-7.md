Trauma often damages trust — in others, in the world, and in yourself. Rebuilding a sense of safety and building connections with supportive people is a critical part of recovery. In this lesson, you'll learn how to create a safety plan, build a support system that works for you, and begin to explore the concept of post-traumatic growth.

## 1. The Safety Plan

A safety plan is a personalized guide for managing moments of crisis or intense distress. It's based on the Brown & Stanley Safety Planning model, which is widely used in mental health care.

**Create your safety plan now, when you're relatively stable.** During a crisis, you may not be able to think clearly enough to figure out what to do — having a written plan removes that barrier.

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Identify Your Warning Signs">Notice the early signals that you are becoming overwhelmed — increased irritability, withdrawing from people, disrupted sleep, feeling numb, or turning to substances. Write down your personal warning signs so you can recognize them before a crisis escalates.</Step>
<Step title="List Internal Coping Strategies">Gather the tools you can use on your own: grounding exercises, slow breathing, physical activity, creative outlets, journaling, or holding a pet. These are your first line of response when warning signs appear.</Step>
<Step title="Identify Social Contacts for Distraction">Think of people and places that provide healthy distraction — friends you can call for casual conversation, or places like a coffee shop or park where you feel comfortable. You do not need to explain what is happening; just being around others can help.</Step>
<Step title="Name People You Can Ask for Help">Identify family members or friends you trust enough to tell what is happening and ask for direct support. Choose people who are patient, non-judgmental, and generally available.</Step>
<Step title="Save Professional and Crisis Resources">Write down your therapist's contact information, your doctor's number, and key crisis lines: 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741), and 911 for immediate danger.</Step>
<Step title="Make Your Environment Safer">Take practical steps to increase safety in your physical space: remove or secure items that could be harmful during a crisis, create a calm corner in your home, and keep grounding objects nearby.</Step>
</StepByStep>

### Step 1: Warning Signs
Recognize the early signs that you're becoming overwhelmed:
**Your personal warning signs might include:**
- Increasing irritability or anger
- Withdrawing from people
- Difficulty sleeping or nightmares returning
- Feeling numb or disconnected
- Increased use of alcohol or substances
- Not eating or eating too much
- Intrusive thoughts becoming more frequent
- Feeling hopeless or trapped
### Step 2: Internal Coping Strategies
Things you can do on your own to manage distress:
- Grounding exercises (5-4-3-2-1, cold water, physical grounding)
- Slow breathing (4-count inhale, 6-count exhale)
- Physical activity (walking, stretching, light exercise)
- Creative activities (drawing, writing, music)
- Journaling about what you're feeling
- Holding or petting an animal
- Taking a warm shower or bath
### Step 3: Social Contacts for Distraction
People and places that provide healthy distraction (you don't necessarily need to tell them what's happening — just being around them helps):
- Friends you can call for casual conversation
- Places to go that feel good (coffee shop, park, library, bookstore)
### Step 4: People You Can Ask for Help
Family members or friends you can tell what's happening and ask for direct support:
- Someone who understands your trauma history
- Someone who is patient and non-judgmental
- Someone who is usually available when you need them
### Step 5: Professionals and Crisis Resources
- Your therapist (name and number)
- Your doctor (name and number)
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text **988**
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text **HOME** to **741741**
- **911** for immediate danger to life
- **National Domestic Violence Hotline:** 1-800-799-7233
### Step 6: Making Your Environment Safer
Steps you can take to increase safety in your physical environment:
- Removing or securing items that could be harmful during a crisis
- Creating a calm, safe space in your home (a corner, a room, a chair)
- Keeping grounding objects nearby (a textured stone, a cold pack, essential oils)
## 2. Building Your Support Network
### Who Counts as Support?
Support doesn't mean one person who does everything. Different people can fill different roles:
| Role | What They Provide | Example |
|------|------------------|---------|
| **Listener** | Hears you without trying to fix things | A friend who is patient and present |
| **Distracter** | Helps you get out of your head | A friend who makes you laugh |
| **Practical helper** | Assists with tasks when you're overwhelmed | Someone who can help with errands, meals, etc. |
| **Understanding person** | Knows about your trauma and "gets it" | A fellow survivor, a therapist, a support group |
| **Professional** | Provides clinical support | Therapist, doctor, crisis counselor |
### How to Build Support When It Feels Hard
Trauma often makes social connection feel dangerous. If building a support network feels overwhelming:
- **Start small:** One person is enough to begin with
- **Use low-risk connections first:** Online support groups, helplines, peer communities
- **Be selective:** Not everyone needs to know your story. You choose who, when, and how much to share
- **Test gradually:** Share a little and see how the person responds before sharing more
- **Allow imperfection:** No one will respond perfectly every time. That doesn't mean they're unsafe
### Support Groups
Trauma-specific support groups can be powerful:
- Being around others who "get it" reduces isolation
- Hearing others' stories normalizes your experience
- You can receive and give support
- Options include in-person groups, online communities, and telephone groups
- Many are free through community organizations, hospitals, or national organizations
## 3. Boundaries: Protecting Your Recovery
Boundaries are limits you set to protect your emotional and physical wellbeing. They are especially important during trauma recovery.
### Types of Boundaries
**Emotional boundaries:**
- Not taking responsibility for others' emotions
- Limiting contact with people who invalidate or dismiss your experience
- Choosing when and how much to share about your trauma
**Physical boundaries:**
- Controlling who touches you and how
- Creating physical space that feels safe
- Removing yourself from situations that feel threatening
**Time boundaries:**
- Not overcommitting when you're managing symptoms
- Protecting time for self-care and rest
- Saying no without guilt
### Setting Boundaries
- **Be clear and direct:** "I'm not comfortable discussing that right now"
- **You don't need to justify:** A simple "no" is a complete sentence
- **It will feel uncomfortable at first** — especially if you're not used to setting boundaries
- **Boundaries are not mean** — they're necessary for your recovery
## 4. When People Don't Understand
Not everyone will understand your trauma or your recovery process. You may encounter:
- **Dismissiveness:** "Just move on" or "It happened so long ago"
- **Minimization:** "Other people have it worse" or "At least you survived"
- **Unwanted advice:** "You should try yoga" or "Just think positive"
- **Blame:** Subtle or overt suggestions that the trauma was your fault

### How to Respond
- **You don't owe anyone an explanation** of your trauma or your recovery
- **Limit contact** with people who consistently invalidate you
- **Educate when you want to** — not when you feel obligated
- **Seek out people who do understand** — support groups, therapists, fellow survivors
- **Protect yourself:** Their ignorance is not more important than your healing
## 5. Post-Traumatic Growth: A Nuanced Perspective
Some people who go through trauma eventually experience what researchers call **post-traumatic growth** — positive psychological changes that emerge from the struggle with a highly challenging life event. This is not about "being grateful" for trauma or "finding the silver lining." It is about recognizing that sometimes, the process of rebuilding can lead to unexpected growth.
<Callout type="warning" title="An Important Note">
Post-traumatic growth is not required. It is not the goal of recovery. It is not something you should pressure yourself to experience. Some people find meaning in their experiences; others do not. Both paths are completely valid. This section is included so you can recognize growth if it happens — not so you feel obligated to find it.
</Callout>

## 6. Key Takeaways
1. **Create a safety plan now**, when you're stable — so it's ready when you need it
2. **Your safety plan has 6 steps** — warning signs, coping strategies, distractions, support people, professionals, and environment
3. **Support networks can be built gradually** — start small and test trust over time
4. **Different people fill different roles** — you don't need one person to do everything
5. **Boundaries protect your recovery** — they're necessary, not mean
6. **Not everyone will understand** — that's their limitation, not yours
7. **Post-traumatic growth is possible but not required** — your recovery is valid either way
