# Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index

## Self-Assessment

> "Before we can improve your sleep, we need to understand where you are starting. This assessment is based on the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, one of the most widely used and validated tools in sleep research. Answer each question honestly -- there are no right or wrong answers, only your experience."
>
> **-- Autumn Persinger, PMHNP-BC**

### Important: Educational Tool Disclaimer

This is a **simplified, educational version** of the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI). It is designed to help you reflect on your sleep patterns and build self-awareness. It is **not a clinical diagnosis** and should not be used as a substitute for evaluation by a healthcare professional.

If your results suggest concerning sleep quality, we encourage you to share them with your healthcare provider.

## How This Assessment Works

You will answer questions about seven areas of your sleep over the **past month**. For each question, select the response that best describes your experience. At the end, you will see a total score with an interpretation to help guide your learning in this course.

Your responses are saved privately to your device and are never sent to a server.

<Callout type="tip" title="Getting the Most From This Assessment">
Take your time with each question. Think specifically about the past month rather than how you slept last night or how you sleep "in general." Your honest answers will give you the most useful starting point for the rest of this course.
</Callout>

## The Seven Components of Sleep Quality

The PSQI evaluates your sleep across seven key dimensions. For each component, rate your experience on a scale from 0 (best) to 3 (worst).

### Component 1: Subjective Sleep Quality

**During the past month, how would you rate your overall sleep quality?**

- 0 = Very Good
- 1 = Fairly Good
- 2 = Fairly Bad
- 3 = Very Bad

### Component 2: Sleep Latency

**During the past month, how long (in minutes) has it usually taken you to fall asleep each night?**

- 0 = 15 minutes or less
- 1 = 16 to 30 minutes
- 2 = 31 to 60 minutes
- 3 = 60 minutes or more

<FlipCard front="What is considered a 'normal' time to fall asleep?" back="Most healthy sleepers fall asleep within 10-20 minutes. If you fall asleep the moment your head hits the pillow (under 5 minutes), it may actually indicate sleep deprivation. If it takes more than 30 minutes regularly, this is called 'sleep onset insomnia.'" />

### Component 3: Sleep Duration

**During the past month, how many hours of actual sleep did you get per night?** (This may be different from the number of hours you spent in bed.)

- 0 = 7 or more hours
- 1 = 6 to 7 hours
- 2 = 5 to 6 hours
- 3 = Less than 5 hours

### Component 4: Sleep Efficiency

**During the past month, how much of the time you spent in bed were you actually sleeping?** (Think of this as your "sleep efficiency.")

- 0 = 85% or more
- 1 = 75% to 84%
- 2 = 65% to 74%
- 3 = Less than 65%

<EnhancedAccordion title="How to Calculate Your Sleep Efficiency">
Sleep efficiency is calculated as: (Hours of actual sleep / Hours spent in bed) x 100. For example, if you spend 8 hours in bed but only sleep 6 hours, your efficiency is 75% (6/8 x 100). A healthy sleep efficiency is 85% or higher. If your efficiency is low, it may help to spend less total time in bed -- this sounds counterintuitive, but it is a core principle of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I).
</EnhancedAccordion>

### Component 5: Sleep Disturbances

**During the past month, how often have you had trouble sleeping because of things like waking up in the night, needing the bathroom, feeling too hot or cold, having bad dreams, or experiencing pain?**

- 0 = Not at all
- 1 = Less than once a week
- 2 = Once or twice a week
- 3 = Three or more times a week

### Component 6: Use of Sleep Medication

**During the past month, how often have you taken medicine (prescribed or over-the-counter) to help you sleep?**

- 0 = Not at all
- 1 = Less than once a week
- 2 = Once or twice a week
- 3 = Three or more times a week

### Component 7: Daytime Dysfunction

**During the past month, how much of a problem has it been to stay awake during the day, keep up your enthusiasm, or get things done?**

- 0 = No problem at all
- 1 = Only a very slight problem
- 2 = Somewhat of a problem
- 3 = A very big problem

## Understanding Your Score

Your total score ranges from **0 to 21**. Here is how to interpret your results:

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Score Range: 0-5 (Good Sleep Quality)">
Your responses suggest that your sleep quality over the past month has been relatively good. You are likely getting adequate rest and your sleep does not appear to be causing significant problems in your daily life.
This is a strong starting point. The lessons ahead will help you maintain and optimize what is already working well. You may discover small adjustments that make your good sleep even better.
</Step>
<Step title="Score Range: 6-10 (Poor Sleep Quality)">
Your responses suggest that your sleep quality has room for improvement. You may be experiencing some difficulties that affect how rested you feel during the day. This is very common -- and the good news is that the strategies in this course are specifically designed for situations like yours.
Many people score in this range, and it is a great place to start making meaningful changes. Pay close attention to the practical tools in the upcoming lessons -- small, consistent improvements can shift your score significantly over a few weeks.
</Step>
<Step title="Score Range: 11-21 (Concerning Sleep Quality)">
Your responses suggest that your sleep has been significantly disrupted over the past month. This level of sleep difficulty can have real effects on your mood, energy, concentration, and overall mental health. You deserve support.
**We strongly encourage you to share these results with a healthcare provider** who can rule out underlying sleep disorders and work with you on a comprehensive plan. This score is not a reflection of personal failure. Sleep difficulties at this level often have multiple contributing factors, and professional guidance can make a real difference.
</Step>
</StepByStep>

### What Your Component Breakdown Tells You

Each of the seven components receives a score from 0 to 3. Areas with higher scores may be good places to focus your attention as you move through the course:

<SlideNavigation persistKey="psqi-components-detail">
<Slide title="Subjective Sleep Quality">
Your overall perception of how well you sleep. This is the broadest measure and reflects your general satisfaction with sleep. If this score is high, the other components will help you identify specific areas to target.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Sleep Latency">
How long it takes you to drift off. If this is your highest-scoring area, the techniques in Lesson 3 (Quieting the Racing Mind) will be especially relevant for you.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Sleep Duration & Efficiency">
How many hours you sleep and what percentage of your time in bed is actually spent sleeping. These two components work together -- sometimes improving efficiency is more impactful than simply spending more time in bed.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Sleep Disturbances & Medication">
How often you wake during the night and whether you rely on sleep aids. Lesson 4 (Building Your Sleep Sanctuary) addresses environmental factors that contribute to disturbances.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Daytime Dysfunction">
How much poor sleep affects your daily life. This component reflects the downstream impact of poor sleep on your mood, energy, and productivity.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>
