## Exercise and Sleep

### The Evidence

Regular physical activity significantly improves sleep quality:
- Meta-analysis of 81 studies shows **significant improvement in sleep quality**
- Exercise improves melatonin production and body temperature regulation
- Benefits include more deep sleep and reduced time to fall asleep
- Works for both insomnia and general sleep quality improvement

### How Exercise Helps Sleep

**Biological Mechanisms:**
- **Boosts melatonin production** — Your natural sleep hormone
- **Regulates body temperature** — Exercise raises temperature; the subsequent drop signals sleep
- **Reduces anxiety and stress** — Major contributors to insomnia
- **Increases time in deep sleep** — The most restorative stage
- **Helps synchronize circadian rhythm** — Especially when done consistently

### Exercise Guidelines for Better Sleep

**Optimal Frequency and Duration:**
- **Frequency:** 4-7 times per week
- **Duration:** 30+ minutes per session
- **Weekly total:** 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise

**Best Types for Sleep:**
- Walking, jogging, swimming, cycling (aerobic exercise)
- Resistance training also beneficial
- Yoga shown to improve sleep quality

**Timing Matters:**

| Timing | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Morning exercise | Good for circadian rhythm; energizes your day |
| Afternoon exercise | Often optimal for performance and sleep |
| Evening exercise (ending 4+ hours before bed) | Generally fine |
| Strenuous exercise ending &lt;4 hours before bed | May disrupt sleep |

**Key Point:** Complete vigorous exercise at least **4 hours before bedtime**. Gentle stretching or yoga close to bedtime is fine.

### If You're Not Currently Exercising

Start small:
- 10-minute walk daily
- Gradually increase duration and intensity
- Consistency matters more than intensity
- Any movement is better than none

Don't let "perfect" be the enemy of "good"—even moderate exercise helps sleep.

## Light Exposure: Your Circadian Rhythm Tool

### Why Light Matters

Light is the most powerful signal for your internal clock (circadian rhythm). Your brain uses light to know when it's day and when it's night:
- **Light -> signals daytime -> suppresses melatonin -> promotes alertness**
- **Darkness -> signals nighttime -> releases melatonin -> promotes sleep**

Modern life disrupts this:
- We spend daytime indoors (less light than our ancestors)
- We spend evenings with screens and bright lights (more light than our ancestors)

### Morning Light (Within 1 Hour of Waking)

**What it does:**
- Advances your circadian clock (earlier sleepiness at night)
- Suppresses morning melatonin
- Boosts serotonin and alertness
- Can shift your clock ~1 hour earlier per day

**Best Practices:**
- Get outside within 30-60 minutes of waking
- Even cloudy days provide beneficial light
- Natural outdoor light before 10 AM is ideal
- 15-30 minutes of exposure is helpful
- Light boxes (10,000 lux) work if outdoor light isn't possible

### Evening Light (2+ Hours Before Bed)

**What it does:**
- Delays your circadian clock (later sleepiness)
- Suppresses melatonin right when you need it
- Signals "daytime" to your brain

**What to Minimize:**
- Bright overhead lights
- Screen time (TV, phone, computer, tablet)
- Blue light especially

**Strategies:**
- Dim lights in your home 2 hours before bed
- Use warm-colored bulbs in evening spaces
- Enable "night mode" on devices (reduces blue light)
- Consider blue-light blocking glasses
- Avoid looking directly at screens close to bedtime

### Light Exposure by Goal

| Goal | Morning Action | Evening Action |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| **Fall asleep earlier** | Get bright light within 30-60 min of waking | Dim lights 2+ hours before bed |
| **Fall asleep later** | Avoid bright light first thing | More light exposure in evening |
| **Maintain rhythm** | Consistent light exposure timing | Consistent dimming routine |

## Maintaining Your Gains

### Weekly Check-In Questions

After you've completed the initial treatment period (typically 6-8 weeks), do a weekly review:

1. **Sleep efficiency staying above 85%?**
2. **Maintaining consistent bed and wake times?**
3. **Getting regular exercise?**
4. **Managing stress and worry before bedtime?**
5. **Sleep environment still optimized?**

### When to Revisit Techniques

Life happens. You may need to re-implement techniques if:
- Sleep problems return for 2+ weeks
- Major life stressors or schedule changes
- Starting new medications
- Seasonal changes affecting light exposure
- Travel or jet lag

**Don't panic** if sleep worsens temporarily. Stress, illness, and life events can disrupt sleep. The techniques you've learned are tools you can use whenever needed.

### Signs of Progress

How do you know the treatment is working?

**Early signs (weeks 2-4):**
- Falling asleep faster
- Fewer nighttime awakenings
- Less time lying awake frustrated
- Starting to feel sleepy at your designated bedtime

**Later signs (weeks 4-8):**
- Sleep efficiency above 85%
- Waking feeling more refreshed
- Less anxiety about sleep
- Sleep window expanding as efficiency improves

**Long-term success:**
- Sleep is no longer a major concern
- You have tools to manage setbacks
- Better daytime energy and functioning

## When to Seek Professional Help

Self-help works for many people, but professional help is appropriate if:

### Symptoms Persist
- Sleep problems continue for **more than 4 weeks** despite consistent self-help efforts
- You've implemented the techniques correctly but aren't seeing improvement

### Other Conditions May Be Involved
- **Sleep apnea symptoms** — Snoring, breathing pauses, gasping, excessive daytime sleepiness
- **Restless legs syndrome** — Crawling sensation in legs that worsens at rest
- **Mood disorders** — Depression, anxiety, or mood changes affecting function
- **Pain conditions** — Chronic pain preventing sleep or causing awakenings
- **Medications** — Prescription drugs affecting sleep

### Safety Concerns
- Excessive daytime sleepiness affecting driving or work
- Regular use of alcohol or OTC medications to sleep
- Severe mood changes or thoughts of self-harm

### Types of Professional Help

**Primary Care Physician:**
- Initial evaluation
- Referral to specialists
- Medication management if needed

**Sleep Medicine Specialist:**
- Comprehensive sleep evaluation
- Sleep studies (polysomnography)
- Diagnosis of sleep disorders

**Behavioral Sleep Medicine Specialist:**
- CBT-I therapy (what you've been learning)
- More intensive, personalized treatment
- Often psychologists with specialized training

**Psychiatrist:**
- When mental health conditions are involved
- Medication management for anxiety/depression
- Complex cases

## Medication Tapering Guidance

**If you're currently taking sleep medications and want to reduce or stop:**

### Critical Principles
- **NEVER stop sleep medications abruptly** without medical guidance
- Stopping suddenly can cause rebound insomnia (temporary worsening) or withdrawal
- **Taper gradually** under healthcare provider supervision
- **CBT-I combined with tapering** is more effective than tapering alone

### General Approach
- Typical taper: 25% dose reduction every 1-2 weeks
- Slower tapers for longer-term use or higher doses
- Expect some adjustment period at each step

### Medication-Specific Notes

| Medication Type | Needs Tapering? | Notes |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------|
| Benzodiazepines (temazepam, triazolam) | Yes, gradual | Risk of withdrawal and rebound |
| Z-drugs (zolpidem, eszopiclone) | Yes, recommended | Possible 1-2 nights rebound insomnia |
| DORAs (suvorexant, lemborexant) | Generally no | Can usually discontinue directly |
| Doxepin, ramelteon | Generally no | No withdrawal expected |
| OTC antihistamines | Can stop | May have 1-2 nights adjustment |

**Always work with your prescriber** — They can create a tapering plan right for you.

## Your Complete Sleep Toolkit

You now have a complete set of evidence-based tools:

### Core Behavioral Techniques
1. **Sleep diary** — Track and understand your sleep
2. **Stimulus control** — Bed = sleep only
3. **Sleep restriction** — Build sleep pressure, consolidate sleep
4. **Environment optimization** — Temperature, darkness, quiet

### Lifestyle Factors
5. **Substance management** — Caffeine, alcohol, food timing
6. **Cognitive techniques** — Worry time, cognitive shuffling, thought challenging
7. **Relaxation** — PMR, diaphragmatic breathing
8. **Exercise** — Regular physical activity (not too close to bedtime)
9. **Light exposure** — Morning light, evening dimness

### Remember
- **These techniques work as well as medication** — with no side effects
- **Consistency is key** — The techniques work best when applied consistently
- **Improvement takes time** — 4-8 weeks to see full benefits
- **Setbacks happen** — You have tools to manage them
- **Professional help is available** — When self-help isn't enough
