Your bedroom environment plays a significant role in sleep quality. While environment changes alone are less powerful than behavioral techniques like stimulus control and sleep restriction, they provide an important foundation. This lesson helps you audit and optimize your sleep space.

## The Science of Sleep Environment

Your body follows predictable patterns when preparing for sleep:
- **Temperature drops** — Core body temperature naturally decreases as you get sleepy
- **Melatonin rises** — This "sleep hormone" is triggered by darkness
- **Alertness systems quiet** — External disruptions can interrupt this process

A properly optimized bedroom **supports** these natural processes. A poorly optimized one **fights against** them.

## Temperature: The Most Underrated Factor

### The Ideal Temperature Range

Research consistently shows: **60-67 degrees F (15.5-19.4 degrees C)** is optimal for sleep.

This feels cooler than most people keep their bedrooms, but there's a good reason:
- Your core temperature needs to **drop 2-3 degrees F** to initiate sleep
- A cool room facilitates this natural cooling
- Being too warm prevents the temperature drop and delays sleep onset

### Signs Your Bedroom is Too Warm
- Kicking off blankets during the night
- Waking up sweating
- Tossing and turning trying to find a cool spot
- Restless leg movements

### Temperature Optimization Checklist

- [ ] Set thermostat to 60-67 degrees F (15.5-19.4 degrees C) at night
- [ ] Use breathable bedding materials (cotton, linen, bamboo)
- [ ] Have a fan available for air circulation
- [ ] Layer blankets so you can adjust during the night
- [ ] Consider cooling mattress pad if you sleep hot
- [ ] Keep feet warm if needed (socks can help signal sleep to your body)

### What About a Partner Who Likes It Warmer?

This is common. Solutions:
- Use separate blankets (each person controls their own temperature)
- Cooling mattress pad on your side only
- Fan pointed toward the warmer sleeper
- Compromise at the higher end (67 degrees F) with better airflow

## Darkness: Protecting Your Melatonin

### Why Darkness Matters

Light is the most powerful signal for your circadian rhythm. Even small amounts of light can:
- **Suppress melatonin production** — Delaying sleep onset
- **Signal "daytime" to your brain** — Triggering alertness
- **Fragment sleep** — Especially in early morning hours

### The Problem with Modern Lighting

Our ancestors slept in near-total darkness. Modern bedrooms often have:
- LED standby lights on devices
- Light from phone chargers
- Street lights through windows
- Bathroom night lights visible from bed
- Early morning sunlight in summer

### Darkness Optimization Checklist

- [ ] Install **blackout curtains** or room-darkening shades
- [ ] Cover or remove all LED lights from electronics
- [ ] Put phone face-down or in a drawer
- [ ] Use electrical tape over stubborn indicator lights
- [ ] Get an **eye mask** if you can't fully darken the room
- [ ] Use a **dim, red-toned nightlight** in the bathroom for nighttime trips

### Why Red Light?

If you need a nightlight for safety:
- **Red/orange light** has minimal impact on melatonin
- **Blue/white light** strongly suppresses melatonin
- Keep it as dim as safely possible
- Position it low (toward the floor)

### Light Exposure Timing (Preview of Lesson 8)

- **Morning light is good** — Get bright light within an hour of waking
- **Evening light is bad** — Dim lights 1-2 hours before bed
- **Night light exposure** — Minimize completely during sleep hours

## Sound: Minimizing Disruption

### The Sleep-Disrupting Power of Noise

Even noises that don't fully wake you can:
- Pull you out of deep sleep into lighter stages
- Trigger stress responses (elevated heart rate, cortisol release)
- Fragment sleep without your awareness
- Make it harder to fall back asleep after awakenings

### Common Noise Disruptions
- Traffic
- Neighbors
- Partner's snoring
- Pets
- Household sounds (HVAC, refrigerator)
- Early morning garbage trucks/construction

### Sound Optimization Checklist

- [ ] Address the most disruptive noise sources if possible
- [ ] Use a **white noise machine** or fan for consistent background sound
- [ ] Try a **white noise app** if you don't have a machine
- [ ] Consider **earplugs** for unavoidable noise (takes time to adjust)
- [ ] Put phone on **silent or Do Not Disturb** mode
- [ ] If partner snores loudly, they may need sleep apnea evaluation

### White Noise vs. Silence

White noise (or pink noise, brown noise, fan sounds) works by:
- **Masking sudden noises** — The garbage truck is less jarring against steady background sound
- **Providing consistency** — Your brain gets used to the constant sound
- **Filling silence** — Some people find total silence anxiety-provoking

**Personal preference**: Some people sleep best in silence. Try both.

## Comfort: Mattress, Pillows, and Bedding

### Does Your Mattress Need Replacing?

Signs your mattress may be contributing to poor sleep:
- Visible sagging or impressions
- You sleep better at hotels
- You wake with aches and pains that fade during the day
- It's more than 7-10 years old
- You and your partner roll toward the middle

**Reality check**: While a good mattress helps, don't fall for marketing that claims an expensive mattress will cure insomnia. Behavioral changes (stimulus control, sleep restriction) are far more impactful.

### Pillow Considerations

Your pillow should keep your head and neck in a neutral position:
- **Side sleepers** need thicker pillows to fill the shoulder gap
- **Back sleepers** need medium support
- **Stomach sleepers** need very thin or no pillow

If you wake with neck pain or headaches, your pillow may need adjustment.

### Comfort Checklist

- [ ] Mattress provides adequate support without sagging
- [ ] Pillow keeps head/neck in neutral position
- [ ] Bedding is comfortable and temperature-appropriate
- [ ] Bed linens are clean (change sheets weekly)
- [ ] Nothing scratchy, bunchy, or uncomfortable

## The Sleep Sanctuary Concept

Your bedroom should be a **dedicated space for sleep**, not a multi-purpose room. This ties directly to stimulus control (Lesson 3).

### Remove From the Bedroom
- TV (strongest recommendation)
- Work desk/computer
- Exercise equipment
- Piles of laundry/clutter
- Anything that reminds you of stress or tasks

### If Space Doesn't Allow Removal
- Cover the TV
- Put a physical barrier around the work area
- At minimum, make sure none of this is visible from bed

### Create a Restful Atmosphere
- Calm, neutral colors
- Minimal decoration
- Clutter-free surfaces
- Pleasant but not strong scents
- Nothing that triggers stress or alertness

### Quick Wins (Do This Week)

- Lower thermostat tonight
- Download a white noise app
- Remove obvious clutter

### Medium Projects (Next Few Weeks)

- Address partner's snoring (sleep study?)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Temperature 60-67 degrees F** — Cooler than most people think; it helps your body's natural temperature drop
2. **Make it DARK** — Cover LEDs, block windows, use eye mask if needed
3. **Minimize noise** — White noise can mask disruptions; address partner snoring
4. **Comfort matters** — But won't cure insomnia on its own
5. **Bedroom = sleep only** — Remove work, TV, and stress-triggers
