What you put in your body significantly affects how well you sleep. Caffeine, alcohol, and even food timing can make the difference between restful sleep and another frustrating night. This lesson examines the evidence and provides clear guidelines.

## Caffeine: The Hidden Sleep Thief

### How Caffeine Affects Sleep

Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors in your brain. Adenosine is a chemical that builds up while you're awake and creates "sleep pressure"—the feeling of sleepiness. By blocking these receptors, caffeine:
- **Delays sleep onset** — Takes longer to fall asleep
- **Reduces sleep quantity** — Average of ~10 minutes less per cup consumed
- **Decreases sleep efficiency** — More time awake during the night
- **Reduces deep sleep** — Less time in restorative sleep stages

### The Half-Life Problem

Caffeine has a **half-life of approximately 5-6 hours**. This means:
- 3 PM coffee -> Half the caffeine still in your system at 8-9 PM
- 3 PM coffee -> Quarter of it still active at 1-2 AM

Many people underestimate how long caffeine lasts because they've developed tolerance to its alerting effects—but it's still affecting their sleep.

### Caffeine Sources

| Source | Approximate Caffeine |
|--------|---------------------|

**Hidden sources**: Some pain medications, cold medicines, and weight loss supplements contain caffeine.

### Caffeine Guidelines for Better Sleep

**The Research Says:**
- Caffeine within 6 hours of bedtime significantly impairs sleep
- Even caffeine consumed early in the day may affect sensitive individuals

**Recommendations:**
- **Stop caffeine by 2 PM** (or earlier if you're sensitive)
- **Limit to 1-2 cups** in the morning
- **Switch to decaf** after your cutoff time (decaf still has 2-15 mg)
- **Track your intake** — You may be consuming more than you realize

### Caffeine Withdrawal

If you currently consume significant caffeine and plan to reduce:
- **Taper gradually** to avoid withdrawal headaches
- Reduce by 1/4 cup every few days
- Expect 3-7 days of adjustment
- Withdrawal symptoms (headache, fatigue) are temporary

## Alcohol: The Deceptive Sleep Aid

### The False Promise

Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant that can help you:
- Feel relaxed
- Feel drowsy
- Fall asleep faster

This makes many people think alcohol helps sleep. **It doesn't.** Here's what actually happens:

### What Alcohol Does to Sleep

**First Half of Night:**
- You may fall asleep faster
- More time in deep sleep (sedation effect)

**Second Half of Night (as alcohol metabolizes):**
- Sleep becomes fragmented
- More awakenings
- Less REM sleep
- Early morning awakening
- Sweating, racing heart possible

**Net Result:**
- 3-4% reduction in sleep quality per drink
- Worse sleep overall, even if you fell asleep fast
- Feeling unrested despite hours in bed

### The Alcohol-Insomnia Cycle

Alcohol is particularly dangerous for people with insomnia because:
1. You have trouble sleeping
2. You drink to "help" sleep
3. Sleep actually worsens
4. You think you need more alcohol to sleep
5. Dependency develops

**This is a self-medication trap** that leads to worse insomnia AND alcohol problems.

### Alcohol Guidelines for Better Sleep

**Best Approach:**
- Avoid alcohol within **3-4 hours of bedtime**
- Limit overall consumption
- Never use alcohol as a sleep aid

**If You Choose to Drink:**
- Earlier in the evening is better
- Allow time for metabolism before bed
- Stay hydrated (alcohol dehydrates)
- Recognize that sleep quality will be compromised

### A Note on Alcohol Dependency

If you've been using alcohol regularly to sleep and find it difficult to sleep without it:
- This may indicate physical dependency
- Stopping suddenly can cause rebound insomnia
- Seek medical guidance for safe tapering
- CBT-I works well alongside alcohol reduction

## Food and Eating Timing

### How Food Timing Affects Sleep

Your digestive system doesn't stop at night. Eating close to bedtime can:
- **Increase WASO** (wake after sleep onset)
- **Cause discomfort** — Lying flat with a full stomach
- **Trigger reflux** — Especially with large or fatty meals
- **Delay sleep onset** — Body temperature rises during digestion

### What the Research Shows

| Eating Timing | Effect |
|--------------|--------|
| **Less than 1 hour before bed** | Significantly increases wake time during night |
| **Less than 3 hours before bed** | 40% increased risk of nocturnal awakening |
| **2-4 hours before bed** | Optimal timing for sleep |
| **High-fat, high-carb meals late** | Extends time to fall asleep |

### Food Timing Guidelines

**Recommendations:**
- Finish dinner **2-4 hours before bed**
- Avoid **heavy, fatty, or spicy meals** in the evening
- If hungry close to bed, have a **small, light snack**
- Limit **fluids** in the evening to reduce nighttime bathroom trips

**Good Late Snacks (if needed):**
- Small bowl of cereal with milk
- Banana
- Few crackers with cheese
- Small yogurt

**Avoid Before Bed:**
- Large meals
- Spicy foods
- High-fat foods
- Excessive fluids
- Sugary foods

## Other Substances

### Nicotine

Nicotine is a stimulant that can:
- Make it harder to fall asleep
- Cause fragmented sleep
- Trigger nighttime awakenings (withdrawal between cigarettes)

**If you smoke:**
- Avoid nicotine close to bedtime
- Don't smoke during nighttime awakenings
- Consider this another reason to quit

### Cannabis/Marijuana

Cannabis effects on sleep are complex:
- **THC** may help with falling asleep initially but reduces REM sleep
- **CBD** effects are less clear; some report improvement
- Regular use can disrupt natural sleep architecture
- Withdrawal after stopping can cause significant sleep disruption

**Recommendation:** Not recommended as a sleep aid; if using, discuss with your healthcare provider.

### Sleep Medications

If you're currently taking sleep medications (prescription or over-the-counter):

**Important principles:**
- **Don't stop suddenly** — Can cause rebound insomnia
- **Work with your prescriber** — Tapering should be supervised
- **CBT-I works WITH tapering** — Often more effective than medication alone
- **OTC medications** (diphenhydramine, doxylamine) have side effects and lose effectiveness

We'll discuss medication tapering in more detail in Lesson 8.

## Putting It All Together: Your Substance Audit

### Current Consumption Assessment

Track for one week:

| Alcohol | | | |
| Evening meals | | | Size, type |
| Late snacks | | | |
| Nicotine | | | |

### Making Changes

**Priority Order:**
1. **Caffeine cutoff at 2 PM** — Often the biggest single improvement
2. **Alcohol-free nights** — See how your sleep responds
3. **Dinner 2-4 hours before bed** — Easy to implement
4. **Nicotine reduction** — Especially close to bedtime
5. **Medication review** — With healthcare provider

### One Change at a Time

Don't try to change everything at once:
- Pick the substance that's most likely affecting your sleep
- Make one change for 1-2 weeks
- Track the results in your sleep diary
- Then address the next priority

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Audit your current intake">For one week, track every caffeine source (including hidden ones like medications and chocolate), every alcoholic drink, and every meal or snack within 4 hours of bedtime. Write down the substance, amount, and exact time consumed.</Step>
<Step title="Compare with your sleep diary">Lay your substance log next to your sleep diary. Look for correlations: Do your worst nights follow late caffeine? Does alcohol coincide with 3 AM awakenings? Do late heavy meals match higher wake-after-sleep-onset times?</Step>
<Step title="Identify your biggest offender">Based on your comparison, pick the ONE substance habit most likely to be disrupting your sleep. For most people, this is either late-day caffeine or evening alcohol.</Step>
<Step title="Make one targeted change">Implement your single change for two full weeks. For example: move your caffeine cutoff to 2 PM, or eliminate alcohol on weeknights. Don't change anything else — you want to see the isolated effect.</Step>
<Step title="Evaluate and iterate">After two weeks, review your sleep diary again. If you see improvement, maintain the change and consider addressing the next substance on your list. If no change, the substance may not be your primary issue — move on to the next suspect.</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Key Takeaways
1. **Caffeine** has a 5-6 hour half-life — Stop by 2 PM
2. **Alcohol** helps you fall asleep but destroys sleep quality — Avoid within 3-4 hours of bed
3. **Late meals** increase nighttime awakenings — Finish dinner 2-4 hours before bed
4. **Self-medication** with alcohol or other substances creates a dangerous cycle
5. **Track your intake** — You may not realize how much you're consuming or how late
