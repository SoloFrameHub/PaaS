## Learn: The CBT Triangle

Cognitive restructuring is a core technique from Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). It doesn't ask you to "think happy thoughts." Instead, it teaches you to become a **detective investigating your own mind** — catching distorted thoughts, checking the evidence, and shifting to a more balanced perspective.

Everything starts with the CBT Triangle:

<CBTTriangleDiagram />

Here's the key insight: your thoughts directly shape your feelings, your feelings drive your behaviors, and your behaviors circle back to reinforce future thoughts. This means that **changing an unhelpful thought pattern at any point in the cycle can reduce anxiety and shift how you respond.**

<Callout type="example" title="The Triangle in Action">
Imagine you send a message and your friend doesn't reply for hours. The *thought* "They're ignoring me" triggers the *feeling* of rejection and anxiety, which leads to the *behavior* of checking your phone obsessively or sending multiple follow-ups — which then fuels more anxious thoughts. Change the thought to "They're probably busy," and the whole cycle shifts.
</Callout>

## Learn: Thinking Traps

When anxiety is running the show, your brain takes mental shortcuts that distort reality. Psychologists call these **cognitive distortions** — and once you learn to spot them, they lose much of their power. Swipe through the eight most common thinking traps below.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="cognitive-distortions">
<Slide title="1. Catastrophizing">
**The Pattern:** Jumping straight to the worst possible outcome, as if disaster is a certainty.
**Example:** "If I make a mistake at work, I'll definitely get fired, and then I'll never find another job."
**Challenge Question:** *What's the most likely outcome — not the worst one?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="2. All-or-Nothing Thinking">
**The Pattern:** Seeing things in black-and-white extremes with no middle ground.
**Example:** "If I'm not perfect at this presentation, I'm a total failure."
**Challenge Question:** *What would the gray zone look like here?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="3. Mind Reading">
**The Pattern:** Assuming you know what other people are thinking — usually something negative about you.
**Example:** "Everyone at this party thinks I'm awkward and doesn't want me here."
**Challenge Question:** *Do I have actual evidence for what they're thinking, or am I guessing?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="4. Fortune Telling">
**The Pattern:** Predicting negative outcomes as though you have a crystal ball.
**Example:** "I know the interview will go terribly. There's no point in preparing."
**Challenge Question:** *Can I actually predict the future? What evidence do I have for this prediction?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="5. Emotional Reasoning">
**The Pattern:** Treating feelings as proof that something is true — "I feel it, so it must be real."
**Example:** "I feel anxious about flying, so the plane must be unsafe."
**Challenge Question:** *Are feelings always accurate reflections of reality? Would I trust this feeling as evidence in court?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="6. Discounting the Positive">
**The Pattern:** Brushing off positive experiences, compliments, or evidence as though they don't count.
**Example:** "They only said my work was good to be nice. It wasn't actually that impressive."
**Challenge Question:** *What if I took the positive feedback at face value? Would I dismiss it if a friend received it?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="7. Overgeneralization">
**The Pattern:** Drawing sweeping conclusions from a single event, using words like "always" or "never."
**Example:** "I failed this exam, so I always fail. I'm just not smart enough."
**Challenge Question:** *Is this really true every single time? What are the exceptions?*
</Slide>
<Slide title="8. Should Statements">
**The Pattern:** Holding rigid, unforgiving rules about how you or the world *should* operate.
**Example:** "I should never feel anxious. I should be over this by now."
**Challenge Question:** *Says who? How would it feel to replace "should" with "would like to"?*
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

<Callout type="tip" title="You Don't Need to Memorize All Eight">
Most people have two or three "go-to" thinking traps that show up again and again. As you read through the list, notice which ones feel the most familiar — those are the ones to watch for first.
</Callout>

## Learn: The THINK Process

When you catch an anxious thought, you need a practical method for working through it. The THINK process gives you five clear steps.

<StepByStep title="The THINK Process">
<Step title="T — Trap It">
Notice the thought and name it out loud, even if just in your head. Try saying, "I'm having the thought that..." or "My mind is telling me that..." This small act of labeling creates a crucial bit of distance between you and the thought.
</Step>
<Step title="H — Happen Often?">
Ask yourself: is this a familiar pattern? You might recognize, "Oh, this sounds like my catastrophizing habit again," or "This is my mind-reading pattern showing up." Recognizing the pattern helps you see the thought as a *thinking trap* rather than a fact.
</Step>
<Step title="I — Investigate">
Examine the evidence like a detective. What actually supports this thought? What contradicts it? What would a neutral friend or observer say if they looked at the same situation? Be honest — evidence means facts, not feelings.
</Step>
<Step title="N — New Thought">
Craft a more balanced perspective. This isn't about forcing positivity — it's about realistic thinking. Acknowledge uncertainty without catastrophizing. Allow for multiple possibilities. A good balanced thought often starts with "It's possible that..." or "The evidence suggests..."
</Step>
<Step title="K — Kindness">
Ask yourself: what would I say to a good friend in this exact situation? Most of us are dramatically harsher with ourselves than we'd ever be with someone we care about. Apply that same warmth and compassion inward.
</Step>
</StepByStep>

<Callout type="reflection" title="Quick Gut Check">
Think of the anxious thought you named at the start of this lesson. Can you identify which step of the THINK process would be hardest for you? For many people, it's the Kindness step — being as gentle with yourself as you'd be with a friend.
</Callout>

## Apply: Challenge the Thought

Let's put the THINK process into practice. Read the scenario and choose the strategy you'd use to challenge the thought.

<InteractiveScenario title="Challenge the Thought" description="You have a job interview tomorrow. The thought hits: 'I'm going to bomb this interview. I'll freeze up, forget everything, and they'll think I'm incompetent.' This is catastrophizing. How would you challenge it?">
<Choice label="Check the evidence: 'What's my actual track record with interviews?'" feedback="Excellent strategy. Investigating the evidence is the core of cognitive restructuring. You might realize you've done well in past interviews, or at least survived them — which directly contradicts the catastrophic prediction. Evidence trumps feelings." type="healthy" />
<Choice label="Use the friend test: 'What would I tell a friend who said this to me?'" feedback="This is the Kindness step in action. You'd probably tell a friend, 'You've prepared well. One interview doesn't define your career. You'll do better than you think.' Now give yourself that same encouragement." type="healthy" />
<Choice label="Estimate the probability: 'How likely is it that the absolute worst case happens?'" feedback="Smart approach. When you assign an actual probability, catastrophic outcomes often shrink from 'certain' to 'unlikely.' Freezing up completely, forgetting everything, and being judged as incompetent — all at once? Probably less than 5% likely." type="healthy" />
<Choice label="Reframe to balanced: 'I might be nervous, but I've prepared and I know my stuff.'" feedback="This is a strong balanced reframe. It acknowledges the anxiety without letting it run the show. Notice it doesn't say 'I'll be perfect' — it says 'I've prepared and I know my stuff,' which is realistic and grounded in evidence." type="healthy" />
</InteractiveScenario>

<Callout type="tip" title="All Four Strategies Work">
You may have noticed that every option above was a "healthy" choice. That's intentional — there are many valid ways to challenge a distorted thought. The best strategy is whichever one feels most natural to you in the moment. Over time, you'll develop a go-to approach.
</Callout>

Over time, try keeping a short list of your most frequent anxious thoughts alongside balanced reframes you've developed. When anxiety spikes, having these ready-made alternatives is far easier than trying to think clearly in the moment.
</Callout>

## Learn: Important Distinctions

There are two things people frequently misunderstand about cognitive restructuring. Let's clear them up.

<FlipCard front="What cognitive restructuring IS: Examining your thoughts like a scientist — weighing evidence, considering multiple perspectives, and arriving at a more realistic interpretation." back="What cognitive restructuring is NOT: Positive thinking, toxic optimism, or pretending problems don't exist. You're not replacing 'bad' thoughts with 'good' ones — you're replacing distorted thoughts with accurate ones." frontIcon="🔍" />

<Callout type="warning" title="Thoughts Are Not Commands">
Having a thought does not mean you have to believe it, act on it, or let it define your character. Thoughts are mental events — they arise, they pass, and you can observe them without following them. An anxious thought about danger doesn't mean you're in danger. A self-critical thought doesn't mean the criticism is true. You are not your thoughts.
</Callout>

## Reflect: What You Learned

Let's pull together the key ideas from this lesson.

<StepByStep title="Key Takeaways">
<Step title="Thoughts are not facts">
They're interpretations — mental habits that can be questioned, examined, and reshaped. Just because a thought feels true doesn't mean it is.
</Step>
<Step title="Cognitive distortions are universal">
Everyone falls into thinking traps. Catastrophizing, mind reading, all-or-nothing thinking — these are normal brain shortcuts, not character flaws.
</Step>
<Step title="The THINK process is your toolkit">
Trap it, ask if it Happens often, Investigate the evidence, form a New balanced thought, and offer yourself Kindness.
</Step>
<Step title="Balanced thinking is realistic, not positive">
The goal isn't to feel great about everything — it's to see situations accurately, without the distortion anxiety adds.
</Step>
<Step title="Practice builds the skill">
Like any ability, cognitive restructuring gets easier and more automatic with repetition. Start with small worries and work your way up.
</Step>
</StepByStep>
