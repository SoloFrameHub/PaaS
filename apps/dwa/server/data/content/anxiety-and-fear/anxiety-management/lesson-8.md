## When to Seek Professional Help

Self-help is valuable, and the skills you've learned are real. But some situations call for professional support. **Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness** — it means you're taking your well-being seriously enough to bring in reinforcements.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="red-flags">
<Slide title="Duration and Intensity">
Pay attention if you experience:
- Persistent worry lasting **6 months or more** that feels out of proportion
- Anxiety that feels **overwhelming or unmanageable** despite consistent self-help efforts
- Symptoms that are **not improving** or are getting worse over time
- Anxiety that has become your **default state** rather than a response to specific situations
These patterns suggest your nervous system may need more support than self-help alone can provide — and that's not a failure. It's information.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Functional Impairment">
Seek help if anxiety is:
- Causing you to **avoid** situations that affect your quality of life
- Making it difficult to complete **daily tasks** or responsibilities
- Leading to **missing work or school** regularly
- Causing you to **withdraw** from relationships or social life
- Preventing you from **enjoying** things that used to bring pleasure
When anxiety starts shrinking your world — when the things you avoid outnumber the things you engage with — professional support can help you reclaim that territory.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Physical Symptoms">
Professional evaluation is important if you experience:
- **Frequent panic attacks** (racing heart, chest pain, feeling of losing control)
- **Chronic physical symptoms** like persistent headaches, stomach problems, or fatigue
- **Sleep disruption** that significantly affects your daily functioning
- Physical symptoms your doctor can't explain medically
These physical manifestations mean your body is carrying a significant stress load. A professional can help determine the right combination of support.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Warning Signs That Need Immediate Attention">
Reach out for help right away if you notice:
- Using **alcohol or drugs** to cope with anxiety
- Thoughts of **self-harm or suicide** — please reach out immediately
- Feeling **hopeless** or unable to see a way forward
- **Anxiety about having anxiety** — a spiraling pattern that feeds on itself
These are not signs of weakness. They are signs that you deserve — and can benefit from — professional care.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

<Callout type="warning" title="Crisis Resources — Save These">

## Professional Help: What's Available

If you decide to seek professional support, it helps to know what's out there. Here are the major evidence-based approaches for anxiety.

<EnhancedAccordion title="CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)">
**Best for:** Most anxiety disorders, including generalized anxiety, social anxiety, and panic disorder.
CBT is the most researched therapy for anxiety. It focuses on identifying and changing the thought patterns and behaviors that maintain anxiety — many of the same principles you've practiced in this course (cognitive restructuring, exposure, behavioral activation). A typical course runs 12–20 sessions. CBT gives you skills you keep using long after therapy ends.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Exposure Therapy">
**Best for:** Specific phobias, panic disorder, OCD, social anxiety, PTSD.
Exposure therapy takes the gradual exposure principles from Lesson 5 and implements them with professional guidance. A therapist helps you build a fear hierarchy and systematically face feared situations in a controlled, supportive environment. For specific phobias, exposure therapy has some of the highest success rates of any psychological treatment — often effective in just a few sessions.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)">
**Best for:** Generalized anxiety, chronic worry, anxiety with avoidance patterns.
ACT focuses on accepting difficult thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them (the mindfulness skills from Lesson 7 are ACT-aligned), and then committing to actions that align with your values. Rather than trying to reduce anxiety directly, ACT changes your relationship to it — so anxiety is present but no longer in the driver's seat of your life.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)">
**Best for:** Trauma-related anxiety, PTSD, anxiety rooted in specific distressing memories.
EMDR helps your brain reprocess traumatic or distressing memories that may be fueling present-day anxiety. During sessions, you recall distressing events while engaging in bilateral stimulation (typically guided eye movements). Research shows EMDR can significantly reduce the emotional charge of traumatic memories, often more quickly than traditional talk therapy.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<Callout type="info" title="A Note on Medication">
Medication can be a helpful part of anxiety treatment, especially when anxiety is severe, when therapy alone isn't sufficient, or when you need support while building coping skills. **SSRIs** (like sertraline and escitalopram) and **SNRIs** (like venlafaxine) are first-line treatments. **Buspirone** is a non-addictive option for generalized anxiety. **Benzodiazepines** are sometimes used short-term but carry dependence risk. Medication decisions should always be made with a healthcare provider who knows your full history.
</Callout>

## Supporting Others

Now that you have this knowledge, you may notice anxiety in people you care about. How you respond matters.

<InteractiveScenario title="A Friend in Need" description="A close friend has been confiding in you about feeling constantly anxious. Today they say: 'I can't stop worrying about everything. I feel like I'm broken. I don't know what's wrong with me.' What do you say or do?">
<Choice label="'You just need to relax — everyone gets stressed. Don't overthink it.'" feedback="This comes from a caring place, but minimizing someone's experience often makes them feel unheard and ashamed. Telling someone with anxiety to 'just relax' is like telling someone with a broken leg to 'just walk.' It implies the problem is simple and they should be able to fix it — which increases their sense of being 'broken.' Your friend likely already knows they're overthinking; what they need is understanding, not a reminder." type="common" />
<Choice label="'Let's go get drinks — that'll take your mind off it.'" feedback="Offering distraction shows you care, but alcohol is one of the worst coping mechanisms for anxiety. As you learned in Lesson 6, alcohol creates rebound anxiety and reinforces the pattern of avoiding difficult feelings rather than addressing them. This suggestion, while well-intentioned, could contribute to an unhealthy coping cycle for someone who's already struggling." type="avoidant" />
<Choice label="'You're not broken — anxiety is something a lot of people deal with, and there are real tools that can help. Have you thought about talking to someone?'" feedback="This is a powerful response. You're normalizing their experience without minimizing it, gently challenging the 'broken' narrative, and pointing toward professional support. By saying 'a lot of people deal with this,' you reduce shame. By mentioning 'real tools,' you offer hope. By suggesting professional help, you're opening a door without pushing them through it." type="healthy" />
<Choice label="'I hear you. Would it help if I went with you to a first therapy appointment? I could wait in the lobby.'" feedback="This is an exceptionally supportive response. For many people, the biggest barrier to getting help isn't willingness — it's the anxiety of making the first move. Offering to physically accompany someone removes a major obstacle. You're not trying to be their therapist; you're being their support person. This kind of concrete, practical help can be the difference between someone getting care and continuing to suffer alone." type="healthy" />

## Your Maintenance Plan

Anxiety management isn't a one-time fix — it's an ongoing practice, like physical fitness. Here's a sustainable structure for keeping your skills sharp.

<StepByStep title="Maintaining Your Progress">
<Step title="Daily Practices (5–15 minutes)">
**Morning:** Brief mindfulness or mindful breathing (5 minutes). Set an intention for the day.
**Throughout the day:** Check in with your body. Use the STOP technique during transitions. Practice grounding as needed.
**Evening:** Gratitude reflection or worry dump journal. Wind-down routine before bed.
The daily layer is about keeping your baseline low and catching stress before it builds.
</Step>
<Step title="Weekly Practices">
- Track your mood and anxiety levels on a 0–10 scale
- Review exposure goals and plan your next step
- Complete 150 minutes of exercise across the week
- Maintain at least one meaningful social connection
- Review what techniques you used and what worked best
The weekly layer is about pattern recognition — noticing what's shifting and adjusting your approach.
</Step>
<Step title="Monthly Check-Ins">
- Review overall progress: How have things changed since last month?
- Identify patterns: What triggers are most active? What's helping most?
- Adjust strategies: What needs more attention or a different approach?
- Celebrate wins: Acknowledge progress, no matter how small
The monthly layer is about the bigger picture — making sure you're moving in the direction you want.
</Step>
<Step title="The 2-Minute Rule">
On days when motivation is low — and those days will come — commit to just **2 minutes** of any practice. Two minutes of breathing. Two minutes of journaling. Two minutes of stretching. Starting is the hardest part. You can stop after 2 minutes, or you might find yourself continuing. Either way, you've maintained the habit.
</Step>
</StepByStep>
