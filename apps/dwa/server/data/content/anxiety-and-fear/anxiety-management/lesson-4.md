## Box Breathing (Square Breathing)

Box breathing is used by Navy SEALs, first responders, and athletes because it works quickly under pressure. The pattern is simple: **inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4**\u2014like tracing the four sides of a square.

When you\u2019re anxious, your breathing becomes shallow and rapid, which signals danger to your brain and creates *more* anxiety. Box breathing breaks that loop by activating your parasympathetic nervous system\u2014the \u201Crest-and-digest\u201D mode that slows your heart rate and lowers blood pressure.

If 4 seconds feels too long, start with 3\u20133\u20133\u20133 and work your way up. You can do this sitting, standing, or even walking\u2014nobody will know.

## Belly Breathing (Diaphragmatic Breathing)

Most anxious breathing is shallow **chest breathing**\u2014your shoulders rise, your breaths are short and quick, and your stress response stays switched on. Diaphragmatic breathing flips the script: you breathe deep into your belly, expanding your diaphragm, which sends a powerful calming signal through your vagus nerve.

The key distinction is simple: if your chest moves, you\u2019re stress-breathing. If your belly moves, you\u2019re calm-breathing.

<StepByStep title="Diaphragmatic Breathing in 5 Steps">
  <Step title="Hand Placement">
    Place one hand on your chest and one on your stomach. These are your feedback sensors\u2014they\u2019ll tell you whether you\u2019re doing it right.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Inhale Into Your Belly">
    Breathe in slowly through your nose, directing the air downward so your **stomach pushes out**. Your lower hand should rise while your upper hand stays relatively still.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Slow Exhale">
    Exhale gently through your mouth. Feel your stomach fall back in. Let the exhale be longer than the inhale\u2014this deepens the calming effect.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Repeat">
    Continue for 5\u201310 breaths. With each cycle, try to make the breath slower and deeper. There\u2019s no rush.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check Your Work">
    Still not sure if you\u2019re doing it right? Lie down and place a book on your stomach. If the book rises when you inhale, you\u2019ve got it. If your chest rises instead, keep practicing\u2014it comes with time.
  </Step>
</StepByStep>

## 5\u20134\u20133\u20132\u20131 Grounding

Anxiety pulls you out of the present moment\u2014into future \u201Cwhat-ifs\u201D or past regrets. The 5\u20134\u20133\u20132\u20131 technique interrupts that spiral by anchoring you firmly in the *here and now* through your five senses. Your brain simply cannot focus on sensory details and spiral into worry at the same time.

Try it now. The guided exercise below will walk you through each sense, one at a time.

<GuidedGrounding />

<Callout type="info" title="Why Sensory Grounding Works">
When you deliberately name what you see, touch, hear, smell, and taste, you activate the sensory-processing regions of your brain. This pulls neural resources *away* from the amygdala (your fear center) and *toward* your prefrontal cortex, which handles calm, rational thinking. It\u2019s not just distraction\u2014it\u2019s a neurological redirect.
</Callout>

## Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR) works on a simple principle: **tense a muscle group hard for 5 seconds, then release it completely.** The sudden release creates a wave of relaxation that\u2019s deeper than what you\u2019d get from trying to \u201Cjust relax.\u201D Over time, PMR also teaches you to recognize the early signs of tension before anxiety takes hold.

Work through these four areas in order. Tense each muscle group for 5 seconds, release suddenly, then rest for 10\u201315 seconds and notice the difference before moving on.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="pmr-muscle-groups">
  <Slide title="Lower Body">
    **Feet:** Curl your toes tightly\u2014squeeze them as hard as you can for 5 seconds. Release. Feel the warmth spread.
    **Lower legs:** Point your toes up toward your face, tensing your shins. Hold for 5 seconds. Release.
    **Upper legs:** Squeeze your thighs together firmly. Hold for 5 seconds. Release and feel the heaviness.
    **Buttocks:** Clench tightly for 5 seconds. Release completely. Notice how different relaxation feels from tension.
  </Slide>
  <Slide title="Core">
    **Stomach:** Suck your stomach in as tightly as you can, as if bracing for impact. Hold for 5 seconds. Release and let your belly soften.
    **Chest:** Take a deep breath and hold it, feeling your chest expand and tighten. Hold for 5 seconds. Exhale and release all tension.
  </Slide>
  <Slide title="Upper Body">
    **Hands:** Make the tightest fists you can. Squeeze for 5 seconds. Open your hands and spread your fingers wide. Notice the tingling.
    **Forearms:** With fists still clenched, bend your wrists and pull your fists toward your shoulders. Hold for 5 seconds. Release.
    **Upper arms:** Flex your biceps like you\u2019re showing off your muscles. Hold the squeeze for 5 seconds. Let your arms drop and go limp.
    **Shoulders:** Shrug your shoulders up toward your ears as high as they\u2019ll go. Hold for 5 seconds. Drop them completely.
  </Slide>
  <Slide title="Head & Neck">
    **Neck:** Gently press your head back against the chair or pillow. Hold for 5 seconds (don\u2019t strain). Release and let your neck settle.
    **Face:** Scrunch everything toward the center\u2014squeeze your eyes shut, wrinkle your nose, press your lips together. Hold for 5 seconds. Release and let your whole face go slack.
    **Full practice** takes 15\u201320 minutes. A **quick version** (combining muscle groups) takes 5\u20137 minutes. A **mini version** (just hands, shoulders, and face) takes 2\u20133 minutes and works well in public.
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Quick Relief Tools

Sometimes you need something *fast*\u2014a single technique you can use in 30 seconds or less. Keep these four tools in your back pocket for moments when a full breathing or relaxation exercise isn\u2019t practical.

<InsightGrid>
  <InsightItem icon="\uD83D\uDCA8" title="Physiological Sigh">
    Discovered by Stanford researchers, this is one of the fastest ways to reduce stress in real time. Take a **double inhale** through your nose (one inhale, then a second quick one on top), followed by a **long, slow exhale** through your mouth. Repeat 1\u20133 times. It resets your respiratory rhythm and activates calming almost immediately.
  </InsightItem>
  <InsightItem icon="\u2744\uFE0F" title="Cold Water Reset">
    Cold activates your body\u2019s \u201Cdive reflex,\u201D which slows your heart rate and calms your nervous system. **Splash cold water on your face**, hold an ice cube in your hand, or run cold water over your wrists. The effect is surprisingly powerful and near-instant.
  </InsightItem>
  <InsightItem icon="\uD83D\uDED1" title="STOP Technique">
    A quick mindfulness circuit-breaker. **S**\u2014Stop what you\u2019re doing. **T**\u2014Take one deep breath. **O**\u2014Observe your thoughts, feelings, and sensations without judgment. **P**\u2014Proceed with awareness. The whole thing takes 15 seconds and pulls you out of autopilot reactivity.
  </InsightItem>
  <InsightItem icon="\uD83D\uDD22" title="Counting Techniques">
    Counting tasks that require concentration leave little room for anxious thoughts. Try counting backward from 100 by 7s, naming animals or countries alphabetically, or counting every object of one color in the room. The cognitive effort is the point\u2014it redirects your brain.
  </InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>
