## Redefining "Success"

It is crucial to redefine what success looks like in this process. Success is **NOT** feeling zero anxiety. If you wait until you are not scared to do something, you might wait forever.

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="🎯" title="Success Is TRYING">
Picking a rung on your ladder and attempting it, regardless of the outcome. Every time you choose to approach rather than avoid, you make a deposit in your courage account.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🧠" title="Success Is LEARNING">
Staying in the situation long enough for your brain to receive new data: "This was uncomfortable, but I survived." Even if anxiety stayed high, your brain collected evidence you can tolerate discomfort.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="💪" title="Success Is SHOWING UP">
Opening this lesson, reading these words, and continuing your journey. Many people never take the first step. You have taken many.
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

## Your Maintenance Plan

The skills you have learned are like muscles -- they get stronger with use and weaker without it. Here is a simple maintenance plan to keep your toolkit sharp.

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Daily (2-5 minutes)">
Practice your chosen resilience habit (mindfulness, gratitude, or self-compassion). Check in with your body — notice if your shoulders, jaw, or stomach are holding tension.
</Step>
<Step title="Weekly (10-15 minutes)">
Review your Fear Ladder — are there new rungs to add or old ones to practice? Practice one boundary script, even in a low-stakes situation. Celebrate one thing you did this week that required courage.
</Step>
<Step title="Monthly (20-30 minutes)">
Revisit your relapse prevention plan — update your warning signs, tools, and support contacts as needed. Re-read one lesson from this course as a refresher. Reflect on your progress — where were you when you started vs. where you are now?
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## When to Seek Additional Support

This course is a powerful self-help tool, but it is not a replacement for professional support. Please consider reaching out to a therapist or counselor if:

- Your anxiety is significantly impacting your daily life, work, or relationships.
- You are experiencing panic attacks that feel unmanageable.
- You are using substances to cope with anxiety.
- You are having thoughts of harming yourself.

<Callout type="tip" title="Seeking Help Is Strength">
Seeking professional support is not a sign of weakness — it is one of the bravest and most effective things you can do for yourself. A therapist can personalize the techniques you have learned here, help you work through deeper patterns, and provide the guided support that accelerates your progress. You have already proven your courage by completing this course.
</Callout>
