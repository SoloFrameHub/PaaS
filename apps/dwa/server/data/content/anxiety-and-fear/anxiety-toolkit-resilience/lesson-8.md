## Your Maintenance Plan
The skills you have learned are like muscles -- they get stronger with use and weaker without it. Here is a simple maintenance plan to keep your toolkit sharp.
### Daily (2-5 minutes)
- Practice your chosen resilience habit (mindfulness, gratitude, or self-compassion).
- Check in with your body -- notice if your shoulders, jaw, or stomach are holding tension.
### Weekly (10-15 minutes)
- Review your Fear Ladder -- are there new rungs to add or old ones to practice?
- Practice one boundary script, even in a low-stakes situation.
- Celebrate one thing you did this week that required courage.
### Monthly (20-30 minutes)
- Revisit your relapse prevention plan -- update your warning signs, tools, and support contacts as needed.
- Re-read one lesson from this course as a refresher.
- Reflect on your progress -- where were you when you started vs. where you are now?
<StepByStep>
<Step title="Set Your Daily Anchor">Choose one resilience habit (mindfulness, gratitude, or self-compassion) and stack it onto an existing routine. This is your non-negotiable daily practice — even 10 seconds counts.</Step>
<Step title="Schedule Your Weekly Review">Set a recurring reminder for 10-15 minutes each week. Review your Fear Ladder, practice a boundary script, and acknowledge one courageous thing you did.</Step>
<Step title="Book Your Monthly Check-In">Once a month, sit down for 20-30 minutes to update your relapse prevention plan, re-read a lesson, and reflect on your growth. Put it in your calendar now.</Step>
<Step title="Track Your Progress">Use a simple journal, a habit tracking app, or even checkmarks on a calendar. Seeing your consistency builds motivation and reinforces your identity as someone who manages anxiety well.</Step>
</StepByStep>

## When to Seek Additional Support
This course is a powerful self-help tool, but it is not a replacement for professional support. Please consider reaching out to a therapist or counselor if:
- Your anxiety is significantly impacting your daily life, work, or relationships.
- You are experiencing panic attacks that feel unmanageable.
- You are using substances to cope with anxiety.
- You are having thoughts of harming yourself.
Seeking help is not a sign of weakness. It is one of the bravest and most effective things you can do for yourself.
<Callout type="warning" title="When to Reach Out for Professional Help">If your anxiety is significantly interfering with your daily life, relationships, or work for more than two weeks despite using your tools, it may be time to seek professional support. A therapist can offer personalized strategies, address underlying patterns, and provide the kind of support that goes beyond what a self-help course can offer. You deserve that level of care.</Callout>
