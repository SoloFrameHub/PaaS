Welcome to the Skills section of the Anxiety Toolkit. In the previous sections, you learned to understand anxiety and recognize how it shows up in your body and mind. Now, it is time to build your first real toolkit of skills for those moments when emotions feel overwhelming.

This section focuses on **distress tolerance** -- the ability to survive a crisis moment without making things worse.

## Why Distress Tolerance?

When emotions are at their peak, that is exactly when we are most likely to act impulsively. We might say something we regret, avoid something important, or try to numb the feeling in unhealthy ways.

Distress tolerance is not about "fixing" the emotion. It is about **getting through the moment safely** so you can deal with the situation when you are calmer and thinking more clearly.

> **A note from Phillip:** "Think of these skills as an emotional first-aid kit. You would not use a first-aid kit to cure an illness, but it keeps you safe in the moment. These skills do the same for your emotions."

## What You Will Learn

In this section, you will build a powerful toolkit with four core skills from Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT):

- **TIPP Skills** -- A crisis intervention technique that rapidly changes your body chemistry to bring you out of the "red zone."
- **5-4-3-2-1 Grounding** -- A sensory technique to pull your mind out of anxious spirals and into the present moment.
- **Riding the Wave** -- The skill of observing intense emotions without fighting them, trusting they will naturally recede.
- **Skills Challenge** -- Hands-on practice matching the right skill to the right situation to build your confidence.

## The TIPP Skill

When you are in an emotional crisis -- like a panic attack or feeling completely overwhelmed -- your body's "gas pedal" is floored. Before you can think clearly, you need to change your body's chemistry. The TIPP skill is a powerful, fast-acting way to do just that.

> **A note from Phillip:** "Think of this as pressing the emergency brake. It is not designed to solve the problem, but to get you out of the red zone so you can think clearly enough to use your other skills."

## T = Temperature
**What it does:** Drastically changes your body's chemistry by activating the "dive response," which is hard-wired in mammals to slow the heart rate when the face is submerged in cold water.
**How to do it safely:**
- Fill a bowl with cold water (and ice, if you can).
- Hold your breath and dip your face into the water for 15-30 seconds.
- Alternatively, hold a cold pack or a bag of frozen vegetables on your eyes and cheeks.
**Safety First:** Do not try this if you have a heart condition or any medical issue that could be affected by sudden temperature changes without consulting your doctor.
<Callout type="tip" title="Quick Alternative">If you do not have ice or cold water handy, try running cold water over your wrists for 30 seconds or holding a cold soda can against your cheeks. Even a cool, damp towel can help activate the dive response.</Callout>
## I = Intense Exercise
**What it does:** Burns off the intense energy created by adrenaline. It helps your body match its physical state to your emotional state, which helps expend the anxious energy more quickly.
**How to do it safely:**
- Engage in brief, vigorous exercise for a few minutes.
- Examples: Run in place, do jumping jacks, walk up and down stairs quickly, or do a fast-paced walk.
- The goal is to get your heart rate up even higher, just for a moment, to burn off the fuel.
## P = Paced Breathing
**What it does:** Directly activates your "brake pedal" (parasympathetic nervous system). Slowing down your exhale tells your brain that the danger has passed and it is safe to calm down.
<InteractiveBreathingExercise />
**How to do it:**
- Breathe in slowly through your nose for a count of 4.
- Breathe out even more slowly through your mouth for a count of 6 (or longer).
- Focus only on the counting and the sensation of your breath. Repeat for at least one minute.
## P = Paired Muscle Relaxation
**What it does:** Forces your muscles to relax by first tensing them. When you tense a muscle group and then release it, it becomes more relaxed than it was before.
**How to do it:**
- While breathing in, intensely tense a muscle group (like your hands, shoulders, or legs) for 5-10 seconds. Notice the tension.
- As you breathe out, release the tension completely and suddenly.
- Notice the difference between the feeling of tension and the feeling of relaxation. Move through different muscle groups in your body.
<StepByStep>
<Step title="Hands and Fists">Clench both fists tightly while breathing in for 5 seconds. Feel the tension in your fingers, palms, and forearms. As you breathe out, release completely. Let your hands fall open and feel the warmth and heaviness of relaxation.</Step>
<Step title="Shoulders and Neck">Raise your shoulders up toward your ears as high as they will go while breathing in for 5 seconds. Hold that tension. Then breathe out and let them drop completely. Notice the contrast between tension and release.</Step>
<Step title="Face and Jaw">Scrunch your entire face -- squeeze your eyes shut, clench your jaw, furrow your brow -- while breathing in for 5 seconds. As you breathe out, let every muscle in your face go slack. Let your mouth hang slightly open.</Step>
<Step title="Legs and Feet">Press your feet flat into the floor and tense your leg muscles while breathing in for 5 seconds. Feel the tension from your toes to your thighs. Breathe out and release completely, noticing how heavy and warm your legs feel.</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] Distress tolerance is about getting through a crisis moment safely, not fixing the emotion
- [ ] The TIPP skill rapidly changes your body chemistry during an emotional crisis
- [ ] Temperature activates the mammalian dive response to slow heart rate
- [ ] Paced Breathing with a longer exhale engages your parasympathetic nervous system
- [ ] Paired Muscle Relaxation uses tension-then-release to force muscle relaxation
## This Week's Practice
Practice each component of the TIPP skill at least once this week, even when you are not in crisis. Familiarity with these techniques when you are calm will make them easier to use when emotions are high. Try the following:
- **Temperature:** Hold a cold pack on your face for 30 seconds and notice how your body responds.
- **Intense Exercise:** Do 2 minutes of jumping jacks or running in place and notice the shift afterward.
- **Paced Breathing:** Practice 4-count inhale, 6-count exhale for 2 minutes before bed each night.
- **Paired Muscle Relaxation:** Tense and release 3 muscle groups (hands, shoulders, legs) before sleep.
