Now that you understand the avoidance trap, it is time to build the tool that will help you escape it. A Fear Ladder (also called an Exposure Hierarchy) is a tool you create to break down a scary situation into small, manageable steps. Instead of trying to jump from the ground to the top of a building, you build a ladder and climb it one rung at a time.

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="🪜" title="One Rung at a Time">A Fear Ladder breaks an overwhelming goal into small, manageable steps. You never have to take a giant leap -- just the next small step.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="📊" title="SUDS Ratings">Each step is rated 0-10 on the Subjective Units of Distress Scale. This gives you a clear, measurable way to track your progress.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🎯" title="Positive Goals">Frame your goal as something you want to move toward, not something you want to escape. "I want to enjoy social events" is more motivating than "I want to stop being scared."</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🔧" title="Flexible by Design">Your ladder is not set in stone. You can add steps, remove steps, or reorder them at any time as you learn more about your own patterns.</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

## Step 1: Choose Your Goal

First, pick one specific situation you have been avoiding due to anxiety. Frame it as a positive goal. For example, instead of "stop being afraid of public speaking," your goal might be **"I want to confidently share my ideas in a team meeting."**

A good goal is:

- **Specific** -- Clearly defined so you know when you have achieved it
- **Meaningful** -- Connected to something you genuinely want in your life
- **Positive** -- Framed as moving toward something, not away from something

## Step 2: Brainstorm All the Steps

Now, think of 10-15 mini-steps related to your goal. Do not worry about the order yet. Just list everything you can think of, from very easy things to the final goal itself. Mix in different variables like time, location, or who you are with.

The key is creating enough steps so that the gap between each rung feels manageable -- never a huge jump.

## Step 3: Rank and Rate Your Steps

Take your list of steps and arrange them in order from least scary to most scary. Assign each one a **SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress Scale)** rating from 0 (completely calm) to 10 (most anxious I have ever felt). Your final ladder should have steps all along that range.

<StepByStep>
<Step title="List Everything">Write down 10-15 mini-steps related to your goal. Do not worry about order yet. Include very easy steps, very hard steps, and everything in between. More steps means smoother transitions.</Step>
<Step title="Rate Each Step">Assign each step a SUDS rating from 0 to 10. Be honest about how anxious each step would make you feel right now. There are no wrong answers -- this is your personal rating.</Step>
<Step title="Arrange in Order">Sort your steps from lowest SUDS to highest. Check that there are no large gaps between rungs. If two steps jump from a 3 to a 6, think of something in between.</Step>
<Step title="Fill the Gaps">Add new steps to fill any gaps larger than 1-2 SUDS points. You can adjust variables like time, location, or who is with you to create intermediate steps.</Step>
<Step title="Review and Refine">Read through your completed ladder. Does the first step feel doable (a 2 or 3)? Does each step feel like a small stretch from the one before it? If so, your ladder is ready.</Step>
</StepByStep>

| Step | SUDS Rating | Description |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | 1/10 | Text a friend to ask about the party |
| 2 | 2/10 | Call a friend to talk about the party for 5 minutes |
| 3 | 3/10 | Go to a coffee shop alone and sit for 15 minutes |
| 4 | 4/10 | Say hello to a cashier and ask how their day is going |
| 5 | 5/10 | Drive to the party and sit in the car for 5 minutes |
| 6 | 6/10 | Walk inside the party and stand near the entrance for 5 minutes |
| 7 | 7/10 | Stand near the food table for 10 minutes |
| 8 | 8/10 | Walk inside and stand near the food table for 10 minutes |
| 9 | 9/10 | Make eye contact and smile at one person |
| 10 | 10/10 | Ask one person a question about their weekend |
## Example 2: Fear of Driving
**Goal: I want to drive on the highway for 15 minutes.**
| Step | SUDS Rating | Description |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | 1/10 | Sit in the driver's seat of the car in the driveway for 5 minutes |
| 2 | 2/10 | Start the car and sit with the engine running for 5 minutes |
| 3 | 3/10 | Drive to the end of the street and back |
| 4 | 4/10 | Drive around a quiet neighborhood block |
| 5 | 5/10 | Drive to a nearby store (5 minutes on local roads) |
| 6 | 6/10 | Drive on a busier road for 10 minutes |
| 7 | 7/10 | Drive on the highway entrance ramp and immediately get off at the first exit |
| 8 | 8/10 | Drive on the highway for one exit |
| 9 | 9/10 | Drive on the highway for 5 minutes during a non-busy time |
| 10 | 10/10 | Drive on the highway for 15 minutes during a non-busy time |
## The Golden Rule: Low and Slow
The key to success is to start low on the ladder (at a step that feels like a 2 or 3 out of 10) and go slow. **Do not move to the next rung until you have practiced the current one multiple times and your anxiety has noticeably decreased while doing it.**
This is not about willpower or forcing yourself. It is about systematic, gradual learning. Your brain needs repetition to form new associations.
<Callout type="warning" title="Do Not Skip Rungs">It can be tempting to jump ahead when a step feels "too easy." Resist this urge. Each rung, no matter how simple, is teaching your brain something new. Skipping steps often leads to a setback that can feel discouraging. Trust the process and let each rung do its work.</Callout>
<FlipCard front="How many times should I practice each rung before moving up?" back="There is no exact number, but a good rule of thumb is to repeat each step until your SUDS rating during the activity drops by at least 50 percent from your initial rating. For most people, this takes 3-5 repetitions. For example, if a step initially causes a SUDS of 6, practice it until it consistently feels like a 3 or lower before moving to the next rung." />

## Tips for Building an Effective Ladder
1. **Make the first step very easy.** If your first rung is already a 5/10, add easier steps below it.
2. **Keep the gaps between rungs small.** No step should be more than 1-2 SUDS points higher than the previous one.
3. **Be specific.** "Go to a party" is vague. "Stand near the food table at Sarah's party for 10 minutes" is specific and measurable.
4. **Include variables.** The same activity can be easier or harder depending on time of day, who is with you, or how long you do it.
5. **It is okay to adjust.** Your ladder is not set in stone. If a step turns out to be harder or easier than expected, move it or add new steps.

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] A Fear Ladder breaks a scary goal into small, manageable steps rated by anxiety level (SUDS)
- [ ] Frame your goal positively and make it specific and meaningful to you
- [ ] Start with a step rated 2-3 out of 10 and keep gaps between rungs small
- [ ] Do not move to the next rung until your anxiety has noticeably decreased at the current level
- [ ] Your ladder is flexible -- adjust steps as you learn what works for you
