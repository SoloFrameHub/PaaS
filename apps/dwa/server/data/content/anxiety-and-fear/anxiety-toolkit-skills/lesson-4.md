You now have three powerful skills in your toolkit: the TIPP Skill, the 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding technique, and Riding the Wave. The final step is building the confidence to know which one to use and when. This lesson will help you practice matching the right skill to the right situation.

## When to Use Each Skill

Before we begin the challenge, here is a quick reference guide for choosing the right tool:

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="🚨" title="TIPP Skill — Full-Blown Crisis">Use TIPP when you are in a physiological emergency: a panic attack, overwhelming rage, or a moment where you feel completely out of control. TIPP rapidly changes your body chemistry to bring you out of the "red zone." Best for panic attacks, intense emotional overwhelm, and moments needing immediate calm.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🧠" title="5-4-3-2-1 Grounding — Thought Spirals">Use Grounding when your mind is the problem: racing thoughts, anxious rumination, replaying past events, or imagining worst-case scenarios. Grounding redirects attention from internal thoughts to external sensory experiences. Best for anxious thought loops, difficulty sleeping, dissociation, and pre-event anxiety.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🌊" title="Riding the Wave — Strong but Manageable">Use Riding the Wave when you feel a strong emotion that is not a crisis: sadness, frustration, disappointment, or moderate anxiety. This skill works by observing the emotion with curiosity and allowing it to pass naturally. Best for sadness, frustration, moderate anxiety, grief, and intense but non-overwhelming anger.</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

<FlipCard front="What makes TIPP different from the other two skills?" back="TIPP is the only skill that directly changes your body chemistry (temperature, intense exercise, paced breathing, paired muscle relaxation). The other skills work primarily with your attention and awareness, while TIPP targets your physiology first." />

<FlipCard front="Can you use Grounding when you are in a full panic attack?" back="Grounding is less effective during a full panic attack because your body's fight-or-flight response is so strong that it is hard to focus on sensory details. Start with TIPP to calm the body first, then transition to Grounding once the physiological intensity drops." />

## Building Your Decision Framework

Over time, choosing the right skill becomes automatic. Here is a simple framework:

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Check Your Body">Is your heart racing? Are you shaking? Are you in a physiological crisis? If your body feels out of control, start with TIPP to change your body chemistry fast.</Step>
<Step title="Check Your Mind">Once your body is calmer (or if it was not in crisis to begin with), ask: is the problem racing thoughts or rumination? If your mind is spiraling, use 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding to anchor yourself in the present moment.</Step>
<Step title="Check Your Emotions">Is the emotion strong but manageable? If you can still think and reflect but the feeling is intense, Ride the Wave — observe it with curiosity and let it pass naturally.</Step>
<Step title="Layer as Needed">Remember, you can combine skills in sequence. Use TIPP to get out of the red zone, then Grounding to anchor yourself, then Ride the Wave as the emotion naturally fades. There is no wrong answer — any skill is better than no skill.</Step>
</StepByStep>
<Callout type="tip" title="The Quick Decision Rule">Ask yourself one question: "Is my body in crisis right now?" If yes, TIPP first. If no, ask "Is my mind spiraling?" If yes, Grounding. If it is just a strong emotion, Ride the Wave. This three-second check becomes second nature with practice.</Callout>
## Key Takeaways
- [ ] TIPP is best for physiological crises like panic attacks that require rapid body chemistry changes
- [ ] 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding is best for thought spirals, rumination, and racing minds
- [ ] Riding the Wave is best for strong but manageable emotions like sadness or frustration
- [ ] Skills can be combined in sequence: TIPP first, then Grounding, then Riding the Wave
- [ ] Choosing the right tool becomes automatic with practice -- any skill used is better than none
