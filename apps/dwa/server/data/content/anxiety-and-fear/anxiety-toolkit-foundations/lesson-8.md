## The Complete CBT Process: A Step-by-Step Review

When you notice anxiety rising, here is the complete process you can now follow. Step through each stage:

<SlideNavigation persistKey="cbt-process-review" showDots>
<Slide title="Step 1: Notice and Pause">
When you feel anxiety building, **pause**. Take a breath. Recognize that you are experiencing an anxious moment.
This is the Observer Stance you practiced in Lesson 3. The simple act of pausing breaks the automatic reaction cycle and gives you a moment of choice.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 2: Identify the Thought">
Ask yourself: **"What is my brain telling me right now?"**
Capture the automatic thought -- the first thing that pops into your mind. Be specific. Write it down if you can.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 3: Name the Trap">
Check your thought against the six thinking traps:
- **Catastrophizing** -- Am I jumping to the worst-case scenario?
- **All-or-Nothing Thinking** -- Am I seeing this in black and white?
- **Mind Reading** -- Am I assuming I know what someone else thinks?
- **Emotional Reasoning** -- Am I treating my feelings as facts?
- **"Should" Statements** -- Am I holding myself to a rigid rulebook?
- **Labeling** -- Am I defining myself by one event?
</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 4: Investigate with Three Questions">
Use your detective toolkit:
1. **What is the evidence?** -- Look at facts, not feelings
2. **Is there another way to look at this?** -- Generate alternatives
3. **What would I tell a friend?** -- Access your compassionate self
</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 5: Create a Balanced Thought">
Combine your findings into a new, realistic perspective.
Remember: **balanced, not blindly positive.** A good balanced thought acknowledges your feelings while incorporating the evidence and alternatives you discovered.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 6: Notice the Shift">
Pay attention to how your emotions and body respond to the balanced thought.
Even a **small reduction** in anxiety intensity is meaningful progress. Rate your anxiety before and after -- any decrease shows the process is working.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Practice Scenarios

Let's walk through three complete scenarios to practice the full process from start to finish.

<ScenarioCard title="The Full CBT Toolkit in Action">
James noticed his anxiety spike when he saw a missed call from his doctor's office. His automatic thought was: "They found something wrong in my blood work. This is going to be terrible news."
Using his toolkit, James paused (Step 1), identified the thought (Step 2), and named the traps: **catastrophizing** and **emotional reasoning** (Step 3). He then investigated with three detective questions (Step 4):
- **Evidence:** The only fact is a missed call. Doctors' offices call for many routine reasons -- scheduling, insurance, normal results.
- **Alternatives:** It could be a reminder about a follow-up, a billing question, or even good news.
- **Friend test:** He would tell a friend, "Don't spiral. Call them back and find out. A missed call is not a diagnosis."
His balanced thought (Step 5): "I do not know why they called. There are many possibilities, and most of them are routine. I will call back and find out instead of assuming the worst."
**Result:** His anxiety dropped from 8/10 to 4/10 -- and when he called back, it was a routine scheduling reminder.
</ScenarioCard>

### Scenario A: The Unanswered Text Message

**Situation:** You sent an important text message to a friend three hours ago and they have not responded.

**Automatic Thought:** "They're ignoring me. I must have said something to upset them. They probably don't want to be friends anymore."

**Thinking Traps:** Mind Reading, Catastrophizing

**Detective Questions:**
1. **Evidence:** The only fact is that they have not responded yet. There is no evidence of being upset. They have been a reliable friend for years.
2. **Alternatives:** They could be busy at work, their phone might be on silent, they could be driving, or they might be thinking about how to respond.
3. **Friend test:** I would tell a friend, "Don't read into it. People get busy. Give it time."

**Balanced Thought:** "There are many reasons someone might not respond right away. Our friendship is solid, and a delayed text response does not change that. I will give them time."

**Anxiety shift:** 7/10 down to 3/10

---

### Scenario B: The Mistake at Work

**Situation:** You sent a report to your team and realized afterward that it contained a small error in one of the data tables.

**Automatic Thought:** "Everyone is going to notice. They're going to think I'm incompetent. I can't believe I let this happen. I'm terrible at my job."

**Thinking Traps:** Catastrophizing, All-or-Nothing Thinking, Labeling

**Detective Questions:**
1. **Evidence:** The error was minor. The rest of the report was thorough and well-researched. Colleagues have made similar errors before and it was not a big deal.
2. **Alternatives:** Most people may not even notice. Those who do will understand that small errors happen. I can send a quick correction.
3. **Friend test:** I would tell a friend, "Everyone makes mistakes. One small error does not define your competence. Just send a correction and move on."

**Balanced Thought:** "I made a small error, which is human. The report was otherwise strong. I can send a quick correction and this will not define how people see my work."

**Anxiety shift:** 8/10 down to 3/10

---

### Scenario C: The Upcoming Family Gathering

**Situation:** A large family gathering is coming up this weekend. Several relatives you find difficult to be around will be there.

**Automatic Thought:** "This is going to be awful. Someone is going to bring up something uncomfortable. I should just make an excuse not to go."

**Thinking Traps:** Catastrophizing, Emotional Reasoning, "Should" Statements

**Detective Questions:**
1. **Evidence:** Past gatherings have had some awkward moments, but they have also included enjoyable conversations and good food. Not every moment was negative.
2. **Alternatives:** I could go for a limited time, focus on the relatives I enjoy, or step outside for a break if I need one. I have more control than I think.
3. **Friend test:** I would tell a friend, "You don't have to stay the whole time. Go, see the people you enjoy, and have an exit plan if you need it."

**Balanced Thought:** "This gathering will have both comfortable and uncomfortable moments. I can plan ahead by setting a time limit, focusing on people I enjoy, and taking breaks when I need them."

**Anxiety shift:** 6/10 down to 3/10

## Building a Daily Practice

These skills get stronger with practice. Like any muscle, the more you use your thought-challenging tools, the more natural they become. Do not worry if it feels effortful at first -- that is completely normal.

### Quick Daily Check-In

Use this step-by-step guide for your 5-minute evening reflection:

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Recall Your Most Anxious Moment">Think back over your day. What was the moment when your anxiety was highest? Briefly describe the situation -- where you were, what was happening, and who was involved.</Step>
<Step title="Identify the Driving Thought">What was your brain telling you in that moment? Capture the automatic thought as specifically as you can. Try to recall the exact words or images that went through your mind.</Step>
<Step title="Name the Thinking Trap">Check your thought against the six traps: catastrophizing, all-or-nothing thinking, mind reading, emotional reasoning, should statements, or labeling. Which one (or more) was at work?</Step>
<Step title="Create a Balanced Alternative">Using your detective questions, write a more balanced version of the thought. Remember: balanced means honest, realistic, and compassionate -- not falsely positive.</Step>
</StepByStep>
Over time, this reflection becomes second nature, and you will start catching and reframing anxious thoughts in real time.
### When to Use Each Tool
| Situation | Best Tool |
|-----------|-----------|
| Quick anxious thought in the moment | Name the trap + one detective question |
| Moderate anxiety that lingers | Three detective questions (mental or written) |
| Significant anxious episode | Full 5-step Thought Record |
| Recurring anxious pattern | Review past thought records for patterns |
| Daily maintenance | Evening check-in (5 minutes) |
