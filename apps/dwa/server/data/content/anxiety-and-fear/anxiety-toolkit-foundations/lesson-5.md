Anxious thoughts often are not random; they follow predictable patterns. Think of them like mental shortcuts your brain takes that lead you down a path of worry. We call these **Thinking Traps**. They are faulty filters that distort reality. Learning to spot them is like taking off foggy glasses -- suddenly, you can see things more clearly.

## What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

- **Name six common thinking traps** that fuel anxiety
- **Recognize these traps** in everyday thoughts and situations
- **Understand why your brain** falls into these patterns
- **Practice identifying traps** using real-world examples

## Part 1: The Field Guide to Thinking Traps

Familiarize yourself with each of the six common thinking traps below. Tap each card to reveal the details -- these are the patterns your brain uses most often when anxiety is in control.

<FlipCard front="Catastrophizing" back="Jumping to the absolute worst-case scenario, even when it is highly unlikely. Example: 'My heart is beating fast... what if I'm having a heart attack?' Your brain overestimates danger and underestimates your ability to cope." frontIcon="⚡" persistKey="trap-catastrophizing" />

<FlipCard front="All-or-Nothing Thinking" back="Seeing things in black-and-white. If it is not perfect, it is a total failure. Example: 'I ate one piece of cake. My diet is ruined.' It is easier for an anxious brain to put things in clear categories than to sit with ambiguity." frontIcon="⚖️" persistKey="trap-all-or-nothing" />

<FlipCard front="Mind Reading" back="Believing you know what others are thinking about you, without any real evidence. Example: 'They didn't text back right away. They must be angry with me.' Your brain tries to predict social threats, but anxiety makes those predictions overwhelmingly negative." frontIcon="🔮" persistKey="trap-mind-reading" />

<FlipCard front="Emotional Reasoning" back="Assuming that because you feel something, it must be true. Example: 'I feel overwhelmed, therefore I must be incompetent.' Emotions are powerful signals, but when they are intense, they feel like undeniable evidence -- feelings are not facts." frontIcon="💭" persistKey="trap-emotional-reasoning" />

<FlipCard front="'Should' Statements" back="Holding yourself to a strict, invisible rulebook. 'Shoulds' often lead to guilt and frustration. Example: 'I should be able to do this all by myself without asking for help.' Anxiety amplifies internalized expectations into rigid demands." frontIcon="📋" persistKey="trap-should-statements" />

<FlipCard front="Labeling" back="Assigning a negative, global label to yourself or others based on a single event. Example: 'I made a mistake on that project. I am such a failure.' Labels feel definitive and certain, which is oddly comforting to an anxious brain -- even when painful." frontIcon="🏷️" persistKey="trap-labeling" />

## Part 2: The Practice Arena

Now, let's practice spotting these traps in action. Work through each challenge -- read the anxious thought and see if you can identify which thinking trap is at work before moving to the next.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="thinking-trap-challenges" showDots>
<Slide title="Challenge 1: The Quiet Friend">
**The thought:** "My friend seemed quiet during lunch. I bet they think I'm boring and don't want to hang out with me anymore."
**Which trap is this?**
- Catastrophizing
- **Mind Reading** (correct)
- Labeling
**Why:** You are assuming you know exactly what your friend is thinking, and jumping to a negative conclusion without any real proof. There could be many reasons your friend was quiet -- a bad day, tiredness, or personal worries that have nothing to do with you.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Challenge 2: The Presentation">
**The thought:** "I got nervous during my presentation and forgot one of my points. The whole thing was a complete disaster."
**Which trap is this?**
- Emotional Reasoning
- Overgeneralizing
- **All-or-Nothing Thinking** (correct)
**Why:** This is a classic case of black-and-white thinking. One small imperfection made you see the entire presentation as a total failure. In reality, forgetting one point does not erase everything else that went well.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Challenge 3: The Anxious Flight">
**The thought:** "I feel so anxious about this flight. Flying must be incredibly dangerous."
**Which trap is this?**
- **Emotional Reasoning** (correct)
- "Should" Statements
- Catastrophizing
**Why:** Here, the feeling of anxiety is being used as evidence for a fact. "I feel scared, therefore it must be dangerous," even though flying is statistically very safe. The intensity of the feeling does not determine the reality of the situation.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Challenge 4: The Hard Worker">
**The thought:** "I've been working so hard, but I'm still feeling stressed. I should be able to handle this better by now."
**Which trap is this?**
- **"Should" Statements** (correct)
- Labeling
- Mind Reading
**Why:** This thought holds you to an unfair, rigid rule ("I should..."). This often leads to feelings of guilt and inadequacy, adding more pressure on top of the stress you are already managing. Progress does not follow a straight line, and healing is not linear.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

<ScenarioCard title="The Email That Spiraled">
Maria checked her email and saw a message from her manager with the subject line "Quick follow-up." Immediately her stomach dropped. "This must be about the report I submitted yesterday," she thought. "I probably made a huge error and now they want to talk about it. Everyone on the team probably saw the mistake too."
Maria spent the next two hours anxiously re-reading her report, unable to focus on anything else. When she finally opened the email, her manager had simply asked if she could attend an optional training session next week.
**The traps at work:** Maria fell into **catastrophizing** (jumping to the worst-case scenario about the email), **mind reading** (assuming her manager and team had negative thoughts), and **emotional reasoning** (her anxiety made her believe something was wrong). Three traps triggered by a two-word subject line.
</ScenarioCard>

## Naming It Helps to Tame It

Learning to name these thinking traps is a foundational skill in managing anxiety. When you catch yourself in a trap, you can say: "That's catastrophizing" or "I notice I'm mind reading again." This simple act of naming creates distance between you and the thought.

You do not need to memorize every trap perfectly. The goal is to develop a general awareness that your anxious thoughts often follow patterns -- and those patterns can be questioned.

## Key Takeaways

- [ ] Anxious thoughts follow predictable patterns called thinking traps
- [ ] The six most common traps are: catastrophizing, all-or-nothing thinking, mind reading, emotional reasoning, "should" statements, and labeling
- [ ] Naming a thinking trap reduces its power over you
- [ ] Your brain falls into these traps as a misguided attempt to keep you safe
- [ ] Recognizing thinking traps is the first step toward challenging them (which you will learn next)
