## Reflection Exercise: Your Anxiety Story

The following reflection is designed to help you observe your anxiety from a safe distance. There are no right or wrong answers. Simply describe what you notice.

### 1. The Situation

Think back to the most recent time you felt a significant wave of anxiety. Describe what was happening right before and during that moment.

*Example: "I was about to join a video call for work" or "I was thinking about my to-do list at night."*

Take a moment to write down your situation. Be as specific as you can about who was there, where you were, and what was happening.

### 2. The Physical Sensations (The "Gas Pedal")

What did you feel in your body? Using the examples from the previous lessons (or your own words), list the top 2-3 sensations you noticed.

*Example: "A tight feeling in my chest, my palms got sweaty, I felt dizzy."*

Remember from Lesson 2 -- these sensations are your sympathetic nervous system activating. They are not dangerous. They are your body's way of trying to protect you.

### 3. The Thoughts

What was your mind telling you? Try to recall any "what if..." thoughts, worries, or self-critical statements that were present.

*Example: "What if I say something stupid?", "I'm going to fail this.", "I can't handle this."*

Notice how the thoughts tend to be about the future and are often predictions of something going wrong.

### 4. The Action

What did you do in response to the anxiety? Did you avoid something, distract yourself, push through, or something else?

*Example: "I kept checking my phone", "I decided not to go to the event", "I paced around the room."*

There is no judgment here. We all have different ways of responding to anxiety, and awareness of your pattern is the first step toward change.

## Why This Matters

Simply writing this down is a huge step in taking back control. You are separating yourself from the anxiety and seeing it for what it is: a pattern of feelings, thoughts, and urges. We will use this information in later lessons to build targeted coping strategies.

## The Observer Perspective

One of the most powerful skills in managing anxiety is learning to be an **observer** of your own experience rather than being swept away by it. This is sometimes called "cognitive defusion" in Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT).

Instead of saying "I am anxious," try saying "I notice I am feeling anxious." This small shift in language creates psychological distance between you and the emotion. You are not your anxiety -- you are the person *experiencing* anxiety.

### Practice the Observer Stance

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Pause">Stop what you are doing for just a moment. Even a single breath creates enough space to shift from reacting to observing.</Step>
<Step title="Name It">Say to yourself, "I notice I am feeling anxious." This small language shift -- from "I am anxious" to "I notice anxiety" -- creates psychological distance.</Step>
<Step title="Scan Your Body">Briefly check your body from head to toe. Where do you feel tension, tightness, or discomfort? Name the sensations without trying to change them.</Step>
<Step title="Accept the Feeling">Acknowledge the anxiety without trying to push it away or fix it. Say, "This is anxiety. It is uncomfortable, but it is not dangerous."</Step>
<Step title="Choose Your Response">Now that you have observed rather than reacted, decide what you want to do next. You have given yourself the gift of choice instead of automatic reaction.</Step>
</StepByStep>
