## Stafford & Canary's 5 Maintenance Strategies

Research-validated behaviors that predict relationship satisfaction and longevity.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="five-strategies">
<Slide title="STRATEGY 1: Positivity (Optimism, Cheerfulness)">
**What it is:** Maintaining cheerful, optimistic interactions; focusing on positive traits; avoiding constant complaints/criticism.

**Why it matters:** Positive interactions create pleasant atmosphere making people WANT to spend time together. Criticism creates defensiveness and withdrawal.

**Examples:**
- Complimenting friend on achievement
- Expressing appreciation for partner's efforts
- Maintaining humor even during stress
- Focusing on gratitude not grievances

**Gottman's 5:1 ratio:** Relationships thrive when positive interactions outnumber negative 5 to 1. Criticism tolerable if balanced with positivity.

**Caution:** Positivity doesn't mean toxic positivity (suppressing real problems). Means addressing issues constructively without constant negativity.

**Self-assessment:** Do you maintain cheerful interactions? Or constant criticism/complaints dominating?
</Slide>

<Slide title="STRATEGY 2: Openness (Self-Disclosure, Meta-Communication)">
**What it is:** Sharing thoughts/feelings/experiences (self-disclosure) + talking about relationship itself (meta-communication: "How are WE doing?").

**Why it matters:** Openness creates intimacy and prevents relationship drift. Meta-communication catches problems early before they escalate.

**Examples:**
- Sharing vulnerable feelings with friend
- Discussing relationship concerns: "I've felt distant lately — can we talk?"
- Regular check-ins about relationship health
- Not avoiding difficult conversations

**Research:** Self-disclosure deepens relationships (Lesson 8.5 on vulnerability), but meta-communication MAINTAINS them by addressing issues proactively.

**Balance:** Too little openness = superficial relationship. Too much = overwhelming. Gradual, reciprocal disclosure optimal.

**Self-assessment:** Are you sharing vulnerably? Meta-communicating about relationship health? Or avoiding important conversations?
</Slide>

<Slide title="STRATEGY 3: Assurances (Expressing Commitment, Reliability)">
**What it is:** Explicitly communicating commitment ("you're important to me"), reassuring relationship value, demonstrating reliability/consistency.

**Why it matters:** Assurances combat insecurity and uncertainty. People need to know relationship matters and will continue.

**Examples:**
- Telling friend "your friendship means the world to me"
- Reassuring partner of commitment during stress
- Showing up consistently when promised
- Following through on commitments

**Attachment theory:** Assurances create secure attachment by signaling "I'm here, I'm staying, you matter to me."

**Frequency:** Not just crisis moments — regular low-stakes assurances ("looking forward to our weekly call") build cumulative security.

**Self-assessment:** Do they know they're important to you? Are you reliable/consistent? Or taking relationship for granted?
</Slide>

<Slide title="STRATEGY 4: Social Networks (Shared Friends, Integration)">
**What it is:** Spending time with mutual friends, integrating into each other's social circles, supporting each other's friendships.

**Why it matters:** Shared networks create "relationship infrastructure" — mutual friends reinforce bond. Isolation weakens it.

**Examples:**
- Couple hosting dinner with mutual friends
- Introducing close friend to other friends creating shared circle
- Attending partner's work events
- Supporting friend's community involvement

**Research:** Relationships embedded in social networks more stable — mutual friends provide support, accountability, shared activities reinforcing connection.

**Balance:** Some individual friendships healthy (autonomy), but NO shared networks = relationship isolated and vulnerable.

**Self-assessment:** Do you have shared friends? Integrated social circles? Or completely separate social lives?
</Slide>

<Slide title="STRATEGY 5: Sharing Tasks (Equitable Distribution)">
**What it is:** Fair, equitable distribution of relationship maintenance work (planning, organizing, emotional labor, practical tasks).

**Why it matters:** Inequity breeds resentment. Perceived fairness predicts satisfaction. Feeling like you're doing all the work destroys goodwill.

**Examples:**
- Taking turns planning friend hangouts (not always one person initiating)
- Partners equally handling household/emotional labor
- Shared responsibility for maintaining family connection

**Equity theory:** People assess relationship fairness by comparing inputs (effort) to outputs (benefits). Perceived inequity = dissatisfaction.

**Reality check:** Perfect 50/50 impossible every moment — but over TIME, both people should feel they're contributing fairly and being valued.

**Self-assessment:** Is relationship work equitable? Or one person doing all initiating/planning? How does that feel?
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

**APPLYING ALL 5:** Research shows using 4+ strategies = 78% higher satisfaction. Relationships using 0-2 strategies = drift/decline. Not about perfection — about INTENTIONAL, CONSISTENT application.

## Strategic vs Routine Maintenance

Two types of relationship maintenance — both essential for longevity.

<EnhancedAccordion title="Understanding the Balance">
<AccordionItem title="ROUTINE MAINTENANCE: Everyday Habits (80%)" badge="Baseline">
**What it is:** Daily/weekly relationship habits — how you normally interact, communicate, spend time together.

**Examples:**
- Regular texting/calling
- Casual conversations
- Routine activities together (coffee, walks, watching shows)
- Everyday courtesy/kindness

**Purpose:** Creates relationship baseline — steady, predictable connection preventing neglect.

**Limitation:** Routine alone → stagnation, taking relationship for granted, lack of novelty/growth.

**Strength:** Low-effort, sustainable — doesn't require intense planning, just consistent showing up.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="STRATEGIC MAINTENANCE: Intentional Building (20%)" badge="Growth">
**What it is:** Deliberate, planned activities specifically designed to strengthen relationship — requires forethought and effort.

**Examples:**
- Planning special birthday celebration
- Scheduling monthly "state of friendship" check-in
- Organizing weekend trip together
- Creating new shared experience/tradition

**Purpose:** Prevents relationship drift, creates novelty/excitement, deepens intimacy, addresses problems proactively.

**Limitation:** Requires time/energy/planning — can't sustain constant strategic maintenance (exhausting).

**Strength:** High-impact — creates memorable experiences, relationship growth, course-correction when needed.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="OPTIMAL BALANCE: 80% Routine, 20% Strategic" badge="Integration">
**Weekly balance example (close friendship):**
- **Routine (80%):** Daily texting, weekly coffee meetup
- **Strategic (20%):** Monthly deeper check-in call, quarterly new experience together

**Monthly balance example (partnership):**
- **Routine (80%):** Daily meals together, regular conversations
- **Strategic (20%):** Monthly date night with new activity, quarterly relationship review

**Warning:**
- All routine, no strategic = relationship stagnation (taken for granted, drifting apart)
- All strategic, no routine = unsustainable (burnout, performative)

**Need BOTH.**
</AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## 🎯 Step 5: Barrier Identification - What Might Stop You

Proactive problem-solving: identify likely obstacles and create solutions BEFORE they derail you.

<EnhancedAccordion title="Common Barriers & Solutions">
<AccordionItem title="TIME/ENERGY SCARCITY: 'I'm too busy/exhausted'" badge="Most Common">
**The barrier:** Life demands (work, family, health) leave little time/energy for relationship maintenance.

**The solution:**
- Start SMALL (one 5-minute appreciation text weekly vs daily hour-long calls)
- Integrate into existing routines (call during commute, walk-and-talk, meal together)
- Prioritize ONE key relationship vs trying to maintain all simultaneously
- Schedule relationship time like important meeting (calendar it or it won't happen)

**Reframe:** "I'm too busy" often means "not prioritized enough." If genuinely no capacity, consider: What needs to DROP to make room for connection? What's truly essential vs habit?
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="FEAR/DISCOMFORT: 'Vulnerability feels risky'" badge="Emotional">
**The barrier:** Fear of rejection, judgment, or awkwardness prevents deeper sharing or difficult conversations.

**The solution:**
- Start with LOW-stakes vulnerability (share preference, opinion) before deep feelings
- Choose SAFE person for initial practice (therapy, trusted friend)
- Remind yourself: Discomfort is growth signal, not danger signal
- Remember Beautiful Mess Effect (Lesson 8.9): Vulnerability appears courageous to others

**Reframe:** "This feels risky" → "I'm practicing courage. Risk is required for reward."
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="OTHER PERSON'S RESISTANCE: 'They won't reciprocate'" badge="Interpersonal">
**The barrier:** Fear that friend/partner won't match your effort or will reject proposed changes.

**The solution:**
- Propose collaboratively, not demandingly: "I'd love to do X together — how does that sound to you?"
- Accept their current capacity: If genuinely can't reciprocate right now, reduce expectations or choose different relationship to focus on
- Give time: People need adjustment period to new relationship patterns
- Assess after 4-6 weeks: If NO reciprocity after consistent effort, may need to accept relationship limitations

**Reframe:** You control YOUR half of relationship. Can't force their participation, but can offer invitation.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="FORGETTING/INCONSISTENCY: 'I start strong then forget'" badge="Habit Formation">
**The barrier:** Initial motivation fades, new behaviors forgotten, fall back to old patterns.

**The solution:**
- Calendar reminders (phone alerts for maintenance activities)
- Habit stacking (attach new behavior to existing routine: "After morning coffee, I'll text appreciation to friend")
- Accountability partner (tell someone your goal, check in weekly)
- Track visibly (journal, checklist, app) — seeing progress reinforces habit

**Reframe:** Habit formation takes 6-8 weeks. Expect imperfection. One missed week doesn't equal failure — just resume next week.
</AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## 🎯 Step 7: Your 4-Week Action Plan

Break 90-day goal into immediate, concrete actions for the next 4 weeks.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="action-plan">
<Slide title="WEEK 1: Foundation & Communication">
**Your actions this week:**

1. **Finalize your SMART goal** (write it down, make it specific and measurable)

2. **Communicate goal to key person if appropriate:**
   - Example for partnership: "I want us to prioritize quality time together — can we schedule monthly date night?"
   - Example for friendship: "I value our friendship and want to stay close despite distance — want to set up monthly call?"

3. **Set up accountability:**
   - Tell accountability partner your goal
   - Calendar first few maintenance activities (calls, appreciation expressions, etc.)
   - Create tracking system (journal, checklist, app)

4. **Identify your FIRST small action:**
   - One text expressing appreciation
   - One vulnerable conversation starter
   - One community meeting attended
   - One ritual proposed

**Week 1 is about SETUP, not perfection. Create infrastructure for success.**
</Slide>

<Slide title="WEEK 2-3: Consistent Implementation">
**Your actions these weeks:**

1. **Execute your weekly maintenance behavior:**
   - If goal is positivity: 2 appreciations per week (4 total these 2 weeks)
   - If goal is openness: 1 deeper conversation these 2 weeks
   - If goal is joining community: Attend second meeting (3-visit rule)
   - If goal is distance maintenance: Schedule/complete first monthly call

2. **Track your progress:**
   - Journal: What did I do? How did it go? How did I feel? How did they respond?
   - Celebrate small wins (you texted appreciation = success, regardless of their response)

3. **Adjust as needed:**
   - Is goal too ambitious? Scale back.
   - Is barrier emerging? Implement solution proactively.
   - Is something working really well? Do more of it.

4. **Check in with accountability partner:**
   - Share what's working, what's challenging
   - Get encouragement and problem-solving support

**Weeks 2-3 are about CONSISTENCY. Building habit through repetition.**
</Slide>

<Slide title="WEEK 4: Review & Refinement">
**Your actions this week:**

1. **Month 1 review:**
   - Did I achieve weekly actions? (4 weeks of maintenance behaviors)
   - What's working well? (double down on this)
   - What's not working? (adjust or replace)
   - How's the relationship feeling? (any positive shifts?)

2. **Refine approach for months 2-3:**
   - Based on what you learned, what needs to change?
   - Is goal still relevant or does it need adjustment?
   - What barriers emerged and how will you address them?

3. **Celebrate progress:**
   - You completed 4 weeks! That's 1/3 of 90-day goal.
   - Even small progress = moving in right direction
   - Acknowledge the effort, not just outcomes

4. **Recommit for next phase:**
   - Months 2-3 continue the same practices (consistency builds results)
   - OR implement refined approach based on learning

**Week 4 is about REFLECTION and RECOMMITMENT. Learning from experience.**
</Slide>

<Slide title="ONGOING: Months 2-3 & Beyond">
**Continuing your practice:**

**Months 2-3 (Weeks 5-12):**
- Continue weekly maintenance behaviors
- Monthly progress check-ins (Week 8 and Week 12)
- Adjust strategies as you learn what works best
- Deepen practices that are producing positive shifts

**End of 90 days (Week 12):**
- **COMPREHENSIVE REVIEW:**
  - Did I achieve my SMART goal? (measurable outcome)
  - How has relationship changed? (quality, connection, satisfaction)
  - What maintenance strategies am I keeping permanently?
  - What's my next 90-day goal? (continuous improvement)

**Quarterly relationship review with partner/friend:**
- Schedule dedicated 30-60 minute conversation
- "How do you think we're doing?"
- "What's working well? What could we do differently?"
- "What should WE focus on next quarter?"

**MAKE IT PERMANENT:**
- The strategies that worked in your 90-day plan become your ONGOING relationship maintenance rituals
- Quarterly reviews become regular practice (every 3 months)
- You've transformed from reactive to PROACTIVE relationship care
