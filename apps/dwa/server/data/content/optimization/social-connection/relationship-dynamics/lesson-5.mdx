# Your Attachment Patterns (Self-Assessment, NOT Labeling)

## Learning Objectives

- Complete evidence-based self-reflection on attachment anxiety and avoidance dimensions
- Understand your patterns in context (relationship-specific, not global traits)
- Recognize attachment patterns as starting points for growth, not fixed diagnoses
- Identify specific behaviors you want to work on based on your self-assessment

## Research Foundation

Attachment patterns are dimensional (continuous scales of anxiety and avoidance), contextual (vary by relationship), and dynamic (change with relational experience). Self-assessment helps you understand your current patterns — not label yourself permanently. A 2026 systematic review found that psychotherapy produces meaningful shifts toward security even when attachment isn't explicitly targeted, confirming that patterns are responsive to intervention.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG for dimensional model; MODERATE for changeability

## Before You Begin: What This Self-Assessment Is and Isn't

<Callout type="warning" title="This Is NOT a Diagnostic Test">

**What this is:**
- A structured self-reflection tool to help you identify where you fall on the anxiety and avoidance dimensions **in your current or most recent relationship**
- A starting point for understanding your relational patterns and areas for growth
- Based on validated attachment research frameworks (ECR-R, Experiences in Close Relationships scale)

**What this is NOT:**
- A clinical diagnosis or personality test
- A label to apply to yourself or others ("I'm anxious-preoccupied")
- Fixed truth about who you are — your patterns can and do shift
- Applicable to all relationships uniformly — you may show different patterns with different partners

**How to use your results:**
Your scores tell you where to focus your attention, not who you "are." High anxiety? Practice self-soothing and challenging catastrophic thoughts. High avoidance? Practice vulnerability and approaching (instead of withdrawing).

</Callout>

For each statement, rate how much it describes you **in your current or most recent romantic relationship**:

**Rating scale:**
1. Strongly disagree
2. Disagree
3. Neutral / Sometimes
4. Agree

<AccordionItem title="Anxiety Dimension — 12 Items" icon="😰">

1. **I worry that my partner doesn't really love me.**
   - Captures: Core fear of not being worthy of love

2. **I need a lot of reassurance that my partner cares about me.**
   - Captures: Reassurance-seeking behavior

3. **I get frustrated when my partner is not available when I need them.**
   - Captures: Difficulty tolerating partner unavailability

4. **I worry a lot about my relationships.**
   - Captures: Rumination and preoccupation

5. **When my partner is out of sight, I worry that they might be interested in someone else.**
   - Captures: Hypervigilance to threat (infidelity, abandonment)

6. **I often want to merge completely with my partner, which sometimes scares them away.**
   - Captures: Desire for extreme closeness that may overwhelm partner

7. **I find that my partner doesn't want to get as close as I would like.**
   - Captures: Perception of partner as insufficiently intimate

8. **I rarely worry about my partner leaving me.** (REVERSE-SCORED)
   - Captures: Absence of abandonment fear (score this one backwards: 5=1, 4=2, 3=3, 2=4, 1=5)

9. **My desire to be very close sometimes scares people away.**
   - Captures: Awareness that intensity of closeness-seeking may create distance

10. **I'm afraid that I will lose my partner's love.**
    - Captures: Core abandonment fear

11. **I often wish that my partner's feelings for me were as strong as my feelings for them.**
    - Captures: Perception of asymmetry in investment

12. **When I don't hear from my partner for a while, I wonder if they still care about me.**
    - Captures: Anxiety during communication gaps

**Scoring (after reversing #8):**
- Add up your scores: ___ / 60
- Divide by 12 to get your average anxiety score: ___

**Interpretation:**
- **1.0–2.5:** Lower attachment anxiety (more confidence in partner availability, less fear of abandonment)
- **2.6–3.5:** Moderate attachment anxiety (some worry about rejection, context-dependent)
- **3.6–5.0:** Higher attachment anxiety (significant worry about abandonment, hypervigilance to rejection cues)

## Mapping Your Attachment Pattern (Two-Dimensional Model)

Your two scores (anxiety and avoidance) place you in a two-dimensional attachment space.

<SlideNavigation>

<Slide title="Four-Quadrant Model (Descriptive, Not Diagnostic)">

**This is a heuristic framework** for understanding how the two dimensions combine. It's NOT a personality type.

**Low Anxiety + Low Avoidance = "Secure" patterns:**
- Comfortable with both closeness and independence
- Trust partner's availability without constant reassurance
- Can express vulnerability and needs directly
- Tolerates conflict without catastrophizing or withdrawing

**High Anxiety + Low Avoidance = "Anxious-Preoccupied" patterns:**
- Strong desire for closeness and intimacy
- Hypervigilance to rejection or abandonment cues
- Difficulty self-soothing (needs partner reassurance to regulate)
- Pursues partner during conflict or distance

**Low Anxiety + High Avoidance = "Dismissive-Avoidant" patterns:**
- Values independence and self-sufficiency
- Uncomfortable with emotional vulnerability
- Withdraws when partner seeks deep connection
- Minimizes importance of relationships ("I don't need anyone")

**High Anxiety + High Avoidance = "Fearful-Avoidant" patterns:**
- Simultaneous desire for and fear of closeness
- "Come here, go away" dynamic
- Internal conflict between intimacy needs and vulnerability fear
- Often rooted in trauma or severely inconsistent caregiving

</Slide>

<Slide title="Why the Dimensional Model Is Better Than Categories">

**Categories suggest:**
- You "are" a type (fixed, global)
- Sharp boundaries between groups (you're either anxious or you're not)
- Stability across time and relationships (once avoidant, always avoidant)

**Dimensions recognize:**
- You have **positions on two continua** that can shift
- Your scores in one relationship may differ from another
- Small changes in behavior can gradually shift your position
- Everyone experiences some anxiety and some avoidance in some contexts

**Example:**
Instead of saying "I'm anxious-preoccupied," say "In my current relationship, I notice higher attachment anxiety (worrying about abandonment, needing reassurance) and lower avoidance (I'm comfortable with closeness). In past relationships with less-available partners, my avoidance was higher."

This framing is more accurate and less stigmatizing.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Context Matters: Relationship-Specific Patterns">

**Critical insight from research:**
Your attachment pattern is **not entirely "in you"** — it emerges in relationship with a specific partner.

**You may show different patterns depending on:**
- **Partner's attachment style:** Being with a highly avoidant partner may increase your anxiety; being with a secure partner may decrease it
- **Relationship stage:** Early dating (more anxiety about commitment) vs. long-term (more comfort with security)
- **Life stress:** External stress (job loss, illness, grief) can temporarily increase anxiety or avoidance
- **Partner behavior:** Inconsistent partner responsiveness increases anxiety; partner criticism or judgment increases avoidance

**What this means:**
Your self-assessment reflects your patterns **in a specific relationship context**, not a global personality trait. If your scores surprise you, consider: "What about this relationship or this partner brings out this pattern in me?"

</Slide>

</SlideNavigation>

## What to Do With Your Results

Your scores are actionable starting points, not life sentences.

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
<Insight title="If You Scored High on Anxiety" icon="😰">

**What this means:**
You may experience significant worry about abandonment, need frequent reassurance, hypervigilance to rejection cues, and difficulty self-soothing during conflict or distance.

**What to work on:**
1. **Challenge catastrophic thoughts:** "Partner is quiet" ≠ "Partner is leaving me"
2. **Practice self-soothing:** Use emotion regulation skills (breathing, self-compassion, distraction) instead of immediately seeking partner reassurance
3. **Build independent sources of security:** Invest in friendships, hobbies, therapy — don't rely solely on partner for emotional regulation
4. **Communicate needs non-demandingly:** "I'd love to spend time together this week" (not "You never make time for me")
5. **Tolerate uncertainty:** Practice sitting with "not knowing" (whether partner is upset, what they're thinking) without demanding immediate resolution

**When to seek professional help:**
If anxiety is severe enough to impair functioning or if self-help strategies haven't helped after 3-6 months, individual therapy (CBT, DBT) or couples therapy (EFT) is appropriate.

</Insight>

<Insight title="If You Scored High on Avoidance" icon="🚶">

**What this means:**
You may experience discomfort with emotional vulnerability, preference for independence over closeness, withdrawal when partner seeks intimacy, and difficulty asking for or receiving support.

**What to work on:**
1. **Practice vulnerability in low-stakes moments:** Share a feeling, ask for help with something small, express appreciation
2. **Approach instead of withdraw:** Initiate connection (text first, suggest couple time, ask about partner's day) instead of waiting for partner to pursue
3. **Notice withdrawal patterns:** When you feel "suffocated" or want to escape, pause and ask "Is this genuine need for space or avoidance of intimacy?"
4. **Stay present during emotional conversations:** Resist urge to change subject, problem-solve, or leave — practice just listening and validating
5. **Reframe closeness as strength, not weakness:** Interdependence and vulnerability are adaptive, not signs of inadequacy

**When to seek professional help:**
If avoidance significantly impairs your ability to form or maintain intimate relationships, or if it's rooted in trauma (abuse, neglect, betrayal), trauma-informed therapy is appropriate.

</Insight>
</InsightGrid>

<Callout type="info" title="If You Scored High on BOTH Anxiety and Avoidance">

**High anxiety + high avoidance = fearful-avoidant pattern:**
- You want closeness but fear vulnerability
- "Come here, go away" dynamic — approach then withdraw
- Often rooted in trauma or severely inconsistent caregiving
- Internal conflict between intimacy needs and fear of hurt

**This pattern is particularly challenging** because your attachment system is sending contradictory signals. Professional therapy (trauma-informed, attachment-focused) is strongly recommended. EFT, AEDP (Accelerated Experiential Dynamic Psychotherapy), or trauma-focused CBT can help.

**Self-help strategies:**
- Recognize the internal conflict without self-judgment ("Part of me wants closeness; part of me fears it — both are valid")
- Start with individual therapy to process trauma before or alongside couples work
- Practice self-compassion (this pattern emerges from painful experiences, not personal failure)

</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- **Self-assessment provides dimensional scores** (anxiety and avoidance), not fixed categorical labels — you're not "an anxious type," you have a position on two continua
- **Scores are relationship-specific and contextual** — your pattern with one partner may differ from another, and life stress or relationship stage affects scores
- **High anxiety = work on self-soothing and challenging catastrophic thoughts** — reduce dependence on partner reassurance for emotional regulation
- **High avoidance = work on vulnerability and approaching** — practice emotional disclosure, initiate closeness, resist withdrawal urges
- **Patterns are changeable** — therapy, secure relationships, and deliberate practice can shift scores toward greater security over time
