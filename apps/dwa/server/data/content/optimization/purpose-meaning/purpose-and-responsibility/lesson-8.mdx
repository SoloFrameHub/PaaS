# Peer Support Models: The Power of Lived Experience

## Learning Objectives

- Understand what peer support is and its evidence base
- Learn the unique benefits of support from someone with lived experience
- Explore different peer support models and when each is appropriate
- Recognize the boundaries and limits of peer support

## Research Foundation

Peer support—assistance provided by people with lived experience of mental health challenges to others facing similar struggles—has a growing evidence base. A 2020 systematic review found peer support associated with reduced hospital admissions, improved hope and empowerment, and enhanced social functioning. The mechanism is distinct from professional care: peers offer experiential knowledge, mutual reciprocity, and hope through modeling recovery. However, peer support complements rather than replaces professional treatment. Evidence shows best outcomes when peer support is integrated with clinical care, not substituted for it.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE for peer support benefits; STRONG for peer support as complement (not replacement) to professional care

## Boundaries in Peer Support

Peer support is powerful but requires clear boundaries to be safe and sustainable.

<Callout type="warning" title="What Peer Support Is NOT">
**Peer support is NOT:**
- Therapy (only licensed clinicians provide therapy)
- Crisis intervention (if someone is suicidal, call 988 or 911—don't try to handle it yourself)
- Medical advice (never tell someone to stop medication or treatment)
- Friendship without limits (you need boundaries to sustain helping)

**Peer support IS:**
- Sharing your experience and what helped you
- Listening and validating
- Offering hope through your own recovery journey
- Connecting to resources
- Walking alongside someone, not carrying them
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Peer support is help from someone with lived experience—offers hope through example, experiential knowledge, and mutuality
- Evidence shows reduced hospital admissions, improved hope/empowerment, enhanced social functioning
- Peer support complements professional care; it doesn't replace it
- Models: formal programs, peer-led groups, online communities, informal mutual aid
- Boundaries essential: peers are not therapists, crisis responders, or medical advisors
- Helper-therapy principle: peer supporters benefit through purpose, insight, connection, and reinforced hope

## Safety Note

⚠️ **If You're Considering Becoming a Peer Supporter:**

Ensure you:
- Are stable in your own recovery (at least 1-2 years)
- Have professional support for yourself (therapist, medication if needed)
- Receive training and supervision (formal programs provide this)
- Maintain clear boundaries (you're not responsible for fixing others)
- Practice self-care (Lesson 19 will cover this extensively)

Peer support is rewarding but emotionally demanding. Don't attempt it while in acute struggle yourself.
