# Mentoring and Coaching Skills: Guiding Others' Growth

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the difference between mentoring, coaching, therapy, and advice-giving
- Learn evidence-based coaching skills for supporting others' development
- Practice powerful questions that facilitate insight and growth
- Recognize when to coach and when to refer to professional support

## Research Foundation

Mentoring and coaching are distinct from therapy: they focus on growth, skill development, and goal achievement rather than treating mental illness. Research shows effective mentoring improves academic outcomes, career development, and psychosocial wellbeing. Coaching psychology demonstrates that skilled questioning, active listening, and goal-setting support facilitate behavior change and performance improvement. The key distinction: coaches help people find their own answers; therapists treat clinical conditions; mentors share wisdom from experience. This lesson teaches coaching skills applicable to peer support, workplace mentoring, and community service contexts.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE-STRONG for mentoring/coaching effectiveness in non-clinical contexts

## Core Coaching Skills

<EnhancedAccordion items={[
  {
    title: "Powerful Questions (Not Leading)",
    content: "**What makes a question powerful:**\n- Open-ended (can't be answered yes/no)\n- Invites reflection and exploration\n- Client-centered (about their experience, not your curiosity)\n- Non-leading (doesn't contain the 'right' answer)\n\n**Examples of powerful questions:**\n- 'What matters most to you about this?'\n- 'What would success look like?'\n- 'What's one small step you could take?'\n- 'What's getting in the way?'\n- 'What would you tell a friend in this situation?'\n\n**Avoid leading questions:**\n- 'Don't you think you should...?' (contains your answer)\n- 'Have you tried just...?' (minimizes complexity)\n- Questions that are really advice in disguise"
  },
  {
    title: "Active Listening (Full Presence)",
    content: "**Elements:**\n- Eliminate distractions (put away phone, close laptop)\n- Appropriate eye contact and body language\n- Listen to understand, not to respond\n- Notice what's NOT said (tone, emotion, hesitation)\n- Reflect back key points: 'So what I'm hearing is...'\n- Validate emotions: 'That sounds frustrating'\n\n**Common listening mistakes:**\n- Interrupting with your story or solution\n- Thinking about your response instead of listening\n- Jumping to problem-solving before understanding\n- Dismissing emotions ('Don't feel that way')"
  },
  {
    title: "Goal Setting (SMART Framework)",
    content: "**SMART Goals:**\n- **Specific:** Clear, concrete (not vague)\n- **Measurable:** You'll know when it's accomplished\n- **Achievable:** Challenging but realistic given resources\n- **Relevant:** Aligns with larger values and priorities\n- **Time-bound:** Has a deadline or timeframe\n\n**Example:**\nVague: 'I want to be healthier'\nSMART: 'I will walk 20 minutes three times per week for the next month'\n\n**Coach role:** Help them articulate SMART goals, not impose your goals on them"
  },
  {
    title: "Accountability (Without Shame)",
    content: "**Effective accountability:**\n- Clear commitments: 'What specifically will you do before we talk next?'\n- Follow-up: 'You said you'd try X—how did it go?'\n- Non-judgmental curiosity if they didn't follow through: 'What got in the way?'\n- Adjusting plans based on obstacles, not shaming for 'failure'\n\n**Shame-based accountability (avoid):**\n- 'You didn't do what you said you would—why not?'\n- 'You're not trying hard enough'\n- Making them feel guilty\n\n**Remember:** You're supporting, not policing"
  },
  {
    title: "Reframing (New Perspectives)",
    content: "**What it is:** Offering alternative ways to view a situation without dismissing their experience\n\n**Examples:**\n- Them: 'I failed at this.' You: 'What did you learn that you can use next time?'\n- Them: 'I'm stuck.' You: 'What options haven't you considered yet?'\n- Them: 'This is impossible.' You: 'What would make it possible?'\n\n**NOT toxic positivity:**\n- Don't reframe away real problems\n- Don't tell them to 'just look on the bright side'\n- Reframing expands perspective; it doesn't deny reality"
  }
]} />

## When to Coach and When to Refer

<SlideNavigation slides={[

## The Mentor's Unique Offering: 'I've Been There'

Mentoring differs from coaching by emphasizing shared experience and guidance.

<Callout type="info" title="What Mentors Offer">
- **Lived experience:** 'When I was navigating X, here's what I learned...'
- **Insider knowledge:** How systems actually work, unwritten rules, who to talk to
- **Advocacy:** Opening doors, making introductions, vouching for mentee
- **Perspective:** 'This challenge you're facing is normal at this stage'
- **Role modeling:** Demonstrating what success/recovery/growth looks like

**Balance:** Mentors share experience AND use coaching skills (powerful questions, active listening). Not just advice-giving.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Coaching focuses on goals/performance; mentoring shares experience/wisdom; therapy treats clinical conditions—different roles for different needs
- Core coaching skills: powerful questions, active listening, SMART goal-setting, accountability without shame, reframing
- Powerful questions are open-ended, client-centered, non-leading—they facilitate insight rather than providing answers
- Refer to professional support for clinical symptoms, crisis, legal/medical issues beyond your expertise
- Mentoring adds lived experience, insider knowledge, advocacy, and role modeling to coaching skills

## Safety Note

⚠️ **Stay in Your Lane:**

- Do not provide therapy if you're not a licensed therapist
- Do not give legal advice if you're not a lawyer
- Do not give medical advice if you're not a healthcare provider
- Recognize clinical symptoms and refer appropriately

Coaching and mentoring are powerful—and have limits. Know when you're outside your scope and connect people to appropriate professionals.
