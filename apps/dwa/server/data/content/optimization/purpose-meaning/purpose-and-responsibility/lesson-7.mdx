# Mental Health First Aid: Supporting Others in Crisis

## Learning Objectives

- Understand what mental health first aid is (and isn't)
- Learn to recognize warning signs of mental health crisis
- Practice evidence-based approaches to supporting someone in distress
- Know when and how to connect people to professional help

## Research Foundation

Mental Health First Aid (MHFA) is an evidence-based training program that teaches laypeople to recognize signs of mental health problems, provide initial support, and connect individuals to professional help. A 2022 systematic review found MHFA training improves mental health literacy, reduces stigma, and increases helping behaviors. Importantly, MHFA is NOT clinical training—it's the mental health equivalent of physical first aid (stabilize and connect to care, not diagnose or treat). This lesson provides psychoeducation on MHFA principles, but formal MHFA certification requires in-person training.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE-STRONG for MHFA training effectiveness on knowledge and attitudes; this lesson is psychoeducation only

## What Mental Health First Aid Is (And Isn't)

<Callout type="warning" title="Critical Scope Limitation">
**Mental Health First Aid IS:**
- Recognizing warning signs of mental health crisis
- Providing initial compassionate support
- Connecting people to professional help
- Reducing stigma and increasing help-seeking

**Mental Health First Aid IS NOT:**
- Diagnosing mental health conditions (only licensed professionals diagnose)
- Providing therapy or treatment (requires clinical training)
- Replacing professional care (you're a bridge to care, not care itself)
- Making you responsible for someone else's mental health (you can support; you can't fix)

This lesson teaches awareness and basic support. It does NOT qualify you to provide mental health treatment.
</Callout>

## Your Limits and Self-Care

Supporting someone in mental health crisis is emotionally demanding. You must set boundaries to sustain your helping.

<Callout type="info" title="You Are Not Responsible for Fixing Them">
**Important truths:**
- You can offer support; you cannot cure someone's mental illness
- You can encourage help-seeking; you cannot force recovery
- You can be present; you cannot be their therapist
- You are not responsible for their choices or outcomes

**Boundaries are essential:**
- It's okay to say 'I care about you and I'm not equipped to help with this. Let's find someone who is.'
- It's okay to take breaks from supporting someone if it's depleting you
- It's okay to prioritize your own mental health

**Caregiver burden is real:** Remember Lesson 2's finding that 63% of caregivers experience severe burden. If supporting someone is overwhelming you, you need support too.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Mental Health First Aid is initial support and connection to care, not diagnosis or treatment
- ALGEE framework: Approach/assess, Listen non-judgmentally, Give reassurance/information, Encourage professional help, Encourage self-help/support
- Asking directly about suicide does NOT increase risk—it saves lives
- Warning signs include mood/behavioral changes, social withdrawal, cognitive changes, physical symptoms
- You are not responsible for fixing someone's mental health; you can support and connect them to care
- Set boundaries and prioritize your own wellbeing when supporting others

## Safety Note

⚠️ **When You Need Professional Support:**

If supporting someone is:
- Triggering your own mental health struggles
- Creating severe stress or burden
- Making you feel responsible for keeping them alive
- Consuming all your time and energy

You need professional support too. Supporting someone doesn't mean sacrificing your own wellbeing. Therapists, support groups, and crisis resources are for helpers as well as those in crisis.
