# Motivational Interviewing for Coaches

## Learning Objectives

- Master the RULE principles (Resist righting reflex, Understand motivations, Listen with empathy, Empower)
- Practice evoking change talk vs. sustain talk
- Learn to work with ambivalence rather than arguing against it
- Understand when MI-informed techniques are appropriate and when referral is needed

## Research Foundation

Motivational interviewing (MI) is a patient-centered approach to behavior change with one of the strongest evidence bases in behavioral health. Meta-analyses show MI produces statistically significant beneficial effects for reducing substance use, increasing physical activity, improving treatment adherence, reducing binge drinking, and supporting health behavior change across diverse populations. Effect sizes are moderate, and MI is equivalent to or better than CBT for substance use outcomes. Critically, MI principles and techniques can be taught to non-clinicians — including community health workers, peer support specialists, and coaches — as demonstrated in WHO mhGAP guidelines and global health worker training programs. Full MI delivery requires intensive training, but MI-informed communication skills are teachable and appropriate for coaching contexts.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (for MI as clinical intervention); MODERATE TO STRONG (for MI-informed skills in non-clinical practice)

## What Makes MI Different from Traditional Advice-Giving

Most helping conversations follow a predictable pattern:

1. Person shares a problem
2. Helper identifies the "obvious" solution
3. Helper gives advice: "You should just..."
4. Person explains why that won't work
5. Helper argues harder for the solution
6. Person digs in and defends their current behavior

**This is called the "righting reflex"** — the automatic urge to fix the problem. And paradoxically, it makes people LESS likely to change.

**Motivational interviewing flips this:**

1. Person shares ambivalence about change
2. Helper listens with empathy and curiosity
3. Helper helps the person articulate their OWN reasons for change
4. Person talks themselves into change
5. Helper supports autonomy: "What feels right for you?"

<Callout type="insight" title="Core Principle">
When YOU argue for change, the person argues against it (sustain talk). When you help THEM articulate reasons to change, they convince themselves (change talk). The research is clear: change talk predicts behavior change; sustain talk predicts maintenance of status quo.
</Callout>

## The RULE Framework: MI's Guiding Principles

<SlideNavigation>
  <div title="R: Resist the Righting Reflex">
    <h3>What It Is</h3>
    <p>The automatic urge to fix, solve, or rescue — to jump immediately to giving advice, solutions, or "the answer."</p>
    
    <h3>Why You Must Resist It</h3>
    <p>When you tell someone what to do, you activate their psychological reactance — the human resistance to having autonomy threatened. They may comply in the moment (to please you), but intrinsic motivation doesn't develop. Or they may defend their current behavior, digging in harder.</p>
    
    <h3>What to Do Instead</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Ask 3-5 clarifying questions before offering any suggestions</li>
      <li>Reflect what you're hearing rather than solving</li>
      <li>Trust that the person has their own answers (your job: help them access those answers)</li>
      <li>If you DO offer advice, ask permission first: "Would it be helpful if I shared what's worked for others?"</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Practice Phrase</h3>
    <p>"It sounds like you're feeling torn about this. Tell me more about both sides."</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="U: Understand Your Client's Motivations">
    <h3>What It Means</h3>
    <p>Change is driven by the person's values, goals, and priorities — not yours. Your job is to deeply understand what matters to THEM, not what you think should matter.</p>
    
    <h3>Key Questions</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>"Why does this matter to you?"</li>
      <li>"What would be different in your life if you made this change?"</li>
      <li>"On a scale of 0-10, how important is this goal to you? What makes it a [number] and not lower?"</li>
      <li>"If you don't make this change, what might happen down the line?"</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>The Insight</h3>
    <p>People change when THEY want to, for THEIR reasons. External motivators ("Your doctor says you should") rarely sustain behavior long-term. Intrinsic motivators ("I want to be able to play with my grandkids") are far more powerful.</p>
    
    <h3>Practice Phrase</h3>
    <p>"What matters most to you about this?"</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="L: Listen with Empathy">
    <h3>What It Means</h3>
    <p>Empathic listening (covered in Lesson 2 and 3) is the foundation of MI. You're not listening to extract information or identify problems to solve — you're listening to understand the person's internal experience.</p>
    
    <h3>How It Shows Up</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Reflective statements outnumber questions (2:1 ratio)</li>
      <li>You reflect emotion, not just content: "You sound frustrated"</li>
      <li>You validate ambivalence: "Part of you wants to change, and part of you is scared"</li>
      <li>You don't rush to "fix" difficult emotions</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Why It Works</h3>
    <p>When people feel heard and understood, their defensiveness drops. The ventral vagal (safety) system activates, allowing them to access higher-order thinking and self-reflection. Empathy is the neurobiological key that unlocks change.</p>
    
    <h3>Practice Phrase</h3>
    <p>"It sounds like this is really hard for you — you're feeling pulled in two different directions."</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="E: Empower Your Client">
    <h3>What It Means</h3>
    <p>Support autonomy. Communicate confidence in their capacity to change. Reinforce that THEY are the expert on their own life, and THEY get to decide what happens next.</p>
    
    <h3>How to Empower</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Affirm strengths and past successes: "You've made hard changes before — you know how to do this"</li>
      <li>Offer choice: "What feels most doable for you?"</li>
      <li>Support self-efficacy: "You're the person who gets to decide what makes sense for your life"</li>
      <li>Avoid prescribing: "Here's what I think you should do"</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Why It Works</h3>
    <p>Self-Determination Theory shows that autonomy support (vs. control) activates intrinsic motivation. When people feel they're choosing freely, they're more committed. When they feel pressured, they resist.</p>
    
    <h3>Practice Phrase</h3>
    <p>"You're the expert on your life. What do you think would work best for you?"</p>
  </div>
</SlideNavigation>

## Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk: The Language of Motivation

One of MI's most powerful insights: the language people use predicts whether they'll change.

**Change talk** = Language that favors change  
**Sustain talk** = Language that favors staying the same

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <div>
    <h3>Change Talk (Predicts Behavior Change)</h3>
    <p><strong>Desire:</strong> "I want to..." "I wish I could..."</p>
    <p><strong>Ability:</strong> "I could..." "I think I can..."</p>
    <p><strong>Reasons:</strong> "If I did this, I'd feel better..." "This would help me..."</p>
    <p><strong>Need:</strong> "I need to..." "I have to..."</p>
    <p><strong>Commitment:</strong> "I will..." "I'm going to..."</p>
    <p><strong>Taking steps:</strong> "I've started..." "I signed up for..."</p>
    
    <h4>Your job when you hear change talk:</h4>
    <p><strong>Evoke more of it.</strong> Reflect it back, ask about it, amplify it.</p>
    <p>Client: "I guess I could try meditating in the mornings."<br />
    You: "You're thinking meditation in the morning might be doable. What makes that feel possible?"</p>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <h3>Sustain Talk (Predicts Staying the Same)</h3>
    <p><strong>Desire to stay the same:</strong> "I don't want to give this up..."</p>
    <p><strong>Ability concerns:</strong> "I can't..." "It's too hard..."</p>
    <p><strong>Reasons not to change:</strong> "But if I quit, I'll miss..." "It helps me cope..."</p>
    <p><strong>Need to stay the same:</strong> "I need this to manage stress..."</p>
    <p><strong>Commitment to status quo:</strong> "I'm not going to..." "I won't..."</p>
    
    <h4>Your job when you hear sustain talk:</h4>
    <p><strong>Reflect it without arguing.</strong> Don't try to convince them otherwise — that makes them dig in harder.</p>
    <p>Client: "I don't think I can give up drinking entirely."<br />
    You (NOT): "Sure you can! You just need willpower!"<br />
    You (MI): "Giving it up entirely feels too hard right now. What might feel more manageable?"</p>
  </div>
</InsightGrid>

**The paradox:** When you argue FOR change, the person argues AGAINST it. When you accept their ambivalence and explore both sides, they often talk themselves into change.

## Working with Ambivalence: The MI Superpower

Ambivalence — feeling two ways about something — is NORMAL. It's not a problem to solve; it's the terrain to explore.

**Example:**
- "Part of me wants to exercise because I know I'll feel better. Part of me just wants to stay on the couch because I'm exhausted."

Most helpers pick a side: "You should exercise! It'll give you energy!" This triggers defensiveness: "But I'm so tired..."

**MI approach:** Honor BOTH sides.

<EnhancedAccordion>
  <div title="Step 1: Reflect the Ambivalence" emoji="⚖️">
    <p>"It sounds like you're feeling torn — one part of you knows exercise would help, and another part is just exhausted and wants rest. Did I get that right?"</p>
    
    <p><strong>Why this works:</strong> You're validating both sides instead of arguing for one. This reduces defensiveness and creates space for honest exploration.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Step 2: Explore Both Sides Equally" emoji="🔍">
    <p>"Tell me about the exhausted part. What's that about?"</p>
    <p>(Listen, reflect.)</p>
    <p>"And tell me about the part that knows exercise would help. What's important about that?"</p>
    <p>(Listen, reflect.)</p>
    
    <p><strong>Why this works:</strong> You're not pushing them toward change; you're helping them understand their own internal conflict. Self-awareness often precedes self-directed change.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Step 3: Ask a Decisional Balance Question" emoji="⚖️">
    <p>"So on one hand, rest feels necessary right now. On the other hand, you know movement might actually give you energy. What feels true for you?"</p>
    
    <p><strong>Why this works:</strong> You're inviting them to sit with the tension and make their own choice. Often, articulating both sides out loud helps people see which side is stronger.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Step 4: Support Autonomy, Whatever They Choose" emoji="🤝">
    <p>"Whatever you decide is okay. You know yourself best."</p>
    
    <p><strong>Why this works:</strong> Paradoxically, giving someone permission NOT to change often frees them to choose change. When they don't feel pressured, resistance drops.</p>
  </div>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Eliciting Change Talk: Strategic Questions

    <p><strong>Question:</strong> "On a scale of 0-10, how important is this goal to you?"</p>

    <p><strong>Follow-up:</strong> "What makes it a 6 and not a 2?"</p>
    <p>(This forces them to articulate reasons it DOES matter — change talk!)</p>
    <p><strong>Then:</strong> "What would it take to move from a 6 to an 8?"</p>
    <p>(Evokes thinking about what might increase motivation.)</p>

  </div>
  <div slot="back">
    <p><strong>Question:</strong> "On a scale of 0-10, how confident are you that you could make this change if you decided to?"</p>
    <p>They say: "Maybe a 4."</p>
    <p><strong>Follow-up:</strong> "What makes it a 4 and not a 0?"</p>
    <p>(They identify existing strengths and resources — empowering!)</p>
    <p><strong>Then:</strong> "What would help move it from a 4 to a 7?"</p>
    <p>(Identifies barriers and potential supports.)</p>

    <p><strong>Follow-up:</strong> "How might those same strategies apply here?"</p>

## When MI Is Appropriate and When to Refer

MI and MI-informed techniques are powerful tools for non-clinical coaching contexts. But there are limits.

**Appropriate for MI-informed coaching:**
- Ambivalence about goal pursuit (e.g., "I want to exercise but I keep putting it off")
- Behavior change in non-clinical populations (sleep, nutrition, movement, productivity)
- Values clarification and motivation exploration
- Supporting engagement with other resources (e.g., "I know therapy might help but I'm not sure I want to go")

**Requires clinical MI (refer to professional):**
- Substance use disorder treatment (alcohol, drugs)
- Eating disorder treatment
- Severe mental health conditions (active suicidal ideation, psychosis, severe depression requiring symptom management)
- Medication adherence for psychiatric conditions (this overlaps with clinical treatment planning)

<Callout type="warning" title="Scope Boundary">
You can use MI-informed communication skills to support someone exploring whether to seek therapy. You cannot use MI to treat addiction, eating disorders, or psychiatric symptoms. If someone discloses substance dependence or active mental health crisis, your role is warm referral — not intervention.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- The RULE framework (Resist righting reflex, Understand motivations, Listen with empathy, Empower autonomy) provides a foundation for MI-informed coaching that respects client autonomy and evokes intrinsic motivation
- Change talk (language favoring change) predicts behavior change; sustain talk (language favoring status quo) predicts no change — your job is to evoke change talk by reflecting it and asking curious questions, not by arguing for change
- Ambivalence is normal and expected; when you honor both sides and explore the internal conflict without pushing toward a resolution, people often talk themselves into change
- MI-informed skills are appropriate for non-clinical coaching contexts (goal ambivalence, behavior change support); clinical MI for substance use disorders, eating disorders, and psychiatric treatment requires specialized training and licensure
