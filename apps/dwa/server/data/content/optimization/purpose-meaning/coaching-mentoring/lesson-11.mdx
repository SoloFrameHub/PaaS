# Crisis Intervention and Mental Health Awareness

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize warning signs of mental health crises requiring professional intervention
- Learn safe messaging principles for discussing suicide, self-harm, and mental health crises
- Practice making warm referrals without abandoning the person in distress
- Understand the critical difference between crisis recognition (coaching scope) and crisis intervention (clinical scope)

## Research Foundation

Mental health crises — including suicidal ideation, acute psychotic episodes, severe panic attacks, and active self-harm — require immediate professional intervention, not coaching. However, non-clinical supporters regularly encounter crisis disclosures and must be prepared to respond safely. Research on suicide prevention messaging (JED Foundation, VA/DOD guidelines) shows that HOW helpers respond matters enormously: safe messaging promotes help-seeking and reduces risk, while harmful messaging (dramatizing, oversimplifying, avoiding direct questions) can increase risk. Mental Health First Aid (MHFA) demonstrates that non-clinicians can be trained in crisis recognition and referral, though evidence for impact on recipient mental health outcomes is limited. This lesson teaches recognition and warm referral — NOT clinical intervention.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (for safe messaging principles); MODERATE (for MHFA training improving helper confidence and knowledge); WEAK (for MHFA improving recipient outcomes)

## The Critical Distinction: Recognition vs. Intervention

**What coaches CAN and MUST do:**
- Recognize warning signs of crisis
- Ask directly about safety using safe messaging language
- Provide crisis resources (988 Lifeline, local crisis lines)
- Make warm referrals to appropriate clinical care
- Follow up to ensure connection was made
- Support the person emotionally without attempting clinical treatment

**What coaches CANNOT do (clinical territory):**
- Assess suicide risk using clinical tools
- Provide crisis intervention or safety planning
- Make decisions about hospitalization
- Diagnose mental health conditions driving the crisis
- Treat acute psychiatric symptoms
- Take responsibility for keeping someone alive (that's clinical work)

<Callout type="warning" title="Life-Safety Boundary">
If someone discloses active suicidal ideation with plan and intent, or is actively self-harming, your role is to connect them to emergency resources IMMEDIATELY (988, 911, local crisis team) and stay with them until help arrives. Do NOT attempt to "coach them through" a psychiatric crisis. This is not within your scope, training, or legal authority.
</Callout>

## Recognizing Crisis Warning Signs

<SlideNavigation>
  <div title="Suicidal Ideation Warning Signs">
    <h3>What to Watch For</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Direct statements:</strong> "I don't want to be here anymore," "I wish I were dead," "Everyone would be better off without me"</li>
      <li><strong>Talking about methods:</strong> "I've been thinking about how I would do it," researching suicide methods</li>
      <li><strong>Giving things away:</strong> Suddenly giving away possessions, saying goodbye to people</li>
      <li><strong>Withdrawal and isolation:</strong> Pulling away from relationships, stopping activities they used to enjoy</li>
      <li><strong>Hopelessness and trapped feelings:</strong> "There's no way out," "Nothing will ever get better," "I'm a burden"</li>
      <li><strong>Dramatic mood changes:</strong> Sudden calmness after period of depression (may indicate decision has been made)</li>
      <li><strong>Increased substance use:</strong> Self-medicating emotional pain</li>
      <li><strong>Reckless behavior:</strong> Taking dangerous risks as if life doesn't matter</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Risk Levels</h3>
    <p><strong>Higher risk:</strong> Specific plan + access to means + timeline + prior attempt history<br />
    <strong>Lower risk (but still serious):</strong> General thoughts without plan or intent</p>
    
    <h3>Your Response</h3>
    <p>ANY suicidal ideation requires response — even "low risk" ideation. Ask directly, provide resources, ensure connection to professional care.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Self-Harm Warning Signs">
    <h3>What to Watch For</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Unexplained cuts, burns, or bruises (especially in patterns)</li>
      <li>Wearing long sleeves/pants in warm weather to cover injuries</li>
      <li>Frequent "accidents" or injuries</li>
      <li>Possession of sharp objects (razors, knives) for unclear reasons</li>
      <li>Talking about self-harm, even "casually" or as jokes</li>
      <li>Pulling out hair, picking skin compulsively</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Important Distinction</h3>
    <p><strong>Self-harm ≠ suicidal behavior</strong> (though they can co-occur). Many people who self-harm are NOT trying to die — they're using pain to regulate overwhelming emotions. However, self-harm still requires professional intervention.</p>
    
    <h3>Your Response</h3>
    <p>Do NOT shame, lecture, or demand they stop. Self-harm is a coping mechanism (unhealthy, but functional for them). Your job: express concern, refer to therapist specializing in self-harm (often DBT-trained), and provide crisis resources.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Psychosis Warning Signs">
    <h3>What to Watch For</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Delusions:</strong> Fixed false beliefs (paranoia, grandiosity, believing they're being controlled)</li>
      <li><strong>Hallucinations:</strong> Seeing or hearing things that aren't there</li>
      <li><strong>Disorganized speech:</strong> Jumping between unrelated topics, making no logical sense</li>
      <li><strong>Extreme agitation or catatonia:</strong> Cannot sit still OR completely immobile</li>
      <li><strong>Loss of functioning:</strong> Cannot care for basic needs (eating, hygiene, safety)</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Your Response</h3>
    <p>Acute psychosis requires psychiatric assessment — often hospitalization. If someone is actively psychotic and at risk to self or others, call 911 or mobile crisis team. Do NOT argue with delusions or try to "reality-test" them. Remain calm, ensure safety, get professional help.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Severe Panic/Anxiety Warning Signs">
    <h3>What to Watch For</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Difficulty breathing, chest pain, feeling like they're dying</li>
      <li>Dizziness, shaking, nausea</li>
      <li>Feeling detached from reality (derealization/depersonalization)</li>
      <li>Terror without clear trigger</li>
      <li>Panic attacks multiple times per day, daily for weeks</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Your Response</h3>
    <p>Severe panic attacks can mimic heart attacks — if it's a first episode or they're in significant physical distress, consider medical evaluation (could be cardiac issue). For known panic: ground them, slow breathing, reassure it will pass, refer to therapist. If panic is pervasive and disabling (can't work, leave house), psychiatric evaluation needed.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Substance Use Crisis Warning Signs">
    <h3>What to Watch For</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Using substances to cope with every difficult emotion</li>
      <li>Increasing tolerance (need more to get same effect)</li>
      <li>Withdrawal symptoms when not using</li>
      <li>Inability to stop despite wanting to</li>
      <li>Neglecting responsibilities, relationships, health</li>
      <li>Dangerous behavior while intoxicated</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Your Response</h3>
    <p>Substance use disorder is a clinical condition requiring specialized treatment (not coaching). Withdrawal from alcohol or benzos can be medically dangerous — requires medical supervision. Refer to addiction counselor, treatment program, or psychiatrist. Use MI-informed approach to explore readiness for treatment, but do NOT attempt to treat addiction yourself.</p>
  </div>
</SlideNavigation>

## Safe Messaging: How to Talk About Suicide

Research shows that HOW you talk about suicide matters. Certain language patterns increase risk; others promote help-seeking.

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <div>
    <h3>❌ Harmful Messaging (Avoid)</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Dramatizing or sensationalizing:</strong> "Suicide is selfish," "Think of how devastated everyone would be"</li>
      <li><strong>Oversimplifying:</strong> "Just think positive!" "Suicide is a permanent solution to a temporary problem"</li>
      <li><strong>Avoiding direct language:</strong> "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" (vague) vs. "Are you thinking about suicide?" (clear)</li>
      <li><strong>Making promises you can't keep:</strong> "I won't tell anyone" (you may need to break confidentiality for safety)</li>
      <li><strong>Expressing shock or judgment:</strong> "How could you think that?!" "That's crazy!"</li>
      <li><strong>Arguing them out of it:</strong> "You have so much to live for!" (invalidates their pain)</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <h3>✅ Safe Messaging (Use)</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Use direct, clear language:</strong> "Are you thinking about suicide?" (research shows asking does NOT plant the idea)</li>
      <li><strong>Listen without judgment:</strong> "I'm glad you told me. That must be incredibly hard."</li>
      <li><strong>Validate pain without validating suicide:</strong> "I hear that you're in a lot of pain. I want to help you get support."</li>
      <li><strong>Promote help-seeking:</strong> "Have you talked to a therapist or doctor about this? I want to help you connect with someone who can support you."</li>
      <li><strong>Provide specific resources:</strong> "I'm going to give you the number for the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline. They're available 24/7. Can we call together?"</li>
      <li><strong>Stay present:</strong> "I'm here with you right now. You're not alone in this."</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</InsightGrid>

**Key research finding:** Asking directly about suicide does NOT increase risk. Avoiding the topic does NOT protect someone. Direct questions with empathic listening open the door to help.

## The Warm Referral: How to Connect Someone to Care

A "warm referral" means actively helping someone connect to professional resources rather than just handing them a phone number and hoping they follow through.

<StepByStep title="Making a Warm Referral">
  <div step="1">
    <h3>Express Concern Clearly</h3>
    <p>"I'm really concerned about what you just shared. This is more than I can support you with — you need to talk to a mental health professional. I care about you, and I want to make sure you get the right help."</p>
  </div>
  
  <div step="2">
    <h3>Provide Specific Resources</h3>
    <p>Don't say: "You should see a therapist."<br />
    Do say: "Here are three options: (1) 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988, 24/7. (2) [Local crisis center name and number]. (3) Your insurance website has a therapist directory — I can help you look right now if that would help."</p>
  </div>
  
  <div step="3">
    <h3>Offer to Help Them Connect</h3>
    <p>"Would it help if we called together right now? I can sit with you while you make the call."</p>
    <p>OR: "Can I follow up with you tomorrow to see if you were able to connect?"</p>
  </div>
  
  <div step="4">
    <h3>Clarify Confidentiality Limits</h3>
    <p>"I want you to know that if I believe you're in immediate danger, I may need to contact emergency services or let someone who can help know. I don't want to do that without talking to you first, but I also can't keep you safe by myself. My priority is making sure you get help."</p>
  </div>
  
  <div step="5">
    <h3>Don't Disappear</h3>
    <p>Warm referral ≠ handing off and walking away. Follow up: "I know you have an appointment with a therapist next week. How did that go? I'm still here to support you, even though you're also working with a professional now."</p>
  </div>
</StepByStep>

**Common fear:** "If I refer them, they'll think I'm abandoning them or that I can't handle them."

**Reality:** Most people feel RELIEF when you acknowledge the seriousness of their situation and help them access appropriate care. You're not abandoning them — you're ensuring they get the level of support they need.

## Crisis Resources to Know

<EnhancedAccordion>
  <div title="988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline" emoji="📞">
    <h3>What it is</h3>
    <p>24/7 national hotline for anyone in suicidal crisis or emotional distress. Launched July 2022 as easy-to-remember number (like 911).</p>
    
    <h3>How to access</h3>
    <p>Call or text **988** from anywhere in the US. Also available via chat at **988lifeline.org**</p>
    
    <h3>Who answers</h3>
    <p>Trained crisis counselors who provide support, resources, and can dispatch mobile crisis teams if needed.</p>
    
    <h3>When to use</h3>
    <p>Suicidal thoughts, emotional crisis, supporting someone in crisis</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Crisis Text Line" emoji="💬">
    <h3>What it is</h3>
    <p>24/7 text-based crisis support for people who prefer texting over calling.</p>
    
    <h3>How to access</h3>
    <p>Text **HELLO** to **741741** from anywhere in the US</p>
    
    <h3>Who answers</h3>
    <p>Trained crisis counselors</p>
    
    <h3>When to use</h3>
    <p>Any mental health crisis, especially for people who are more comfortable texting</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="SAMHSA National Helpline" emoji="🏥">
    <h3>What it is</h3>
    <p>24/7 treatment referral and information service for mental health and substance use disorders. NOT a crisis line.</p>
    
    <h3>How to access</h3>
    <p>Call **1-800-662-HELP (4357)**</p>
    
    <h3>What they provide</h3>
    <p>Referrals to local treatment facilities, support groups, community organizations</p>
    
    <h3>When to use</h3>
    <p>Finding therapists, treatment programs, support groups (not for immediate crisis)</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="911 or Local Emergency Services" emoji="🚨">
    <h3>When to call</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Person has actively attempted suicide or self-harm</li>
      <li>Person is threatening immediate harm to self or others</li>
      <li>Person is acutely psychotic and cannot care for themselves</li>
      <li>Person is intoxicated and in physical danger</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>What to know</h3>
    <p>Police response to mental health crises has risks, especially for BIPOC individuals. If available in your area, request mobile crisis team or Crisis Intervention Team (CIT)-trained officers. Stay with the person if safe to do so.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Mobile Crisis Teams" emoji="🚑">
    <h3>What they are</h3>
    <p>Mental health professionals (not police) who respond to psychiatric crises. Not available everywhere but expanding.</p>
    
    <h3>How to access</h3>
    <p>Google "[your city/county] mobile crisis team" or ask local crisis line. Some areas have numbers like **988 Option 1** for mobile dispatch.</p>
    
    <h3>When to use</h3>
    <p>Alternative to 911 for psychiatric crises when physical safety is not immediately threatened</p>
  </div>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## After the Crisis: Self-Care for Helpers

Encountering someone in crisis is emotionally heavy work. You need to care for yourself afterward.

<Callout type="insight" title="Vicarious Trauma and Compassion Fatigue">
Hearing about someone's suicidal thoughts, self-harm, or trauma can affect you — even if you're "just" the coach or supporter. This is normal. Signs of compassion fatigue: emotional numbness, intrusive thoughts about the person's crisis, difficulty sleeping, feeling hopeless about helping anyone. If you experience these, seek your own support: supervision, therapy, peer consultation.
</Callout>

**Post-crisis debrief checklist:**
- [ ] Did I connect the person to appropriate resources?
- [ ] Do I need to follow up with them? When?
- [ ] How am I feeling after this? Do I need support?
- [ ] Is there anything I would do differently next time?
- [ ] Do I need to consult with a supervisor or peer about this situation?

## Key Takeaways

- Coaches must recognize mental health crises (suicidal ideation, self-harm, psychosis, severe panic, substance crises) but refer for intervention — your scope is recognition and warm referral, not clinical crisis intervention or risk assessment
- Safe messaging for suicide: use direct, clear language ("Are you thinking about suicide?"), listen without judgment, validate pain without validating suicide, promote help-seeking, and provide specific resources — asking directly does NOT increase risk
- A warm referral means actively helping someone connect to care (offering to call together, following up) rather than just handing them a phone number — this ensures connection and communicates you're not abandoning them
- Crisis work impacts helpers: watch for compassion fatigue and vicarious trauma, seek supervision/support after encountering crises, and debrief to process your own emotional response

## Safety Note

⚠️ **When to Seek Professional Help:** If someone discloses active suicidal ideation, ongoing self-harm, psychotic symptoms, or substance use crisis, they need licensed clinical care immediately. Your role: provide crisis resources (988 Lifeline, local crisis lines, 911 if imminent danger), make warm referrals, and follow up to ensure connection. Do NOT attempt to provide crisis intervention — that requires clinical training and legal authority you do not have as a non-clinical coach.
