# Strengths-Based Coaching Approaches

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the evidence base for strengths-based interventions vs. deficit-focused approaches
- Learn to identify signature strengths using validated frameworks (VIA Character Strengths, CliftonStrengths)
- Practice strengths-spotting and affirmation techniques that build self-efficacy
- Apply strengths-based goal setting and problem-solving strategies

## Research Foundation

Strengths-based approaches — focusing on identifying and amplifying existing strengths rather than solely fixing weaknesses — have robust evidence from positive psychology research. Studies show that using signature strengths daily increases wellbeing, life satisfaction, and reduces depression symptoms (moderate effect sizes). Strengths-based interventions predict goal attainment, work performance, and engagement more reliably than deficit-correction approaches. The VIA Character Strengths framework (Peterson & Seligman) provides 24 scientifically validated character strengths across 6 virtue categories. Importantly, strengths-based coaching is appropriate for non-clinical populations pursuing growth and performance; it does not replace clinical treatment for mental health conditions.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (for strengths interventions improving wellbeing and performance); MODERATE (for strengths-based approaches in coaching contexts)

## The Case for Strengths: Why Fixing Weaknesses Isn't Enough

Traditional development models focus on identifying deficits and fixing them: "What's wrong with you? Let's fix it."

**The problem:** Deficit-focused approaches:
- Activate shame and defensiveness
- Focus energy on bringing weaknesses to mediocrity (expensive effort, minimal return)
- Miss opportunities to amplify existing excellence
- Undermine intrinsic motivation

**Strengths-based alternative:** "What's already working? How can we do more of that?"

<Callout type="insight" title="Research Finding">
Gallup research across millions of employees found that people who use their strengths daily are 6x more likely to be engaged at work and 3x more likely to report excellent quality of life. Yet most development plans focus 80% on weaknesses and 20% on strengths — exactly backward from what the evidence supports.
</Callout>

**This doesn't mean ignore weaknesses.** It means: Build from strengths first. Address weaknesses only when they're true barriers.

## Identifying Strengths: Three Approaches

<SlideNavigation>
  <div title="Approach 1: Validated Assessments">
    <h3>VIA Character Strengths Survey</h3>
    <p><strong>What it is:</strong> Free 120-item assessment identifying your top "signature strengths" from the 24 VIA strengths</p>
    <p><strong>Where:</strong> VIACharacter.org</p>
    <p><strong>Use in coaching:</strong> Have clients take it between sessions, then explore their top 5 strengths and how to use them more intentionally</p>
    
    <h3>CliftonStrengths (formerly StrengthsFinder)</h3>
    <p><strong>What it is:</strong> Paid assessment identifying top talents from 34 talent themes, focused on workplace performance</p>
    <p><strong>Where:</strong> Gallup CliftonStrengths</p>
    <p><strong>Use in coaching:</strong> Particularly useful for career/performance coaching; provides detailed reports on how to leverage strengths</p>
    
    <h3>Note on Assessments</h3>
    <p>Assessments are helpful starting points, but they're not gospel. Use them to spark reflection, not to box people into categories.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Approach 2: Strengths-Spotting in Conversation">
    <h3>What to Listen For</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Energy:</strong> What lights them up when they talk about it?</li>
      <li><strong>Excellence:</strong> What do they do exceptionally well, even effortlessly?</li>
      <li><strong>Rapid learning:</strong> What skills did they pick up quickly?</li>
      <li><strong>Flow states:</strong> What activities make them lose track of time?</li>
      <li><strong>Values-driven behavior:</strong> What do they do consistently even without external reward?</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Strengths-Spotting Questions</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>"Tell me about a time you felt most alive and engaged. What were you doing?"</li>
      <li>"What do people most often come to you for help with?"</li>
      <li>"When you look back at your biggest accomplishments, what strengths were you using?"</li>
      <li>"What activities give you energy vs. drain your energy?"</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Reflect It Back</h3>
    <p>"I'm noticing that every time you talk about solving complex problems, you light up. That sounds like a real strength — the ability to analyze systems and find creative solutions. Does that resonate?"</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Approach 3: Past Successes Mining">
    <h3>The Exercise</h3>
    <p>Ask the client to identify 3-5 moments when they felt most successful, proud, or effective. For each:</p>
    <ol>
      <li>What was the situation?</li>
      <li>What did you do?</li>
      <li>What strengths were you using? (Name them specifically)</li>
      <li>What was the outcome?</li>
    </ol>
    
    <h3>Look for Patterns</h3>
    <p>Which strengths show up repeatedly across multiple successes? Those are likely signature strengths — reliable, energizing, and effective.</p>
    
    <h3>Example</h3>
    <p>Client shares 3 success stories:<br />
    1. Organized a community fundraiser (leadership, teamwork)<br />
    2. Helped a struggling colleague learn new software (kindness, patience, teaching)<br />
    3. Solved a recurring technical problem no one else could fix (creativity, perseverance, analytical thinking)</p>
    
    <p>Pattern: This person thrives when solving problems for others (kindness + analytical thinking) and leading collaborative efforts. Coaching insight: Structure goals around impact on others, not just personal achievement.</p>
  </div>
</SlideNavigation>

## Strengths-Based Goal Setting

Once you've identified strengths, the question becomes: How do we leverage them to achieve goals?

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <div>
    <h3>Traditional Goal-Setting</h3>
    <p>"I want to improve my public speaking (weakness). I'll take a course and practice."</p>
    <p><strong>Problem:</strong> If public speaking drains you and doesn't align with your strengths, improvement will be slow, effortful, and unsustainable.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <h3>Strengths-Based Goal-Setting</h3>
    <p>"I want to increase my influence at work. My strengths are writing and one-on-one connection. Instead of forcing myself to do big presentations, what if I write compelling proposals and build relationships through individual conversations?"</p>
    <p><strong>Benefit:</strong> You're achieving the same outcome (influence) but through a path aligned with your strengths — faster, more energizing, more sustainable.</p>
  </div>
</InsightGrid>

**The framework:**

1. **Clarify the outcome:** What do you actually want to achieve?
2. **Identify your top strengths:** What are you naturally good at and energized by?
3. **Design a strengths-aligned path:** How can you reach that outcome BY USING your strengths rather than fighting your weaknesses?

## Strengths Overuse: When Strengths Become Liabilities

**Important nuance:** Every strength, when overused or applied in the wrong context, can become a weakness.

<Callout type="warning" title="When Strengths Focus Isn't Appropriate">

Strengths-based coaching works for **non-clinical populations seeking growth, performance enhancement, or life satisfaction**. It is NOT appropriate as a primary intervention for:

- **Clinical depression:** When anhedonia/low mood is present, focusing only on strengths may bypass needed treatment. Behavioral activation and cognitive therapy from licensed professionals are evidence-based; strengths coaching alone is not.
- **Active crisis or severe distress:** Strengths language can feel invalidating when someone is overwhelmed. Clinical stabilization comes first.
- **Skill-deficit situations requiring remediation:** If someone lacks foundational competencies for their role, skill-building (not just strengths amplification) may be needed.

**When to use strengths-based coaching:** With functioning individuals who have adequate baseline wellbeing and want to optimize, differentiate, or find meaning in work/life.

**When to refer:** Client presents with persistent low mood, expresses hopelessness, shows signs of clinical depression/anxiety, or reports that "nothing feels enjoyable anymore" (anhedonia).

</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Strengths-based approaches — focusing on amplifying existing strengths rather than solely fixing weaknesses — have strong evidence for improving wellbeing, performance, and goal attainment (Gallup research shows people using strengths daily are 6x more engaged)
- The VIA Character Strengths framework provides 24 scientifically validated strengths across 6 virtue categories; identifying signature strengths (through assessments, strengths-spotting, or past successes mining) enables coaches to design goals aligned with natural talents
- Strengths-based goal-setting asks: How can you achieve this outcome BY USING your strengths rather than fighting your weaknesses? Same goal, more energizing and sustainable path
- Every strength can become a liability when overused — perseverance becomes stubbornness, kindness becomes self-neglect, prudence becomes paralysis; coaches help clients use strengths strategically rather than reflexively
