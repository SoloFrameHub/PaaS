# Ethical Considerations and Professional Standards

## Learning Objectives

- Understand confidentiality boundaries and when to break confidentiality
- Recognize dual relationship risks and how to manage boundaries
- Learn scope of practice limits and referral obligations
- Explore professional standards from ICF and SAMHSA frameworks

## Research Foundation

Ethics in coaching and mentoring center on protecting clients from harm while maximizing benefit. Core ethical principles include: beneficence (do good), non-maleficence (do no harm), autonomy (respect client's self-determination), justice (treat people fairly), and fidelity (keep commitments). The International Coaching Federation (ICF) Code of Ethics and SAMHSA Peer Support Ethics provide frameworks for non-clinical helpers. Key ethical risks include: boundary violations (dual relationships, inappropriate intimacy), confidentiality breaches, practicing outside scope of competence, and failing to refer when clinical needs exceed coaching capacity.

## The Four Pillars of Coaching Ethics

<SlideNavigation>
  <div title="1. Confidentiality (with limits)">
    <h3>The Principle</h3>
    <p>What's shared in coaching stays confidential — except when safety is at risk.</p>
    
    <h3>When Confidentiality MUST Be Broken</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Imminent danger to self:</strong> Active suicidal ideation with plan/intent</li>
      <li><strong>Imminent danger to others:</strong> Credible threat to harm another person</li>
      <li><strong>Child/elder abuse:</strong> Mandated reporting in most jurisdictions</li>
      <li><strong>Court order/subpoena:</strong> Legal requirement to disclose</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>How to Communicate Limits Upfront</h3>
    <p>"What we discuss is confidential with a few exceptions: if I believe you're in immediate danger to yourself or someone else, or if there's abuse of a child or vulnerable adult, I'm ethically and legally required to connect you with appropriate help."</p>
    
    <h3>Gray Areas</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Employer-sponsored coaching:</strong> Clarify what (if anything) is reported to employer. Best practice: only aggregate data, never individual content.</li>
      <li><strong>Group coaching:</strong> You can't guarantee others' confidentiality — set norms but acknowledge risk.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  
  <div title="2. Scope of Practice">
    <h3>The Principle</h3>
    <p>Only work within your training, competence, and legal authority.</p>
    
    <h3>What's Outside Coaching Scope</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Diagnosing mental health conditions</li>
      <li>Providing psychotherapy or clinical treatment</li>
      <li>Prescribing or recommending medications</li>
      <li>Treating addiction, eating disorders, or trauma</li>
      <li>Crisis intervention (you can recognize and refer, not intervene)</li>
      <li>Legal or financial advice (unless separately licensed)</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>When in Doubt</h3>
    <p>Ask yourself: "Did my training equip me for this? Do I have legal authority? If something goes wrong, would I be liable?" If any answer is no or uncertain, refer.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="3. Dual Relationships">
    <h3>What They Are</h3>
    <p>When you have multiple roles with a client: coach + friend, coach + employer, coach + romantic interest.</p>
    
    <h3>Why They're Risky</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Power imbalances: The coaching role inherently has power; adding another role (boss, friend, lover) amplifies it</li>
      <li>Conflicting interests: What's best for the coaching relationship may conflict with the other relationship</li>
      <li>Exploitation risk: Vulnerable clients may agree to things they wouldn't otherwise</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Examples of Problematic Dual Relationships</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Coaching your direct report (you're also their boss)</li>
      <li>Dating a current or recent client</li>
      <li>Coaching a family member</li>
      <li>Asking a client to invest in your business</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>When Dual Relationships Are Unavoidable</h3>
    <p>Small communities, rural areas: Sometimes you can't avoid knowing clients in other contexts. Minimize harm by: naming it explicitly, setting clear boundaries, getting supervision, documenting your reasoning.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="4. Informed Consent">
    <h3>What It Is</h3>
    <p>Ensuring the client understands what coaching is, what it isn't, what to expect, and agrees voluntarily.</p>
    
    <h3>Elements of Informed Consent</h3>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Nature of coaching:</strong> "Coaching is not therapy. We focus on goals, growth, and performance — not treating mental health conditions."</li>
      <li><strong>Confidentiality limits:</strong> (see Pillar 1)</li>
      <li><strong>Your qualifications:</strong> "I'm a certified coach through [X], with training in [Y]. I'm not a licensed therapist."</li>
      <li><strong>Structure and fees:</strong> Session length, frequency, cost, cancellation policy</li>
      <li><strong>Right to terminate:</strong> "You can end coaching at any time, no questions asked."</li>
    </ul>
    
    <h3>Documentation</h3>
    <p>Ideally in writing (email or signed agreement). If verbal, document in session notes.</p>
  </div>
</SlideNavigation>

## Professional Standards: ICF vs. SAMHSA

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <div>
    <h3>ICF Code of Ethics (Coaching)</h3>
    <p><strong>Core competencies:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Demonstrates ethical practice</li>
      <li>Embodies a coaching mindset</li>
      <li>Establishes and maintains agreements</li>
      <li>Cultivates trust and safety</li>
      <li>Maintains presence</li>
      <li>Listens actively</li>
      <li>Evokes awareness</li>
      <li>Facilitates client growth</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Key principle:</strong> Client autonomy and self-directedness</p>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <h3>SAMHSA Ethics (Peer Support)</h3>
    <p><strong>Core values:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Voluntary participation</li>
      <li>Hopeful and optimistic</li>
      <li>Non-judgmental and accepting</li>
      <li>Mutual and reciprocal</li>
      <li>Equally shared power</li>
      <li>Strengths-focused</li>
      <li>Transparent and honest</li>
      <li>Person-driven and self-determined</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Key principle:</strong> Shared lived experience as credential</p>
  </div>
</InsightGrid>

Both frameworks emphasize: respect for autonomy, non-judgment, clear boundaries, and referral when needs exceed scope.

## When to Seek Supervision or Consultation

<EnhancedAccordion>
  <div title="You're uncertain about scope" emoji="❓">
    <p>"Is this within my competence? Should I refer?"</p>
    <p><strong>Action:</strong> Consult supervisor, peer, or more experienced coach before next session.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Boundary feels blurry" emoji="🌫️">
    <p>"Am I getting too personally involved? Is this client depending on me in an unhealthy way?"</p>
    <p><strong>Action:</strong> Supervision helps you see patterns you're too close to notice.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="You're triggered or reactive" emoji="😤">
    <p>"This client pushes my buttons. I find myself getting defensive/frustrated/overly invested."</p>
    <p><strong>Action:</strong> Supervision or personal therapy to process your reactions.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div title="Ethical dilemma with no clear answer" emoji="⚖️">
    <p>"Two ethical principles conflict. I'm not sure what's right here."</p>
    <p><strong>Action:</strong> Consultation helps you think through consequences and make an informed decision.</p>
  </div>
</EnhancedAccordion>

**Supervision is not weakness — it's professional excellence.** The best coaches seek regular consultation.

## Key Takeaways

- Confidentiality is foundational but has limits: must break it for imminent danger to self/others, child/elder abuse, or court order — communicate these limits explicitly in informed consent
- Scope of practice violations (diagnosing, providing therapy, treating addiction/trauma without licensure) create legal liability and harm clients — when in doubt, refer to appropriate professional
- Dual relationships (coach + friend/employer/romantic interest) create power imbalances and conflicting interests — avoid when possible, minimize harm when unavoidable through transparency and supervision
- Informed consent ensures clients understand what coaching is/isn't, your qualifications, confidentiality limits, fees, and their right to terminate — document this in writing
