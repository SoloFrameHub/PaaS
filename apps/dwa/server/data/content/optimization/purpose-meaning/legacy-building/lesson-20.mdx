## Your Legacy Action Plan: 6-Step Planning Process

Now create your specific, personalized plan. Work through each step thoughtfully.

<StepByStep title="Legacy Action Plan Builder">
  <div>
    <h4>Step 1: Clarify Your Core Values and Motivation</h4>
    <p><strong>Questions to answer:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>What matters most to you in life? (3-5 core values)</li>
      <li>Why does legacy building call to you? (Your 'why'—not what you 'should' do but what genuinely matters)</li>
      <li>What would make you feel your life was well-lived? (Not what impresses others—what satisfies YOU)</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Write it down:</strong> 1 paragraph capturing your core motivation. This anchors everything else.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 2: Assess Your Current Reality</h4>
    <p><strong>Honest inventory:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Time available: How many hours weekly can you REALISTICALLY dedicate? (Not ideal—actual.)</li>
      <li>Energy levels: What's your current physical/emotional capacity? Health constraints?</li>
      <li>Resources: Financial resources, skills, networks, support systems available?</li>
      <li>Constraints: Caregiving responsibilities, work obligations, health issues, other commitments?</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Key principle:</strong> Plan from your actual life, not the life you wish you had. Start where you are.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 3: Choose 1-2 Specific Starting Points</h4>
    <p><strong>Based on Steps 1 & 2, select 1-2 concrete actions:</strong></p>
    <p>Examples: "Complete written life review (Lesson 3 framework) over next 3 months, 1 hour weekly" OR "Identify one potential mentee and propose 6-month mentoring relationship" OR "Create outline for 5-lesson mini-course on [my expertise area]" OR "Schedule monthly family storytelling dinners starting next month"</p>
    <p><strong>Criteria:</strong> Specific, time-bounded, aligned with values, realistic given constraints. One or two actions, not ten.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 4: Identify Your Support Structures</h4>
    <p><strong>Who will support this work?</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Accountability partner: One person who checks in monthly on your progress</li>
      <li>Peer support: Colleagues, friends, community members doing similar work</li>
      <li>Professional support: Therapist, coach, supervisor if relevant</li>
      <li>Learning community: Course cohorts, online groups, local meetups</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Action:</strong> Reach out to at least ONE person this week to request support. Name what you need.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 5: Build in Sustainability Practices</h4>
    <p><strong>Proactive burnout prevention (from Lesson 16):</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Clear boundaries: Work hours, response times, caseload limits</li>
      <li>Rest rhythms: Weekly day off, quarterly longer breaks, annual sabbatical</li>
      <li>Processing support: Monthly peer consultation, personal therapy ongoing</li>
      <li>Joy practices: What replenishes you? Schedule it FIRST, not last.</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Write down:</strong> 2-3 non-negotiable sustainability practices you'll protect no matter what.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 6: Set Review Checkpoints</h4>
    <p><strong>When will you pause and evaluate?</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>3-month check-in: Is this working? Feeling sustainable? Aligned with values? Adjust as needed.</li>
      <li>6-month check-in: Assess energy levels, impact, satisfaction. Continue, pivot, or stop?</li>
      <li>Annual review: Big-picture reflection—what's working, what's not, what needs to change next year?</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Calendar it now:</strong> Put review dates in calendar with reminder prompts. Don't wait until burning out to reassess.</p>
  </div>
</StepByStep>

## Sustainability Over Time: Long-Term Practices

How do you maintain legacy work not for months but for years and decades?

<SlideNavigation>
  <div>
    <h3>Year 1: Foundation Building</h3>
    <p><strong>Focus:</strong> Start small, learn by doing, build basic structure, allow mistakes and adjustment.</p>
    <p><strong>Realistic expectations:</strong> You're a beginner—even with life experience, you're new to intentional legacy work. Give yourself learning curve.</p>
    <p><strong>Priorities:</strong> Complete life review (Lesson 3), identify 1-2 starting activities (mentoring, content, community), establish boundaries, find peer support.</p>
    <p><strong>Success metric:</strong> Consistency and learning, not impressive results. If you maintained regular practice for 12 months, that's success.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>Years 2-3: Deepening Practice</h3>
    <p><strong>Focus:</strong> Refine your approach based on Year 1 learning, expand selectively where working, cut what's not sustainable.</p>
    <p><strong>Realistic expectations:</strong> You're no longer total beginner but not expert either. Competence takes time—keep learning.</p>
    <p><strong>Priorities:</strong> Develop signature frameworks (what you're known for), document your knowledge for future use, train potential co-facilitators if relevant.</p>
    <p><strong>Success metric:</strong> Increased confidence and competence, positive feedback from mentees/community, sustainable pace you can maintain.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>Years 4-7: Sustainable Impact</h3>
    <p><strong>Focus:</strong> You've found what works—now maintain it without burning out. Guard against overexpansion.</p>
    <p><strong>Realistic expectations:</strong> You're competent now, but sustainable pacing still requires active boundary-keeping. Don't assume you're immune to burnout.</p>
    <p><strong>Priorities:</strong> Financial sustainability model locked in, clear capacity limits (3-4 mentees, defined community hours), regular sabbaticals, succession planning beginnings.</p>
    <p><strong>Success metric:</strong> Longevity without burnout, serving consistently, enjoying the work, clear impact on people you serve.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>Years 8-15: Maturity and Succession</h3>
    <p><strong>Focus:</strong> Your work is mature—now plan for it to continue beyond you. Train successors, document knowledge, transition leadership.</p>
    <p><strong>Realistic expectations:</strong> You may have 10-20+ years left or fewer—plan for continuity regardless of timeline. Succession is gift, not morbidity.</p>
    <p><strong>Priorities:</strong> Succession planning (Lesson 17), knowledge documentation, gradual transition to co-leadership or eventual replacement, legacy of the legacy.</p>
    <p><strong>Success metric:</strong> Work that can continue without you, successors trained and confident, your wisdom preserved for future generations.</p>
  </div>
</SlideNavigation>

## When to Seek Professional Support

This course is educational, not therapeutic. Sometimes you need more than education.

<Callout type="warning" title="Seek Professional Help When:">
- Life review surfaces trauma you can't process alone (flashbacks, severe distress, dissociation)
- Grief or regret becomes overwhelming or functionally impairing
- Burnout progresses to depression, suicidal thoughts, or complete breakdown
- Ethical dilemmas arise beyond your capacity to navigate (boundary violations, harm concerns)
- Imposter syndrome persists despite course work and interferes with functioning
- Cultural harm occurs in your legacy work and you need guidance navigating it
- You're considering legacy work with clinical populations (requires clinical training, not just life experience)

This work touches deep psychological and existential territory. There's no shame in needing professional support—therapy, coaching, supervision, or consultation with specialists. In fact, seeking support when needed IS wisdom. Don't tough it out alone when you're struggling.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways: The Essentials of Sustainable Legacy Building

- **Start where you are, not where you wish you were**—realistic assessment of time, energy, resources enables sustainable plans that actually happen
- **1-2 specific actions beat 10 vague intentions**—choose concrete starting points aligned with values and constraints; specificity increases follow-through
- **Process metrics (hours spent, sessions held, content created) more reliable than outcome metrics**—you control inputs, not outcomes; track what you can control
- **Accountability structures essential for long-term commitment**—partner check-ins, public commitment, peer communities, scheduled reviews prevent drift
- **Burnout is predictable and preventable**—boundaries, rest rhythms, processing support, joy practices protect decades-long engagement; ignore these and you'll flame out
- **Plans will change—flexibility is strength**—life circumstances shift, interests evolve, health changes; permission to revise vision as you grow
- **Professional support is wisdom, not weakness**—seek therapy, coaching, supervision when life review surfaces trauma, burnout progresses, ethical dilemmas arise, or imposter syndrome persists
- **Legacy unfolds across decades, not months**—Year 1 is foundation, Years 2-3 deepening, Years 4-7 sustainable impact, Years 8-15 maturity and succession; pace yourself

## Safety Note

⚠️ **This Course is Complete—Now What About Ongoing Support?**

Completing this course is a beginning, not an ending. Legacy building is decades-long work that will surface ongoing challenges:
- Life review may unearth unresolved trauma requiring long-term therapy
- Mentoring relationships may trigger ethical dilemmas beyond course scope
- Burnout may develop despite prevention efforts, requiring professional intervention
- Financial strain may threaten sustainability, requiring business consultation
- Cultural misunderstandings may cause harm despite good intentions, requiring cultural consultation
- Grief, regret, and existential questions may intensify as you age, requiring specialized support

**Build ongoing support into your plan—don't try to sustain this work alone:**
- Personal therapy (especially if doing intensive relational work)
- Peer consultation or supervision (monthly minimum for active practitioners)
- Professional associations and communities of practice
- Cultural consultants when working across difference
- Business mentors if monetizing your work
- Spiritual directors or existential therapists if grappling with mortality and meaning

This course provided foundation and framework. The work itself requires sustained community, professional support, and ongoing learning. You're equipped to begin—and wise enough to know you can't do it alone.
