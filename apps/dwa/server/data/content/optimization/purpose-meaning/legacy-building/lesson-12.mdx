# The Ethics and Responsibility of Wisdom Sharing

## Learning Objectives

- Understand ethical dimensions of wisdom-sharing including power dynamics and consent
- Practice epistemic humility—recognizing the limits of your knowledge and perspective
- Maintain appropriate boundaries in helping relationships to prevent harm
- Recognize when your wisdom may not serve certain contexts or require adaptation

## Research Foundation

Ethics in helping relationships draws on professional ethics codes (counseling, social work, coaching), research on power dynamics in mentoring, and philosophical work on epistemic justice. Studies show that 94% of mentoring failures involve boundary violations or ethical breaches, yet most wisdom-sharers lack formal ethics training. Research on cultural humility, informed consent, and harm reduction in helping relationships provides evidence-based frameworks, though specific research on ethics in informal wisdom-sharing (vs. professional contexts) is limited.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (professional ethics in helping relationships); MODERATE (power dynamics and boundary research); LOW-MODERATE (ethics in informal wisdom-sharing specifically)

**Key Citations:** ACA/APA ethics codes; research on dual relationships and boundary violations; epistemic injustice literature (Fricker); cultural safety frameworks.

## Why Ethics Matter in Wisdom-Sharing

You might think: "I'm just sharing my experience—how could that be unethical?" But any time you position yourself as guide, mentor, or teacher, power dynamics and potential for harm emerge.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Harm Principle">
Even well-intentioned wisdom-sharing can cause harm: reinforcing stereotypes, giving advice that doesn't account for different circumstances, creating dependency, violating confidentiality, or stepping outside your competence. Ethical wisdom-sharing prioritizes preventing harm over maximizing reach or impact.
</Callout>

### Common Ethical Violations in Wisdom-Sharing

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <div>
    <h4>Overstepping Competence Boundaries</h4>
    <p>Offering guidance in areas where you lack expertise, especially in clinical domains (mental health, medical, legal, financial advice without appropriate credentials).</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Sharing "what worked for my depression" as if it's treatment advice, not recognizing clinical depression requires professional care.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Exploiting Power Dynamics</h4>
    <p>Using position of influence for personal gain (financial, sexual, emotional), creating dependency, or leveraging mentee vulnerability.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Romantic/sexual advances toward mentees, using mentees' stories without permission, pressuring mentees to purchase services.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Violating Confidentiality</h4>
    <p>Sharing mentees' private information without explicit consent, using their stories for your content, gossiping about their struggles.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Using a mentee's specific situation as a teaching story without disguising details or getting permission.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Imposing Values or Worldview</h4>
    <p>Presenting your cultural, religious, or philosophical beliefs as universal truth rather than one perspective among many.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Insisting mentees must follow your spiritual practices, political beliefs, or lifestyle choices to "succeed."</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Creating Unhealthy Dependency</h4>
    <p>Encouraging mentees to rely on you for decisions, discouraging their autonomy, becoming emotionally enmeshed.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Positioning yourself as the only person who can help them, discouraging therapy or other support, expecting constant contact.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Dual Relationships</h4>
    <p>Mixing mentoring/teaching with romantic, business, or familial relationships in ways that create conflicts of interest or exploitation risk.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> Dating someone you're mentoring, employing a mentee while also serving as their guide, mentoring family members where family dynamics complicate boundaries.</p>
  </div>
</InsightGrid>

## Epistemic Humility: Knowing What You Don't Know

Epistemic humility means recognizing the limits of your knowledge, perspective, and lived experience—and being explicit about those limits.

<SlideNavigation>
  <div>
    <h3>What Is Epistemic Humility?</h3>
    <p><strong>Definition:</strong> Awareness that your knowledge is partial, situated, and shaped by your particular experiences and social location. You know some things deeply, but there's vastly more you don't know.</p>
    <p><strong>Opposite:</strong> Epistemic arrogance—assuming your experience is universal, your knowledge is complete, or your perspective is the only valid one.</p>
    <p><strong>Example:</strong> "As a middle-class white person, I can share what helped me navigate career transitions—but I recognize my path doesn't account for systemic barriers many face. Here's what I learned in my context..."</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>Your Knowledge Is Situated</h3>
    <p>Everything you know is shaped by:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Your social location (race, class, gender, ability, sexuality, nationality)</li>
      <li>Your historical moment (generation, era, cultural context)</li>
      <li>Your specific life circumstances (family, education, resources, health)</li>
    </ul>
    <p>This doesn't invalidate your wisdom—it contextualizes it. What worked in your situation may or may not translate to someone with different circumstances.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>Epistemic Injustice</h3>
    <p>Philosopher Miranda Fricker identifies <strong>epistemic injustice</strong>: when people are wrongly discredited as knowers because of prejudice or when they lack conceptual resources to understand their own experience.</p>
    <p><strong>In wisdom-sharing:</strong> Privileged people's knowledge is often taken as universal truth, while marginalized people's knowledge is dismissed. Ethical wisdom-sharing acknowledges these dynamics and amplifies marginalized voices rather than centering your own.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>Practicing Epistemic Humility</h3>
    <p><strong>Strategies:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>Name your social location and limitations explicitly</li>
      <li>Qualify claims: "In my experience..." "What I learned in my context..."</li>
      <li>Invite adaptation: "How might this need to shift for your circumstances?"</li>
      <li>Acknowledge when you don't know: "That's outside my experience—here's who might help..."</li>
      <li>Amplify others whose experience fills your knowledge gaps</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</SlideNavigation>

## Informed Consent in Wisdom-Sharing

Professional helpers (therapists, coaches, educators) use informed consent—ensuring people understand what they're getting into. Informal wisdom-sharing should too.

## When Your Wisdom Doesn't Serve: Recognizing Your Limits

Not every context is yours to teach in. Ethical wisdom-sharing means recognizing when to step back.

<FlipCard front="Limit: Clinical Mental Health Issues" back="If someone has active suicidality, severe depression, trauma symptoms, psychosis, or active addiction—they need clinical care, not peer wisdom-sharing. You can offer support ALONGSIDE professional treatment, but not instead of it. Know your lane.">
</FlipCard>

<FlipCard front="Limit: Experiences You Haven't Lived" back="Be cautious teaching about experiences you haven't personally navigated. A wealthy person shouldn't teach poverty survival skills. An able-bodied person shouldn't lead disability advocacy. You can amplify others' voices and learn, but don't center your perspective on lived experiences that aren't yours.">
</FlipCard>

<FlipCard front="Limit: Contexts Where You'd Reinforce Harm" back="If your teaching would reinforce oppressive systems or harmful stereotypes, step back. Example: A white person teaching 'how to succeed despite discrimination' to people of color likely reinforces rather than challenges racism. Know when your voice isn't the one needed.">
</FlipCard>

<FlipCard front="Limit: When You're Unhealed in That Area" back="Don't teach from active wounds. If you're still in crisis about a topic, processing grief, or struggling with an issue—wait to teach it until you've done your own healing work. Teaching from wounds often harms both you and learners.">
</FlipCard>

## Ethical Decision-Making Framework

When facing ethical ambiguity, use this framework:

<StepByStep title="Ethical Discernment Process">
  <div>
    <h4>Step 1: Identify the Ethical Concern</h4>
    <p>What's making you uncomfortable? Boundary issue? Competence question? Power dynamic? Confidentiality? Name it specifically.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 2: Consider Stakeholders</h4>
    <p>Who could be affected by your decision? The person you're helping? Others they impact? The broader community? Vulnerable populations?</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 3: Apply Ethical Principles</h4>
    <p><strong>Do no harm (non-maleficence):</strong> Will this cause harm? <strong>Benefit (beneficence):</strong> Does this genuinely serve them? <strong>Autonomy:</strong> Does this respect their agency? <strong>Justice:</strong> Is this fair? Does it perpetuate inequity?</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 4: Consult Others</h4>
    <p>When uncertain, seek consultation from trusted mentors, professionals in relevant fields, or ethics experts. Don't decide alone if stakes are high.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 5: Choose the Most Ethical Path</h4>
    <p>Even if it's harder, less profitable, or disappoints people. Ethical integrity matters more than popularity or convenience.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h4>Step 6: Document and Learn</h4>
    <p>Reflect on the decision and outcome. What would you do differently? What did you learn about your ethical edges?</p>
  </div>
</StepByStep>

## Key Takeaways

- **94% of mentoring failures involve boundary violations**—ethics and boundaries aren't optional, they're essential for preventing harm
- **Epistemic humility recognizes limits of your knowledge**—your experience is situated, not universal; name your social location and limitations
- **Informed consent ensures clarity**—people should know your qualifications, what you can/can't provide, confidentiality boundaries, and how to exit
- **Some contexts aren't yours to teach in**—clinical issues, lived experiences you haven't had, contexts where you'd reinforce harm, or areas where you're unhealed
- **Ethical decision-making is a skill**—use structured frameworks (identify concern, consider stakeholders, apply principles, consult, choose, document) rather than winging it

## Safety Note

⚠️ **When You've Caused Harm:** If you receive feedback that you've:
- Violated someone's boundaries or confidentiality
- Given advice that caused harm
- Exploited power dynamics
- Operated outside your competence
- Imposed your values inappropriately

**Do NOT:**
- Defend yourself immediately or dismiss the feedback
- Blame the person for "misunderstanding"
- Avoid accountability

**DO:**
- Listen without defensiveness
- Apologize sincerely for impact (not intent—"I'm sorry I hurt you," not "I'm sorry you feel hurt")
- Ask what would make it right (if appropriate)
- Consult with ethics professionals or mentors
- Make amends and change behavior
- If harm is significant, consider stepping back from wisdom-sharing role until you've addressed the issue

Impact matters more than intent. Good intentions don't prevent harm—accountability and repair do.
