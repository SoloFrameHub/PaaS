# Anxiety Disorders and Panic Attack Response

## Learning Objectives

- Differentiate normal anxiety, clinical anxiety disorders (GAD, social anxiety, panic disorder, phobias), and acute panic attacks based on duration, severity, and functional impairment
- Apply grounding techniques (5-4-3-2-1, breathing regulation, present-moment focus) and de-escalation strategies for acute panic episodes without minimizing or over-reassuring
- Support long-term anxiety management by connecting to evidence-based professional treatments (CBT, exposure therapy, medication evaluation) and addressing avoidance patterns

## Research Foundation

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health condition, affecting 19% of U.S. adults annually (~40 million people) and 31% lifetime. Evidence-based treatments include cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) with **strong** effect sizes (d = 0.7-1.0), exposure therapy for phobias/OCD with **strong** evidence, and SSRIs/SNRIs with **moderate-to-strong** evidence. However, treatment gap is severe: only ~37% receive treatment, and of those, many receive inadequate care. Grounding techniques and breathing regulation have **moderate** evidence in community settings for acute symptom management.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (CBT, exposure therapy); MODERATE (grounding techniques, peer support)

## Normal Anxiety vs. Clinical Anxiety Disorder

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Normal Anxiety (Adaptive)" color="green">
    - **Proportional to threat:** Job interview, medical test, safety concern
    - **Time-limited:** Resolves when stressor passes
    - **Motivating:** Helps you prepare, stay safe, perform
    - **Doesn't impair functioning:** Can still work, socialize, function
    - **Example:** Nervous before public speaking, calms after
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Clinical Anxiety Disorder" color="orange">
    - **Out of proportion:** Intense worry about unlikely or minor threats
    - **Persistent:** Lasts 6+ months (DSM criteria), doesn't resolve
    - **Paralyzing:** Interferes with daily life, avoidance patterns develop
    - **Impairs functioning:** Can't work, socialize, or engage in normal activities
    - **Example:** Can't leave house due to fear of panic attack, avoids all social situations
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Types of Anxiety Disorders

<EnhancedAccordion title="Common Anxiety Disorder Presentations">
  <AccordionItem title="Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)">
    **Presentation:** Excessive worry about multiple things (work, health, family, finances) most days for 6+ months. Hard to control worry.
    
    **Symptoms:** Restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.
    
    **Functional impact:** Chronic tension, exhaustion from constant worry, difficulty making decisions.
    
    **Treatment:** CBT (teaching worry management, cognitive restructuring), medication (SSRIs), mindfulness-based approaches.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Panic Disorder">
    **Presentation:** Recurrent unexpected panic attacks + persistent fear of future attacks + avoidance of situations where attacks occurred.
    
    **Panic attack symptoms:** Intense fear, racing heart, shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness, fear of dying/losing control, peaks in ~10 minutes.
    
    **Functional impact:** Avoidance of places/activities where attacks happened (agoraphobia), ER visits for chest pain.
    
    **Treatment:** CBT (interoceptive exposure — safely triggering panic sensations to reduce fear), medication (SSRIs).
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Social Anxiety Disorder">
    **Presentation:** Intense fear of social situations where one might be judged, embarrassed, or humiliated. Fear is persistent (6+ months) and out of proportion.
    
    **Common triggers:** Public speaking, eating in public, meeting new people, performance situations.
    
    **Functional impact:** Avoidance of work/school, isolation, career limitations.
    
    **Treatment:** CBT with exposure therapy (gradual, systematic exposure to feared social situations), medication (SSRIs).
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Specific Phobias">
    **Presentation:** Intense, irrational fear of specific object or situation (heights, animals, flying, blood/injections, enclosed spaces).
    
    **Functional impact:** Depends on phobia — may avoid specific situations but otherwise function well.
    
    **Treatment:** Exposure therapy (gradual, controlled exposure to feared stimulus) — STRONG evidence, often resolved in 1-10 sessions.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder)">
    **Presentation:** Recurrent intrusive thoughts (obsessions) + repetitive behaviors or mental acts (compulsions) to reduce anxiety.
    
    **Examples:** Contamination fears → excessive washing; harm obsessions → checking rituals; need for symmetry → ordering/arranging.
    
    **Functional impact:** Hours per day on rituals, significant distress, interference with work/relationships.
    
    **Treatment:** Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) — STRONG evidence; SSRIs (moderate evidence).
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Responding to an Acute Panic Attack

Panic attacks are terrifying but not medically dangerous (in most cases). Your calm presence can help significantly.

<StepByStep title="Panic Attack Response Protocol">
  <Step number={1} title="Stay Calm — Model Regulation">
    Your calm demeanor helps their nervous system regulate. Speak slowly, gently. Do NOT panic yourself.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={2} title="Reassure (But Don't Minimize)">
    **Say:** "This is a panic attack. I know it feels terrifying, but it's not dangerous. It will pass in about 10 minutes."
    
    **Don't say:** "Calm down" or "There's nothing to worry about" (dismissive).
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={3} title="Guide Breathing (If They're Receptive)">
    "Let's breathe together. In through your nose for 4... hold for 4... out through your mouth for 6."
    
    **Do NOT:** Force breathing into a paper bag (outdated, can worsen symptoms). Natural breathing is fine.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={4} title="Ground in Present Moment">
    Use **5-4-3-2-1 grounding**:
    - "Name 5 things you can see"
    - "4 things you can touch"
    - "3 things you can hear"
    - "2 things you can smell"
    - "1 thing you can taste"
    
    **Why it works:** Brings attention to present sensory experience (not catastrophic thoughts).
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={5} title="Stay With Them Until It Passes">
    Panic attacks typically peak in ~10 minutes, resolve in 20-30. Your presence provides safety. Don't leave unless they ask for space.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={6} title="Encourage Professional Evaluation">
    **After the attack:** "Have you had panic attacks before? It would be good to talk to a doctor to make sure this is panic and not something medical, and to explore treatment options."
    
    **Why:** First-time chest pain could be cardiac issue (especially in people 40+ with risk factors). Professional evaluation rules out medical causes.
  </Step>
</StepByStep>

<Callout type="warning" title="When to Call 911 for Panic Symptoms">
**Call 911 if:**
- First-time chest pain in person 40+ or with cardiac risk factors (rule out heart attack)
- Symptoms don't resolve within 30 minutes
- Person loses consciousness or has seizure
- You're unsure if it's panic or medical emergency

Better to err on side of caution. Panic attacks don't cause heart attacks, but both can present with chest pain.
</Callout>

## Grounding Techniques for Anxiety Management

<SlideNavigation>
  <Slide title="5-4-3-2-1 Sensory Grounding">
    Brings attention to present moment through five senses. Interrupts catastrophic thinking.
    
    **How to use:**
    1. Name 5 things you see
    2. 4 things you can physically touch
    3. 3 things you hear
    4. 2 things you smell
    5. 1 thing you taste
    
    **Best for:** Panic, acute anxiety, dissociation
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="4-7-8 Breathing (Dr. Weil)">
    Activates parasympathetic nervous system (calm response).
    
    **How to use:**
    - Breathe in through nose for 4 counts
    - Hold for 7 counts
    - Exhale through mouth for 8 counts
    - Repeat 4 cycles
    
    **Best for:** General anxiety, sleep difficulties
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Body Scan">
    Progressive attention to body sensations, head to toe.
    
    **How to use:**
    "Notice your head... your shoulders... your chest... your stomach... your legs... your feet. Where do you feel tension? Can you soften it?"
    
    **Best for:** Generalized anxiety, somatization
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Worry Time (Containment)">
    Schedule 15 minutes daily for worry. When worries arise outside that time, note them and postpone to worry time.
    
    **Why it works:** Reduces chronic worry's intrusion into daily life. Evidence-based component of CBT for GAD.
    
    **Best for:** GAD, chronic worry
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Supporting Long-Term Anxiety Management

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Recognize Avoidance Patterns" color="purple">
    **Avoidance reinforces anxiety** (you feel relief short-term, but fear grows long-term).
    
    **Common avoidance:**
    - Not driving after panic attack on highway
    - Skipping social events due to social anxiety
    - Excessive safety behaviors (always checking locks 10x)
    
    **MHFA approach:** Gently point out pattern without judgment: "I've noticed you've been avoiding [situation]. That's understandable — anxiety is hard. But avoidance usually makes it worse over time. Would you be open to talking to someone who specializes in this?"
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Encourage Evidence-Based Treatment" color="blue">
    **CBT is first-line:** Strong evidence for all anxiety disorders.
    
    **What to say:**
    - "Anxiety is very treatable. CBT helps you change the thought patterns that fuel anxiety."
    - "Exposure therapy sounds scary, but it's done gradually and it works really well for phobias."
    - "Medication can help too — talk to your doctor."
    
    **Treatment locators:**
    - ADAA (Anxiety & Depression Association): adaa.org/finding-help
    - Psychology Today therapist directory (filter by specialties)
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Key Takeaways

- Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health condition (19% annual prevalence, 31% lifetime) with STRONG evidence for CBT and exposure therapy, but severe treatment gap (~37% receive treatment)
- Normal anxiety is proportional, time-limited, motivating; clinical anxiety is disproportionate, persistent (6+ months), paralyzing, and impairs functioning
- Panic attack response: stay calm, reassure without minimizing ("I know it feels terrifying but it's not dangerous, will pass in ~10 min"), guide breathing, use 5-4-3-2-1 grounding, stay with them
- Avoidance reinforces anxiety short-term but worsens it long-term — gently encourage professional treatment (CBT, exposure therapy) to break avoidance cycles
- Call 911 if: first-time chest pain in person 40+ with cardiac risk, symptoms don't resolve in 30 min, loss of consciousness, or unsure if panic vs. medical emergency
