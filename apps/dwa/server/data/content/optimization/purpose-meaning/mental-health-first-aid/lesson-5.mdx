# Suicide Prevention and Risk Assessment

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize acute suicide warning signs (talking about death, giving away possessions, saying goodbye) and chronic risk factors (prior attempts, mental illness, social isolation) using evidence-based frameworks
- Conduct direct, compassionate inquiry about suicidal thoughts using safe messaging guidelines (AFSP/SPRC/WHO) — understanding that asking does NOT increase risk
- Understand the scope and limits of MHFA suicide risk assessment: assess for indicators and escalate to professionals, NOT conduct clinical risk assessment or promise safety

## Research Foundation

The evidence base for gatekeeper training (identifying suicide warning signs and connecting at-risk individuals to help) shows **moderate evidence** for short-term improvements in knowledge, self-efficacy, and attitudes (effect sizes d = 0.49 for self-efficacy). However, long-term evidence is **weak**: knowledge decays over time, attitudes return to baseline in 57% of studies within ~4 months, and translation to actual intervention behavior is inconsistent. Critically, high-quality evidence that gatekeeper training reduces population-level suicide rates is **absent** — most studies lack the methodological rigor to establish this causal link. Safe messaging guidelines from AFSP, SPRC, and WHO are consensus-based with substantial indirect evidence from contagion research.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (for safe messaging guidelines); MODERATE (for warning sign recognition training); WEAK (for long-term behavior change); ABSENT (for population suicide rate reduction)

## Understanding Suicide: Prevalence, Myths, and Reality

**Prevalence (United States):**
- **~49,000 deaths by suicide per year** (CDC 2023) — 1 death every ~11 minutes
- **10th leading cause of death** overall; 2nd for ages 10-34
- **For every death by suicide:** ~25 attempts, ~250 seriously consider it
- **12.3 million adults** had serious thoughts of suicide in 2023

**Critical reality:** Suicide is preventable in many cases, but it is NOT inevitable. Most people in suicidal crisis are ambivalent — they want the pain to stop, not necessarily to die. Your support can tip the balance toward life.

<FlipCard frontTitle="Myths About Suicide" backTitle="Evidence-Based Reality">
  <FrontContent>
    **Myth 1:** "Asking about suicide will plant the idea or make it more likely."
    
    **Myth 2:** "People who talk about suicide don't actually do it — they're just seeking attention."
    
    **Myth 3:** "If someone is determined to die by suicide, nothing can stop them."
    
    **Myth 4:** "Suicide happens without warning."
    
    **Myth 5:** "Only people with mental illness die by suicide."
  </FrontContent>
  <BackContent>
    **Reality 1:** Research consistently shows that asking about suicide does NOT increase risk and can provide relief. People experiencing suicidal thoughts often feel isolated and afraid to talk about it — your question signals it's safe to share.
    
    **Reality 2:** All expressions of suicidal thoughts should be taken seriously. Many people who die by suicide communicated their intent beforehand. Dismissing it as "attention-seeking" is dangerous.
    
    **Reality 3:** Most people in suicidal crisis are ambivalent. They want the pain to end, not necessarily their life. Support, connection, and reducing access to lethal means can save lives.
    
    **Reality 4:** ~80% of people who die by suicide showed warning signs. The issue is recognition and response, not absence of signals.
    
    **Reality 5:** While mental illness is a major risk factor (~90% of suicide deaths involve mental illness or substance use), suicide is multifactorial: loss, trauma, isolation, access to means, cultural factors, and impulsivity all play roles.
  </BackContent>
</FlipCard>

## Safe Messaging About Suicide: AFSP/SPRC/WHO Guidelines

All content in this course follows evidence-based safe messaging guidelines from the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention (AFSP), Suicide Prevention Resource Center (SPRC), and World Health Organization (WHO). These guidelines reduce contagion risk and promote help-seeking.

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="DO Use This Language" color="green">
    - "Died by suicide" (not "committed suicide" — language from when it was a crime)
    - "Suicide attempt" (factual)
    - "Survived a suicide attempt" (strength-focused)
    - "Experiencing suicidal thoughts" (person-first language)
    - Emphasize **help is available, recovery is possible, suicide is preventable**
    - Provide crisis resources: 988, local services
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="DO NOT Use This Language" color="red">
    - "Committed suicide" (implies crime/sin)
    - "Successful/failed attempt" (implies death is success)
    - Do NOT describe method or location of death
    - Do NOT glorify, romanticize, or normalize suicide
    - Do NOT present suicide as solution to suffering
    - Do NOT simplify causes ("X caused their suicide")
    - Do NOT include sensational details
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Recognizing Suicide Warning Signs: What to Look For

Warning signs indicate someone may be in crisis and requires immediate support. Not everyone shows all signs, and presence of signs does NOT mean suicide is inevitable — but they warrant direct inquiry and action.

<SlideNavigation>
  <Slide title="Verbal Warning Signs (What They Say)">
    **Direct statements:**
    - "I want to die" / "I wish I were dead"
    - "I'm going to kill myself"
    - "I can't do this anymore"
    - "Everyone would be better off without me"
    - "There's no point in living"
    
    **Indirect statements:**
    - "You won't have to worry about me much longer"
    - "I won't be a problem soon"
    - "I'm tired of being a burden"
    - Talking about death, dying, or the afterlife frequently
    - Saying goodbye as if it's final
    
    **Action required:** Take ALL statements seriously. Ask directly about suicide.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Behavioral Warning Signs (What They Do)">
    **HIGH-RISK behaviors:**
    - **Giving away possessions** — especially cherished items
    - **Saying goodbye** to people as if it's final
    - **Searching online for suicide methods** or acquiring means (pills, weapons)
    - **Writing a note or will** or making final arrangements
    - **Sudden calm after depression** — may indicate decision to attempt suicide
    
    **Other behavioral changes:**
    - Withdrawing from social contact, isolating
    - Reckless behavior (driving recklessly, unsafe sex, substance use)
    - Sleeping too much or too little
    - Dramatic mood swings
    - Visiting or calling people to say goodbye
    
    **Action required:** If person has plan, intent, or means → call 988 or 911 immediately.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Emotional Warning Signs (What They Feel)">
    - **Hopelessness:** "Things will never get better"
    - **Feeling trapped:** "There's no way out"
    - **Unbearable emotional or physical pain**
    - **Being a burden:** "Everyone would be better off without me" (key predictor)
    - **Shame, guilt, or self-hatred**
    - **Rage, anger, or desire for revenge**
    - **Anxiety, agitation, inability to calm**
    
    **Note:** "Perceived burdensomeness" (feeling like a burden to others) is one of the strongest predictors of suicide risk in interpersonal-psychological theory of suicide.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Circumstantial Risk Factors (What's Happened)">
    **Recent precipitating events:**
    - Significant loss (death, divorce, job loss, financial crisis)
    - Relationship breakup or conflict
    - Legal problems or public humiliation
    - Recent discharge from psychiatric hospitalization (high-risk period)
    - Diagnosis of serious medical illness
    
    **Historical risk factors:**
    - Prior suicide attempt (strongest single predictor)
    - Family history of suicide
    - History of trauma, abuse, or neglect
    - Mental illness (depression, bipolar, psychosis, PTSD, substance use)
    - Access to lethal means (especially firearms)
    - Chronic pain or terminal illness
    
    **Important:** Risk factors describe population patterns, NOT individual prediction. Someone with many risk factors may be fine; someone with few may be in crisis.
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## How to Ask About Suicide: The Direct, Compassionate Approach

**The most important thing you will learn in this entire course:**

**Asking someone directly if they are thinking about suicide does NOT increase their risk. Research shows it can provide relief.**

<StepByStep title="How to Have the Conversation">
  <Step number={1} title="Create Privacy and Safety">
    - Find a private, quiet space away from others
    - Ensure you have time (don't rush)
    - Turn off distractions (phone, TV)
    - Be calm and present — your affect matters
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={2} title="Name What You've Noticed">
    - "I've noticed you seem really down lately, and I'm concerned about you."
    - "You mentioned feeling hopeless, and that worries me."
    - "I saw your post, and it made me worried for your safety."
    
    Express genuine care. This is not an interrogation — it's compassion.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={3} title="Ask Directly — Use Clear Language">
    **Say this:**
    - "Are you thinking about suicide?"
    - "Are you thinking about killing yourself?"
    - "Are you having thoughts of hurting or killing yourself?"
    
    **Do NOT soften the language:**
    - ❌ "You're not thinking of doing anything stupid, are you?"
    - ❌ "You don't want to hurt yourself, do you?"
    - ❌ "You seem sad — are you okay?" (Too vague)
    
    **Why direct language matters:** Euphemisms ("hurt yourself," "do something stupid") create ambiguity. The person may not realize you're asking about suicide. Clear language gives them permission to be honest.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={4} title="Listen Without Judgment — Stay Calm">
    - If they say **YES**: Stay calm. Do not panic, argue, or minimize.
    - Listen to their experience: "Tell me more. How long have you been feeling this way?"
    - Validate: "Thank you for telling me. I'm glad you trusted me with this."
    - Do NOT say: "You have so much to live for!" or "That's selfish!" (shaming increases risk)
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={5} title="Assess for Plan, Intent, and Means">
    **Ask these follow-up questions:**
    - "Have you thought about how you would do it?" (Plan)
    - "Have you decided when you would do it?" (Intent/timeline)
    - "Do you have access to [pills/weapon/etc.]?" (Means)
    
    **Why this matters:**
    - **Thoughts alone** = support needed, encourage professional help (988, therapist)
    - **Plan + Intent + Means** = IMMEDIATE 911/988 escalation; do not leave person alone
    
    **If they say NO:** Still provide support. They trusted you enough to ask. Encourage professional help.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={6} title="Do NOT Promise Confidentiality — Be Honest">
    - "I care about you, and I can't keep this to myself if your safety is at risk. I need to get you help."
    - If they have plan/intent/means: "I'm going to call 988/911 because I'm worried about your safety. I'm doing this because I care about you."
    
    **Do NOT:**
    - Promise to keep it secret if they're at risk
    - Extract a "promise" that they won't hurt themselves (clinically discredited and harmful)
    - Leave them alone if immediate risk is present
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={7} title="Connect to Help Immediately">
    **If immediate risk (plan + intent + means):**
    - Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) or 911
    - Stay with them until help arrives (if safe)
    - Remove means if safe and consensual (do NOT forcibly take weapon)
    
    **If suicidal thoughts but no immediate plan/intent:**
    - Call 988 together: "Let's call 988 right now. They're trained in this and can help."
    - Encourage them to see their doctor, therapist, or go to ER
    - Provide concrete next steps: "Will you call your therapist today? Can I help you make that appointment?"
    - Follow up: Check in the next day
  </Step>
</StepByStep>

## What Happens When You Call 988?

<Callout type="success" title="988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — What to Expect">
**Who answers:** Trained crisis counselors (NOT police, unless imminent danger requires it)

**Available:** 24/7, free, confidential

**Languages:** English, Spanish, interpretation for 150+ languages

**Specialized services:** LGBTQ+ youth line, Veterans Crisis Line (press 1), Spanish line (press 2), TTY for deaf/hard-of-hearing

**What they do:**
- Listen without judgment
- Assess risk and safety
- Provide emotional support
- Connect to local resources
- Develop safety plan if appropriate
- Dispatch mobile crisis team or emergency services if needed

**They do NOT:**
- Automatically call police (only if imminent danger and person refuses voluntary help)
- Judge, shame, or minimize
- Require your name or personal information (confidential)

**Text option:** Text 988 if talking feels too hard

**Chat option:** 988lifeline.org/chat
</Callout>

## Protective Factors You Can Help Strengthen

While you cannot eliminate all risk, you can strengthen protective factors that buffer against suicide.

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Relational Protective Factors" color="blue">
    - **Social connection:** Reduce isolation — your presence matters
    - **Sense of belonging:** Connect them to community, groups, support networks
    - **Reasons for living:** Help identify (children, pets, future goals, faith, relationships)
    - **Trusted relationships:** Be a consistent, caring presence
    
    **What you can do:** Show up. Check in regularly. Reduce loneliness.
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Access & Skills Protective Factors" color="green">
    - **Access to effective mental health care:** Help reduce barriers (cost, transportation, stigma)
    - **Coping skills:** Encourage healthy coping (not prescriptively — ask what's helped before)
    - **Problem-solving skills:** Support (don't solve for them) in addressing specific problems
    - **Impulse control:** Reduce immediate access to lethal means
    
    **What you can do:** Facilitate access to professionals. Encourage coping. Support means reduction conversations (see below).
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Means Reduction: Reducing Access to Lethal Means Saves Lives

**Evidence:** Reducing access to lethal means (especially firearms) is one of the most effective suicide prevention interventions. Suicide is often impulsive — creating time and distance between impulse and means saves lives.

<EnhancedAccordion title="Means Reduction Strategies (For Discussion, Not MHFA Implementation)">
  <AccordionItem title="Firearms (Most Lethal Means in U.S.)">
    **Evidence:** Firearms account for ~50% of U.S. suicide deaths. Firearm access significantly increases suicide risk.
    
    **Safer storage:** Gun locks, lockboxes, storing firearms outside the home temporarily (friend, family, gun shop storage)
    
    **Your role in MHFA:** Mention safer storage as a public health intervention. Do NOT attempt to remove firearms yourself (dangerous). Suggest they talk to trusted person about temporary storage.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Medications">
    **Evidence:** Medication overdose is a common suicide method.
    
    **Safer storage:** Lock up medications, store only small quantities, dispose of unused prescriptions
    
    **Your role:** Suggest they talk to doctor/pharmacist about safe disposal of unused meds. Do NOT remove medications yourself.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Other Means">
    Reducing access to heights (barriers on bridges), toxic substances, etc. — most are public health/policy interventions beyond individual MHFA scope.
    
    **Your role:** Focus on connection, professional referral, and reducing isolation (relational protective factors you CAN influence).
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

**Important:** Means reduction conversations should be had by trained clinicians or with the person's consent. MHFA role is to mention it as a strategy and encourage professional conversation, NOT to implement it yourself (especially removing firearms, which is dangerous).

## After Someone Discloses Suicidal Thoughts: Ongoing Support

Your role doesn't end after the crisis conversation.

<Callout type="success" title="Ongoing Support Guidelines">
**In the hours/days after:**
- **Follow up:** Check in the next day, and regularly after. "I'm thinking about you. How are you doing today?"
- **Reduce isolation:** Invite low-pressure connection (walk, coffee, sitting together)
- **Encourage professional help:** "Have you been able to call that therapist yet? Want help finding one?"
- **Be patient:** Recovery is not linear. Suicidal thoughts may return.
- **Set boundaries:** You can support them, but you are NOT their therapist. Encourage professional care.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do NOT become their sole support (unsustainable and exceeds your scope)
- Do NOT promise you'll keep them alive (that's their decision and their clinician's work)
- Do NOT feel guilty if the worst happens — you are not responsible for someone else's suicide

**Self-care for you:** Supporting someone with suicidal thoughts is exhausting. Debrief with someone you trust. Seek your own support. Know your limits.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Asking directly about suicide ("Are you thinking about killing yourself?") does NOT increase risk and can provide relief — clear language gives permission to share and access help
- Suicide warning signs include verbal (talking about death, saying goodbye), behavioral (giving away possessions, acquiring means, sudden calm after depression), emotional (hopelessness, perceived burdensomeness), and circumstantial (loss, prior attempts) indicators
- MHFA suicide assessment is about recognizing indicators and escalating, NOT conducting clinical risk assessment — ask about plan, intent, and means, then connect to 988/911 immediately if high risk
- Safe messaging guidelines (AFSP/SPRC/WHO) require using respectful language ("died by suicide," not "committed suicide"), not describing methods, and always providing crisis resources (988)
- Evidence for gatekeeper training shows MODERATE short-term knowledge/confidence gains, but WEAK long-term behavior change and ABSENT evidence for population suicide rate reduction — training helps but is not a magic bullet
