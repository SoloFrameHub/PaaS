# Supporting Youth and Adolescent Mental Health

## Learning Objectives

- Adapt MHFA approaches for developmental stages (children, adolescents, young adults) and adolescent communication styles that differ from adult interactions
- Navigate confidentiality and safety balance with minor populations, including mandatory reporting obligations and when to involve parents/guardians
- Support LGBTQ+ youth (higher risk for depression, anxiety, suicide due to minority stress), address bullying/peer victimization, and connect to youth-specific resources

## Research Foundation

Half of all lifetime mental illness begins by age 14, three-quarters by age 24. Adolescent brain development (prefrontal cortex not fully mature until ~25) affects risk-taking, emotion regulation, and help-seeking. Youth MHFA has **moderate** evidence for improving adult confidence/intentions to help; downstream effects on youth outcomes have **weak** evidence (similar to adult MHFA). LGBTQ+ youth face 2-3x higher rates of depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation due to minority stress, family rejection, bullying; supportive adults reduce risk significantly.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE (Youth MHFA training outcomes); WEAK (youth recipient outcomes); STRONG (minority stress impact on LGBTQ+ youth)

## Developmental Considerations

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Children (5-12)" color="blue">
    **Communication:** Concrete thinking, limited emotional vocabulary. May express distress through behavior (tantrums, withdrawal) more than words.
    
    **Approach:**
    - Use simple language
    - Ask about behaviors: "What happens when you feel bad?"
    - Use drawings, play to facilitate expression
    - Always involve parents/guardians (except abuse concerns)
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Adolescents (13-18)" color="purple">
    **Communication:** Abstract thinking emerging, peer influence dominant, identity formation, autonomy-seeking.
    
    **Approach:**
    - Respect autonomy while ensuring safety
    - Don't talk down or dismiss concerns ("teen angst")
    - Privacy matters (but safety overrides)
    - Peer relationships are CRITICAL — don't minimize
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Confidentiality with Minors: The Balance

<Callout type="warning" title="Confidentiality Limits with Youth">
**You can offer privacy, but NOT absolute confidentiality.** Safety concerns override privacy.

**When you MUST break confidence:**
- Suicidal thoughts with plan/intent
- Abuse or neglect (mandatory reporting)
- Immediate danger to self or others

**Be transparent upfront:** "What you tell me is private, UNLESS I'm worried about your safety or someone is hurting you. Then I need to get you help, and that might mean telling your parents or calling for support."
</Callout>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Navigating Youth Confidentiality">
  <AccordionItem title="When to Involve Parents">
    **Involve parents if:**
    - Youth is in danger (suicidal, being abused by someone other than parent)
    - Youth needs professional treatment (parents must consent for minors)
    - Youth is struggling significantly and needs family support
    
    **How to involve:**
    - Tell youth FIRST that you'll involve parents: "I care about you and I'm worried. I need to tell your parents so we can get you help."
    - Frame as caring, not punishment
    - If possible, help youth tell parents themselves (you're there for support)
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Mandatory Reporting (Child Abuse/Neglect)">
    **You MUST report if you suspect:**
    - Physical abuse by parent/caregiver
    - Sexual abuse
    - Neglect (basic needs not met)
    - Emotional abuse
    
    **How to report:**
    - Call Child Protective Services (CPS) or state hotline
    - Tell child: "You were right to tell me. It's not your fault. I'm going to get you help."
    - Do NOT confront alleged abuser
    - Do NOT investigate yourself — that's CPS's role
    
    **Your role:** Bridge to protection, not investigator.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="When Parent IS the Problem">
    **Scenarios:**
    - Parent is abuser → Report to CPS, do NOT tell parent
    - Parent is unsupportive (esp. LGBTQ+ youth) → Help youth access school counselor, GSA, Trevor Project
    - Parent minimizes youth's distress → Educate parent if safe, or connect youth to other supportive adults
    
    **Balance:** Youth needs support, but minors usually need adult advocates for treatment access.
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Supporting LGBTQ+ Youth

<Callout type="info" title="LGBTQ+ Youth Face Higher Mental Health Risks">
**Statistics:**
- LGBTQ+ youth are **2-3x more likely** to experience depression, anxiety
- **4x more likely** to attempt suicide than heterosexual youth
- 40% of transgender youth have attempted suicide

**Root cause:** Minority stress (discrimination, family rejection, bullying), NOT sexual orientation/gender identity itself.

**Protective factor:** ONE accepting adult reduces suicide risk 40%.
</Callout>

<SlideNavigation>
  <Slide title="Be an Accepting Adult">
    **How:**
    - Use chosen name and pronouns (if shared)
    - "I'm glad you trusted me with this."
    - Don't require them to educate you — learn on your own time
    - Believe them: "You know yourself best."
    
    **Don't:**
    - "Are you sure?" / "It's just a phase"
    - "Have you tried NOT being gay/trans?"
    - Out them to others without permission
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Address Family Rejection">
    **If family is rejecting:**
    - Validate: "That's really hard. You deserve to be accepted."
    - Connect to LGBTQ+ affirming resources: Trevor Project (1-866-488-7386, text START to 678-678)
    - School GSA (Gender-Sexuality Alliance), LGBTQ+ youth centers
    - Help find supportive adults (teachers, counselors, extended family)
    
    **Don't force them to come out or confront family if unsafe.**
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="School-Based Support">
    **Bullying/peer victimization:**
    - LGBTQ+ youth face higher rates
    - Reporting mechanisms matter (tell trusted adult at school)
    - School counselors can facilitate safety plans
    
    **If school is unsafe:** Online support (Trevor Project chat/text), LGBTQ+ youth hotlines, transition to affirming environment if possible.
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Common Youth Mental Health Presentations

<InsightGrid columns={1}>
  <Insight title="Warning Signs in Youth" color="red">
    **Academic:** Sudden grade drop, school refusal, concentration problems
    
    **Social:** Withdrawal from friends, dramatic peer group change, victimization/bullying
    
    **Behavioral:** Risky behaviors (substance use, reckless driving, sexual activity), self-harm (cutting often hidden), giving away possessions
    
    **Emotional:** Irritability (depression in teens often looks like anger), hopelessness, frequent crying
    
    **Physical:** Sleep changes, appetite changes, unexplained aches/pains, fatigue
    
    **Always assess suicide risk** — youth suicide rates have risen significantly in past decade.
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Key Takeaways

- Half of lifetime mental illness begins by age 14; adolescent brain development affects risk-taking, emotion regulation — youth need specialized approaches respecting developmental stage
- Confidentiality with minors is limited: offer privacy but NOT absolute confidentiality; safety (suicide, abuse) overrides privacy; be transparent upfront about limits
- LGBTQ+ youth face 2-3x higher depression/anxiety, 4x higher suicide attempts due to minority stress (discrimination, family rejection, bullying); ONE accepting adult reduces suicide risk 40%
- Mandatory reporting applies for child abuse/neglect; if parent is abuser, report to CPS without telling parent; if parent is unsupportive (not abusive), connect youth to other supportive adults
- Youth mental health warning signs: academic decline, social withdrawal, behavioral changes, irritability (depression in teens), self-harm, suicide references — always assess directly for suicidal thoughts
