# Substance Use Disorders and Dual Diagnosis

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize substance use disorder signs across different substances (alcohol, opioids, stimulants, cannabis) using DSM-5 criteria and understand progression from use to dependence
- Apply motivational interviewing principles (express empathy, develop discrepancy, roll with resistance, support self-efficacy) for non-judgmental, person-centered support
- Understand dual diagnosis (co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders) complexity, integrated treatment needs, and harm reduction approaches

## Research Foundation

Substance use disorders (SUDs) affect 20% of U.S. adults in their lifetime (~46M people). Co-occurring mental health disorders (dual diagnosis) are the rule, not exception: ~50% of people with severe mental illness have SUD, ~50% with SUD have mental illness. Evidence-based treatments include: motivational interviewing (**strong** evidence), medication-assisted treatment/MAT for opioids (**strong**: reduces overdose death 50%+), CBT (**moderate**), 12-step facilitation (**moderate**). However, treatment gap is massive: only ~10% receive treatment. Stigma is the primary barrier.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (MAT, motivational interviewing); MODERATE (CBT, peer support)

## Understanding Substance Use Disorder

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="DSM-5 Criteria (2+ = SUD)" color="purple">
    1. Using more/longer than intended
    2. Unsuccessful efforts to cut down
    3. Time spent obtaining/using/recovering
    4. Cravings
    5. Failure to fulfill obligations (work, school, home)
    6. Continued use despite social/interpersonal problems
    7. Giving up activities for substance
    8. Use in hazardous situations
    9. Continued use despite physical/psychological harm
    10. Tolerance (need more for effect)
    11. Withdrawal symptoms
    
    **Severity:** 2-3 mild, 4-5 moderate, 6+ severe
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Common Substances & Effects" color="orange">
    **Depressants (slow CNS):** Alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids
    - Overdose risk: respiratory depression, death
    - Withdrawal: alcohol/benzos can be FATAL (seizures)
    
    **Stimulants (speed CNS):** Cocaine, meth, prescription stimulants
    - Overdose risk: heart attack, stroke, psychosis
    - Withdrawal: depression, fatigue, suicidal thoughts
    
    **Cannabis:** Lower acute risk but can cause dependence, motivation issues, psychosis (esp. high-THC products in vulnerable individuals)
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

<Callout type="danger" title="Medical Emergencies Requiring 911">
- **Overdose:** Unresponsive, slow/stopped breathing, blue lips → Call 911, administer Narcan if available and trained
- **Severe withdrawal:** Seizures, hallucinations, extreme agitation → Call 911
- **Acute psychosis from stimulants:** Threat to self/others → Call 911
</Callout>

## Motivational Interviewing Principles for MHFA

Traditional confrontation ("You're an addict, you need to stop") increases resistance. Motivational interviewing (MI) is person-centered, non-judgmental, and evidence-based.

<SlideNavigation>
  <Slide title="1. Express Empathy">
    **Principle:** Understand their perspective without judgment. Ambivalence about change is normal.
    
    **Say:** "It sounds like part of you wants to cut back, and part of you feels like it's the only thing helping you cope. That's really common."
    
    **Don't say:** "You're destroying your life. You HAVE to quit."
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="2. Develop Discrepancy">
    **Principle:** Help them see gap between current behavior and their own values/goals (not YOUR values).
    
    **Say:** "You mentioned wanting to be there for your kids. How does your drinking affect that goal?"
    
    **Don't say:** "Normal parents don't drink that much."
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="3. Roll With Resistance">
    **Principle:** Don't argue. Resistance is information about their readiness for change.
    
    **Say:** "It sounds like you're not ready to stop right now. That's okay. What would need to change for you to consider it?"
    
    **Don't say:** "You're in denial. You need to accept you have a problem."
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="4. Support Self-Efficacy">
    **Principle:** Believe in their ability to change. Highlight past successes.
    
    **Say:** "You quit smoking 5 years ago — that took huge strength. You have what it takes."
    
    **Don't say:** "You're too weak to do this on your own. You'll fail."
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Dual Diagnosis: Mental Health + Substance Use

<EnhancedAccordion title="Dual Diagnosis Complexity">
  <AccordionItem title="Why Dual Diagnosis is Common">
    - **Self-medication:** People use substances to cope with depression, anxiety, trauma, psychosis
    - **Shared risk factors:** Trauma, genetics, stress all increase risk for both
    - **Substance-induced mental illness:** Chronic use can cause/worsen depression, anxiety, psychosis
    
    **Which came first?** Often unclear and doesn't matter for treatment — both need addressing.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Integrated Treatment is Essential">
    **Old model (sequential):** Treat mental health first, THEN substance use (or vice versa) → FAILED. People relapsed.
    
    **Evidence-based model (integrated):** Treat BOTH simultaneously in coordinated care.
    
    **What this looks like:** Psychiatrist manages meds for depression + SUD, therapist does CBT for both, peer support for recovery.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Harm Reduction Approach">
    **Philosophy:** Meet people where they are. Reducing harm is progress even if not abstinent.
    
    **Examples:**
    - Narcan distribution (reverses opioid overdose)
    - Clean needle programs (prevents HIV/hepatitis)
    - Switching from injection to oral use
    - Reducing amount/frequency
    
    **NOT enabling:** Harm reduction saves lives while person works toward recovery. Abstinence is goal for many, but reduction is meaningful.
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Key Takeaways

- Substance use disorders affect 20% lifetime prevalence with massive treatment gap (only ~10% receive treatment) due to stigma as primary barrier
- Motivational interviewing (MI) — express empathy, develop discrepancy, roll with resistance, support self-efficacy — has STRONG evidence and is more effective than confrontation
- Dual diagnosis (co-occurring mental health + SUD) is the rule not exception (~50% overlap); integrated treatment (addressing both simultaneously) is essential, not sequential
- Harm reduction (Narcan, clean needles, reducing use) saves lives and is NOT enabling — abstinence is goal for many but reduction is meaningful progress
- Medical emergencies requiring 911: overdose (unresponsive, slow breathing, blue lips), severe withdrawal (seizures), acute stimulant psychosis with danger
