# Community Resource Navigation and Referrals

## Learning Objectives

- Map local mental health resources including crisis services (988, mobile crisis), ongoing care (community mental health centers, private practice), and peer support (NAMI, support groups)
- Overcome referral barriers (cost, insurance, wait times, transportation) through problem-solving and advocacy
- Follow up on referrals to ensure connection occurred and support ongoing engagement

## Research Foundation

Treatment gap largely due to access barriers, not lack of effective treatments. Navigation support increases treatment connection rates. Warm handoffs (personal connection vs. giving phone number) more effective. Peer support shows moderate evidence for improving engagement. Community mental health centers serve uninsured/Medicaid populations.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE (navigation support, warm handoffs, peer support)

<EnhancedAccordion title="Making Effective Referrals">
  <AccordionItem title="Warm Handoff > Cold Referral">
    **Cold:** "Here's a phone number, call them." (Low connection rate)
    
    **Warm:** "Let's call together now" or "I'll sit with you while you search" (Higher connection)
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Follow Up">

## Key Takeaways

- Treatment gap due to access barriers (cost, insurance, wait times, transport, stigma), not lack of effective treatments; navigation support increases connection
- Warm handoffs (call/search together) more effective than cold referrals (just giving phone number)
- Core resources: crisis (988, mobile crisis), ongoing (CMHC, private practice), peer (NAMI, support groups), specialized (LGBTQ+, trauma, cultural-specific)
- Follow up critical: "Did you connect? What barriers came up? How can I help?"
- Resource locators: findtreatment.gov, Psychology Today, 211, NAMI
