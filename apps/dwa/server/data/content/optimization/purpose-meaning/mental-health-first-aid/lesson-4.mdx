# De-Escalation and Crisis Intervention

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize escalating crisis situations (agitation, threat, psychosis, panic) and implement immediate safety measures for yourself and the person in distress
- Apply verbal de-escalation techniques (calm tone, simple language, validation, offering choices) proven effective in reducing agitation and preventing violence
- Determine when to continue community MHFA support vs. when to escalate to emergency services (911/988) based on clear, evidence-based criteria

## Research Foundation

Verbal de-escalation techniques are widely endorsed in crisis intervention training programs (Crisis Intervention Team / CIT models, SAMHSA guidance, psychological first aid frameworks), though high-quality RCT evidence for specific techniques in community settings is limited. Most evidence comes from psychiatric emergency settings and law enforcement CIT programs. The core principles — remain calm, use simple language, validate feelings, offer choices, maintain safe distance — are supported by consensus guidelines and trauma-informed care literature. The MHFA Australia Delphi consensus guidelines provide expert-validated protocols for crisis recognition and escalation.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE (consensus-based guidelines); EMERGING (RCT evidence for community de-escalation)

## What is a Mental Health Crisis?

A mental health crisis is any situation in which a person's behavior puts them or others at risk of harm, or when they are unable to care for themselves or function in the community due to mental health symptoms.

**Crisis indicators:**
- Active suicidal ideation with plan or intent
- Threat of harm to others
- Severe disorganization or psychosis preventing basic safety
- Acute intoxication with medical risk
- Panic attack with inability to function
- Self-harm requiring medical attention
- Inability to meet basic needs (eating, drinking, hygiene) due to mental health symptoms

**NOT necessarily a crisis (though support still needed):**
- Feeling sad or anxious without immediate safety risk
- Conflict or relationship problems
- Chronic mental health symptoms that are stable
- Someone expressing suicidal *thoughts* but no plan/intent (still requires assessment and support, but not always immediate 911)

<Callout type="warning" title="Your Safety Comes First">
**Non-negotiable principle:** If you feel unsafe at any point, remove yourself from the situation and call 911. You cannot help anyone if you are injured. Recognizing when a situation exceeds safe community response is a critical skill, not a failure.
</Callout>

## The De-Escalation Decision Tree

When you encounter someone in potential crisis, assess quickly and systematically.

<StepByStep title="Crisis Assessment & Response Protocol">
  <Step number={1} title="Assess Immediate Danger">
    **Ask yourself:**
    - Is there a weapon or object that could be used as a weapon?
    - Is the person threatening violence to self or others?
    - Are they severely disorganized or unable to communicate?
    - Am I afraid for my safety or theirs?
    
    **If YES to any:** Create distance. Call 911. Do not approach.
    
    **If NO:** Proceed to Step 2.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={2} title="Approach with Caution and Assess">
    - Approach slowly and calmly from the front (not behind — startling can escalate)
    - Maintain 3-6 feet distance (respect personal space)
    - Introduce yourself: "Hi, my name is [name]. I'm here to help. Are you okay?"
    - Observe: Are they agitated? Intoxicated? Responding to internal stimuli (hallucinations)?
    
    **If they engage calmly:** Proceed with ALGEE (Listen, provide support, encourage professional help).
    
    **If they appear agitated/escalating:** Proceed to Step 3 (de-escalation).
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={3} title="Apply Verbal De-Escalation">
    - Remain calm and speak slowly
    - Use simple, clear language
    - Validate their feelings: "I can see you're upset. That makes sense."
    - Offer choices: "Would you like to sit down, or would you prefer to stand?"
    - Do NOT argue, threaten, or make demands
    
    **If de-escalation is working (person calming):** Continue to Step 4.
    
    **If escalating despite de-escalation:** Call 911. Maintain safe distance.
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={4} title="Assess Crisis Level">
    - Are they actively suicidal with plan/intent? → Call 988 or 911
    - Are they experiencing psychosis preventing safety? → Call 911
    - Are they intoxicated with medical risk? → Call 911
    - Are they in severe distress but safe? → ALGEE + encourage 988 or professional help
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={5} title="Stay or Escalate">
    **Stay and support IF:**
    - Person is safe and can ensure their own safety
    - They're engaging in conversation
    - No immediate threat to self or others
    
    **Escalate to 911/988 IF:**
    - Active suicidal plan/intent
    - Threat to others
    - Severe psychosis or disorganization
    - Medical emergency (overdose, chest pain, injury)
    - You feel unsafe
  </Step>
  
  <Step number={6} title="After Crisis Resolution or Handoff">
    - If you stayed and supported: Document what happened, debrief with someone you trust
    - If you called 911/988: Stay until help arrives if safe; provide info to responders
    - **Self-care:** Witnessing or responding to crisis is traumatic — seek support for yourself
  </Step>
</StepByStep>

## Verbal De-Escalation Techniques: The Core Skills

De-escalation is about reducing arousal and helping someone regain emotional control. Your calm presence and communication style can prevent violence.

<SlideNavigation>
  <Slide title="Technique 1: Stay Calm (The Foundation)">
    **Why it matters:** Your nervous system affects theirs through co-regulation (mirror neurons). If you're agitated, they escalate. If you're calm, you model regulation.
    
    **How to do it:**
    - Take slow, deep breaths before approaching
    - Lower your voice (speak softly, not loudly)
    - Slow your speech
    - Keep your body language open (no crossed arms, no pointing)
    - If you feel yourself getting activated, pause or step back
    
    **What not to do:** Match their energy, raise your voice, show fear or anger
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Technique 2: Use Simple, Clear Language">
    **Why it matters:** When someone is in crisis, their prefrontal cortex (thinking brain) is offline. They cannot process complex instructions or abstract concepts.
    
    **Examples:**
    - ✅ "Can you sit down?" (clear, simple)
    - ❌ "It would be helpful if we could transition to a seated position to facilitate our conversation" (too complex)
    
    **How to do it:**
    - Short sentences (5-7 words max)
    - Concrete, specific language
    - Avoid jargon or abstract concepts
    - Repeat if needed (calmly, not impatiently)
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Technique 3: Validate Their Feelings (Not Their Actions)">
    **Why it matters:** Validation communicates "I see you; your feelings make sense." This reduces defensiveness and agitation.
    
    **Examples:**
    - "I can see you're really upset right now. That's understandable."
    - "It sounds like you're frustrated. I get that."
    - "You seem scared. That makes sense given what you're going through."
    
    **Important distinction:** Validate the FEELING (upset, scared, frustrated), not dangerous ACTIONS ("It's okay to hit someone when you're angry" ❌).
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Technique 4: Offer Choices (Restore Control)">
    **Why it matters:** Crisis often involves feeling out of control. Offering choices restores a sense of agency and reduces resistance.
    
    **Examples:**
    - "Would you like to sit down, or would you prefer to stand?"
    - "Do you want to talk here, or would somewhere quieter be better?"
    - "Would it help to take a few deep breaths, or would you rather just talk?"
    
    **What not to do:** Give ultimatums ("Calm down or I'm leaving") or make demands ("You need to sit down NOW").
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Technique 5: Maintain Safe Distance">
    **Why it matters:** Personal space violations escalate agitation. Respecting physical boundaries signals safety.
    
    **How to do it:**
    - Maintain 3-6 feet of distance (arm's length plus)
    - Do NOT corner the person or block exits (they need an escape route)
    - Do NOT touch them without explicit permission
    - If they step back, you step back
    
    **When to increase distance:** If they're pacing, clenching fists, or showing signs of escalation.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Technique 6: Do NOT Argue, Threaten, or Shame">
    **Why it matters:** These behaviors escalate conflict and trigger defensiveness.
    
    **What NOT to say:**
    - "You're being ridiculous"
    - "Calm down!" (rarely works; often escalates)
    - "If you don't stop, I'm calling the cops"
    - "You're acting crazy"
    - "There's no reason to be upset"
    
    **What TO say instead:**
    - "I hear you. Let's figure this out together."
    - "I want to help. What do you need right now?"
    - "That sounds really hard."
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## When De-Escalation Is NOT Appropriate: Immediate 911 Situations

Some situations require emergency services immediately — do NOT attempt community de-escalation.

<EnhancedAccordion title="Mandatory 911 Escalation Situations">
  <AccordionItem title="Active Violence or Weapon Present">
    **Escalate if:**
    - Person has a weapon (knife, gun, object being used as weapon)
    - Physical violence is occurring or imminent
    - Threats of violence with means to carry out
    
    **Action:** Create distance. Call 911. Do not approach. Describe the situation clearly to dispatch: "Person with a knife, threatening self-harm, at [location]."
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Severe Psychosis with Danger">
    **Escalate if:**
    - Command hallucinations telling them to harm self/others
    - Severe behavioral disorganization (cannot communicate, highly erratic)
    - Paranoid delusions leading to aggression
    - Inability to ensure basic safety
    
    **Action:** Call 911. Maintain safe distance. Speak calmly if safe: "Help is coming. I'm not going to hurt you."
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Medical Emergency / Overdose">
    **Escalate if:**
    - Unresponsive or semi-conscious
    - Slow or stopped breathing, blue lips
    - Chest pain, seizure, severe injury
    - Suspected overdose
    
    **Action:** Call 911 immediately. Provide basic first aid if trained (recovery position for unconscious person, CPR if no pulse and you're trained). Do NOT give food/water to unconscious person.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Active Suicidal Crisis with Means">
    **Escalate if:**
    - Person has access to lethal means (pills, weapon, height)
    - Expresses plan and intent to die imminently
    - Has made preparations (note, given away belongings, said goodbye)
    - Is taking steps toward suicide attempt
    
    **Action:** Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) or 911. Do not leave them alone if safe to stay. Remove means if safe and consensual (do NOT forcibly take weapon — escalates danger). Communicate your concern: "I'm calling for help because I'm worried about your safety. I care about you."
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="You Feel Unsafe">
    **Escalate if:**
    - Your intuition says this is not safe
    - Person is advancing toward you aggressively
    - You feel trapped or threatened
    - Situation is escalating despite de-escalation attempts
    
    **Action:** Remove yourself. Call 911. Your safety is paramount — you cannot help if you're injured.
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

<Callout type="danger" title="De-Escalation Limits">
**De-escalation is NOT magic.** It reduces risk, but it cannot guarantee safety. If a situation is escalating despite your best efforts, or if you feel unsafe at any point, calling 911 is the appropriate response — not a failure.
</Callout>

## Special Considerations: De-Escalating Specific Presentations

Different crisis presentations require tailored approaches.

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Panic Attack" color="blue">
    **Recognition:** Intense fear, racing heart, shortness of breath, dizziness, fear of dying/losing control, peaks in ~10 min.
    
    **De-escalation:**
    - Reassure: "This is a panic attack. It's scary but not dangerous. It will pass."
    - Guide breathing: "Let's breathe together — in through your nose, out through your mouth."
    - Ground in present: "Can you feel your feet on the floor? Can you name 5 things you see?"
    - Do NOT tell them to "just calm down" (escalates panic)
    
    **When to call 911:** If this is first-time chest pain in someone over 40 or with cardiac risk — rule out heart attack.
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Acute Psychosis" color="purple">
    **Recognition:** Hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech/behavior, paranoia.
    
    **De-escalation:**
    - Approach calmly, introduce yourself
    - Speak simply and clearly
    - Do NOT argue with delusions: "I understand you believe that. I don't see/hear it, but I believe you're experiencing it."
    - Do NOT collude with delusions: "Yes, the government is watching you" ❌
    - Focus on feelings: "That sounds frightening."
    - Low stimulation environment (quiet, few people)
    
    **When to call 911:** If person is a danger to self/others, highly agitated, or cannot ensure safety.
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Acute Intoxication" color="orange">
    **Recognition:** Slurred speech, unsteady gait, confusion, aggression, altered responsiveness.
    
    **De-escalation:**
    - Maintain safe distance (aggression more likely)
    - Do NOT argue or confront about their substance use
    - Simple language, calm tone
    - Do NOT expect rational decision-making
    - Assess for injury (intoxication masks pain)
    
    **When to call 911:** If overdose suspected (unresponsive, slow breathing, blue lips), severe aggression, or medical deterioration. Alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal can be life-threatening.
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Acute Mania" color="red">
    **Recognition:** Elevated/irritable mood, decreased sleep, pressured speech, grandiosity, impulsivity, high energy.
    
    **De-escalation:**
    - Stay calm (do NOT match their energy)
    - Do NOT argue with grandiose beliefs
    - Do NOT tell them to "calm down" (irritability is high)
    - Offer choices, maintain boundaries
    - Reduce stimulation
    
    **When to call 911:** If person is a danger to self/others (manic states carry HIGH suicide risk, especially in mixed states), severe agitation, or threatening behavior. Do NOT give them money or allow financial decisions.
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## After a Crisis: Self-Care for Helpers

Witnessing or responding to a mental health crisis is emotionally and physiologically taxing. Helpers experience vicarious trauma.

<Callout type="success" title="Post-Crisis Self-Care Protocol">
**Immediately after:**
- **Debrief:** Talk to someone you trust about what happened. Do NOT carry it alone.
- **Physical regulation:** Your body is in stress response — move (walk, stretch), breathe deeply, drink water.
- **Document:** Write down what happened (for your own processing and if needed for reporting).

**In the days after:**
- **Monitor yourself:** Signs of vicarious trauma include intrusive thoughts, avoidance, hypervigilance, sleep disruption.
- **Seek support:** Talk to a counselor if the experience was traumatic for you.
- **Know your limits:** Repeated crisis exposure without support leads to burnout. It's okay to step back.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Crisis recognition requires assessing immediate danger (weapon? Threat? Severe disorganization?) and determining safe approach vs. immediate 911 escalation
- Verbal de-escalation techniques — stay calm, use simple language, validate feelings, offer choices, maintain safe distance, avoid arguing/shaming — can reduce agitation and prevent violence
- De-escalation is NOT appropriate for active violence, weapons, severe psychosis with danger, overdose, or when you feel unsafe — these require immediate 911 response
- Different presentations (panic, psychosis, intoxication, mania) require tailored de-escalation approaches based on symptom recognition
- Post-crisis self-care for helpers is mandatory — witnessing crisis is traumatic, debrief with support, monitor yourself for vicarious trauma signs
