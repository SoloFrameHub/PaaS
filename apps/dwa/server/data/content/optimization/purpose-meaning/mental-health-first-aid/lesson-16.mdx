# Legal and Ethical Considerations

## Learning Objectives

- Understand mandatory reporting obligations (child abuse, elder abuse, imminent harm) and confidentiality limits in MHFA
- Navigate duty to warn/protect when someone threatens harm to specific person
- Recognize legal protections (ADA, HIPAA, Good Samaritan laws) and liability limits for lay responders

## Research Foundation

Mandatory reporting laws vary by state and role. Healthcare providers have stricter confidentiality (HIPAA); MHFA responders are lay helpers with different obligations. Tarasoff duty to warn applies when specific identifiable threat exists. Good Samaritan laws generally protect lay helpers acting in good faith. Legal consultation recommended for complex situations.

<Callout type="danger" title="When You MUST Break Confidentiality">
**Mandatory reporting situations:**
1. Child abuse/neglect (call CPS)
2. Elder/dependent adult abuse (call APS)
3. Imminent danger to self (suicidal with plan/intent/means) → 988/911
4. Imminent danger to others (specific threat) → warn intended victim + call 911

**Be transparent:** Tell people upfront confidentiality has limits.
</Callout>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Key Legal/Ethical Concepts">
  <AccordionItem title="HIPAA (Doesn't Usually Apply to You)">
    HIPAA applies to healthcare providers, insurers, clearinghouses. As MHFA lay responder, you're NOT bound by HIPAA.
    
    **But:** Still maintain privacy/dignity. Don't gossip. Share only what's necessary for safety.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Duty to Warn (Tarasoff)">
    If someone makes credible specific threat against identifiable person, you may have duty to warn that person + call police.
    
    **Example:** "I'm going to kill my boss John Smith tomorrow" → warn John Smith, call 911.
    
    **Vague threats:** "I hate everyone" → not specific enough for Tarasoff, but still concerning (encourage professional eval).
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Good Samaritan Protection">
    Most states protect lay helpers acting in good faith during emergencies from liability.
    
    **Limits:** Doesn't protect gross negligence or exceeding your training scope.
    
    **Stay in scope:** MHFA is assessment → referral, NOT treatment.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="ADA Protections">
    Americans with Disabilities Act protects people with mental illness from discrimination (employment, housing, public accommodations).
    
    **Your role:** Know these protections exist, refer for legal advocacy if needed.
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Ethical Principles" color="purple">
    **Beneficence:** Act in person's best interest
    **Non-maleficence:** Do no harm
    **Autonomy:** Respect their decisions (except crisis)
    **Justice:** Fair treatment regardless of identity
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="When In Doubt" color="blue">
    - Consult supervisor/crisis line (988 for guidance)
    - Document what happened
    - Err on side of safety
    - Seek legal consultation if unsure
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Key Takeaways

- Mandatory reporting: child abuse (CPS), elder abuse (APS), imminent danger to self/others (988/911) — be transparent about confidentiality limits upfront
- Duty to warn (Tarasoff): specific credible threat against identifiable person requires warning victim + calling 911
- HIPAA doesn't apply to MHFA lay responders, but maintain privacy/dignity; Good Samaritan laws generally protect lay helpers acting in good faith
- ADA protects people with mental illness from discrimination; stay in MHFA scope (assessment → referral, NOT treatment)
- Ethical principles: beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy (except crisis), justice; when in doubt, consult/document/err on safety
