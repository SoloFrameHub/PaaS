# Recognizing Warning Signs and Risk Factors

## Learning Objectives

- Identify physical, emotional, behavioral, and cognitive warning signs indicating emerging mental health challenges across different conditions
- Distinguish temporary stress responses from clinical mental health conditions requiring professional intervention
- Assess observable risk factors (loss, trauma, isolation, substance use) and protective factors (social support, coping skills, access to care) to inform helping approach

## Research Foundation

Early identification of mental health challenges significantly improves outcomes. Research demonstrates that early intervention can prevent progression to severe conditions in 40–60% of cases. The MHFA Australia Delphi consensus guidelines (updated 2021) provide expert-validated warning sign recognition criteria for depression, anxiety, psychosis, substance use, and suicide risk across 89 endorsed items. However, it's critical to note that **non-clinicians cannot diagnose** — recognition of warning signs is for the purpose of initiating support and referral, not clinical assessment.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (for warning sign education improving recognition); MODERATE (for Delphi consensus guidelines)

## The Four Domains of Warning Signs

Mental health warning signs appear across four interconnected domains: physical, emotional, behavioral, and cognitive. Recognizing patterns across multiple domains increases confidence that professional evaluation is warranted.

<SlideNavigation>
  <Slide title="Physical Warning Signs">
    **Body-based indicators that something may be wrong:**
    
    - **Sleep changes:** Insomnia, hypersomnia, or drastically altered sleep patterns
    - **Appetite/weight changes:** Significant increase or decrease, eating for comfort or not eating
    - **Energy changes:** Chronic fatigue despite rest, or unusual restlessness/agitation
    - **Physical complaints:** Unexplained headaches, stomach problems, chronic pain without medical cause
    - **Appearance changes:** Decline in grooming, hygiene, or self-care
    - **Substance use:** Increased alcohol, drug use, or new substance patterns
    
    **Important:** These signs are NOT diagnostic. Many medical conditions cause similar symptoms. The presence of physical warning signs suggests the person should see a healthcare provider for evaluation.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Emotional Warning Signs">
    **Mood and affect changes:**
    
    - **Persistent sadness or hopelessness:** Lasting weeks, not responsive to positive events
    - **Excessive worry or fear:** Out of proportion to circumstances, interfering with daily life
    - **Irritability or anger:** Short fuse, aggression, outbursts that are new or worsening
    - **Emotional numbness:** Feeling "nothing," detachment, inability to experience joy
    - **Extreme mood swings:** Rapid shifts between euphoria and despair
    - **Overwhelming guilt or shame:** Self-blame, feeling worthless or like a burden
    
    **Key distinction:** Sadness after a loss is normal grief. Persistent hopelessness that doesn't improve and interferes with functioning suggests clinical depression.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Behavioral Warning Signs">
    **Observable changes in actions and patterns:**
    
    - **Social withdrawal:** Isolating from friends, family, activities previously enjoyed
    - **Work/school decline:** Missing deadlines, absences, drop in performance
    - **Risk-taking:** Reckless driving, unsafe sex, financial impulsivity
    - **Agitation:** Pacing, hand-wringing, inability to sit still
    - **Self-harm:** Cutting, burning, scratching (often hidden under long sleeves)
    - **Giving away possessions:** Especially cherished items, as if preparing for death
    - **Sudden calm after depression:** May indicate decision to attempt suicide — reassess risk
    
    **Red flag:** Giving away possessions combined with statements like "you won't need to worry about me much longer" is a high-risk suicide warning sign requiring immediate intervention.
  </Slide>
  
  <Slide title="Cognitive Warning Signs">
    **Changes in thinking, perception, and speech:**
    
    - **Concentration problems:** Difficulty focusing, completing tasks, making decisions
    - **Memory issues:** Forgetting appointments, conversations, responsibilities
    - **Disorganized thinking:** Jumping between topics, hard to follow conversation
    - **Unusual beliefs:** Paranoia, delusions, beliefs that others are plotting against them
    - **Hallucinations:** Seeing, hearing, or feeling things others don't (psychosis indicator)
    - **Suicidal thoughts:** Thoughts of death, dying, or ending one's life
    - **Hopelessness about the future:** "Things will never get better," "There's no point"
    
    **Critical:** Disorganized thinking, unusual beliefs, and hallucinations may indicate psychosis — urgent professional evaluation needed.
  </Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Recognizing Specific Mental Health Challenges

Different conditions present with characteristic warning sign patterns. This section helps you recognize common presentations — but remember, **only licensed clinicians can diagnose.**

<EnhancedAccordion title="Warning Signs by Condition (Recognition, Not Diagnosis)">
  <AccordionItem title="Depression">
    **Classic presentation:**
    - Persistent sadness, hopelessness, loss of interest in activities
    - Sleep changes (insomnia or hypersomnia), appetite changes
    - Fatigue, low energy, difficulty concentrating
    - Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
    - Social withdrawal, decline in self-care
    - Thoughts of death or suicide
    
    **Severity indicator:** When symptoms interfere with work, relationships, or basic functioning for 2+ weeks, professional evaluation is warranted. Always assess for suicidal thoughts.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Anxiety Disorders">
    **Classic presentation:**
    - Excessive worry out of proportion to circumstances
    - Restlessness, feeling "on edge," irritability
    - Physical symptoms: racing heart, shortness of breath, sweating, trembling
    - Avoidance of situations that trigger anxiety
    - Panic attacks: intense fear with physical symptoms, peak in ~10 minutes
    - Sleep problems due to worry
    
    **When to escalate:** If panic symptoms include first-time chest pain in someone with cardiac risk factors, call 911 to rule out medical emergency (heart attack can mimic panic attack).
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Psychosis (Requires Urgent Evaluation)">
    **Classic presentation:**
    - Hallucinations: seeing, hearing, or feeling things others don't
    - Delusions: fixed false beliefs (paranoia, grandiosity, persecution)
    - Disorganized speech: hard to follow, jumping between topics
    - Disorganized or catatonic behavior
    - Significant decline in self-care and functioning
    - Social withdrawal, unusual or bizarre statements
    
    **URGENT:** Psychosis requires medical evaluation to rule out medical causes (infection, drug-induced, neurological) and initiate treatment. If person is a danger to self/others or severely disorganized, call 911.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Mania / Bipolar Disorder">
    **Classic presentation:**
    - Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood (not just "happy")
    - Decreased need for sleep without fatigue
    - Pressured speech (talking fast, hard to interrupt)
    - Grandiosity: inflated self-esteem, unrealistic beliefs about abilities
    - Impulsivity: risky financial decisions, sexual behavior, reckless driving
    - Distractibility, flight of ideas
    
    **Important:** Mania carries HIGH suicide risk, especially in mixed states (depressive symptoms + manic energy). Person may not recognize they're ill. Urgent professional evaluation needed.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Substance Use Disorders">
    **Classic presentation:**
    - Increased tolerance (need more to get same effect)
    - Withdrawal symptoms when not using
    - Using more than intended, unable to cut down
    - Continued use despite negative consequences (relationship problems, work issues)
    - Spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering from substance
    - Giving up activities to use
    
    **Caution:** Withdrawal from alcohol and benzodiazepines can be medically dangerous (seizures, death). Do NOT encourage abrupt cessation without medical supervision. Overdose (unresponsive, slow/stopped breathing, blue lips) = 911 immediately.
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Trauma Reactions (Acute Stress / PTSD)">
    **Classic presentation:**
    - Intrusive memories, flashbacks, nightmares
    - Avoidance of reminders of the trauma
    - Hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response
    - Emotional numbness or detachment
    - Irritability, anger outbursts
    - Sleep problems, concentration difficulties
    
    **Approach:** Trauma-informed care principles apply. Do NOT require the person to retell their trauma. Create safety, offer choices, validate their experience. Professional trauma-specialized therapy is most effective treatment.
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Risk Factors vs. Protective Factors: Context Matters

Warning signs don't exist in a vacuum. Understanding risk and protective factors helps you assess urgency and tailor your support approach.

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Risk Factors (Increase Vulnerability)" color="orange">
    **Situational:**
    - Recent significant loss (death, divorce, job loss, trauma)
    - Social isolation, lack of support network
    - Financial stress, housing instability
    - Chronic medical illness or pain
    - Access to lethal means (especially firearms)
    
    **Historical:**
    - Previous mental health challenges or suicide attempts
    - Family history of mental illness or suicide
    - History of trauma, abuse, or neglect
    - Substance use disorder
    
    **Identity-Based:**
    - LGBTQ+ individuals (higher rates due to minority stress, discrimination)
    - Veterans (trauma exposure, transition challenges)
    - Marginalized communities facing discrimination
    
    **Important:** Risk factors are NOT predictive in individual cases — they describe population-level patterns. Someone with many risk factors may be fine; someone with few may be in crisis.
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Protective Factors (Build Resilience)" color="green">
    **Individual:**
    - Effective coping skills
    - Problem-solving ability
    - Sense of purpose and meaning
    - Help-seeking attitudes
    - Physical health and self-care
    
    **Relational:**
    - Strong social connections
    - Trusted relationships (family, friends, mentors)
    - Sense of belonging to community
    - Cultural or faith community (for those who engage)
    
    **Systemic:**
    - Access to mental health care
    - Economic security and housing stability
    - Supportive workplace or school environment
    - Reduced access to lethal means
    
    **Your role in MHFA:** You can strengthen protective factors by connecting people to resources, reducing isolation, validating their experience, and facilitating access to care.
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Temporary Stress Response vs. Clinical Condition: How to Tell the Difference

Not every period of sadness is depression. Not every moment of worry is an anxiety disorder. Here's how to distinguish normal stress responses from clinical conditions.

<FlipCard 
  frontTitle="Normal Stress Response" 
  backTitle="May Be Clinical Condition">
  <FrontContent>
    - **Duration:** Days to a couple weeks, improves as stressor resolves
    - **Functioning:** May struggle temporarily but still managing basics
    - **Responsiveness:** Can still experience moments of joy, laughter, connection
    - **Cause:** Clearly linked to identifiable stressor
    - **Support needs:** May improve with social support, self-care, time
  </FrontContent>
  <BackContent>
    - **Duration:** Persists for weeks/months, doesn't improve or worsens
    - **Functioning:** Significant interference with work, relationships, self-care
    - **Responsiveness:** Doesn't respond to normally positive events
    - **Cause:** May have no clear trigger, or reaction is disproportionate
    - **Support needs:** Requires professional evaluation and potentially treatment
    
    **When in doubt:** Encourage professional evaluation. It's better to over-refer than to minimize real suffering.
  </BackContent>
</FlipCard>

## Red Flags Requiring Immediate Action

Some warning signs indicate IMMEDIATE risk and require urgent escalation, not "wait and see."

<Callout type="danger" title="Immediate Escalation Required (Call 911 or 988)">
**Act immediately if you observe:**

1. **Suicide warning signs:**
   - Talking about wanting to die or kill oneself
   - Looking for ways to kill oneself (searching online, obtaining means)
   - Talking about feeling hopeless or having no reason to live
   - Talking about being a burden to others
   - Increasing alcohol or drug use
   - Acting anxious or agitated; behaving recklessly
   - Sleeping too little or too much
   - Withdrawing or isolating
   - Showing rage or talking about seeking revenge
   - Displaying extreme mood swings
   - **Giving away possessions, saying goodbye**

2. **Psychosis with danger:** Command hallucinations, severe disorganization, aggression

3. **Overdose symptoms:** Unresponsive, slow breathing, blue lips

4. **Any situation where person cannot ensure their own safety**

**Action:** Call 911 or 988. Do not leave person alone if safe to stay. Do not attempt to manage alone.
</Callout>

## Cultural Considerations in Recognizing Warning Signs

Warning signs can present differently across cultures. Eye contact norms, emotional expression, help-seeking attitudes, and stigma vary significantly.

<InsightGrid columns={1}>
  <Insight title="Cultural Humility in Recognition" color="purple">
    - **Expression of distress varies:** Some cultures somatize (express emotional distress as physical symptoms). Headaches, stomach problems, or fatigue may be how depression presents.
    - **Eye contact norms differ:** Lack of eye contact may be respect, not avoidance (varies by culture and neurodivergence).
    - **Stigma affects disclosure:** In cultures with high mental health stigma, people may minimize symptoms or avoid discussing emotions directly.
    - **Spiritual/religious frameworks:** Some communities explain distress through spiritual language ("spiritual attack," "loss of harmony"). Meet people where they are; you don't need to change their framework to support them.
    - **Ask, don't assume:** "I've noticed you seem different lately — is everything okay?" is culturally safer than diagnosing-sounding statements.
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

## Key Takeaways

- Warning signs appear across four domains: physical (sleep, appetite, energy), emotional (mood changes), behavioral (withdrawal, decline in functioning), and cognitive (concentration, unusual beliefs, suicidal thoughts)
- Recognizing warning signs is for initiating support and referral, NOT for diagnosis — only licensed clinicians can diagnose mental health conditions
- Distinguishing temporary stress from clinical conditions requires assessing duration (weeks vs. days), functional impairment, and responsiveness to positive events
- Risk factors (loss, isolation, trauma, substance use) and protective factors (social support, coping skills, access to care) provide context for assessing urgency and tailoring support
- RED FLAGS requiring immediate escalation: suicide warning signs (especially giving away possessions, saying goodbye), psychosis with danger, overdose symptoms — call 911 or 988, do not manage alone

## Safety Note

⚠️ **When to Seek Professional Help:** If you notice warning signs in yourself or someone else — especially suicidal thoughts, severe withdrawal, or inability to function — professional evaluation is needed. Call 988 to talk to a trained counselor, see your GP, or contact a mental health professional. In crisis, call 911 or 988 immediately.
