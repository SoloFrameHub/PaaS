# Self-Care and Secondary Trauma Prevention

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize vicarious trauma/secondary traumatic stress signs (intrusive thoughts, hypervigilance, numbness, cynicism) and distinguish from burnout
- Implement evidence-based self-care strategies (boundaries, debrief, diversification, monitoring, seeking support) to sustain helping work
- Create personal self-care plan with concrete practices across physical, emotional, social, and spiritual domains

## Research Foundation

Vicarious trauma affects 40-85% of helping professionals. Self-care reduces burnout, vicarious trauma, and turnover. Effective strategies: supervision/debriefing (STRONG evidence), work-life balance, mindfulness (MODERATE), peer support (MODERATE). Burnout prevention requires organizational change, not just individual resilience. Helpers who don't practice self-care experience compassion fatigue, reduced effectiveness, health problems.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (vicarious trauma prevalence, supervision/debriefing benefits); MODERATE (individual self-care practices)

## Vicarious Trauma vs. Burnout

<InsightGrid columns={2}>
  <Insight title="Vicarious Trauma (Secondary Traumatic Stress)" color="red">
    **Cause:** Exposure to others' traumatic stories
    
    **Signs:** Intrusive thoughts about cases, hypervigilance, emotional numbness, cynicism, nightmares, avoidance
    
    **Feels like:** Being traumatized yourself through others' experiences
  </Insight>
  
  <Insight title="Burnout" color="orange">
    **Cause:** Chronic workplace stress, overwork, lack of support/resources
    
    **Signs:** Exhaustion, cynicism, reduced efficacy ("I can't make a difference")
    
    **Feels like:** Running on empty, nothing left to give
  </Insight>
</InsightGrid>

<Callout type="warning" title="Both Are Real — Not Weakness">
Vicarious trauma and burnout are NORMAL responses to helping work demands. Experiencing them doesn't mean you're weak or unsuited for this work. They mean you need support and self-care.
</Callout>

## Evidence-Based Self-Care Strategies

<EnhancedAccordion title="Multi-Domain Self-Care">
  <AccordionItem title="Boundaries (Non-Negotiable)">
    - **Time boundaries:** Set work hours, don't be available 24/7
    - **Emotional boundaries:** Care about people, but you're not responsible for their outcomes
    - **Saying no:** You can't help everyone; selective engagement preserves quality
    
    **Why it works:** Prevents depletion, maintains sustainability
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Debrief Regularly">
    **STRONG evidence:** Supervision/debriefing reduces vicarious trauma
    
    - Talk to colleague, supervisor, therapist after difficult cases
    - Do NOT carry traumatic stories alone
    - Formal debriefing after critical incidents
    
    **Why it works:** Processes trauma, prevents internalization
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Diversify Your Life">
    Helping work cannot be your ONLY source of meaning, identity, connection.
    
    - Hobbies unrelated to helping
    - Relationships outside work
    - Physical activity
    - Creative expression
    - Spiritual practice (if meaningful to you)
    
    **Why it works:** Provides renewal, prevents over-identification with helper role
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Monitor Yourself">
    Monthly self-check using vicarious trauma/burnout signs.
    
    **If experiencing signs:** Increase self-care, seek supervision/therapy, consider reducing caseload temporarily
    
    **Why it works:** Early intervention prevents escalation
  </AccordionItem>
  
  <AccordionItem title="Seek Your Own Support">
    Therapists need therapists. Helpers need help.
    
    - Personal therapy (not optional for sustained helping work)
    - Peer support groups
    - Supervision
    
    **Why it works:** You cannot give what you don't have
  </AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Creating Your Self-Care Plan

## When to Step Back

<Callout type="info" title="Knowing Your Limits">
**Signs you need to step back:**
- Vicarious trauma symptoms interfering with life
- Compassion fatigue (feeling nothing for people you help)
- Health problems from stress
- Relationships suffering
- Substance use to cope
- Suicidal thoughts

**Stepping back is NOT failure.** It's self-preservation. You can return to helping work after recovery, or choose different path. Your wellbeing matters.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Vicarious trauma affects 40-85% of helpers (intrusive thoughts, hypervigilance, numbness, cynicism); burnout from chronic stress/overwork — both are NORMAL responses to helping demands, not weakness
- Evidence-based self-care: boundaries (time, emotional, saying no), regular debriefing (STRONG evidence), diversify life beyond helping, monitor yourself monthly, seek own support/therapy
- Self-care plan needs practices across 4 domains: physical (sleep, movement, food), emotional (therapy, journaling, mindfulness), social (quality time, fun, boundaries), spiritual (if meaningful)
- Stepping back when experiencing severe vicarious trauma/burnout is self-preservation, NOT failure — you can return or choose different path
- Organizational factors matter: burnout prevention requires systemic change (caseload limits, supervision, resources), not just individual resilience
