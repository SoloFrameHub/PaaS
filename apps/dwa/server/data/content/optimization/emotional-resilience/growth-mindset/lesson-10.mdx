# Flexible Goal Adjustment: When to Persist and When to Pivot

## Learning Objectives

- Understand Brandtstädter's assimilation-accommodation model: tenacious goal pursuit (TEN) and flexible goal adjustment (FLEX) as complementary processes
- Learn why both persistence and disengagement predict wellbeing — and why knowing when to apply each is critical
- Recognize signals that a goal has become blocked or unattainable and requires adjustment
- Develop strategies for re-engaging with new goals after disengaging from blocked ones

## Research Foundation

Brandtstädter and Renner (1990) identified two complementary coping modes that together predict wellbeing across the lifespan: Tenacious Goal Pursuit (TEN) — persistence toward valued goals despite obstacles — and Flexible Goal Adjustment (FLEX) — revising or disengaging from goals when they become unattainable. Both scales predict higher life satisfaction and lower depression; they are orthogonal (independent) processes. The willingness to disengage from blocked goals and re-engage with new ones is consistently associated with better wellbeing, fewer depressive symptoms, and reduced anxiety — particularly under chronic adversity or major life transitions. This is equally well-evidenced as persistence but frequently underrepresented in growth mindset education.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (longitudinal, cross-sectional, lifespan developmental evidence over 30+ years)

## The Two Pathways to Wellbeing

Most growth mindset content emphasizes persistence. But the evidence shows **two independent pathways** to wellbeing.

<InsightGrid>

<InsightItem title="Tenacious Goal Pursuit (TEN)">

**What it is:** Persistence toward valued goals despite obstacles; problem-focused coping; effortful striving

**When it supports wellbeing:** Goals are attainable with sustained effort; obstacles are surmountable

**Evidence:** TEN predicts life satisfaction, goal achievement, and sense of agency

</InsightItem>

<InsightItem title="Flexible Goal Adjustment (FLEX)">

**What it is:** Disengaging from blocked goals; revising goals to fit constraints; re-engaging with new goals

**When it supports wellbeing:** Goals become objectively unattainable; persistence produces suffering without progress

**Evidence:** FLEX predicts life satisfaction, lower depression, reduced anxiety — especially under chronic adversity

</InsightItem>

<InsightItem title="Why Both Matter">

**The Finding:** TEN and FLEX are orthogonal — they're independent processes, not opposites

**Implication:** Psychologically healthy individuals can do both: persist when persistence serves goals AND disengage when goals become blocked

**Flexibility:** Knowing which to apply when is the skill

</InsightItem>

<InsightItem title="The Growth Mindset Gap">

**What's Missing:** Popular growth mindset emphasizes TEN ("never give up," "persist through challenge") but underemphasizes FLEX

**Why It Matters:** Unyielding persistence toward blocked goals produces rumination, exhaustion, and demoralization — not growth

**The Correction:** Teach both persistence and adaptive disengagement

</InsightItem>

</InsightGrid>

## When Persistence Works

Persistence is valuable — when applied to the right goals.

<EnhancedAccordion>

<AccordionItem title="Goals That Are Difficult but Attainable">

**Characteristic:** Progress is possible with sustained effort, strategy refinement, and time

**Example:** Learning a complex skill (programming, instrument, new language) where practice produces gradual improvement

**Why TEN Works:** Persistence through the "frustration zone" is how mastery develops. Early disengagement prevents skill-building

**Signal to Persist:** You see incremental progress, even if slow. Feedback suggests improvement is possible with continued effort and strategy adjustment

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Goals Aligned With Core Values">

**Characteristic:** The goal matters intrinsically, not just for outcomes

**Example:** Maintaining a relationship that's going through a difficult period but fundamentally healthy and valued

**Why TEN Works:** Values-based persistence generates meaning even through difficulty. The goal is worth the effort because it aligns with who you want to be

**Signal to Persist:** You'd regret giving up. The difficulty is temporary or surmountable, and the goal reflects deeply held values

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Goals With High Stakes and Controllable Factors">

**Characteristic:** Success has significant consequences, and you have agency over key factors

**Example:** A high-stakes project where your skills, effort, and strategy genuinely influence outcome

**Why TEN Works:** The stakes justify sustained effort, and you have sufficient control to make persistence effective

**Signal to Persist:** Your actions matter. Effort + strategy produce results, even if slowly

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Short-Term Discomfort for Long-Term Gain">

**Characteristic:** Temporary difficulty that leads to meaningful improvement or achievement

**Example:** Pushing through challenging training for a marathon, rigorous coursework for a valued degree

**Why TEN Works:** The discomfort is finite and instrumental — it serves a larger goal you care about

**Signal to Persist:** You have a clear endpoint. The difficulty is time-limited and productive

</AccordionItem>

</EnhancedAccordion>

## When Disengagement Is Adaptive

Flexible goal adjustment is not "giving up" — it's psychologically healthy adaptation to reality.

<EnhancedAccordion>

<AccordionItem title="Goals That Have Become Objectively Unattainable">

**Characteristic:** External constraints make success impossible regardless of effort

**Example:** Pursuing a career in professional athletics after a disabling injury; holding onto a relationship after the other person has definitively ended it

**Why FLEX Works:** Continued pursuit produces suffering without progress. Disengagement allows re-engagement with attainable goals

**Signal to Disengage:** Reality has changed in ways you cannot control. Persistence is not producing progress and likely never will

**Research:** FLEX predicts lower depression after uncontrollable loss (health decline, bereavement, forced career change)

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Goals That Require Unacceptable Trade-Offs">

**Characteristic:** Success is possible but requires sacrificing other core values or wellbeing

**Example:** A promotion that would require sacrificing family time or health; a relationship that requires abandoning your core values

**Why FLEX Works:** Even attainable goals aren't worth pursuing if the cost is too high. Disengagement protects what matters most

**Signal to Disengage:** You're achieving the goal but losing yourself in the process. The trade-offs outweigh the benefits

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Goals Based on Others' Expectations, Not Your Values">

**Characteristic:** You're pursuing the goal because you "should," not because it matters intrinsically

**Example:** Career path chosen to please parents; lifestyle goals based on social media comparison

**Why FLEX Works:** External motivation is unsustainable and unfulfilling. Disengagement frees energy for intrinsically valued goals

**Signal to Disengage:** Success feels empty. You're checking boxes but not building a life you value

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Goals That Are Blocked by Chronic Adversity">

**Characteristic:** Prolonged, uncontrollable obstacles prevent progress despite sustained effort

**Example:** Trying to maintain pre-illness lifestyle after chronic illness diagnosis; pursuing goals that systemic barriers consistently block

**Why FLEX Works:** Chronic persistence toward blocked goals produces learned helplessness, rumination, and depression. Adjusting goals to fit new constraints supports wellbeing

**Signal to Disengage:** Repeated effort produces no progress due to factors outside your control. The goal needs to be revised, not abandoned entirely (accommodation)

**Research:** FLEX is especially protective under chronic adversity and major life transitions (aging, disability, caregiving demands)

</AccordionItem>

</EnhancedAccordion>

This is the hardest skill: distinguishing temporary obstacles (persist) from insurmountable barriers (adjust).

## Strategies for Flexible Goal Adjustment

<SlideNavigation>

<Slide title="1. Recognize Blocked Goal Signals">

**Red Flags That a Goal May Be Blocked:**
- Repeated effort produces no progress over extended time (years)
- Progress depends entirely on factors outside your control
- Pursuing the goal requires sacrificing core values or wellbeing
- You're achieving metrics but feeling empty (misaligned values)
- The goal was never yours — it was imposed or adopted uncritically

**Practice:** Periodically audit your goals. Ask: "Is this still attainable? Still aligned with my values? Still worth the cost?"

</Slide>

<Slide title="2. Practice Goal Revision (Not Just Abandonment)">

**FLEX ≠ Giving Up Entirely**

**Flexible Adjustment:**
- Original goal: "Become a professional musician"
- Reality: Severe hearing loss
- Revision: "Teach music, compose, produce" (preserves core value of music engagement)

**Practice:** When a goal becomes blocked, ask: "What core value was this goal serving? How else can I honor that value?"

</Slide>

<Slide title="3. Normalize Re-Engagement">

**The Process:**
1. Disengage from blocked goal (acceptance, letting go)
2. Grieve the loss if needed (it's okay to feel sad)
3. Identify core values the goal was serving
4. Generate new goals aligned with those values
5. Commit to a new direction

**Why It Matters:** FLEX includes active re-engagement, not passive resignation. Research shows re-engagement after disengagement predicts wellbeing

</Slide>

<Slide title="4. Use Self-Compassion for 'Letting Go'">

**Self-Criticism:** "I'm a quitter. I should have tried harder."

**Self-Compassion:** "I worked hard toward this goal, and circumstances changed in ways I couldn't control. Letting go is an act of self-care and wisdom, not weakness. I can redirect my energy toward what's actually possible."

**Why It Matters:** Shame blocks adaptive disengagement. Self-compassion enables it.

</Slide>

<Slide title="5. Distinguish Difficulty From Impossibility">

**Difficult (Persist):**
- Progress is slow but present
- Effort + strategy produce incremental improvement
- Obstacles are surmountable with resources/time
- Setbacks are temporary

**Impossible (Adjust):**
- No progress despite sustained effort and strategy changes
- Obstacles are structural and outside your control
- Success requires sacrificing core values or health
- The goal no longer aligns with who you are

**Practice:** Ask: "If I gave this two more years of full effort, would success be likely? Would it be worth it?"

</Slide>

<Slide title="6. Build a Portfolio of Goals">

**Single-Goal Trap:** All your identity and wellbeing depend on one goal → disengagement feels catastrophic

**Portfolio Approach:** Multiple valued goals across life domains → disengaging from one blocked goal doesn't destroy your sense of purpose

**Practice:** Invest in goals across domains: relationships, health, work, learning, contribution, creativity. Resilience comes from diversification.

</Slide>

</SlideNavigation>

## Key Takeaways

- **Tenacious pursuit (TEN) and flexible adjustment (FLEX) are independent, complementary processes** — both predict wellbeing
- **Knowing when to apply each is the critical skill** — persistence serves attainable, valued goals; adjustment serves blocked goals
- **30+ years of evidence support FLEX** — disengaging from unattainable goals and re-engaging with new ones protects wellbeing, especially under chronic adversity
- **Popular growth mindset underemphasizes FLEX** — "never give up" is incomplete advice; sometimes letting go is the adaptive choice
- **FLEX is not giving up entirely** — it's disengaging from blocked specific goals while re-engaging with adjusted or alternative goals
- **Self-compassion enables disengagement** — shame keeps people trapped in futile pursuit; compassion allows adaptive pivoting
- **Goal portfolios build resilience** — diversification across life domains protects wellbeing when single goals become blocked
