# Know Your Values — The Foundation of Authentic Limits

## Learning Objectives

- Clarify your personal values using ACT-based values identification exercises
- Link values to specific boundary decisions (values-based vs. fear/guilt-driven limits)
- Practice "I value/need" language over "I have a boundary" language
- Understand why values-based communication is motivationally sustainable and culturally transferable

## Research Foundation

Values-based communication comes from Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which has a robust evidence base for mental health outcomes. Applied to interpersonal effectiveness, values clarification provides the motivational architecture for authentic limit-setting: you decline a request not because of a rule ("I have a boundary") but because it conflicts with a personal value. This framing has two key advantages: it is motivationally sustainable (intrinsic rather than compliance-based) and culturally transferable (values vary across individuals and cultures without negating the framework). This lesson grounds your boundary-setting in what matters most to you.

**Evidence Grade:** MODERATE-STRONG (ACT psychological flexibility framework, assertiveness literature)

## Why Values Matter More Than Rules

Most "boundaries" advice teaches you to create rules: "I don't answer emails after 7pm." "I don't lend money." "I say no to social events on weeknights."

These rules can work — but they're fragile. They require willpower to maintain. And when someone you care about asks for an exception, the rule feels arbitrary.

**Values-based limits are different:** They're anchored in what you care about most. They flex without breaking. And when you communicate them, others understand your "why" — which makes them easier to respect.

<SlideNavigation>

<Slide title="Rule-Based Boundaries (External Compliance)">

**What they sound like:**
- "I have a boundary about X"
- "My therapist told me I need to set limits"
- "I read that I shouldn't do Y"

**Why they feel fragile:**
- Based on external authority (someone else's rule)
- Require willpower to enforce
- Feel arbitrary when you care about the person asking
- Collapse under guilt or pressure

**Example:**
"I have a boundary about not lending money to friends." Your close friend has an emergency. The rule says no, but you feel terrible. You either break the rule (and feel like you failed) or enforce it rigidly (and feel heartless).

**What's missing:**
Connection to your values. WHY do you have this limit? What matters to you that this protects?

</Slide>

<Slide title="Values-Based Limits (Internal Compass)">

**What they sound like:**
- "I value [X], so I'm not available for [Y]"
- "This matters to me because..."
- "I need [X] to stay aligned with my values"

**Why they're sustainable:**
- Based on internal motivation (what YOU care about)
- Self-renewing (you want to honor your values)
- Flexible in context (values can be prioritized differently in different situations)
- Resistant to guilt (you're honoring something important, not following an arbitrary rule)

**Example:**
"I value financial stability and security because growing up poor was terrifying. I don't lend money I can't afford to lose, because maintaining my emergency fund is how I take care of myself. If you need help, I'm happy to connect you with resources or support you in other ways."

**What's different:**
The person asking understands your WHY. They know it's not about them. The limit makes sense because it's connected to your values.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Fear/Guilt-Driven Limits (Reactive)">

**What they sound like:**
- "I'm not going to let you treat me like that anymore!"
- "I'm done being a doormat!"
- "You can't make me feel guilty!"

**Why they backfire:**
- Reactive, not values-aligned (you're pushing against something, not moving toward what matters)
- Anger-fueled (which burns out)
- Often emerge after long periods of people-pleasing (pendulum swing from passive to aggressive)
- The other person experiences it as an attack, not a limit

**Example:**
After months of saying yes to extra work projects, you explode at your boss: "I'm not doing this anymore! I have boundaries!" Your boss is confused (you never said anything before) and defensive (it feels like an accusation).

**What's missing:**
Groundedness. You're reacting to being violated, but you haven't clarified what you're protecting or why it matters.

</Slide>

</SlideNavigation>

## Linking Values to Boundary Decisions

Once you know your values, you can use them to guide your limits. Here's how.

<EnhancedAccordion>
<AccordionItem title="Example: Work-Life Boundaries (Value: Family Time + Rest)" icon="💼">

**The request:** Your boss asks if you can work late again this week.

**Rule-based response:**
"I have a boundary about working late."

**Values-based response:**
"I value being present for my family in the evenings — that's when we connect and I get to be the parent I want to be. I also value rest, because I've learned I can't sustain high performance without it. I can't work late this week. If there's a true emergency, let's talk about how to prioritize so I can help without sacrificing what matters most to me."

**Why it works:**
- Your boss understands your WHY (not arbitrary)
- You've left room for negotiation in true emergencies (flexible, not rigid)
- You've communicated self-respect without attacking them

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Example: Social Boundaries (Value: Solitude + Energy Management)" icon="🧘">

**The request:** A friend wants you to join a group outing this weekend.

**Rule-based response:**
"I don't do group events on weekends."

**Values-based response:**
"I'm an introvert, and I've learned I need solitude to recharge — especially after a full week of work. I value our friendship, and I also value protecting my energy so I can show up well in my life. I'm going to pass on the group outing, but I'd love to grab coffee one-on-one next week if you're up for it."

**Why it works:**
- You've explained that it's about your needs, not them
- You've offered an alternative that honors both the friendship and your values
- They understand it's not rejection — it's self-care

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Example: Family Boundaries (Value: Emotional Safety + Authenticity)" icon="👨‍👩‍👧">

**The request:** A family member asks you to attend a family event where someone who hurt you will be present.

**Rule-based response:**
"I don't go to events if [person] is there."

**Values-based response:**
"I value family connection, and I also value emotional safety and authenticity. Being around [person] right now doesn't feel emotionally safe for me — I'm still processing what happened. I'm not ready to be in that space yet. I'd love to see you separately, or attend future events when I feel more grounded."

**Why it works:**
- You've named both values (connection + safety)
- You've explained it's not permanent (you're processing, not cutting off)
- You've offered an alternative that honors the relationship

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Example: Financial Boundaries (Value: Security + Generosity)" icon="💰">

**The request:** A friend asks to borrow money.

**Rule-based response:**
"I have a boundary about lending money."

**Values-based response:**
"I value financial security deeply — growing up without it taught me how important it is to protect my stability. I also value generosity and supporting people I care about. I can't lend money I need for my own security, but I'm happy to help you think through other options, or connect you with resources, or support you in non-financial ways."

**Why it works:**
- You've explained the WHY (not arbitrary)
- You've affirmed the relationship (you care about them)
- You've offered alternative support (generous within your limits)

</AccordionItem>

## Practice: "I Value/Need" Language

Values-based communication uses "I value" and "I need" language instead of "I have a boundary" or "You can't."

Here's the structure:

**Template:**
1. **Acknowledge the request/relationship:** "I value [relationship/their need]..."
2. **Name your value:** "...and I also value [your core value]..."
3. **State your limit:** "...so I'm not available for [request] because [how it conflicts with your value]."
4. **Offer alternative if appropriate:** "What I CAN do is [alternative that honors both]."

<Callout type="info" title="Values-Based Communication Template">

**Example 1 (declining extra work):**
"I value doing excellent work and contributing to the team, and I also value rest and sustainability. I can't take on this extra project right now because I'm at capacity and taking it on would compromise the quality of my current work and my wellbeing. What I CAN do is help you prioritize, or identify someone else who has bandwidth."

**Example 2 (declining social event):**
"I value our friendship, and I also value alone time to recharge. I'm going to skip the group outing this weekend because I need solitude right now. What I CAN do is meet you one-on-one next week — that would feel more aligned with where I'm at."

**Example 3 (declining family obligation):**
"I value family and staying connected, and I also value financial stability and rest. I can't host Thanksgiving this year because it's more than I can take on financially and emotionally. What I CAN do is contribute a dish, or attend a gathering someone else hosts."

</Callout>

## Why Values-Based Communication Works Across Cultures

This is one of the most important advantages of values-based limits: they're culturally transferable.

## Key Takeaways

- **Values-based limits are sustainable** because they're intrinsically motivated (you want to honor your values) rather than externally imposed (someone told you to set boundaries)
- **Values provide your "why"** — which makes limits understandable and respectable to others, rather than arbitrary or defensive
- **Values vary across people and cultures** — the framework is transferable because it doesn't impose individualistic norms as universal
- **"I value/need" language** communicates limits without blame, defensiveness, or rigidity — it centers your internal experience rather than policing others' behavior
- **You can honor multiple values simultaneously** — boundary-setting isn't about choosing yourself over others; it's about finding ways to honor all your values sustainably

## Cultural Note

This lesson uses ACT's values framework, which is evidence-based and culturally adaptable. However, the examples lean toward North American/Western contexts. If you're from a collectivist culture, you may need to adapt the language:

**Instead of:** "I value autonomy and independence"  
**Consider:** "I value family connection AND sustainable capacity to contribute to family wellbeing"

**Instead of:** "I value putting myself first"  
**Consider:** "I value taking care of myself so I can show up well for my community"

Your values are legitimate. The framework serves you, not the other way around.
