# Recognizing Coercive and Manipulative Patterns ⚠️ SAFETY CRITICAL

## Learning Objectives

- Identify coercive control patterns: monitoring, isolation, gaslighting, financial control, DARVO
- Distinguish coercive control (ongoing pattern designed to dominate) from conflict or poor communication
- Understand when assertiveness training is insufficient and potentially dangerous
- Access safety resources and understand safety planning as the appropriate response

## Research Foundation

Coercive control is an ongoing, repetitive pattern of behaviors designed to establish dominance and induce psychological, practical, and social dependency in a partner — distinct from episodic physical violence or poor communication. A 2024 Australian Institute of Family Studies (AIFS) rapid literature review identified key coercive control patterns: invasion, confusion (gaslighting, DARVO — Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender), and isolation. A 2025 Australian national survey found that over 25% of young people aged 14-20 confuse controlling behaviors with "healthy boundaries" — highlighting the critical need for coercive control education at the psychoeducational level. A 2021 systematic review of safety planning with intimate partner violence (IPV) survivors (17 studies) found that effective interventions were empowerment-and-advocacy focused — NOT communication skills training.

**Evidence Grade:** EMERGING-MODERATE (coercive control research expanding; policy-relevant and safety-critical)

## What Is Coercive Control?

Coercive control is an ongoing pattern of behaviors designed to dominate, control, and induce dependency in a partner. It is **not** the same as conflict, poor communication, or even isolated incidents of anger.

<SlideNavigation>

<Slide title="Coercive Control: The Pattern">

**Definition (AIFS 2024):**
Ongoing, repetitive behaviors to control and isolate a partner, creating dominance and dependency. Distinct from episodic physical violence — coercive control is about psychological domination.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Pattern, not incident:** It's not one bad fight; it's a consistent pattern over time
- **Designed to control:** The goal is dominance, not conflict resolution
- **Creates dependency:** Psychological, social, financial, or practical dependency
- **Escalates over time:** Control tightens progressively

**Why it's hard to recognize:**
- Often no physical violence (or physical violence is rare/strategic)
- Abuser may be charming to outsiders (isolation is easier if no one believes you)
- Victim experiences self-doubt ("Maybe I'm overreacting")
- Cultural narratives normalize control as "love" or "care"

**Why "better boundaries" don't work:**
The abuser is not struggling with communication — they are *intentionally controlling you*. Assertiveness training cannot address intentional domination.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Conflict vs. Coercive Control">

**Healthy conflict (even intense):**
- Both people can express their perspective
- Goal is resolution or understanding
- Repair happens after rupture
- You feel heard (even if you disagree)
- Power is relatively balanced
- You can disagree without fear

**Coercive control:**
- One person's perspective dominates consistently
- Goal is compliance, not resolution
- No genuine repair (may apologize but pattern continues)
- You feel silenced, dismissed, or crazy
- Power is profoundly imbalanced
- Disagreement is punished (silent treatment, rage, threats)

**The test:**
- In healthy conflict, you can say "I disagree" and be heard
- In coercive control, saying "I disagree" leads to punishment, intimidation, or gaslighting

</Slide>

<Slide title="Poor Communication vs. Coercive Control">

**Poor communication (skill deficit):**
- Both people struggle to express needs clearly
- Misunderstandings happen frequently
- Both people want to improve but lack skills
- Conflict feels frustrating but not frightening
- Power imbalance is situational, not chronic
- Therapy/skills training helps

**Coercive control (intentional domination):**
- One person communicates clearly when it serves them; confusion is strategic
- "Misunderstandings" are often gaslighting (intentional reality distortion)
- One person wants control; the other wants peace
- Conflict feels frightening (you're walking on eggshells)
- Power imbalance is structural and intentional
- Therapy/skills training can escalate danger (abuser learns new manipulation tactics)

**The test:**
- Poor communication improves with psychoeducation and practice
- Coercive control does NOT improve with psychoeducation — it may escalate

</Slide>

</SlideNavigation>

## DARVO: A Common Manipulation Tactic

DARVO is one of the most documented tactics used by abusers to confuse victims and evade accountability.

<Callout type="info" title="DARVO: Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender">

**How it works:**

1. **Deny:** "I never said that. That didn't happen. You're imagining things."
2. **Attack:** "You're too sensitive. You're crazy. You always make things up."
3. **Reverse Victim and Offender:** "I'm the one who's hurt here. YOU'RE abusing ME by accusing me of this."

**Why it's so effective:**
- You start defending yourself instead of addressing the original issue
- You question your own reality (gaslighting effect)
- The abuser becomes the "victim" and you become the "aggressor"
- Outsiders may believe the abuser's version (they're often skilled at public performance)

**Example:**

You: "When you yelled at me in front of your friends, I felt humiliated."

DARVO response:
- **Deny:** "I didn't yell. You're exaggerating."
- **Attack:** "You're so oversensitive. No one else had a problem with it."
- **Reverse:** "I can't believe you're attacking me right now. I'm the one who should be upset — you embarrassed ME by bringing this up."

**Result:** You feel confused, guilty, and like you're the problem. The original issue (being yelled at) is never addressed.

</Callout>

## Other Coercive Control Patterns

DARVO is one tactic. Here are others commonly identified in coercive control research.

<EnhancedAccordion>
<AccordionItem title="Monitoring and Surveillance" icon="👁️">

**What it looks like:**
- Checking your phone, email, or social media without permission
- Tracking your location constantly (GPS, apps, showing up unannounced)
- Demanding detailed accounts of where you've been and who you've seen
- Installing cameras or spyware to monitor you

**Framed as:**
"I just care about you so much" / "I want to keep you safe" / "If you have nothing to hide, why do you care?"

**Why it's control:**
It's not about care — it's about dominance. You lose privacy, autonomy, and the ability to have relationships or experiences outside the abuser's control.

**Research finding:**
Monitoring is associated with psychological distress, loss of autonomy, and fear. It often escalates to other forms of control or violence.

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Isolation from Support Systems" icon="🔒">

**What it looks like:**
- Discouraging or preventing contact with friends/family ("They don't really care about you like I do")
- Criticizing your support system until you stop seeing them
- Creating conflict whenever you spend time with others
- Moving you away from your support network geographically
- Monopolizing your time so you have no space for other relationships

**Framed as:**
"I just want us to be close" / "Your friends are a bad influence" / "Why do you need other people when you have me?"

**Why it's control:**
Isolation increases dependency and makes it harder to leave. When you have no outside perspective, the abuser controls your reality.

**Research finding:**
Social isolation is one of the strongest predictors of severe abuse and difficulty leaving. Abusers know: if you have no one else, you're easier to control.

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Gaslighting (Reality Distortion)" icon="🌀">

**What it looks like:**
- Denying things they clearly said or did ("I never said that — you're making it up")
- Telling you you're "too sensitive" or "crazy" when you express hurt
- Rewriting history to make you doubt your memory
- Trivializing your feelings ("You're overreacting")
- Turning their behavior into your fault ("I only yelled because YOU made me")

**Framed as:**
"You're remembering wrong" / "You're too emotional to see this clearly" / "I'm just being honest — you can't handle the truth"

**Why it's control:**
When you doubt your own perception of reality, you lose the ability to trust yourself — which makes you dependent on the abuser's version of reality.

**Research finding:**
Gaslighting is psychologically damaging and associated with anxiety, depression, and loss of self-trust. It's a core component of coercive control.

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Financial Control" icon="💰">

**What it looks like:**
- Controlling all money and giving you an "allowance" like a child
- Preventing you from working or sabotaging your job
- Running up debt in your name
- Hiding financial information or making major financial decisions without you
- Taking your paycheck or controlling how you spend your own money

**Framed as:**
"I'm better with money than you" / "I'm taking care of us" / "You don't need to worry about finances"

**Why it's control:**
Financial dependency makes it nearly impossible to leave. Without money, you can't access housing, transportation, legal help, or safety.

**Research finding:**
Financial abuse is present in 99% of intimate partner violence cases (2015 study). It's both a form of control and a barrier to leaving.

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Reproductive Coercion" icon="⚠️">

**What it looks like:**
- Sabotaging birth control (poking holes in condoms, hiding/throwing away pills)
- Pressuring or forcing pregnancy
- Pressuring or forcing abortion/miscarriage
- Using pregnancy/children to maintain control ("You can't leave — we have kids")

**Framed as:**
"I want to start a family with you" / "If you really loved me, you'd want my baby"

**Why it's control:**
Pregnancy and children create long-term ties and dependency — physical, financial, emotional. Reproductive coercion is about trapping you, not about family.

**Research finding:**
Reproductive coercion is associated with intimate partner violence, unintended pregnancy, and difficulty leaving abusive relationships.

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Threats and Intimidation" icon="😨">

**What it looks like:**
- Threatening to harm you, your children, your pets, or themselves
- Threatening to destroy your property or reputation
- Threatening to take your children or report you to authorities (immigration, CPS)
- Displaying weapons or using size/strength to intimidate
- Punching walls, throwing objects, driving recklessly

**Framed as:**
"I would never hurt YOU — I just got frustrated" / "I'm just venting" / "You made me so angry I couldn't control myself"

**Why it's control:**
The threat doesn't have to be carried out — the fear it creates is enough to control behavior. You modify what you say/do to avoid triggering the threat.

**Research finding:**
Threats are a strong predictor of future physical violence. Intimidation is designed to create fear-based compliance.

</AccordionItem>

<Callout type="error" title="Why Couples Therapy Is Contraindicated in Abusive Relationships">

**Popular belief:** "We just need to learn to communicate better — couples therapy will help."

**Research reality:** Couples therapy is **dangerous** in abusive relationships. Here's why:

1. **Therapy assumes both parties are contributing to the problem** — but abuse is not a "relationship issue," it's one person's choice to dominate the other
2. **Abusers weaponize therapy language** — they learn terms like "boundaries," "triggers," and "needs" and use them to justify control ("I have a boundary that you can't see your friends without me")
3. **Therapist may not recognize abuse** — if the abuser is charming and the victim is traumatized/emotional, the therapist may misidentify the victim as "the problem"
4. **Sharing vulnerabilities in therapy gives the abuser ammunition** — they'll use what you said against you later
5. **Safety cannot be addressed in couples format** — the victim cannot speak freely with the abuser present

**What the research recommends instead:**
- **Individual therapy for the victim** (trauma-informed, empowerment-focused)
- **Specialized abuser intervention programs** for the abuser (NOT couples therapy)
- **Safety planning and advocacy** (domestic violence specialists)
- **Legal support** (protection orders, custody support, divorce advocacy)

</Callout>

## Safety Planning Basics

If you're in or leaving an abusive relationship, safety planning is essential. Here are research-supported components.

<EnhancedAccordion>
<AccordionItem title="Safety Planning Components (2021 Systematic Review)" icon="🛡️">

**What safety planning includes:**

1. **Comprehensive needs assessment:** Understanding your specific situation, risks, and resources
2. **Psychoeducation about IPV:** Naming the pattern (like this lesson does) reduces self-blame
3. **Identifying threats to safety:** Who, what, when, where — specific risk factors
4. **Developing concrete safety strategies:**
   - Safe place to go (friend's house, shelter, hotel)
   - Important documents gathered and stored safely (ID, birth certificates, bank info, legal papers)
   - Emergency bag packed (clothes, medications, cash, phone charger)
   - Safety plan for children and pets
   - Code word with trusted friend/family to signal danger
5. **Linking with resources:** Legal aid, financial assistance, housing, employment support, therapy

**Research finding (2021 PMC):**
Effective safety planning interventions are empowerment-and-advocacy focused, NOT skills-training focused. The goal is safety, not communication improvement.

**Where to get safety planning help:**
- National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233)
- Local domestic violence advocacy organizations
- Women's shelters (many serve all genders despite the name)
- Legal aid clinics

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Why Leaving Is Hard (It's Not Weakness)" icon="💔">

**Common question:** "Why don't they just leave?"

**Research-backed reasons leaving is difficult:**

1. **Financial dependency:** Abuser controls money; victim has no resources to leave
2. **Isolation:** Abuser has cut off support system; victim has nowhere to go
3. **Children:** Fear of losing custody, concern for children's safety, desire to keep family intact
4. **Threats:** Abuser has threatened to harm victim, children, pets, or themselves
5. **Trauma bonding:** Intermittent reinforcement (cycles of abuse + apology/affection) creates powerful psychological attachment
6. **Cultural/religious beliefs:** Divorce is stigmatized; family/community pressure to stay
7. **Immigration status:** Abuser threatens to report victim to immigration authorities
8. **Realistic fear:** **Leaving is the most dangerous time** — risk of severe violence or homicide increases when victim attempts to leave
9. **Shame and self-blame:** "Maybe I'm overreacting. Maybe it's my fault. Maybe I can fix it."
10. **Love:** You love them. You remember who they were/can be. You grieve the relationship you hoped for.

**All of these reasons are valid. Staying does not mean you're weak — it means you're surviving in an impossible situation.**

**Research finding:** On average, it takes **seven attempts** to leave an abusive relationship permanently. Each attempt is not failure — it's courage.

</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Supporting Someone in an Abusive Relationship" icon="🤝">

**If someone you care about is experiencing coercive control:**

**DO:**
- Believe them (many victims aren't believed, which increases isolation)
- Listen without judgment ("I'm here for you, no matter what you decide")
- Validate their reality ("That sounds really hard. You don't deserve to be treated that way.")
- Offer specific, practical help ("I can hold onto important documents for you," "You can stay with me anytime")
- Respect their timeline (they may not be ready to leave — that's okay)
- Help them access resources (hotlines, advocates, shelters)
- Stay connected even if they go back (most people leave multiple times before leaving permanently)

**DON'T:**
- Pressure them to leave ("Just leave!" feels dismissive and increases shame)
- Badmouth the abuser excessively (they may still love them; this creates conflict of loyalty)
- Offer couples therapy (contraindicated in abuse)
- Confront the abuser (this can escalate danger for the victim)
- Give up on them if they return to the relationship (your consistent presence is a lifeline)

**Research finding:** Social support is one of the strongest protective factors for domestic violence survivors. Your non-judgmental presence matters.

</AccordionItem>

## Key Takeaways

- **Coercive control** = ongoing pattern of behaviors designed to dominate, control, and create dependency — distinct from conflict or poor communication
- **DARVO** (Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender) is a common manipulation tactic that creates confusion and self-doubt
- **Other coercive control patterns:** monitoring, isolation, gaslighting, financial control, reproductive coercion, threats
- **Assertiveness is contraindicated in abusive relationships** — it can escalate danger; safety planning is the appropriate response
- **Couples therapy is dangerous in abusive relationships** — abusers weaponize therapy; individual support and advocacy are appropriate
- **Leaving is the most dangerous time** — safety planning with specialists is essential
- **25% of young people confuse controlling behaviors with "healthy boundaries"** — education matters
- **You are not the problem** — abuse is not a relationship issue; it's one person's choice to control another

## A Final Message

If you recognized yourself in this lesson — if you're experiencing monitoring, isolation, gaslighting, financial control, or any pattern described here — **please know this:**

**You are not crazy. You are not too sensitive. You are not overreacting. You are not the problem.**

Coercive control is designed to make you doubt yourself. The confusion you feel is not a character flaw — it's the intended outcome of manipulation.

**You deserve:**
- To be believed
- To feel safe in your home
- To have friendships and support outside your relationship
- To make your own decisions
- To have access to your own money
- To disagree without fear
- To be treated with respect

**Reaching out for help is not betrayal. It's survival.**

The National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233) is confidential, free, and available 24/7. They will not judge you. They will not pressure you. They will help you understand your options and support you in whatever decisions you make.

**You are not alone. There is help available. You deserve safety and freedom.**
