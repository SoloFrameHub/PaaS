# Nature Prescribing and Green Social Prescribing

Imagine your doctor writing a prescription: "Walk in green space 3× per week, 30 minutes minimum." Or a mental health provider referring you to a park-based walking group instead of (or alongside) medication.

This is **nature prescribing** — an emerging clinical delivery model where healthcare providers formally refer patients to structured nature-based activities as part of treatment plans. When combined with social connection, it becomes **green social prescribing**: linking patients to community-based outdoor activities with peer support.

<InfoBox title="What Is Nature Prescribing?" variant="note">
**Nature prescribing** (also called "park prescriptions" or "green prescriptions") is a formal clinical practice where healthcare providers (physicians, nurses, mental health clinicians) assess patients and write prescriptions or referrals for nature-based activities.

**Core elements:**
- **Clinical assessment:** Provider evaluates patient's mental/physical health needs
- **Structured recommendation:** Specific nature activity, duration, frequency (e.g., "30 min walks in [park name], 3×/week for 8 weeks")
- **Follow-up tracking:** Monitor adherence and outcomes like medication compliance
- **Integration with treatment:** Nature prescription complements (not replaces) standard care

**Green social prescribing** adds:
- **Community linkage:** Connection to organized outdoor groups (walking groups, gardening programs)
- **Peer support:** Shared experience with others facing similar health challenges
- **Link workers:** Staff who help patients access programs and navigate barriers

**Evidence strength:** Emerging-to-Moderate. A 2024 RCT-level study of nature-based social prescribing found significant mental health improvements vs. waitlist control. NHS England pilots show promise but need larger-scale evaluation.
</InfoBox>

<InfoBox title="How Nature Prescribing Works in Clinical Practice" variant="info">
**Step 1: Clinical Assessment**
- Provider screens for mental health (depression, anxiety), physical health (hypertension, obesity, diabetes), social isolation
- Assesses baseline outdoor activity, nature access, barriers (mobility, safety, time)

**Step 2: Tailored Prescription**
- **For depression/anxiety:** Green exercise prescription (walking, hiking 30+ min, 3–5×/week)
- **For stress/burnout:** Forest bathing or park time (20–30 min daily or near-daily)
- **For loneliness/isolation:** Green social prescribing (connection to walking groups, gardening programs)
- **For chronic disease:** Outdoor activity appropriate to condition (gentle walks, seated nature time, aquatic exercise)

**Step 3: Resource Connection**
- Provide specific locations (nearby parks, trails, gardens with accessibility features)
- Connect to programs (walking groups, park naturalist-led walks, adaptive outdoor programs)
- Issue passes or vouchers if cost/access barriers exist

**Step 4: Follow-Up**
- Track adherence at follow-up visits (like medication compliance)
- Adjust prescription based on patient feedback (too challenging, weather barriers, safety concerns)
- Measure outcomes (mood scales, blood pressure, physical activity levels)

**Link worker role (green social prescribing):** Dedicated staff who help patients navigate barriers, accompany them to first group session, address transportation/safety concerns, maintain connection over weeks.
</InfoBox>

<CalloutBox variant="success">
**Benefits of Clinical Integration (Why "Prescribing" Matters)**

**1. Provider endorsement increases adherence:** Patients follow recommendations more consistently when framed as "prescription" vs. casual advice. Clinical authority signals importance.

**2. Addresses "green care deserts":** Prescribing identifies access gaps when patients lack nearby quality green space — prompts policy advocacy for equitable park access.

**3. Trackable outcomes:** Structured prescriptions enable measurement (did patient adhere? Did symptoms improve?) — building evidence base for nature-based interventions.

**4. Insurance/payment pathways:** Some systems exploring reimbursement for nature prescriptions (like cardiac rehab or physical therapy) — clinical framework enables this.

**5. Reduces healthcare utilization:** Preliminary data shows reduced GP visits, emergency room use, and medication needs when nature prescriptions are followed.

**6. Equity tool:** Link workers help disadvantaged patients overcome access barriers (transportation, safety concerns, lack of social networks) — addressing Nature Gap.
</CalloutBox>

<CalloutBox variant="info">
**The Future of Nature Prescribing: Policy and Research Directions**

**Needed policy changes:**
- **Reimbursement pathways:** Insurance coverage for nature prescriptions (like physical therapy) requires ICD billing codes, outcome standards
- **Equitable green space access:** Investment in park development in "green care deserts" — environmental justice and public health converge
- **Link worker workforce:** Training and funding for staff who support patients in accessing nature programs

**Research priorities:**
- **Large-scale RCTs:** Adequately powered trials comparing nature prescriptions to standard care for specific conditions (major depression, GAD, hypertension)
- **Dose-response refinement:** Optimal frequency, duration, setting type for different mental health conditions
- **Long-term outcomes:** Do nature prescriptions produce sustained mental health benefits (1+ year follow-up)?
- **Mechanism studies:** Which components drive benefits (exercise, nature, social, placebo/expectancy)?
- **Cost-effectiveness:** Healthcare cost savings from reduced medication, hospitalization, GP visits

**Promise:** If RCTs demonstrate clinical efficacy comparable to standard treatments, nature prescribing could become standard-of-care for mild-to-moderate depression, anxiety, and stress-related conditions by 2030.
</CalloutBox>

## Lesson Summary

You've explored nature prescribing as an emerging clinical delivery model:

1. **Nature prescribing** is formal clinical practice where healthcare providers assess patients and write prescriptions for structured nature-based activities as part of treatment plans
2. **Green social prescribing** adds community linkage (outdoor groups) and link workers (staff who help patients access programs and overcome barriers)
3. **Evidence (emerging-to-moderate):** 2024 RCT showed significant mental health improvements; NHS England pilots promising; needs larger-scale evaluation
4. **Clinical formats:** Depression/anxiety prescriptions (green exercise 30–45 min, 3–5×/week), stress reduction (forest bathing 20–30 min daily), social isolation (group programs), chronic disease management (adapted outdoor activity)
5. **Benefits of clinical integration:** Provider endorsement increases adherence, trackable outcomes build evidence, addresses access inequity via link workers, potential insurance reimbursement pathways
6. **Implementation challenges:** Access inequity (green care deserts), weather/seasonal barriers, provider training needs, reimbursement/sustainability gaps
7. **Future directions:** Policy advocacy for insurance coverage, equitable green space investment, large-scale RCTs, long-term outcome studies
