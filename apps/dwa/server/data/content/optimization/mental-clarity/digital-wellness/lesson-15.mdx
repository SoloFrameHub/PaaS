# Family Media Practices and Parental Communication

## Learning Objectives

- Understand why **quality parent-child communication reduces cyberbullying** (both perpetration and victimization)
- Learn **age-appropriate guidelines** from WHO, APA, and AAP (under 1, 1-2, 3-4, 5-12, 13-19)
- Apply **family media plan frameworks** and youth-centered approaches
- Recognize the difference between **monitoring and surveillance** in supporting healthy digital habits

## Research Foundation

This lesson synthesizes family-level digital wellness strategies grounded in developmental psychology and prevention research. Quality of parental communication significantly reduces both cyberbullying perpetration and victimization — communication quality matters more than restrictive control. APA (2023) recommends consistent monitoring, family media plans, and open conversations about platform risks and benefits for adolescents. WHO (2019), AAP, and APA provide age-specific guidelines for children (under 1: no screens; 1-2: none to max 1 hour; 3-4: max 1 hour; focus on sedentary screen time's displacement of physical activity and sleep). The Stanford Youth Safety and Digital Wellbeing Report (2025) advocates a youth-centered approach that avoids placing sole responsibility on parents while providing accessible, evidence-based family tools. The underlying principle: collaborative, developmentally appropriate guidance supports healthier technology relationships than surveillance or prohibition alone.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG for parent-child communication reducing cyberbullying; MODERATE for structured media plans

## Why Family Media Practices Matter

**The research is clear:** Quality parent-child communication about technology significantly reduces:
- Cyberbullying perpetration (children are less likely to bully others)
- Cyberbullying victimization (children are more likely to tell parents when targeted, enabling early intervention)
- Problematic smartphone use
- Exposure to harmful content

**What doesn't work as well:**
- Surveillance without conversation (monitoring every message but never discussing values or safety)
- Outright prohibition ("no social media until you're 18")
- Inconsistent boundaries (rules that change arbitrarily or aren't enforced)

**What DOES work:**
- **Open, non-judgmental conversations** about what kids are seeing and experiencing online
- **Collaborative rule-setting** (family media plans developed together, not imposed top-down)
- **Modeling healthy digital behavior** (parents who doomscroll or ignore family during meals teach kids to do the same)
- **Developmentally appropriate boundaries** (different rules for a 4-year-old vs. a 14-year-old)

<Callout type="info" title="Communication Quality > Control">
**Research finding (cyberbullying prevention):**
Quality of parental communication — warmth, openness, trust, responsiveness — was MORE protective against cyberbullying than restrictive monitoring or device confiscation.

**Why:** Kids who trust their parents are more likely to:
- Tell parents when something bad happens online (enabling intervention)
- Internalize values (respect, empathy, consequences of actions)
- Ask for help rather than hiding problems

**Kids who fear punishment are more likely to:**
- Hide their online activity
- Not tell parents when victimized (fear of losing device privileges)
- Find workarounds (secret accounts, friends' devices)

**Implication:** Build trust FIRST. Rules without relationship create resistance.
</Callout>

## Age-Appropriate Guidelines: What the Evidence Supports

Different ages require different approaches. Here's what WHO, APA, and AAP recommend:

<SlideNavigation persistKey="age-guidelines">
<Slide title="Under 1 Year: No Screen Time">
**WHO Recommendation:** Children under 1 year should have **no screen time** (except video chat with family/friends).

**Why:**
- **Critical brain development period:** First year is when foundational neural connections form through sensory experiences, physical movement, and social interaction
- **Screens displace active learning:** Passive screen time replaces tummy time, crawling, object exploration, face-to-face interaction — all critical for development
- **No evidence of benefit:** Babies don't learn from screens the way they learn from real-world interaction

**What to do instead:**
- Face-to-face interaction (talking, singing, reading)
- Physical play (tummy time, reaching for objects, crawling)
- Sensory exploration (touching different textures, listening to sounds)

**Exception:** Video calls with grandparents or distant family are fine — this is interactive, not passive viewing.
</Slide>

<Slide title="Ages 1-2: None to Maximum 1 Hour">
**WHO/AAP Recommendation:** Ages 1-2 should have **none to maximum 1 hour** of sedentary screen time per day. Less is better.

**Why:**
- **Language development:** This age is critical for language acquisition — which requires interactive conversation, not passive video watching
- **Physical activity:** Toddlers need movement (running, climbing, exploring). Screen time displaces active play.
- **Attention span:** Excessive screen exposure in early childhood may affect attention development (though evidence is mixed)

**If you do use screens:**
- **Co-view with your child:** Watch together and talk about what you're seeing ("Look, the dog is running! Can you run like the dog?")
- **Choose high-quality content:** Sesame Street, Daniel Tiger, educational programs (not just entertainment)
- **No screens during meals or before bed**

**What to prioritize instead:**
- Reading books together (physical books, not screens)
- Outdoor play
- Creative play (blocks, drawing, pretend play)
</Slide>

<Slide title="Ages 3-4: Maximum 1 Hour, Less Preferred">
**WHO/AAP Recommendation:** Ages 3-4 should have **maximum 1 hour** of sedentary screen time per day. Less is still better.

**Why:**
- **Social-emotional development:** Preschoolers are learning empathy, emotional regulation, social skills — best learned through play with peers and adults, not screens
- **Imagination and creativity:** Open-ended play (building, pretending, creating) develops executive function and creativity better than passive screen consumption
- **Physical activity:** Still critical for motor development, health, sleep

**If you do use screens:**
- **Continue co-viewing:** Talk about emotions characters are feeling, connect to your child's experiences
- **Set clear boundaries:** "After this episode, screen time is done. Then we'll go outside."
- **Model healthy use:** Don't use your phone during meals or playtime with your child

**Red flags:**
- Tantrums when screen time ends (may indicate overuse or lack of other coping skills)
- Requesting screens constantly
- Difficulty engaging in non-screen play
</Slide>

<Slide title="Ages 5-12: Content and Context Over Time Limits">
**APA/AAP Recommendation:** Focus on **content quality and context** more than strict time limits.

**Key principles:**

1. **No screens during meals, homework, or 1 hour before bed**
   - Protects family connection, academic focus, and sleep

2. **Co-view and discuss content**
   - Watch shows/play games together; talk about what you see
   - Teach critical thinking: "Do you think that's realistic? How would you handle that situation?"

3. **Balance screen time with other activities**
   - Physical activity (1+ hour/day recommended)
   - In-person social time with friends
   - Creative hobbies (music, art, building, writing)
   - Family time

4. **Introduce digital citizenship**
   - Teach kindness, respect, privacy online
   - Discuss consequences of sharing information or images
   - Role-play: "What would you do if someone sent you a mean message?"

**Platform considerations:**
- **Ages 5-8:** Supervised use of kid-focused apps (PBS Kids, educational games)
- **Ages 9-12:** May start requesting social media (most platforms have 13+ age restrictions, but enforcement is weak). Consider waiting until 13+ or using kid-specific platforms (Messenger Kids).

**Cyberbullying prevention:** Start conversations about bullying, bystander intervention, and telling a trusted adult BEFORE problems arise.
</Slide>

<Slide title="Ages 13-19: Collaboration, Not Control">
**APA Recommendation (2023):** Adolescent social media use should involve:
- Family agreements on use (developed collaboratively)
- Monitoring for displacement of sleep and physical activity
- Limiting use before bed
- Parental involvement in platform choices
- Awareness of social comparison, targeting, and privacy

**Why this age is different:**
- **Developmental need for autonomy:** Teens need independence to develop identity — overly restrictive rules backfire
- **Peer relationships are primary:** Social connection happens online; total prohibition isolates teens from their peer group
- **Risk-taking is developmental:** Adolescent brains are wired for exploration and risk-taking (both online and offline). The goal is teaching judgment, not preventing all risk.

**Effective strategies:**

1. **Open conversations, not interrogation**
   - "What are you seeing on TikTok lately?" (not "Show me your phone right now.")
   - "Have you seen anyone being mean online?" (opens door to discussing cyberbullying)

2. **Collaborative rule-setting**
   - "What do YOU think are reasonable boundaries for phone use?"
   - Negotiate together: bedtime phone charging, screen-free family dinners, etc.

3. **Focus on values, not surveillance**
   - "We value respect and kindness — how does that apply online?"
   - "We protect our privacy — what information should you never share online?"

4. **Know the platforms they use**
   - Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, Discord, whatever is current
   - Understand how they work (algorithms, privacy settings, risks)

5. **Monitor for RED FLAGS, not every detail**
   - Changes in mood, sleep, academic performance, social withdrawal (signs of cyberbullying, PSU, or mental health issues)
   - Don't read every text message — trust unless you have a reason to be concerned

**When to intervene more actively:**
- Evidence of cyberbullying (victim or perpetrator)
- Signs of depression, anxiety, self-harm
- Exposure to harmful content (self-harm promotion, eating disorder content, extremism)
- Contact from strangers or adults
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Building a Family Media Plan

**What is a family media plan?**
A written or verbal agreement among family members about:
- When and where screens are used
- What content is appropriate
- Expectations for behavior (online and offline)
- Consequences for violations

**Why it works:**
- **Clarity:** Everyone knows the rules; reduces arguments
- **Consistency:** Applied to all family members (not just kids)
- **Collaboration:** Kids who participate in creating the plan are more likely to follow it

<StepByStep title="Creating Your Family Media Plan">
<Step title="Step 1: Identify Your Family's Values">
**What to discuss:**
"What matters most to us as a family?"

**Common values:**
- Connection and quality time together
- Health (sleep, physical activity, mental wellbeing)
- Learning and growth
- Respect and kindness
- Privacy and safety

**Why this matters:** Your media plan should reflect these values. If you value connection, you might have "no phones during family dinner." If you value sleep, you might have "all devices charge outside bedrooms."
</Step>

<Step title="Step 2: Set Screen-Free Zones and Times">
**Screen-free zones (WHERE):**
- Bedrooms (especially at night — Lesson 13)
- Dining table during meals
- Car (for short trips) — encourages conversation

**Screen-free times (WHEN):**
- During meals (breakfast, dinner, family meals)
- 1 hour before bedtime (supports sleep)
- During homework/study time (reduces distraction)
- Family activities (game night, outings, conversations)

**Key:** These rules apply to PARENTS too. Modeling matters.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 3: Discuss Content Guidelines">
**What's appropriate for different ages?**
- **Young children (under 8):** Co-viewed educational content, no unsupervised internet access
- **Older children (9-12):** Age-appropriate games, videos, websites; discuss why certain content is off-limits (violence, sexual content, misinformation)
- **Teens (13+):** More autonomy, but discuss risks (privacy, social comparison, misinformation, cyberbullying)

**Teach critical evaluation:**
- "Is this source trustworthy?"
- "How does this content make you feel?"
- "Would you be comfortable with Grandma seeing this?"
</Step>

<Step title="Step 4: Agree on Consequences for Violations">
**What happens if rules are broken?**

**Effective consequences:**
- Natural consequences: "You stayed up late on your phone, so you're tired today. Tonight, phone charges in the kitchen."
- Time-limited device restriction: "Phone is in the basket for the rest of today. Tomorrow we try again."
- Loss of specific privileges: "You can still text friends, but no social media for 3 days."

**Ineffective consequences:**
- Permanent confiscation (creates resentment, doesn't teach self-regulation)
- Public shaming ("I'm posting about your bad behavior online")
- Inconsistent enforcement (rules enforced randomly)

**Key principle:** Consequences should teach, not punish. The goal is helping kids develop self-regulation, not controlling them through fear.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 5: Schedule Regular Check-Ins">
**Why regular reviews matter:**
Technology changes. Kids grow. What worked at age 10 doesn't work at age 14.

**What to do:**
- **Monthly or quarterly family meetings** to discuss: "How is the media plan working? What needs to change?"
- **Ask kids for input:** "Is this rule still fair? What would you suggest?"
- **Adjust as needed:** Developmental stages require different boundaries

**This is a living document, not a rigid contract.**
</Step>

<Step title="Step 6: Model Healthy Digital Behavior">
**The most powerful teaching tool: YOUR behavior.**

**What kids notice:**
- Do you put your phone away during meals, or scroll while they talk?
- Do you check your phone constantly, or have intentional boundaries?
- Do you doomscroll news and complain about stress, or manage screen time mindfully?

**If you want kids to have healthy digital habits, model them.**

**Common parental pitfalls:**
- "Do as I say, not as I do" (enforce rules on kids you don't follow yourself)
- Checking work email during family time ("But I HAVE to")
- Using phone as a constant babysitter, then criticizing kids for screen dependence

**Fix:** Apply the family media plan to yourself. If bedrooms are device-free for kids, they're device-free for parents too.
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Youth-Centered Approach: Avoiding Sole Parental Responsibility

**The problem with "it's all on parents":**
The Stanford Youth Safety and Digital Wellbeing Report (2025) warns against placing sole responsibility on parents to manage platforms designed by billion-dollar corporations to maximize engagement.

**Why this framing is harmful:**
- **Parents lack control over platform design:** You can't prevent algorithmic manipulation, targeted advertising, or design features meant to maximize time on app
- **Creates guilt and blame:** When things go wrong (cyberbullying, PSU, harmful content exposure), parents blame themselves
- **Ignores systemic issues:** Platforms, schools, policymakers, and tech companies all share responsibility

**A youth-centered approach:**

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="👂" title="Listen to Young People">
**What this means:**
Ask kids what THEY think would help. Don't assume you know their online experiences.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What do you like about [platform]?"
- "What bothers you or makes you uncomfortable?"
- "What rules would make sense to you?"

**Why it works:** Kids are more likely to follow rules they helped create.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="🤝" title="Collaboration, Not Control">
**Shift from:**
"I'm taking your phone away because I said so."

**To:**
"I'm worried about your sleep. Let's figure out a phone charging plan that works for both of us."

**Why it works:** Adolescents need autonomy. Collaborative problem-solving teaches self-regulation; authoritarian control creates resistance.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="🛡️" title="Platform Accountability">
**What parents can advocate for:**
- Stronger age verification (not just "click yes if you're 13+")
- Better moderation of harmful content (self-harm, cyberbullying, misinformation)
- Transparent algorithms (explain what content is being promoted and why)
- Opt-out defaults for targeted advertising to minors

**How to advocate:**
- Contact elected representatives about platform regulation
- Support organizations working on youth digital safety
- Use parental feedback tools within platforms

**Parents can't fix design problems alone — but they can demand better from platforms.**
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="🏫" title="School and Community Involvement">
**Digital citizenship education should happen in schools:**
- Teaching respectful online behavior
- Cyberbullying prevention and intervention
- Media literacy (evaluating sources, recognizing misinformation)
- Healthy technology use

**Parents can advocate for:**
- School-based digital wellness programs
- Anti-cyberbullying policies
- Mental health support for students

**Community-level support matters:** You're not alone in this.
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

## Monitoring vs. Surveillance: The Critical Distinction

**Monitoring (healthy):**
Awareness of your child's online activity through conversation, observation of behavior changes, and age-appropriate oversight.

**Examples:**
- Knowing what platforms your child uses
- Discussing what they see and do online
- Noticing changes in mood, sleep, or social behavior (red flags)
- Checking privacy settings together
- Reviewing content younger children (under 10) are accessing

**Why it works:** Builds trust; teaches values; allows for early intervention when problems arise.

---

**Surveillance (unhealthy):**
Secretly monitoring every message, using spyware, reading private conversations without consent, tracking location constantly.

**Examples:**
- Installing spyware to read every text without your child knowing
- Demanding passwords and reading all messages
- GPS-tracking your teenager's every movement without their knowledge
- Creating fake accounts to "check up on" your child

**Why it backfires:**
- Destroys trust (kids learn parents don't respect their privacy)
- Teaches kids to hide things (secret accounts, friends' devices, encrypted apps)
- Doesn't teach self-regulation (you're controlling them, not helping them develop judgment)
- Can harm mental health (constant surveillance creates anxiety and resentment)

---

**The balance:**

| Age | Appropriate Monitoring | Inappropriate Surveillance |
|-----|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Under 10** | Co-viewing content; knowing what apps they use; checking browser history together | Secretly reading their messages to friends |
| **10-13** | Discussing online activity; knowing passwords (but not using them unless safety concern); checking privacy settings together | Installing spyware without their knowledge |
| **13-17** | Open conversations; monitoring for red flags (mood changes, withdrawal); knowing platforms they use | Reading every text message; tracking location constantly; demanding access to private conversations with friends |
| **18+** | Conversations about digital wellbeing; offering support if asked | Any surveillance (they're adults) |

## Talking About Cyberbullying with Kids

**When to have the conversation:**
BEFORE it happens. Don't wait for a crisis.

**What to say (by age):**

<EnhancedAccordion>
<AccordionItem title="Ages 5-8: Introduce the Concept of Kindness" icon="💚">
**What to say:**
- "Being kind online is just as important as being kind in person."
- "If someone says something mean to you on a game or app, tell me right away."
- "We don't say hurtful things to people — on screens or in real life."

**Role-play:** "What would you do if someone called you a mean name in a game?"
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Ages 9-12: Teach Cyberbullying Recognition and Response" icon="🛡️">
**What to say:**
- "Cyberbullying is when someone is mean to you online — sending hurtful messages, posting embarrassing photos, leaving you out on purpose."
- "If it happens to you: Don't respond. Save the evidence (screenshot). Tell me or another trusted adult."
- "If you see it happening to someone else: Don't join in. Tell an adult. Send a kind message to the person being targeted."

**Discuss consequences:** "What do you think happens when someone bullies others online?"
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Ages 13-19: Discuss Peer Pressure, Bystander Intervention, Mental Health" icon="🤝">
**What to say:**
- "If you're being cyberbullied, it's not your fault. I'm here to help — you won't lose your phone or get in trouble."
- "Bystanders have power. Standing up (publicly or privately) makes a difference."
- "If you're struggling with social media (comparison, FOMO, feeling worse), we can talk about it. Mental health is just as important as physical health."

**Open-ended questions:**
- "Have you seen people being mean online? How did you handle it?"
- "What do you think are the worst parts of social media?"

**Create safety:** Make it clear they can come to you without fear of punishment.
</AccordionItem>
</AccordionItem>

## Key Takeaways

- **Quality parent-child communication reduces cyberbullying** (both perpetration and victimization) — communication quality matters more than restrictive monitoring
- **Age-appropriate guidelines:** Under 1 (no screens); 1-2 (max 1 hr); 3-4 (max 1 hr); 5-12 (content/context over time); 13-19 (collaboration, not control)
- **Family media plans work best when collaborative** — kids who help create the rules are more likely to follow them
- **Model healthy behavior:** Parents who doomscroll, ignore family during meals, or use phones in bed teach kids to do the same
- **Monitoring (awareness through conversation) ≠ surveillance (secret spying)** — trust-building is more effective than control
- **Youth-centered approach:** Avoid placing sole responsibility on parents; platforms, schools, and policymakers share responsibility for digital safety
