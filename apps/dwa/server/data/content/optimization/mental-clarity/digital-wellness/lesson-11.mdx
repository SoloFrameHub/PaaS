# Cyberbullying: Recognition, Response, and Referral

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the **prevalence and mental health impact** of cyberbullying (19.1% median victimization; 90% depression indicators)
- Recognize **victim, aggressor, and bully-victim profiles** and population-specific vulnerabilities
- Learn **bystander intervention strategies** and when to seek professional help
- Apply **safety protocols and referral criteria** for high-risk situations

## Research Foundation

This lesson examines cyberbullying — a critical digital wellness and safety issue. A 2026 PRISMA-compliant systematic review (42 studies, ages 10-19) found median cyberbullying victimization prevalence of 19.1%. Among victims, 90% reported depression indicators and 87% reported anxiety indicators. Suicide attempts were observed in 19.0% of victims versus 3.0% of aggressors; self-harm was reported in 28.9% of victims. Population-specific vulnerability: adolescents with ASD showed victimization rates of 64.1-68.9%; females and LGBTQ+ adolescents showed higher susceptibility. "Bully-victims" (those who are simultaneously perpetrators and targets) show the worst outcomes across depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, and psychosomatic symptoms. Quality of parental communication significantly reduced both perpetration and victimization risk.

**Evidence Grade:** STRONG (for mental health harm); STRONG (for elevated adolescent risk)

## What Is Cyberbullying?

**Cyberbullying** is bullying that occurs through digital devices — smartphones, computers, tablets — via text, apps, social media, forums, or gaming platforms.

**Key characteristics:**

1. **Intentional harm** — deliberate actions meant to hurt, embarrass, or threaten
2. **Repetition** — happens multiple times or has the potential to be seen repeatedly (e.g., a harmful post shared widely)
3. **Power imbalance** — the person targeted has difficulty defending themselves (due to popularity, social status, access to embarrassing information, technical skills, or sheer numbers)

<Callout type="warning" title="Why Cyberbullying Is Different from Traditional Bullying">
**Traditional bullying:**
- Happens in specific locations (school, playground, neighborhood)
- Visible to witnesses who can intervene
- Ends when you leave the location

**Cyberbullying:**
- Follows you everywhere (24/7 via devices)
- Can be anonymous or hard to trace
- Reaches wider audiences (posts, screenshots, shares)
- Permanent record (content can resurface years later)
- Harder for adults to detect (happens in private messages, group chats, gaming platforms)

**Result:** Victims often feel there's no escape, no safe space, and no way to make it stop.
</Callout>

## Forms of Cyberbullying

<EnhancedAccordion>
<AccordionItem title="Harassment" icon="💬">
**What it looks like:**
- Sending offensive, threatening, or insulting messages repeatedly
- Posting hurtful comments on someone's social media
- Tagging someone in mean or embarrassing posts

**Example:** "You're so ugly. No one likes you. Why don't you just leave?"
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Exclusion" icon="🚫">
**What it looks like:**
- Intentionally leaving someone out of group chats, online games, or social media groups
- Creating private groups specifically to exclude one person
- Publicly discussing plans while making it clear one person isn't invited

**Why it's harmful:** Social exclusion triggers the same brain regions as physical pain. Digital exclusion is visible and public, amplifying the hurt.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Denigration" icon="📸">
**What it looks like:**
- Spreading rumors or false information about someone online
- Sharing embarrassing photos or videos without consent
- Creating fake profiles to impersonate and humiliate someone

**Example:** Photoshopping someone's image and posting it with cruel captions; creating a fake profile to send inappropriate messages pretending to be someone else.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Cyberstalking" icon="👁️">
**What it looks like:**
- Repeatedly sending threatening or intimidating messages
- Monitoring someone's online activity and commenting on their every move
- Using location-sharing features to track someone without consent

**This crosses into serious safety concerns** — if someone is stalking you online or offline, contact local authorities.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Outing and Trickery" icon="🔓">
**What it looks like:**
- Sharing private information about someone publicly (sexual orientation, personal secrets, private conversations)
- Tricking someone into sharing sensitive information, then spreading it

**Why it's particularly harmful:** Violates trust and privacy; can have severe consequences for LGBTQ+ youth in unsupportive environments.
</AccordionItem>

## The Mental Health Impact: What the Evidence Shows

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="😢" title="Depression and Anxiety">
**Research finding:**
Among cyberbullying victims:
- **90% reported depression indicators**
- **87% reported anxiety indicators**

**Why it happens:**
Repeated victimization → social isolation + helplessness + rumination → depressive and anxiety symptoms. The 24/7 nature of cyberbullying prevents psychological recovery between incidents.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="⚠️" title="Self-Harm and Suicidal Behavior">
**Research finding:**
- **19.0% of victims** reported suicide attempts
- **3.0% of aggressors** reported suicide attempts
- **28.9% of victims** reported self-harm

**Critical safety concern:**
Cyberbullying victimization significantly increases suicide risk, particularly in adolescents. This is a **HIGH-STAKES issue** requiring immediate intervention when suicidal ideation is present.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="🔄" title="Bully-Victims: Worst Outcomes">
**Who they are:**
"Bully-victims" are people who are both perpetrators AND targets of cyberbullying (they bully others and are also bullied themselves).

**Why they're at highest risk:**
- Experience both victimization trauma and social consequences of perpetration
- Show the worst outcomes across depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, and psychosomatic symptoms
- Often caught in cycles of retaliation and escalation

**Implication:** Both victims and aggressors need support — but bully-victims need the most intensive intervention.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="👥" title="Population-Specific Vulnerabilities">
**Who is at highest risk:**
- **Adolescents with ASD:** 64.1-68.9% victimization rate (compared to 19.1% median for general population)
- **Females:** Higher susceptibility to relational aggression (exclusion, rumor-spreading, image-based harm)
- **LGBTQ+ youth:** Higher victimization rates; outing and identity-based harassment
- **Adolescents (vs. adults):** More vulnerable due to developmental stage (identity formation, peer dependence, emotional regulation)

**Why it matters:** Intervention strategies must be tailored to these higher-risk groups.
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

## Recognizing the Signs: Is Someone Being Cyberbullied?

**Behavioral and emotional indicators:**

<StepByStep title="Warning Signs of Cyberbullying Victimization">
<Step title="Changes in Device Use">
**What to look for:**
- Suddenly stops using devices or social media
- Appears anxious, upset, or angry during or after device use
- Hides or guards their screen when others are nearby
- Receives large numbers of texts, calls, or notifications and appears distressed

**Why it matters:** Abrupt changes in technology behavior often signal something is wrong.
</Step>

<Step title="Emotional and Social Changes">
**What to look for:**
- Withdrawal from friends, family, or activities they used to enjoy
- Increased sadness, anxiety, or mood swings
- Reluctance to go to school or participate in social events
- Expressed feelings of helplessness or hopelessness

**Context:** Cyberbullying often overlaps with in-person bullying — social withdrawal may reflect both.
</Step>

<Step title="Academic and Sleep Disruption">
**What to look for:**
- Declining grades or loss of interest in school
- Difficulty sleeping, nightmares, or changes in sleep patterns
- Physical complaints (headaches, stomachaches, fatigue) with no clear medical cause

**Why it matters:** Chronic stress from cyberbullying manifests in physical and academic domains.
</Step>

<Step title="Self-Harm or Suicidal Ideation">
**URGENT WARNING SIGNS:**
- Talking about wanting to die or hurt themselves
- Searching online for ways to die
- Giving away possessions or saying goodbye
- Self-injury (cutting, burning, hitting)

**If you see these signs: IMMEDIATE INTERVENTION REQUIRED** (see Safety Protocol section below).
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Bystander Intervention: What to Do If You Witness Cyberbullying

**The bystander effect:** Most people who witness cyberbullying don't intervene because they assume someone else will, fear becoming the next target, or don't know what to do.

**But bystanders have significant power to stop cyberbullying.**

**Effective bystander strategies:**

1. **Don't participate** — Don't like, share, or comment on bullying content
2. **Defend publicly (if safe)** — "This isn't okay. Leave them alone."
3. **Support privately** — Message the victim: "I saw what happened. Are you okay? I'm here if you want to talk."
4. **Report to authorities** — Use platform reporting tools; tell a trusted adult, teacher, or HR (if workplace)
5. **Document evidence** — Take screenshots (with timestamps) in case it's needed for reporting or legal action

## Response Protocol: What to Do If You're Being Cyberbullied

<StepByStep title="If You're the Target of Cyberbullying">
<Step title="Step 1: Don't Respond or Retaliate">
**Why this matters:**
Responding gives the bully attention (often what they want) and can escalate the situation. Retaliation turns you into a "bully-victim" with worse outcomes.

**What to do instead:**
Walk away from the screen. Take a break from the platform. Focus on self-care and seeking support.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 2: Document Everything">
**What to save:**
- Screenshots of messages, posts, comments (with timestamps and usernames)
- URLs of harmful content
- Details of when and where it happened

**Why:** Evidence is critical for reporting to platforms, schools, employers, or law enforcement.

**How to organize:** Create a dedicated folder (digital or physical) with all documentation.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 3: Block the Bully">
**What to do:**
Use platform-specific blocking tools to prevent the person from contacting you or seeing your content.

**Why it works:**
Removes their access to you; signals you're not engaging; reduces ongoing harm.

**Limitation:** They may create new accounts or have others continue the behavior. Continue documenting and reporting.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 4: Report to the Platform">
**Where to report:**
- Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, Facebook, Twitter/X: All have built-in reporting tools
- Gaming platforms (Xbox, PlayStation, Steam): Report through player profiles or support
- Schools: Report to administrators, counselors, or anti-bullying coordinators

**What to report:**
Harassment, threats, impersonation, sharing of private images, hate speech.

**Follow up:** If the platform doesn't act, escalate to school authorities or parents.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 5: Tell a Trusted Adult or Authority">
**Who to tell:**
- Parents or guardians
- School counselor, teacher, or administrator
- HR representative (if workplace cyberbullying)
- Mental health professional

**Why it's critical:**
Adults can intervene in ways you can't (contact parents of the bully, involve school discipline, provide mental health support).

**If the first person doesn't help, tell someone else.** Keep advocating for yourself.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 6: Seek Emotional Support">
**Where to get support:**
- Trusted friends or family members
- School counselor or therapist
- Support groups (online or in-person)
- Crisis resources (if experiencing suicidal thoughts)

**Why it matters:**
Cyberbullying is isolating. Social support is one of the strongest protective factors against depression and anxiety.
</Step>

<Step title="Step 7: When to Involve Law Enforcement">
**Contact police if cyberbullying involves:**
- Threats of violence or physical harm
- Stalking or unwanted contact after being blocked
- Sharing of explicit images without consent (may be illegal in your jurisdiction)
- Hate crimes (targeting based on race, religion, sexual orientation, disability)
- Extortion or blackmail

**This is not overreacting.** These behaviors are crimes, not just "online drama."
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Safety Protocol: When to Seek IMMEDIATE Help

<Callout type="danger" title="URGENT: Signs Requiring Immediate Intervention">
**If you or someone you know is experiencing:**
- **Active suicidal ideation** ("I want to die." "I'm going to kill myself." "I have a plan to end my life.")
- **Self-harm behavior** (cutting, burning, hitting, overdosing)
- **Psychosis or severe dissociation** (hearing voices, feeling detached from reality)
- **Imminent threats of violence** from a cyberbully

**TAKE ACTION NOW:**

1. **Do NOT leave the person alone**
2. **Call 988** (National Suicide Prevention Lifeline) or **text HOME to 741741** (Crisis Text Line)
3. **Go to the nearest emergency room** or call 911 if safety is at immediate risk
4. **Remove access to means of self-harm** (medications, weapons, sharp objects)
5. **Notify parents/guardians and mental health professionals immediately**

**This is a medical emergency.** Do not wait. Do not assume it will pass.
</Callout>

**Referral criteria for professional mental health support (non-urgent but important):**

| Indicator | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Persistent depression or anxiety lasting 2+ weeks | Schedule appointment with therapist or psychiatrist |
| Social withdrawal, declining grades, loss of interest in activities | School counselor + mental health assessment |
| Sleep disruption, appetite changes, physical symptoms | Medical evaluation + mental health referral |
| Bully-victim status (both perpetrator and target) | Specialized intervention (therapy, school mediation, family support) |
| ASD, LGBTQ+, or other high-risk population | Tailored support with professionals familiar with these populations |

## Prevention: Family Communication and School Climate

**What the research shows:**
Quality of parental communication significantly reduces both cyberbullying perpetration and victimization.

**Effective family practices:**

1. **Open, non-judgmental conversations** about online experiences ("What are you seeing online? Are people being kind?")
2. **Teach digital citizenship** (respect, empathy, consequences of online actions)
3. **Monitor without surveillance** (know what platforms kids use; don't secretly read every message)
4. **Model healthy digital behavior** (parents who bully online or engage in toxic behavior model that for children)

**School-level prevention:**
- Anti-bullying policies that explicitly address cyberbullying
- Bystander intervention training for students
- Restorative justice approaches (not just punishment — address harm and rebuild relationships)
- Mental health support for both victims and aggressors

## Key Takeaways

- **Cyberbullying affects 19.1% of adolescents** and is associated with 90% depression indicators, 87% anxiety indicators, 19% suicide attempts in victims
- **Bully-victims** (both perpetrators and targets) show the worst mental health outcomes — both groups need support
- **Population vulnerabilities:** ASD (64-69% victimization), LGBTQ+ youth, females (relational aggression)
- **Bystanders have power:** Defend publicly if safe, support privately, report to authorities, document evidence
- **If targeted:** Don't respond/retaliate, document everything, block, report to platform, tell a trusted adult, seek emotional support
- **Quality parental communication is protective** — open conversations reduce both perpetration and victimization

## Safety Note

**If you or someone you know is experiencing suicidal thoughts, self-harm, or severe distress related to cyberbullying:**

**CRISIS RESOURCES (24/7):**
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text 988
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text HOME to 741741
- **Trevor Project (LGBTQ+ youth):** 1-866-488-7386 or text START to 678-678
- **SAMHSA National Helpline:** 1-800-662-4357

**These are trained crisis counselors available 24/7. You are not alone. Help is available.**
