# When to Seek Help — Comprehensive Escalation Guide

## Learning Objectives

- Recognize **red flags** for severe burnout, depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, trauma, and harassment
- Learn **escalation pathways** for each condition: EAP, occupational health, primary care, mental health specialists, crisis services
- Understand the **distinction** between self-guided learning and professional treatment
- Know **immediate action steps** for crisis situations

## Research Foundation

Burnout's exhaustion dimension is independently associated with suicidal ideation (OR=1.47), including in non-depressed individuals. Workplace bullying predicts anxiety, depression, PTSD, and suicidal ideation. Major depressive disorder requires professional diagnosis and treatment — not self-management. Trauma exposure in healthcare and emergency services predicts PTSD and moral injury. A self-guided educational course can teach recognition and referral pathways but cannot substitute for professional mental health care. Knowing when and how to escalate is a critical mental health literacy skill.

## Red Flag Framework: When to Seek Professional Help

The following conditions require **professional evaluation and treatment** — not self-management through a course, app, or wellness program.

<EnhancedAccordion title="Red Flags by Condition">
<AccordionItem title="Severe Burnout (Escalation Indicators)" id="severe-burnout">
**Red flags**:
- Exhaustion that is NOT relieved by rest, weekends, or vacation
- Cynicism or depersonalization affecting relationships outside work (not just work-specific)
- Functional impairment: difficulty completing basic daily tasks, missed work, reduced quality of life
- Duration: Symptoms persisting 4-6+ weeks without improvement
- Physical symptoms: chronic insomnia, GI issues, headaches, cardiovascular symptoms

**Why professional help is needed**: Severe burnout may co-occur with depression, anxiety, or other clinical conditions. Professional evaluation differentiates burnout from clinical mental health disorders and guides appropriate treatment.

**Where to seek help**:
- **EAP** (Employee Assistance Program): First-line confidential counseling and assessment
- **Occupational health**: Work-related health evaluation and fitness-for-work assessment
- **Primary care physician**: Rule out medical causes, consider sick leave or work accommodations
- **Mental health professional**: Psychologist, therapist, or psychiatrist for clinical evaluation and treatment
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Major Depressive Disorder (Clinical Depression)" id="depression">
**Red flags** (DSM-5 criteria — presence of 5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks, including at least one of the first two):
1. Persistent low mood, sadness, or emptiness most of the day, nearly every day
2. Loss of interest or pleasure in activities you used to enjoy (anhedonia)
3. Significant weight loss or gain, or change in appetite
4. Insomnia or hypersomnia (sleeping too much) nearly every day
5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation (restlessness or slowed movement)
6. Fatigue or loss of energy nearly every day
7. Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
8. Difficulty concentrating, thinking, or making decisions
9. Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation

**Critical distinction**: Burnout is work-specific; depression pervades all life domains. Burnout exhaustion can look like depression, but if symptoms extend beyond work context and include pervasive low mood, loss of pleasure, and hopelessness — that's clinical depression.

**Why professional help is required**: MDD is a clinical condition requiring diagnosis and treatment (therapy, medication, or both). Self-management is insufficient.

**Where to seek help**:
- **Primary care physician** or **psychiatrist**: For diagnosis, medication evaluation, and treatment plan
- **Psychologist or therapist**: For evidence-based psychotherapy (CBT, interpersonal therapy, behavioral activation)
- **EAP**: For initial assessment and referral to mental health providers
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: If experiencing suicidal ideation (see below)
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Anxiety Disorders (Generalized Anxiety, Panic, Social Anxiety)" id="anxiety">
**Red flags**:
- Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple areas of life (not just work) for 6+ months
- Physical symptoms: restlessness, muscle tension, rapid heartbeat, shortness of breath, GI distress
- Panic attacks: sudden intense fear with physical symptoms (chest pain, dizziness, feeling of losing control)
- Avoidance of situations due to anxiety (social anxiety, agoraphobia)
- Functional impairment: anxiety interferes with work, relationships, or daily activities

**Why professional help is needed**: Anxiety disorders respond well to treatment (CBT, medication, or both) but do not typically resolve without intervention.

**Where to seek help**:
- **Mental health professional**: Therapist or psychologist trained in CBT for anxiety (exposure therapy, cognitive restructuring)
- **Psychiatrist**: For medication evaluation (SSRIs, SNRIs, or other anxiolytics)
- **Primary care physician**: For initial assessment and referral
- **EAP**: For short-term counseling and referral to specialized anxiety treatment
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Suicidal Ideation (IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED)" id="suicidal-ideation">
**Red flags**:
- **Passive ideation**: Thoughts like "I wish I weren't here" or "People would be better off without me"
- **Active ideation**: Thoughts about specific methods, planning, or intent to end your life
- Hopelessness, feeling trapped, or belief that things will never improve
- Withdrawing from people, activities, or responsibilities
- Giving away possessions, saying goodbye, or putting affairs in order
- Previous suicide attempts or family history of suicide

**CRITICAL**: Burnout's exhaustion dimension is independently associated with suicidal ideation (OR=1.47), even in people without depression. If you're experiencing work-related exhaustion AND suicidal thoughts, this is a serious medical emergency.

**IMMEDIATE ACTION**:
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** (US): Call or text 988 for immediate crisis support (24/7, free, confidential)
- **Emergency services**: Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room if you're in immediate danger
- **Crisis Text Line** (US): Text HOME to 741741 for crisis counseling via text
- **International crisis lines**: [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com) for crisis support worldwide
- **EAP crisis line**: Many EAPs have 24/7 crisis support (check your EAP materials or call the main number)
- **Tell someone**: A trusted friend, family member, colleague, or supervisor — do not isolate

**DO NOT**:
- Wait for symptoms to worsen before seeking help
- Try to "handle it alone" — suicidal ideation requires immediate professional support
- Dismiss thoughts as "not serious" — passive ideation can progress to active ideation
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="PTSD and Trauma Exposure (Secondary Traumatic Stress, Moral Injury)" id="ptsd">
**Red flags** (especially for healthcare workers, emergency services, social workers, journalists, military-adjacent roles):
- **Intrusive symptoms**: Unwanted memories, nightmares, flashbacks of traumatic events
- **Avoidance**: Avoiding reminders of trauma (places, people, conversations, thoughts)
- **Hypervigilance**: Constant alertness, exaggerated startle response, difficulty sleeping, irritability
- **Negative thoughts and mood**: Persistent negative beliefs about self or world, loss of interest, emotional numbing
- **Duration**: Symptoms lasting 1+ month and causing functional impairment

**Moral injury symptoms** (may co-occur with PTSD):
- Persistent guilt, shame, or anger related to actions or inactions that violated your values
- Spiritual or existential distress (loss of faith, meaning, or sense of self)
- Betrayal by leadership or system that prevented you from acting ethically

**Why professional help is needed**: PTSD and moral injury require specialized trauma-focused therapy (CPT, PE, EMDR). General counseling is insufficient.

**Where to seek help**:
- **Trauma-specialized therapist**: Look for providers trained in Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT), Prolonged Exposure (PE), or EMDR
- **EAP**: For initial assessment and referral to trauma specialists
- **VA or military resources** (if applicable): Specialized trauma care for veterans and military personnel
- **Occupational health**: For work-related trauma documentation and accommodations
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Workplace Harassment and Bullying (Organizational and Legal Response)" id="harassment">
**Red flags**:
- Systematic harassment, public humiliation, threats, exclusion, or sabotage
- Harassment based on protected characteristics (race, gender, age, disability, religion, etc.)
- Physical safety concerns or threats
- Retaliation for reporting harassment
- Organizational inaction or dismissal of complaints

**Why organizational and legal response is required**: Harassment is an organizational and legal failure — not a personal coping issue.

**Where to seek help**:
- **HR**: For formal complaints and investigation (document all interactions)
- **EEOC** (US): File a charge of discrimination at [eeoc.gov](https://www.eeoc.gov) (must be filed within 180-300 days of incident)
- **Employment lawyer**: For legal consultation on harassment, discrimination, hostile work environment, or retaliation
- **EAP**: For emotional support and coping strategies
- **Union or employee representative**: For advocacy and grievance procedures
- **Occupational health**: For documentation of mental health impacts of harassment
</AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Comprehensive Referral Pathways

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="📞" title="EAP (Employee Assistance Program)">
**What it is**: Confidential counseling, assessment, and referral service provided by most employers (free to employees, typically 3-8 sessions).

**When to use**: First-line support for work stress, anxiety, depression, relationship issues, substance use, financial stress. EAP counselors can assess and refer to specialized care.

**How to access**: Check your employee handbook, HR portal, or insurance card for EAP contact info. Calls are confidential and NOT reported to your employer.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="🏥" title="Primary Care Physician">
**What they do**: Initial mental health screening, rule out medical causes of symptoms, prescribe basic psychotropic medications (antidepressants, anxiolytics), refer to specialists.

**When to use**: Persistent symptoms (depression, anxiety, insomnia), physical symptoms (fatigue, GI issues, headaches), need for sick leave or work accommodations.

**How to access**: Schedule appointment, describe symptoms honestly (duration, severity, functional impact), ask for mental health screening or referral.
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="🧠" title="Mental Health Specialists">
**Who they are**:
- **Psychologist**: PhD or PsyD, provides therapy (CBT, DBT, ACT, psychodynamic), psychological testing
- **Therapist/Counselor**: Licensed clinical social worker (LCSW), licensed professional counselor (LPC), or marriage and family therapist (MFT) — provides therapy
- **Psychiatrist**: MD specializing in mental health, can prescribe medication and provide therapy (though many focus on medication management)

**When to use**: Clinical depression, anxiety, PTSD, burnout with co-occurring mental health conditions, need for specialized therapy (trauma-focused, CBT, etc.)

**How to access**: EAP referral, insurance provider directory, [Psychology Today therapist finder](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/therapists), community mental health centers
</InsightItem>

<InsightItem icon="⚕️" title="Occupational Health">
**What they do**: Evaluate work-related health impacts, fitness-for-work assessments, medical documentation for accommodations, return-to-work planning after sick leave.

**When to use**: Work-related mental health impacts (burnout, moral injury, harassment-related stress), need for accommodations or sick leave, documentation for legal or HR purposes.

**How to access**: Through your employer (check with HR) or external occupational health physicians (if employer doesn't provide).
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

## Crisis Resources (IMMEDIATE HELP)

<StepByStep title="If You're in Crisis Right Now">
<Step title="988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US)">
**Call or text 988** — 24/7, free, confidential crisis support for suicidal ideation, mental health crisis, or substance use crisis.

**What to expect**: Trained crisis counselor will listen, provide support, and connect you to local resources if needed. Calls are confidential.

**For Spanish**: Press 2 after dialing 988
**For LGBTQ+ youth**: Trevor Lifeline at 1-866-488-7386 or text START to 678-678
</Step>

<Step title="Crisis Text Line (US)">
**Text HOME to 741741** — 24/7 text-based crisis support. Useful if you prefer texting over calling.

**What to expect**: Exchange texts with trained crisis counselor who will support you and provide resources.
</Step>

<Step title="Emergency Services (911 or local equivalent)">
**When to use**: Immediate danger to yourself or others, active suicide attempt, severe self-harm, psychosis, or inability to ensure your own safety.

**What happens**: Emergency responders (EMTs, police) will provide immediate assistance and transport to emergency room if needed for psychiatric evaluation.
</Step>

<Step title="Emergency Room">
**When to use**: Suicidal ideation with plan or intent, severe mental health crisis, safety concerns.

**What to expect**: Psychiatric evaluation, safety assessment, potential admission to inpatient psychiatric unit if safety risk is high, discharge planning with outpatient referrals if safe.
</Step>

<Step title="International Crisis Lines">
Visit **[findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com)** for crisis support worldwide (organized by country).
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## How to Talk to a Professional About Workplace Mental Health

Many people delay seeking help because they don't know what to say. Here's how to communicate effectively:

<Callout type="example" title="What to Say to Your Doctor, Therapist, or EAP Counselor">
**Opening**:
"I'm experiencing [burnout/anxiety/depression/stress] related to my work. I'm here because it's affecting my [sleep/mood/relationships/ability to function], and I need help."

**Describe symptoms specifically**:
- Duration: "This has been going on for [X weeks/months]."
- Severity: "I'm exhausted even after a full night's sleep. I cry frequently. I dread going to work."
- Functional impact: "I'm having trouble concentrating at work. I've withdrawn from friends and family. I can't enjoy things I used to love."
- Physical symptoms: "I have chronic insomnia, headaches, and GI issues."

**Describe work context (if relevant)**:
- "I'm a [role] in [sector, e.g., healthcare, tech, education]."
- "I'm experiencing [chronic understaffing/toxic manager/harassment/moral injury]."
- "I've tried [boundaries, communication, coping strategies], but symptoms persist."

**What you're asking for**:
- "I need an assessment to determine if this is clinical depression/anxiety/PTSD."
- "I'd like a referral to a therapist who specializes in [trauma/CBT/workplace stress]."
- "I'm wondering if medication might be appropriate."
- "I need documentation for sick leave or workplace accommodations."

**Don't minimize**: Healthcare providers need accurate information. Don't downplay symptoms or apologize for seeking help.
</Callout>

## When Self-Guided Learning Ends and Professional Help Begins

This course has provided **education, frameworks, and skills**. But it is NOT:
- A substitute for therapy or medication when clinically needed
- A treatment for burnout, depression, anxiety, PTSD, or other mental health conditions
- An organizational solution to structural problems (understaffing, toxic culture, harassment)

**Self-guided learning is appropriate for**:
- Psychoeducation (understanding workplace stress, burnout, JD-R model)
- Individual skills (boundaries, CBT-informed coping, assertive communication)
- Recognition and referral literacy (knowing when and how to seek help)

**Professional help is required for**:
- Clinical mental health conditions (depression, anxiety, PTSD, etc.)
- Severe burnout with functional impairment or co-occurring conditions
- Suicidal ideation, self-harm, or crisis situations
- Trauma exposure requiring specialized treatment
- Workplace harassment requiring legal or organizational intervention

<Callout type="info" title="Seeking Help Is a Sign of Strength — Not Weakness">
The stigma around mental health suggests that needing help means you're weak, broken, or failing.

**This is false.**

Seeking help when you need it is **informed, responsible, and self-protective**. It's what competent, self-aware people do when they recognize their limitations.

You wouldn't try to treat a broken bone with self-help articles. Mental health is no different. When symptoms are clinical, professional care is appropriate — and effective.
</Callout>
