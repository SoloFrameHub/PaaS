# Building Your Workplace Mental Health Assessment

## Learning Objectives

- Synthesize course learning to **assess your workplace demands, resources, boundaries, and coping strategies**
- Identify **specific gaps** in job resources, psychological safety, or organizational support
- Distinguish **individual-level actions** from **organizational-level requirements**
- Create a **personal action plan** based on your assessment

## Course Integration

This is a synthesis lesson. You've learned about:
- The JD-R model (demands and resources predict burnout and engagement)
- Burnout, moral injury, and when stress requires professional help
- Organizational factors (leadership, PSC, staffing, harassment)
- Individual strategies (boundaries, CBT skills, assertive communication)
- Population-specific contexts (remote work, caregiving, early-career)

Now you'll apply this knowledge to **your own workplace** — assessing where you are, what you need, and what action to take.

## Why Self-Assessment Matters (and What It Can't Do)

A workplace mental health self-assessment helps you:
- **Name** what you're experiencing (burnout, stress, resource deficiency, boundary challenges)
- **Recognize** whether problems are individual (your coping, boundaries) or structural (organizational culture, staffing, leadership)
- **Prioritize** action steps based on what's most urgent and most changeable
- **Make informed decisions** about whether to stay, advocate, escalate, or exit

**What self-assessment CANNOT do**:
- Diagnose clinical mental health conditions (if you suspect depression, anxiety, PTSD, or other conditions, seek professional evaluation)
- Fix structural problems (understaffing, toxic culture, inadequate PSC)
- Substitute for professional support (therapy, occupational health, EAP)

Think of this assessment as a **map** — it shows you where you are and helps you plan where to go next. But you still need to take the journey.

## Assessment Part 1: Job Demands

**Job demands** are the aspects of your work that require sustained effort and deplete energy.

<Checkin type="reflect" question="List 2-4 major job demands you experience regularly. Be specific: What exactly depletes your energy at work? Consider: workload volume, time pressure, emotional labor, role conflict, unpredictability, or other demands unique to your role." />

Now, for each demand you listed, ask:
- **Is this demand changeable or unchangeable?** (e.g., workload volume controlled by staffing = organizational; your personal task organization = individual)
- **Is this demand excessive or sustainable?** (e.g., occasional time pressure = normal; chronic overload exceeding capacity = excessive)
- **Is this demand a **challenge stress** (difficult but achievable with resources) or **threat stress** (exceeds your resources)?

<Callout type="tip" title="Red Flags for Excessive Demands">
Your demands are likely **excessive** (and require organizational response) if:
- You regularly work beyond contracted hours to complete basic job requirements
- Workload volume exceeds what can be done to acceptable standards in available time
- You're experiencing physical symptoms (headaches, insomnia, GI issues) from chronic overload
- Colleagues are leaving due to workload, and those remaining absorb their work without additional resources
- You've communicated concerns and been told to "manage your time better" without workload adjustment
</Callout>

---

## Assessment Part 2: Job Resources

**Job resources** are the aspects of your job that help you cope, grow, and recover. They buffer the effects of high demands.

<Checkin type="reflect" question="Assess your job resources in each category. Rate each 1-10 (1=absent, 10=excellent) and note specific examples or gaps: 1) Autonomy and job control, 2) Supervisor support, 3) Peer support and team cohesion, 4) Skill development and feedback, 5) Role clarity and predictability." />

Now, analyze your ratings:
- **Which resources are strongest?** (These are protective — maintain and leverage them)
- **Which resources are weakest?** (These are gaps — can you advocate for them, or are they structurally missing?)
- **Do your demands outweigh your resources?** (High demands + low resources = highest burnout risk)

<Callout type="info" title="The JD-R Model in Practice">
**High demands + high resources**: You're stretched, but you have support, autonomy, and feedback. This is the "active job" profile — challenging but sustainable if resources are maintained.

**High demands + low resources**: You're stretched without support. This is the **highest-risk profile** for burnout. This requires **immediate attention** — either resource addition (organizational) or demand reduction (organizational) or exit.

**Low demands + high resources**: You're supported but potentially under-challenged. Some people thrive here; others feel unfulfilled. Not a mental health risk, but consider whether the work is meaningful.

**Low demands + low resources**: The "passive job" — boring, disconnected, unmotivating. Not high-stress, but can lead to disengagement and career stagnation.
</Callout>

## Assessment Part 3: Psychological Safety Climate and Leadership Quality

**PSC (Psychosocial Safety Climate)** is your organization's management commitment to protecting worker psychological health. **Leadership quality** is your manager's behavior and support.

<Checkin type="reflect" question="Answer the following questions honestly: 1) Does senior leadership genuinely prioritize employee mental health through policy, resources, and accountability — or is wellbeing just marketing language? 2) Does your manager provide autonomy, support, fairness, and psychological safety — or control, neglect, unfairness, or abuse? 3) Are workplace stressors (workload, harassment, understaffing) addressed when raised — or dismissed, ignored, or blamed on employees?" />

**Interpretation**:
- **High PSC + supportive manager**: You're in a protective environment. Individual strategies (boundaries, CBT skills) will work well because the organization supports them.
- **Low PSC or toxic manager**: You're in a structural hazard. Individual strategies may help you cope short-term, but long-term wellbeing requires organizational change or exit.

<Callout type="warning" title="PSC Deficiency Is a Deal-Breaker">
If your organization:
- Frames mental health as individual responsibility (offers mindfulness apps but ignores understaffing)
- Punishes employees who raise wellbeing concerns
- Tolerates toxic leadership, harassment, or bullying
- Expects chronic overwork and constant availability as cultural norms

That's **low Psychosocial Safety Climate**. No amount of individual coping will make this environment sustainable long-term.

You can advocate, escalate, or exit — but you cannot fix this alone.
</Callout>

## Assessment Part 4: Work-Life Boundaries and Control

**Boundary control** is your ability to decide when work enters personal time (or vice versa). High boundary control predicts wellbeing even when boundaries are permeable.

<Checkin type="reflect" question="Assess your boundary control: 1) Can you disconnect from work during non-work time, or are you expected to be available 24/7? 2) Do you have control over WHEN work happens (flexibility), or are schedules rigid and imposed? 3) Do you experience work-life integration by choice, or work-life invasion by organizational demand? 4) Have you set clear boundaries (work hours, after-hours availability), and are they respected?" />

**Interpretation**:
- **High boundary control**: You choose when and how work and personal life integrate. This is protective.
- **Low boundary control**: Work invades personal time unpredictably, or organizational culture punishes boundaries. This predicts stress and burnout.

**Action**:
- If you have **organizational permission** to set boundaries but struggle personally (anxiety, habit, perfectionism): Individual strategies (psychological detachment, technology boundaries, assertive communication) will help.
- If your **organization punishes boundaries** or expects constant availability: This is a structural problem. Escalate or exit.

## Assessment Part 5: Coping Strategies and Support

**Coping strategies** are what you do to manage stress. Adaptive coping matches strategy to stressor (problem-focused for changeable stressors, emotion-focused for unchangeable stressors).

<Checkin type="reflect" question="Reflect on your current coping strategies: 1) What do you do when you're stressed at work? (e.g., talk to colleagues, reframe thoughts, problem-solve, avoid, overwork, substance use, seek help) 2) Are you using problem-focused coping for changeable stressors and emotion-focused coping for unchangeable stressors — or the reverse? 3) Are you avoiding or procrastinating in ways that increase stress? 4) Have you sought professional support (EAP, therapy, occupational health) or are you coping alone?" />

**Interpretation**:
- **Adaptive coping**: You match strategy to stressor, seek support when needed, and use both problem-focused and emotion-focused approaches flexibly.
- **Maladaptive coping**: You avoid changeable problems, try to problem-solve unchangeable stressors, cope alone without support, or use harmful strategies (substance use, chronic overwork).

**Action**:
- Build **coping flexibility**: practice both problem-focused and emotion-focused strategies
- Seek **professional support** if you're experiencing clinical symptoms (depression, anxiety, burnout, PTSD)
- Recognize when **coping is insufficient** and structural change or exit is needed

## Assessment Part 6: Red Flags for Escalation or Exit

Some workplace situations require **immediate action** — not gradual improvement or individual coping.

<EnhancedAccordion title="Red Flags for Escalation or Exit">
<AccordionItem title="You're Experiencing Severe Burnout or Mental Health Symptoms" id="severe-burnout">
**Red flags**: Exhaustion not relieved by rest, persistent anxiety or depression, suicidal ideation, physical health impacts, functional impairment.

**Action**: Seek professional help immediately (EAP, therapist, psychiatrist, occupational health). Consider medical leave if needed. Do not try to "push through" severe symptoms.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="You're Experiencing Harassment, Bullying, or Abuse" id="harassment">
**Red flags**: Systematic harassment, public humiliation, threats, discrimination, sexual harassment, physical safety concerns.

**Action**: Document, report to HR, consult employment lawyer, access EAP for support. If organization does not respond, escalate externally (EEOC, legal action) or exit.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Organizational Demands Are Structurally Unsustainable" id="unsustainable">
**Red flags**: Chronic understaffing with no plan to address it, workload that requires 50-60+ hour weeks consistently, moral injury from inability to meet professional standards.

**Action**: Escalate to senior leadership, HR, or union. Document workload and impacts. If organization does not respond, consider transfer or exit. You cannot sustain this long-term.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="You've Advocated, Escalated, and Nothing Changes" id="no-response">
**Red flags**: You've communicated concerns clearly, proposed solutions, escalated through appropriate channels — and organizational response is dismissive, retaliatory, or absent.

**Action**: This is a Psychosocial Safety Climate failure. The organization does not prioritize employee wellbeing. Consider exit as the most protective response.
</AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>

## Building Your Action Plan

Based on your assessment, create a **personal action plan** with three categories:

<StepByStep title="Your Workplace Mental Health Action Plan">
<Step title="Individual Actions (What You Can Do Now)">
Examples:
- Set boundaries around work hours and communicate them assertively
- Practice cognitive reframing for stress-amplifying thoughts
- Seek EAP support or therapy for anxiety/stress management
- Build peer support network for emotional connection
- Improve boundary control through technology limits and psychological detachment practices
</Step>

<Step title="Advocacy and Escalation (What You Can Request or Escalate)">
Examples:
- Request workload clarification or priority negotiation with manager
- Request flexible work arrangements for caregiver needs
- Escalate harassment or toxic leadership to HR or legal channels
- Advocate for staffing increases or resource allocation (individually or collectively)
- Request manager training on supportive leadership behaviors
</Step>

<Step title="Decision-Making (When to Stay, Transfer, or Exit)">
Examples:
- **Stay**: High PSC, supportive manager, adequate resources, fixable individual challenges (boundaries, coping skills)
- **Transfer**: Organization has pockets of better culture; different team/manager could improve fit
- **Exit**: Low PSC, toxic culture, structural unsustainability, organization unresponsive to escalation, severe mental health impact
</Step>
</StepByStep>

<Checkin type="reflect" question="Write your personal action plan. Identify: 1) ONE individual action you'll take this week (boundaries, coping skill, support-seeking), 2) ONE advocacy or escalation step you'll take if appropriate (request, communication, formal complaint), and 3) Your decision-making criteria: What would need to change for you to stay long-term? What would signal it's time to exit?" />

## Key Takeaways

<StepByStep title="What You Learned">
<Step title="Self-assessment helps you map your current situation — demands, resources, PSC, boundaries, coping">
You've assessed job demands (excessive or sustainable?), job resources (adequate or deficient?), PSC and leadership quality (protective or hazardous?), boundary control (high or low?), and coping strategies (adaptive or maladaptive?).
</Step>

<Step title="High demands + low resources = highest burnout risk">
This is the Job Demands-Resources model in practice. If your demands outweigh your resources, you need either resource addition or demand reduction — both organizational responsibilities.
</Step>

<Step title="Low PSC or toxic leadership is a structural hazard — not an individual coping challenge">
If your organization frames wellbeing as individual responsibility, punishes boundaries, or tolerates harassment, that's a systemic failure. Individual strategies help you survive, but they don't fix the system.
</Step>

<Step title="Your action plan should include individual actions, advocacy/escalation, and decision-making criteria">
You have agency in all three categories: what you can do now, what you can request or escalate, and when to stay vs. exit.
</Step>

<Step title="Red flags for escalation or exit: severe symptoms, harassment, structural unsustainability, organizational unresponsiveness">
Some situations require immediate action — professional help, formal complaints, or exit. Recognizing these red flags protects you from prolonged harm.
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Reflection

<Callout type="reflection" title="You've Done the Work — Now Use What You've Learned">
Self-assessment is not just reflection — it's **data** that informs **action**.

You now know:
- Where your workplace demands exceed your resources

- Which problems are individual (coping, boundaries) and which are structural (understaffing, toxic culture)
