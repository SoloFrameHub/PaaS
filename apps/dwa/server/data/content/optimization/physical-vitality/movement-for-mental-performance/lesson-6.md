# Building Your Personal Movement Assessment

## Learning Objectives

- Assess your **current movement baseline** (frequency, duration, intensity, modality)
- Identify your **intrinsic motivations** (why movement matters to you, beyond "should")
- Recognize **barriers and facilitators** (what helps or hinders your movement practice)
- Understand **self-efficacy** as the strongest predictor of adherence
- Use this assessment to inform your personalized movement plan (Lesson 1-20)

## Research Foundation

**Adherence is the most critical variable** in exercise for mental health — without it, no protocol produces benefit. Research identifies key barriers in mental health populations: low motivation, negative self-perception, fatigue, stigma, and environmental constraints. Conversely, **self-efficacy** (belief in your ability to exercise) is the strongest independent predictor of both exercise behavior and depressive symptom reduction. Successful programs use **motivational interviewing, graded exposure, cognitive reappraisal, social support, and individualization**. This lesson provides a structured self-assessment to identify *your* specific starting point, motivations, barriers, and facilitators — the foundation for a sustainable practice.

---

## Why Assessment Comes Before Prescription

Most exercise advice jumps straight to "the plan": Do this workout, this many times per week, for this duration.

And most people fail. Not because the plan is bad — but because it wasn't built for *them*.

Here's the truth: **The "best" exercise program is the one you'll actually do.**

And the one you'll actually do is the one that:
- **Matches your current capacity** (not where you "should" be)
- **Aligns with your intrinsic motivations** (what genuinely matters to you)
- **Accounts for your specific barriers** (time, energy, environment, mental health symptoms)
- **Builds on your facilitators** (what already helps you move)
- **Strengthens your self-efficacy** (belief in your ability to succeed)

This lesson walks you through a structured assessment. Treat it seriously — this is the foundation for everything that follows.

## Part 1: Current Movement Baseline

Let's start with where you are *right now*. No judgment. Just data.

<Callout type="reflection" title="Your Current Reality">
Answer honestly. This is for you, not anyone else.

**Frequency:** In the past 4 weeks, how many days per week did you engage in intentional movement (any type, any duration)?
- 0 days/week (sedentary)
- 1-2 days/week (minimally active)
- 3-4 days/week (moderately active)
- 5+ days/week (highly active)

**Duration:** On days you moved, how long was a typical session?
- Less than 10 minutes
- 10-20 minutes
- 20-40 minutes
- 40-60 minutes
- 60+ minutes

**Intensity:** How would you describe the typical intensity?
- Light (can talk easily, barely breathing harder)
- Moderate (can talk but not sing, noticeably breathing harder)
- Vigorous (can only speak a few words, breathing hard)
- Mixed (varies session to session)

**Modality:** What types of movement have you done in the past 4 weeks? (Check all that apply)
- Walking (outdoor or indoor)
- Running or jogging
- Cycling
- Swimming
- Yoga or stretching
- Strength training (weights, bodyweight)
- Team sports
- Individual sports (tennis, golf, martial arts, etc.)
- Dance
- Household/yard work (gardening, cleaning, etc.)
- Other: __________
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Interpreting Your Baseline">
**If you're currently sedentary or minimally active (0-2 days/week):**
- Start with the absolute minimum viable dose: 10 minutes, 3x/week
- Focus on *consistency* before duration or intensity
- Your biggest win is establishing the habit

**If you're moderately active (3-4 days/week):**
- You have a foundation. Your challenge is likely adherence (maintaining it) or optimization (making it more effective for mental health)
- Focus on strategic timing and modality matching

**If you're highly active (5+ days/week):**
- You may already be at or above optimal dose for mental health benefits
- Your challenge may be *type* (is your movement serving mental health, or is it compulsive/avoidant?)
- Consider whether your movement is sustainable, enjoyable, and aligned with your goals
</Callout>

## Part 2: Intrinsic Motivation — Why Movement Matters to You

**Extrinsic motivation** (external rewards, guilt, "should") doesn't sustain behavior long-term. **Intrinsic motivation** (internal values, enjoyment, meaning) does.

<Callout type="tip" title="Strengthen Your Intrinsic Motivation">
**Reflection prompt:** Complete this sentence in as many ways as resonate:

*"I want to move because it would help me _______________."*

Examples:
- "...sleep better, which would make me less irritable with my family."
- "...manage my anxiety so I don't rely on Xanax as much."
- "...feel strong and capable in my body again."
- "...have the energy to do things I care about (hobbies, time with friends)."
- "...experience 30 minutes where my mind isn't racing."

The more specific and personally meaningful your "why," the stronger your motivation.
</Callout>

## Part 3: Barriers — What Gets in the Way?

Research on exercise adherence in mental health populations identifies consistent barriers. Which apply to you?

  title="Map Your Current Physical State"
  prompt="Click the areas where you currently experience tension, discomfort, pain, or low energy. This helps identify physical barriers to movement and areas that need attention."
  persistKey="movement-physical-baseline-l6"
/>

**Common barriers (check all that apply):**

**Internal (psychological/physical):**
- ☐ **Low energy/fatigue** (depression, chronic illness)
- ☐ **Low motivation** (anhedonia, apathy)
- ☐ **Negative self-perception** ("I'm not athletic," "I'm bad at exercise")
- ☐ **Fear of judgment** (body image, performance anxiety in group settings)
- ☐ **Perfectionism** (if I can't do it "right," I don't do it at all)
- ☐ **Anergia** (the physical sensation of heaviness/inability to initiate)
- ☐ **Pain or injury** (chronic pain, past injury, physical limitation)
- ☐ **Anxiety about body sensations** (exercise-induced arousal feels unsafe)

**External (environmental/logistical):**
- ☐ **Time constraints** (work, caregiving, commute)
- ☐ **Cost** (gym memberships, equipment, classes)
- ☐ **Access** (no safe outdoor space, no gym nearby, transportation)
- ☐ **Weather** (seasonal barriers — heat, cold, rain)
- ☐ **Lack of social support** (no exercise partner, no one encourages it)
- ☐ **Caregiving responsibilities** (can't leave kids, eldercare)
- ☐ **Stigma** (cultural or family messages that exercise is selfish/unnecessary)

<Callout type="tip" title="Barrier-Specific Solutions">
**For low energy/fatigue:**
- Start with 5-10 minutes (you can do *anything* for 5 minutes)
- Choose low-intensity modalities (walking, gentle yoga)
- Schedule movement at your highest-energy time of day

**For low motivation:**
- Use behavioral activation: action *precedes* motivation, not the other way around
- Externalize commitment (accountability partner, scheduled class)
- Start so small that motivation isn't required ("I'll put on shoes and step outside")

**For negative self-perception:**
- Reframe "I'm not athletic" → "I'm building capacity"
- Avoid comparison (your movement is for YOU, not for performance)
- Choose beginner-friendly, non-competitive formats

**For time constraints:**
- Break into 10-minute chunks (3×10 min = 30 min total; same mental health benefit)
- Integrate into existing routines (walk during lunch, stretch before bed)
- Use high-efficiency modalities (HIIT if tolerated, or strength training 2x/week)

**For cost:**
- Free options: walking, jogging, bodyweight exercises (push-ups, squats), YouTube yoga
- Public resources: library fitness videos, community center classes
- One-time purchases: resistance bands ($15), jump rope ($10)

**For lack of social support:**
- Join online communities (Strava, Peloton, Reddit fitness groups)
- Find a local walking group or running club (often free)
- Ask one friend to commit to weekly walks
</Callout>

## Part 4: Facilitators — What Already Helps?

Facilitators are the opposite of barriers — they're the conditions, contexts, or strategies that *already* help you move.

<Checkin
  type="choice"
  question="Which of these facilitators have worked for you in the past (even if you're not currently using them)?"
  options="Scheduled classes or appointments|Accountability partner or group|Tracking progress (app, journal)|Outdoor/nature settings|Music or podcasts during movement|Structured program with clear goals|Social/competitive element|Variety (changing modalities)|Alone time (solo movement)|Morning routine (first thing)|Something else"
/>

<Callout type="tip" title="Double Down on What Works">
**The principle:** Don't just address barriers — *amplify facilitators*.

If you know that:
- **Scheduled classes** work → join a gym with class schedule, not just open access
- **Accountability** works → find a workout partner or join a group challenge
- **Music** works → create playlists specifically for movement
- **Outdoor settings** work → prioritize outdoor modalities (walking, running, outdoor yoga)
- **Tracking** works → use an app (Strava, Fitbit, Apple Health) or journal
- **Alone time** works → protect solo movement as non-negotiable self-care

**Sustainable movement leverages what already works for you — not what works for someone else.**
</Callout>

## Part 5: Self-Efficacy — Do You Believe You Can Do This?

**Self-efficacy** = your belief in your ability to successfully perform a behavior.

In exercise research, **self-efficacy is the strongest predictor of adherence**. It predicts who sticks with movement *independent of* baseline fitness, depression severity, or environmental barriers.

<Checkin
  type="scale"
  question="On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can engage in regular movement (3-5x/week, 20-30 min) for the next 8 weeks?"
  low="Not confident at all (1)"
  high="Completely confident (10)"
/>

<Callout type="info" title="Interpreting Your Self-Efficacy Score">
**1-3 (Low self-efficacy):**
- This is your *primary* barrier. Knowledge and motivation won't help if you don't believe you can succeed.
- **Strategy:** Start absurdly small. Your goal is *not* optimal dose — it's proving to yourself that you can do *something*. Success builds self-efficacy.
- **Target:** 5 minutes, 3 days this week. That's it. Once you prove you can do that, you'll believe you can do more.

**4-6 (Moderate self-efficacy):**
- You believe you *could* do it, but you're not sure it'll stick.
- **Strategy:** Use external supports (accountability, scheduled classes, tracking). Externalize the burden of willpower.
- **Target:** 15-20 minutes, 3-4 days/week. Build consistency before increasing duration.

**7-10 (High self-efficacy):**
- You believe you can do this. Your challenge is execution and optimization.
- **Strategy:** Focus on strategic timing, modality selection, and sustainability (avoiding burnout).
- **Target:** Evidence-based dose (150-300 min/week) with a modality that matches your goals.
</Callout>

<Step title="The best plan is the one you'll actually do">

</Step>

</Step>
