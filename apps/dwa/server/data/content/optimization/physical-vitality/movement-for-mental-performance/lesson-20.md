# Building Your Personal Movement Medicine Plan

## Learning Objectives

- **Synthesize** 20 lessons of evidence into a personalized, actionable movement plan
- Apply the **minimum effective dose** (320 MET-min/week) and **optimal dose** (860 MET-min/week) frameworks
- Design a plan using **graded exposure**, **self-efficacy building**, and **adherence strategies**
- Identify your **barriers, supports, and accountability mechanisms**
- Recognize **when to seek professional help** (red flags review)

## Research Foundation

The most consistently supported exercise protocol for mental health is **supervised group exercise, 30-40 minutes, 3 times per week, for a minimum of 9-12 weeks**. The WHO-recommended dose is **150-300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity** (or 75-150 minutes of vigorous). For depression specifically, the minimum clinically meaningful dose is approximately **320 MET-min/week**; the optimal dose is around **860 MET-min/week**. However, adherence is the bottleneck — without it, no protocol works. This final lesson helps you build a realistic, sustainable plan that works for *your* life, barriers, and goals.

### Step 3: Set a Realistic Dose Target

**Use the evidence to guide your dose, but start where you are.**

<SlideNavigation persistKey="dose-targets">
<Slide title="Minimum Effective Dose (If You're Starting From Sedentary)">
**Target:** 50-100 minutes per week for the first 4 weeks, then gradually increase

**Why:** Starting too high = burnout, injury, dropout. Starting small builds self-efficacy and habit formation.

**Example plan:**
- Week 1-2: 10 minutes walking, 3 days/week (30 min total)
- Week 3-4: 15 minutes walking, 3 days/week (45 min total)
- Week 5-6: 20 minutes walking, 3 days/week (60 min total)
- Week 7-8: 20 minutes walking/jogging, 4 days/week (80 min total)

**Evidence:** Even small amounts of movement (10-20 min/day) produce measurable mental health benefits, especially if you're starting from zero.
</Slide>

<Slide title="WHO Minimum (If You're Lightly Active)">
**Target:** 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity (or 75 minutes vigorous)

**Why:** This is the evidence-based minimum for general health and wellbeing. Achievable for most people.

**Example plan:**
- 30 minutes, 5 days/week (walk, jog, cycle, swim, yoga)
- OR 50 minutes, 3 days/week (longer sessions, fewer days)
- OR 25 minutes, 6 days/week (daily habit)

**Evidence:** This dose meets WHO guidelines and produces consistent mental health benefits across studies.
</Slide>

<Slide title="Optimal Dose for Depression (If You're Targeting Symptoms)">
**Target:** 200-300 minutes per week, or ~860 MET-min/week

**Why:** Research shows this is the "sweet spot" for maximal depression reduction. More isn't necessarily better (U-shaped curve).

**Example plan:**
- 40 minutes, 5 days/week (200 min total)
- Mix modalities: 3 days aerobic (walk/jog), 2 days strength OR yoga

**Evidence:** This aligns with the "supervised group, 30-40 min, 3x/week" protocol that has the strongest evidence for depression.
</Slide>

<Slide title="Maintenance Dose (If You're Already Active)">
**Target:** 150-300 minutes per week with variety (aerobic + resistance + mind-body)

**Why:** Prevents burnout, reduces injury risk, keeps it interesting. Variety supports adherence.

**Example plan:**
- 2 days resistance training (30-40 min each)
- 2 days aerobic (30-40 min each: run, cycle, swim)
- 1-2 days yoga or active recovery (stretching, walking)

**Evidence:** Combined exercise (aerobic + resistance) shows the best outcomes for cognition and overall wellbeing.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

### Step 4: Choose Your Modalities

Based on the evidence from Lessons 7-15, choose 1-3 modalities that:
1. Match your goals (depression → walking/yoga/strength; anxiety → yoga/gentle aerobic; sleep → aerobic/mind-body)
2. You actually enjoy (or can tolerate)
3. Are accessible given your resources and environment

### Step 5: Identify Barriers and Design Solutions

**What will get in your way — and how will you work around it?**

<EnhancedAccordion title="Common Barriers and Evidence-Based Solutions">
<AccordionItem title="Barrier: I don't have time" badge="⚠️">
**Solution 1: Start ridiculously small**
10 minutes counts. 5 minutes counts. You have 5 minutes.

**Solution 2: Habit stacking**
"After I drop kids at school, I'll walk one lap around the parking lot before driving home."

**Solution 3: Reframe**
"I don't have time NOT to exercise. 20 minutes of movement = 4 hours of better focus and mood."
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Barrier: I'm too tired (depression fatigue, low energy)" badge="⚠️">
**Solution 1: Start with 3 minutes**
Promise yourself you'll stop after 3 minutes if it's unbearable. Usually, you'll continue once started (action generates energy).

**Solution 2: Choose low-intensity**
Gentle walk, stretching, slow yoga. Not every session needs to be intense.

**Solution 3: Seek clinical evaluation if severe**
Profound fatigue (sleeping 10-14 hours, still exhausted) is a symptom requiring medical assessment, not willpower.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Barrier: I'm afraid of being judged (gym anxiety, body image concerns)" badge="⚠️">
**Solution 1: Start at home or outdoors**
YouTube workouts, walking trails, neighborhood runs. No audience.

**Solution 2: Beginner-friendly environments**
Look for "beginner yoga," "couch to 5K" groups, or gyms with private/women-only areas.

**Solution 3: Reframe**
"Most people at the gym are focused on their own workout. And if they're judging, that's their issue, not mine."
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Barrier: I've tried before and failed" badge="⚠️">
**Solution 1: Past attempts were data, not failures**
What did you learn? (Set the bar too high? Chose activities you hated? No accountability?)

**Solution 2: This time, design differently**
Use graded exposure, start smaller, choose enjoyable activities, add social support.

**Solution 3: Redefine success**
Success = showing up, not perfect execution. One workout this week is better than zero.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Barrier: I have chronic pain or physical limitations" badge="⚠️">
**Solution 1: Consult a provider first**
Physical therapist, sports medicine doctor, or primary care provider can guide safe modifications.

**Solution 2: Choose low-impact modalities**
Swimming, water aerobics, chair yoga, recumbent bike, tai chi.

**Solution 3: Focus on what you CAN do**
Can't run? Walk. Can't walk? Chair exercises. Can't stand? Seated strength training. There's always *something*.
</AccordionItem>

<AccordionItem title="Barrier: Lack of motivation / don't feel like it" badge="⚠️">
**Solution 1: Action generates motivation, not vice versa**
Wait to feel motivated = wait forever. Start anyway. Motivation follows.

**Solution 2: Use the 2-minute rule**
"Put on my shoes" is the only commitment. Once shoes are on, walking is easy.

**Solution 3: Social accountability**
Text a friend after each workout. Join a group class. Public commitment increases follow-through.
</AccordionItem>

### Step 6: Build Accountability and Support

**Who or what will help you stay consistent?**

Evidence shows that **social support** and **public commitment** significantly increase adherence.

**Options:**
1. **Exercise buddy or accountability partner**: Someone who checks in weekly (text, call, or workout together)
2. **Group class or team sport**: Built-in social connection + structured time
3. **Online community**: Reddit r/EOOD, Strava clubs, app-based challenges
4. **Coach or therapist integration**: Make movement part of your treatment plan with regular check-ins
5. **Technology**: Habit tracker (streaks), reminders, progress logs
6. **Public commitment**: Tell someone your plan ("I'm walking 10 min M/W/F — can I text you after?")

## Safety Check: When to Seek Professional Help

Before you begin, review these red flags. If any apply, **seek professional evaluation before relying on exercise alone:**

<Callout type="warning" title="Red Flags Requiring Professional Assessment">
**Seek help NOW (crisis-level):**
- Active suicidal ideation or self-harm urges
- Severe functional impairment (can't work, care for self, or complete daily tasks)
- Psychosis (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thinking)
- Manic episode (extreme energy, racing thoughts, risky behavior, no sleep)

**Seek help SOON (clinical-level):**
- Moderate-to-severe depression (persistent low mood, anhedonia, hopelessness for 2+ weeks)
- PTSD with significant somatic symptoms or trauma triggers
- Eating disorder with compulsive exercise component
- Severe anxiety with panic disorder or debilitating avoidance
- Chronic pain or injury that needs medical clearance before exercise

**Exercise can be PART of your treatment in these cases — but not sufficient as standalone intervention.**

**Crisis resources:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: Call or text 988 (US)
- **Crisis Text Line**: Text HOME to 741741 (US)
- **International**: [findahelpline.com](https://findahelpline.com)
</Callout>
