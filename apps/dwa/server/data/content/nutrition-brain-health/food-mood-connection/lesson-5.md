## Why Meal Timing Matters for Your Brain

Your brain uses about 20% of your total daily energy, even though it makes up only about 2% of your body weight. When you go too long without eating, your blood sugar drops, and your brain sends out stress signals -- leading to irritability, brain fog, anxiety, and difficulty concentrating.

Consistent meal timing helps maintain steady blood sugar levels throughout the day, giving your brain a reliable supply of the glucose it needs to function at its best. Think of it as keeping your mental energy "tank" topped off rather than running on fumes.

## The Science of Timing

<SlideNavigation persistKey="meal-timing-science">
<Slide title="Morning: Within 1-2 Hours of Waking">
Eating within the first couple hours of waking signals to your brain that it is safe and resources are available, helping to reduce morning cortisol spikes. Cortisol is your body's primary stress hormone, and it naturally peaks in the morning. A balanced breakfast helps bring it back to healthy levels, setting a calm tone for your day.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Every 3-4 Hours Throughout the Day">
Spacing meals and snacks every 3-4 hours prevents the blood sugar crashes that trigger anxiety, irritability, and cravings for quick-fix sugary foods. When blood sugar drops too low, your body releases adrenaline and cortisol to compensate — the same hormones involved in the fight-or-flight response. Regular eating prevents this unnecessary stress activation.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Evening: 2-3 Hours Before Bed">
Finishing your last substantial meal 2-3 hours before bedtime supports better sleep quality, which is essential for mental health recovery and mood regulation. Eating too close to bedtime can cause digestive discomfort, increase body temperature, and disrupt the production of melatonin — your sleep hormone.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Balance Each Meal">
Including protein, healthy fat, and fiber at each meal slows digestion and provides a steady stream of energy, preventing the spike-and-crash cycle. Think of it as the "trifecta" for steady mental energy: protein keeps you full, fat slows absorption, and fiber feeds your gut bacteria.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Recommended Daily Eating Schedule

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="🌅" title="Breakfast (7-8 AM)">Include protein and complex carbs to fuel your morning. Examples: eggs with whole grain toast, oatmeal with nuts and berries, or Greek yogurt with seeds.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🍎" title="Morning Snack (10-10:30 AM)">A small, balanced snack to bridge the gap. Examples: apple with almond butter, a handful of trail mix, or hummus with vegetables.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🥗" title="Lunch (12:30-1:30 PM)">A balanced meal with protein, healthy fats, and plenty of vegetables. Examples: salad with grilled chicken and avocado, lentil soup with whole grain bread.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🥜" title="Afternoon Snack (3-3:30 PM)">Combat the "3 PM slump" proactively. Examples: nuts and dark chocolate, cheese with whole grain crackers, or a small smoothie.</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🍽️" title="Dinner (6-7 PM)">A nourishing meal that includes anti-inflammatory foods. Examples: salmon with roasted vegetables, stir-fry with tofu and brown rice.</InsightItem>

## Meal Prep Strategies for Consistent Timing

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Sunday Planning Session">Spend 15 minutes on Sunday mapping out your meals for the week. You do not need elaborate recipes — focus on simple combinations of protein, healthy fats, and vegetables.</Step>
<Step title="Batch Prep Basics">Cook a large batch of grains (brown rice, quinoa) and a protein (chicken, beans, tofu) that you can mix and match throughout the week.</Step>
<Step title="Snack Stations">Pre-portion snacks into containers or bags at the start of the week. Keep them visible in your fridge or bag so they are the easy, default choice.</Step>
<Step title="Set Meal Reminders">Use your phone to set alarms for meal and snack times until the rhythm becomes automatic. Most people find they can stop using reminders after 2-3 weeks.</Step>
<Step title="Keep Emergency Options Ready">Stock your desk, car, or bag with non-perishable brain-healthy snacks like nuts, seeds, or whole grain crackers for days when plans fall apart.</Step>
</StepByStep>
<EnhancedAccordion title="What If My Schedule Is Unpredictable?">
If you work shifts, have irregular hours, or your days are genuinely unpredictable, focus on the *intervals* rather than the clock times. The key principle is: eat within 1-2 hours of waking (whenever that is), then every 3-4 hours after that, and stop eating 2-3 hours before sleep.
Keep portable, non-perishable snacks with you at all times. Good options include:
- Individual nut butter packets
- Trail mix portions in zip-lock bags
- Whole grain crackers with cheese
- Protein bars with minimal added sugar (look for under 8g)
- Dried fruit and seed mixes
The goal is never to go more than 4 hours without eating during your waking hours, regardless of when those hours fall.
</EnhancedAccordion>
