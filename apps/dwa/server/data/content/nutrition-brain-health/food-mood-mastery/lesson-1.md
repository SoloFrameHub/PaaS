For the first time, an international expert panel has produced actionable, evidence-based recommendations for using dietary interventions alongside conventional psychiatric treatment.

## The ISNPR 2024 Guidelines: A Turning Point

In December 2024, the British Journal of Nutrition published what many consider the most important document in the history of nutritional psychiatry. Through a rigorous Delphi consensus process, 18 international experts across psychiatry, nutrition science, dietetics, and public health produced 49 specific recommendations for clinical practice.

This was not a single study or a narrative review. The Delphi method requires multiple rounds of anonymous voting, discussion, and re-voting until the panel reaches consensus. Each recommendation had to achieve agreement from the vast majority of experts. The result is a set of guidelines grounded in the strongest available evidence and refined through expert clinical judgment.

The guidelines address a fundamental gap in mental health care. Despite decades of evidence linking diet quality to mental health outcomes, most psychiatric training programs include minimal nutrition education. Most clinicians lack a practical framework for assessing dietary status, recommending interventions, or monitoring outcomes. The ISNPR guidelines change that.

### What the Guidelines Cover

The 49 recommendations span several domains: general principles of nutritional psychiatry, dietary assessment in clinical settings, specific nutrient considerations, dietary pattern recommendations, implementation strategies, and special populations. They position nutritional interventions as *adjunctive* — meaning they work alongside, not instead of, conventional treatments like psychotherapy and medication.

## Dietary Assessment Tools for Mental Health

One of the most practical contributions of the guidelines is the recommendation to integrate dietary assessment into standard mental health evaluations. Just as clinicians routinely screen for sleep quality, substance use, and physical activity, the guidelines argue that dietary assessment should become standard practice.

Several validated tools are recommended for different clinical contexts:

**The Mediterranean Diet Adherence Screener (MEDAS)** is a 14-item questionnaire that takes about five minutes to complete. It evaluates adherence to Mediterranean dietary patterns — the dietary approach with the strongest evidence for mental health benefits. Each item scores 0 or 1, with higher total scores indicating better adherence. A score of 9 or above typically indicates good adherence.

**The Dietary Inflammatory Index (DII)** quantifies the inflammatory potential of an individual's overall diet. Since chronic inflammation is a key mechanism linking poor diet to depression and anxiety, the DII provides a useful clinical metric. Higher DII scores indicate a more pro-inflammatory diet, which has been consistently associated with increased risk for depression.

**The NOVA Classification** categorizes foods by their degree of processing rather than their nutrient content. This is increasingly relevant because ultra-processed food consumption — regardless of macronutrient or calorie content — has been independently associated with worse mental health outcomes. The NOVA system divides foods into four groups: unprocessed or minimally processed, processed culinary ingredients, processed foods, and ultra-processed foods.

**Food Frequency Questionnaires (FFQ)** provide a more detailed assessment of habitual dietary intake over weeks or months. While more time-consuming than screening tools, they give a richer picture of overall dietary patterns and can identify specific nutrient gaps.

## Integrative Care Models: Nutrition in the Treatment Team

The ISNPR guidelines emphasize that nutritional psychiatry works best within an integrative care model. This means dietitians, nutritionists, psychiatrists, psychologists, and primary care physicians collaborating as a team rather than working in isolation.

### The Stepped-Care Approach

The guidelines recommend a stepped-care model where the intensity of nutritional intervention matches the severity and complexity of the mental health condition:

**Step 1 — Universal dietary guidance.** All individuals receiving mental health care should receive basic information about the relationship between diet quality and mental health. This includes encouraging whole food consumption, adequate hydration, regular meal timing, and reducing ultra-processed food intake.

**Step 2 — Structured dietary assessment and targeted recommendations.** For individuals with moderate symptoms or those not responding adequately to first-line treatments, formal dietary assessment using validated tools should be conducted. Specific dietary recommendations are made based on identified gaps.

**Step 3 — Specialized nutritional intervention.** For complex cases, individuals with comorbid metabolic conditions, or those on medications with significant nutritional side effects, referral to a dietitian with mental health expertise is recommended. This level involves individualized meal planning, supplement protocols, and close monitoring.

**Step 4 — Intensive nutritional therapy.** For severe, treatment-resistant cases or those involving eating disorders alongside other psychiatric conditions, intensive collaboration between psychiatric and nutritional specialists is necessary. This may include therapeutic diets under medical supervision.

### The Economic Case

The economic argument for integrating nutrition into mental health care is compelling. Mental health conditions cost the global economy an estimated $2.5 trillion annually in lost productivity, healthcare costs, and social services. The ISNPR guidelines cite projections suggesting that widespread adoption of nutritional psychiatry approaches — particularly population-level dietary improvement — could contribute to savings of up to $5 trillion when combined with other preventive health measures. These figures come from modeling studies and should be interpreted as estimates rather than precise predictions, but they illustrate the scale of potential impact.

>
> **Study**: Global burden modeling studies cited in ISNPR 2024 guidelines · Sample: Population-level projections · Design: Economic modeling and systematic review
>
> **Finding**: Integration of evidence-based nutritional approaches into mental health care could contribute to projected savings of up to $5 trillion globally when combined with broader dietary public health measures, through reduced treatment costs and improved productivity.
>
> **Context**: These are modeling estimates based on current cost-of-illness data and intervention effectiveness studies. Actual savings depend on implementation scale, policy adoption, and healthcare system structure. The figures represent potential rather than guaranteed outcomes.
>
> **Practical Application**: Even modest improvements in population dietary quality could reduce the mental health burden significantly, making nutritional psychiatry a cost-effective addition to existing care models.
>
> **Evidence Quality**: ⭐⭐⭐ Moderate (Economic modeling based on intervention data)

## Practical Application: Using the Guidelines in Your Life

Whether you are a healthcare professional, a student, or someone managing your own mental health, the ISNPR guidelines offer practical direction.

## Key Takeaways
- The ISNPR 2024 guidelines represent the first comprehensive international consensus for integrating nutrition into psychiatric care, with 49 specific recommendations developed through a rigorous Delphi process.
- Validated dietary assessment tools (MEDAS, DII, NOVA, FFQ) can be integrated into mental health evaluations to identify nutritional gaps and guide intervention.
- A stepped-care model matches the intensity of nutritional intervention to the complexity of the mental health condition, from universal guidance to intensive nutritional therapy.
- Nutritional interventions are positioned as adjunctive — working alongside conventional treatments like psychotherapy and medication, not replacing them.
- The economic case for nutritional psychiatry integration is substantial, with projections suggesting significant cost savings from improved dietary patterns at the population level.
- Cultural sensitivity, individual adaptation, and progress over perfection are core principles of the guidelines.
## This Week's Practice
**Conduct your own dietary assessment.** Using the MEDAS framework, answer these 14 questions about your typical weekly diet to estimate your Mediterranean diet adherence score. For each "yes" answer, give yourself 1 point:
1. Do you use olive oil as your main cooking fat?
2. Do you consume 2+ tablespoons of olive oil daily?
3. Do you eat 2+ servings of vegetables daily (at least one raw)?
4. Do you eat 3+ pieces of fruit daily?
5. Do you eat less than 1 serving of red meat or processed meat daily?
6. Do you eat less than 1 serving of butter, margarine, or cream daily?
7. Do you drink fewer than 1 sugar-sweetened beverage daily?
8. Do you drink 3+ glasses of wine weekly? (If you do not drink alcohol, score based on other polyphenol sources like tea or berries.)
9. Do you eat 3+ servings of legumes weekly?
10. Do you eat 3+ servings of fish or seafood weekly?
11. Do you eat fewer than 3 commercial pastries or sweets weekly?
12. Do you eat 3+ servings of nuts weekly?
13. Do you prefer chicken, turkey, or rabbit over red meat?
14. Do you eat 2+ servings of sofrito (tomato-based sauce with garlic and onion) weekly?
Record your score and reflect on which areas offer the easiest opportunities for improvement. Bring this assessment to your next healthcare appointment if appropriate.
