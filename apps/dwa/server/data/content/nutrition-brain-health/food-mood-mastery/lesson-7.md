Aging is a universal human experience, and the intersection of nutrition, mental health, and cognitive function becomes increasingly critical as we age. This lesson addresses a population often underserved in nutritional psychiatry: older adults. We examine the MIND diet — specifically designed for brain health — the underappreciated problem of polypharmacy-related nutrient depletion, and practical strategies for maintaining mental wellness in later life.

The stakes are high. Depression affects approximately 10-15% of community-dwelling older adults and up to 40% of those in long-term care settings. Cognitive decline, from mild impairment to dementia, affects millions worldwide. What makes this lesson particularly relevant is the growing evidence that dietary patterns can meaningfully modify risk for both depression and cognitive decline in older adults — and that many of the nutritional interventions are safe, accessible, and complementary to existing treatments.

## The MIND Diet: Designed for Brain Health

The MIND diet (Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay) was developed by researchers at Rush University Medical Center specifically to protect brain health. It combines elements of the Mediterranean diet and the DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension), emphasizing the foods from each pattern that have the strongest evidence for neurological benefit.

### MIND Diet Components

The MIND diet identifies 10 brain-healthy food groups and 5 unhealthy food groups:

**Brain-healthy foods (eat more)**:
1. Green leafy vegetables (at least 6 servings per week) — kale, spinach, collard greens, lettuce
2. Other vegetables (at least 1 serving per day)
3. Berries (at least 2 servings per week) — especially blueberries and strawberries
4. Nuts (at least 5 servings per week)
5. Olive oil (as the primary cooking oil)
6. Whole grains (at least 3 servings per day)
7. Fish (at least 1 serving per week, non-fried)
8. Beans (at least 4 servings per week)
9. Poultry (at least 2 servings per week)
10. Wine (1 glass per day, optional — the evidence for wine specifically is debated, and other polyphenol sources like berries and tea are reasonable alternatives)

**Foods to limit**:
1. Red meat (fewer than 4 servings per week)
2. Butter and margarine (less than 1 tablespoon per day)
3. Cheese (less than 1 serving per week)
4. Pastries and sweets (fewer than 5 per week)
5. Fried or fast food (less than 1 serving per week)

### The cMIND Study (2026)

A major 2026 study examining the MIND diet in a Chinese population (cMIND — adapted for Chinese dietary patterns) found that higher adherence was associated with a 21% lower risk of cognitive impairment. Importantly, the study identified a nonlinear relationship — meaning the greatest benefit came from moving from low to moderate adherence. The incremental benefit of moving from moderate to high adherence was smaller.

This finding has a profoundly practical implication: you do not need perfect adherence to benefit. Simply incorporating more of the brain-healthy food groups — even if you cannot follow the full pattern — provides meaningful cognitive protection.

> **Research Brief:** cMIND Diet and Cognitive Impairment
>
> **Study**: Chinese MIND Diet (cMIND) and cognitive impairment study, 2026 · Sample: Large Chinese community-dwelling older adult cohort · Design: Prospective cohort study
>
> **Finding**: Higher adherence to the cMIND dietary pattern was associated with a 21% lower risk of cognitive impairment, with a nonlinear relationship showing the greatest benefit from moving from low to moderate adherence rather than from moderate to high adherence.
>
> **Context**: This study extends the MIND diet evidence to a non-Western population, demonstrating cross-cultural applicability when the diet is appropriately adapted. The nonlinear finding is important for public health messaging — it means that modest dietary improvements can yield significant cognitive protection. Limitations include the observational design (causation cannot be confirmed) and the difficulty of accurately measuring long-term dietary patterns.
>
> **Practical Application**: Focus on achievable increases in brain-healthy food groups rather than pursuing perfect MIND diet adherence. Even adding two to three additional servings of leafy greens, berries, or nuts per week could contribute to cognitive protection.
>
> **Evidence Quality**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Strong (Large prospective cohort with clinically meaningful effect size)
>
> *Source: Research Document, cMIND 2026 Study*


### The US MIND Study (2024)

A 2024 US-based study found a more modest but still significant association: a 4% overall reduced risk of cognitive decline with higher MIND diet adherence, with a stronger 6% risk reduction observed in women specifically. The smaller effect size compared to the cMIND study may reflect differences in baseline diet quality (US adults may have generally lower dietary quality to begin with), population characteristics, or measurement methods.

The gender difference is intriguing and may relate to hormonal changes during menopause that affect brain metabolism and neuroprotection. Estrogen has neuroprotective properties, and its decline during menopause may create a window of vulnerability where dietary factors become especially important. The MIND diet's emphasis on anti-inflammatory and antioxidant-rich foods may partially compensate for this hormonal shift.

## Polypharmacy and Nutrient Depletion

Polypharmacy — defined as the regular use of five or more medications — is extremely common among older adults. Approximately 40% of adults over 65 take five or more prescription medications, and many take additional over-the-counter supplements. While these medications are often necessary, many of them deplete nutrients critical for brain function and mental health.

This is one of the most underrecognized contributors to mental health difficulties in older adults. A person may be appropriately treated for depression with an SSRI, for gastric reflux with a proton pump inhibitor (PPI), and for a chronic pain condition with another medication — and the combination may be depleting nutrients that worsen the very mental health condition being treated.

### Common Medication-Nutrient Depletions

**SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors)**: Can deplete sodium (hyponatremia), calcium, and folate. The sodium depletion is particularly concerning in older adults and can cause confusion, falls, and worsened depression — symptoms that may be misattributed to the psychiatric condition rather than the medication.

**Lithium**: Depletes thyroid hormones (requiring monitoring), and can affect calcium and magnesium metabolism. Thyroid dysfunction from lithium can mimic or worsen mood symptoms.

**Antipsychotics**: Associated with metabolic syndrome, which increases inflammation and worsens insulin resistance — both of which have mental health implications. Some antipsychotics also affect vitamin D metabolism.

**Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)**: These widely prescribed acid-reducing medications significantly impair absorption of vitamin B12, magnesium, calcium, and iron — all critical for brain function. Long-term PPI use has been independently associated with increased risk of dementia, though whether this is due to nutrient depletion, other mechanisms, or confounding factors is still debated.

**Metformin**: Commonly prescribed for type 2 diabetes, metformin depletes vitamin B12 and folate. Given the high rates of comorbid diabetes and depression in older adults, this depletion can significantly affect mental health.

**Diuretics**: Deplete potassium, magnesium, zinc, and B vitamins. Magnesium depletion alone can cause anxiety, insomnia, and muscle cramps.

### The B12 Assessment Problem

Vitamin B12 deserves special attention because standard blood tests often miss clinically significant deficiency. Serum B12 levels can appear "normal" even when functional B12 status is inadequate. This is because a significant portion of serum B12 may be bound to haptocorrin (an inactive carrier) rather than transcobalamin (the active carrier that delivers B12 to cells).

More reliable markers of B12 status include:
- **Methylmalonic acid (MMA)**: Elevated levels indicate functional B12 deficiency even when serum B12 is in the "normal" range
- **Homocysteine**: Elevated levels suggest insufficient B12 (and/or folate) for methylation reactions
- **Mean corpuscular volume (MCV)**: Elevated MCV (enlarged red blood cells) can indicate B12 deficiency, though it is a late marker

For older adults on medications known to deplete B12 (PPIs, metformin), requesting MMA and homocysteine levels in addition to serum B12 provides a more accurate picture.

## Geriatric Screening Tools

Two validated screening tools are particularly useful for assessing nutrition and mental health in older adults:

### Mini Nutritional Assessment - Short Form (MNA-SF)

The MNA-SF is a validated six-item screening tool that can be completed in under five minutes. It assesses:
1. Decline in food intake over the past three months
2. Weight loss in the past three months
3. Mobility
4. Psychological stress or acute disease in the past three months
5. Neuropsychological problems (dementia or depression)
6. Body mass index or calf circumference

Scores of 0-7 indicate malnutrition, 8-11 indicate risk of malnutrition, and 12-14 indicate normal nutritional status. The MNA-SF is recommended by geriatric medical societies as a routine screening tool for older adults in all healthcare settings.

### Geriatric Depression Scale - 15 Item (GDS-15)

The GDS-15 is a brief, validated depression screening tool designed specifically for older adults. Unlike general depression scales, it avoids somatic symptoms (such as fatigue, appetite changes, and sleep difficulties) that may reflect physical illness rather than depression in older populations. A score of 5 or above suggests depression warranting further evaluation.

Using the MNA-SF and GDS-15 together allows clinicians to assess both nutritional status and depression simultaneously — identifying the intersection where nutritional interventions may be most beneficial.

## Practical Application: Supporting Brain Health Across the Lifespan

**For older adults themselves**:
- Discuss your full medication list (including over-the-counter medications) with your doctor or pharmacist, specifically asking about nutrient depletion risks
- Request comprehensive blood work including B12, MMA, homocysteine, vitamin D, folate, magnesium, and iron/ferritin
- Focus on incorporating more MIND diet foods rather than perfect adherence — even modest increases in leafy greens, berries, and nuts provide benefit
- If appetite is reduced (common in older adults), prioritize nutrient-dense foods and consider smaller, more frequent meals

**For family members and caregivers**:
- Help compile a complete medication list for healthcare appointments
- Support access to brain-healthy foods through meal preparation, grocery shopping, or meal delivery services
- Be aware that nutritional deficiency can present as cognitive impairment, confusion, fatigue, or worsened depression — symptoms that may be incorrectly attributed to "just aging"
- Advocate for comprehensive nutritional screening, especially during hospitalizations or care transitions

**For healthcare providers**:
- Integrate the MNA-SF into routine geriatric assessments
- Review medication lists specifically for nutrient depletion risk at every visit
- Order functional B12 markers (MMA, homocysteine) rather than relying on serum B12 alone
- When prescribing new medications, proactively address potential nutrient depletions
- Consider the MIND diet as a first-line, low-risk intervention for cognitive protection and mood support

## Key Takeaways

- The MIND diet was specifically designed for brain health and has been shown to reduce cognitive impairment risk by 4-21% depending on population and adherence level.
- The cMIND study (2026) found a nonlinear relationship — the greatest cognitive benefit comes from moving from low to moderate MIND diet adherence, making even modest dietary improvements meaningful.
- Polypharmacy affects approximately 40% of adults over 65, and many common medications (SSRIs, PPIs, metformin, lithium, diuretics) deplete nutrients critical for brain function and mental health.
- Standard serum B12 tests can miss functional deficiency — MMA and homocysteine levels provide more accurate assessment, especially in older adults on depleting medications.
- The MNA-SF and GDS-15 are validated geriatric screening tools that can identify nutritional risk and depression simultaneously, helping target nutritional interventions appropriately.
- Nutritional deficiency in older adults can present as cognitive impairment, confusion, fatigue, or worsened depression — symptoms that may be misattributed to aging or psychiatric illness.

## This Week's Practice

**Medication-nutrient interaction review.** Whether for yourself or an older adult in your care:

1. **List all medications and supplements** currently being taken (prescription, over-the-counter, and dietary supplements).
2. **Research nutrient depletions** for each medication using a reliable drug-nutrient interaction database (your pharmacist can help with this).
3. **Identify overlap**: Are multiple medications depleting the same nutrient? This compounding effect is where the greatest risk lies.
4. **Plan a conversation**: Bring this list to the next healthcare appointment and ask specifically about nutrient monitoring and whether supplementation is warranted.
5. **MIND diet inventory**: Review the 10 brain-healthy food groups. Which ones are currently well-represented in the diet? Which ones could be increased? Identify one to two achievable additions for next week.
