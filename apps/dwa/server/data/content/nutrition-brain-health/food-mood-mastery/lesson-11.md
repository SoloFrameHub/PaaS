We began this track by looking at how food affects your brain. In this penultimate lesson, we zoom out to the widest possible lens: how our collective food systems affect both planetary health and global mental wellness. This is not a detour from nutritional psychiatry — it is its logical extension. The food system that damages human mental health is the same food system that damages the planet, and the dietary patterns that protect your brain are remarkably similar to those that protect the environment.

The 2025 update to the landmark EAT-Lancet Commission report makes this connection explicit. The commission's analysis shows that a global shift toward sustainable, plant-forward dietary patterns could simultaneously prevent millions of premature deaths, save trillions in healthcare costs, and reduce greenhouse gas emissions by more than half. This lesson explores these findings and their implications for your personal food choices and for the communities you are part of.

## The EAT-Lancet 2025 Update: Food, Planet, Health

The original EAT-Lancet Commission report, published in 2019, was the first comprehensive scientific assessment of what constitutes a healthy diet within planetary boundaries. The 2025 update refines and extends these findings with updated data and modeling.

### Key Findings

**Scale of impact**: The updated analysis models the effects of dietary shifts for a projected global population of 9.6 billion people. It estimates that widespread adoption of the planetary health diet could prevent approximately 15 million premature deaths annually — a staggering figure that includes deaths from cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancer, and nutrition-related illness.

**Economic projections**: The commission projects healthcare and productivity savings of approximately $5 trillion globally from these dietary shifts — the same figure cited in the ISNPR guidelines (Lesson 1), reflecting the enormous economic burden of diet-related disease.

**Environmental impact**: Shifting to the planetary health diet could reduce food system greenhouse gas emissions by more than 50%, while also reducing land use, water consumption, and biodiversity loss. The food system currently accounts for approximately 26-30% of global greenhouse gas emissions, making dietary change one of the most powerful climate actions available.

### The Planetary Health Diet

The planetary health diet is not a rigid prescription but a framework that flexes across cultural and geographic contexts. Its core composition includes:

- **Fruits and vegetables**: Approximately half of the plate (roughly 500g per day)
- **Whole grains**: Major source of calories and nutrients
- **Legumes, nuts, and seeds**: Primary protein sources
- **Fish**: Moderate amounts (about 28g per day)
- **Poultry and eggs**: Small amounts
- **Red meat**: Very limited (about 14g per day — approximately one serving per week)
- **Dairy**: Moderate amounts (about 250g per day)
- **Added sugars and saturated fats**: Minimal

## The Mental Health Connection: Why Planetary Health Matters for Your Brain

The link between planetary health and mental health operates through several pathways:

### Dietary Acculturation and Psychiatric Prevalence

When populations shift from traditional dietary patterns to Westernized diets — a process called dietary acculturation — rates of psychiatric disorders increase. This has been documented in immigrant populations, in countries undergoing rapid economic development, and in Indigenous communities experiencing loss of traditional food systems.

The mechanism is both nutritional and cultural. Nutritionally, Westernized diets are higher in ultra-processed foods, refined sugars, and inflammatory fats, and lower in the fiber, polyphenols, and omega-3 fatty acids that support brain health. Culturally, the loss of traditional food practices disrupts social bonds, intergenerational knowledge, and cultural identity — all of which are protective factors for mental health.

This is not a call to freeze food cultures in time. Dietary evolution is natural and inevitable. But the speed and nature of dietary acculturation driven by global food corporations often undermines both nutritional quality and cultural food traditions simultaneously.

### Food Insecurity and Mental Health

Food insecurity — defined as limited or uncertain access to adequate food — is one of the strongest social determinants of mental health. The relationship is consistent across countries, cultures, and age groups:

- Adults experiencing food insecurity have 2-3 times higher rates of depression and anxiety compared to food-secure adults
- Children in food-insecure households show higher rates of behavioral problems, anxiety, and depression
- The stress of food insecurity affects mental health through both nutritional pathways (poor dietary quality) and psychosocial pathways (chronic stress, shame, social exclusion)
- Food insecurity is both a cause and consequence of mental health problems, creating a vicious cycle

Climate change is projected to increase food insecurity globally, particularly in low-income countries and vulnerable communities. Extreme weather events, shifting growing seasons, reduced crop yields, and disrupted supply chains all threaten food access. This means that climate change is, among many other things, a mental health crisis.

<ScenarioCard title="The Vicious Cycle in Action">Consider a single parent working two jobs in a neighborhood with no grocery store within walking distance. They rely on the nearby convenience store for ultra-processed foods, which further depletes the nutrients their brain needs. Their children experience behavioral difficulties linked to both food insecurity stress and poor nutritional quality. This is not a failure of individual willpower — it is a systemic failure that operates through both nutritional and psychosocial pathways simultaneously.</ScenarioCard>

## Food Sovereignty, Equity, and Access

### Beyond Individual Choice

Much of nutritional psychiatry — including most of this track — has focused on individual dietary choices. But individual choice operates within systems. If you live in a food desert (an area with limited access to affordable, nutritious food), your ability to follow a Mediterranean diet or a planetary health diet is constrained by geography, infrastructure, and economics, regardless of your knowledge or motivation.

Approximately 23.5 million Americans live in food deserts, and these areas disproportionately affect low-income communities and communities of color. The same communities that face the highest rates of food insecurity also face the highest rates of mental health burden and the least access to mental health care.

### Food Sovereignty

Food sovereignty is the right of peoples to healthy and culturally appropriate food produced through ecologically sound and sustainable methods, and their right to define their own food and agriculture systems. This concept — developed by the international peasant movement La Via Campesina — goes beyond food security (having enough food) to address who controls food systems, who benefits from them, and whose food traditions are valued.

For mental health, food sovereignty matters because:
- Control over one's food system is empowering — a direct counter to the helplessness that characterizes depression
- Traditional food systems often embody the dietary diversity, fermentation practices, and whole-food approaches that support brain health
- Community food systems build social connections that protect against isolation and loneliness
- Self-determined food practices support cultural identity, which is a protective factor for mental health

<Callout type="reflection" title="From Plate to System">Notice the shift in this lesson: we have moved from asking "What should I eat for my brain?" to "What food system supports collective mental health?" Both questions matter. Individual nutritional choices happen within systems — and changing those systems is itself a form of mental health advocacy.</Callout>

### Cultural Sensitivity in Nutritional Recommendations

Throughout this track, we have emphasized that the Mediterranean diet is the most studied dietary pattern for mental health. But it is not the only healthy dietary pattern. Traditional diets from Japan, West Africa, South Asia, Latin America, Indigenous cultures worldwide, and many other traditions share the core features that support brain health: emphasis on whole foods, dietary diversity, fermentation, plant-forward eating, and communal meals.

Effective nutritional psychiatry must respect and incorporate these diverse food traditions rather than imposing a single cultural framework. When we recommend "Mediterranean-style" eating, we mean the *principles* — whole foods, healthy fats, abundant vegetables, legumes, moderate fish, limited ultra-processed food — not the specific cultural expression.

## Practical Application: Connecting Personal Choices to Systemic Change

**At the personal level**:
- Recognize the overlap: the dietary pattern that best supports your brain health (Mediterranean, MIND, traditional whole-food diets) closely aligns with the planetary health diet. Choosing these patterns benefits both you and the planet.
- Reduce ultra-processed food consumption — for your brain health, for the environment, and as a step toward a more sustainable food system.
- When possible, choose locally grown, seasonal food. This supports local food systems, reduces transportation emissions, and often provides fresher, more nutrient-dense options.
- Reduce food waste — approximately one-third of all food produced globally is wasted, contributing to both environmental destruction and food insecurity.

**At the community level**:
- Support community food initiatives: community gardens, food cooperatives, farmers' markets, mutual aid food programs.
- Advocate for food access in underserved communities — this is a mental health intervention.
- Learn about and support traditional food practices in your community, particularly those of Indigenous and immigrant communities.
- Consider volunteering with or donating to organizations addressing food insecurity.

**At the systemic level**:
- Support policies that make nutritious food more affordable and accessible (universal school meals, SNAP improvements, food desert initiatives).
- Advocate for food system transparency — labeling, corporate accountability, and regulation of ultra-processed food marketing (especially to children).
- Support climate action as mental health action — the two are inseparable.
- Engage with food sovereignty movements and support the rights of communities to define their own food systems.

## This Week's Practice

**Food system awareness exercise.** This week, expand your nutritional awareness from your plate to your food system:

1. **Food origin tracking**: For three days, note where your food comes from. How much is locally produced? How much is imported? How much is ultra-processed? You do not need to change anything — just observe.

2. **Food access mapping**: Identify the nearest sources of fresh, affordable, nutrient-dense food to your home. How far do you have to travel? How does this compare to the nearest fast food or convenience store? If there is a gap, you have identified a food access issue in your community.

3. **Cultural food reflection**: What traditional food practices from your cultural background support brain health? (Shared meals, fermented foods, seasonal eating, specific preparations.) Is there one practice you could revive or strengthen?

4. **Community action step**: Identify one community-level food initiative you can support this month — a food bank, community garden, school meal program, farmers' market, or food sovereignty organization. Take one concrete step (volunteer, donate, attend a meeting, write to a representative).

5. **Personal-planetary alignment**: Review your typical weekly diet. Identify one change that would benefit both your brain health and planetary health. This might be replacing one red meat meal with a legume-based meal, buying more seasonal produce, reducing food waste, or choosing less-packaged options.
