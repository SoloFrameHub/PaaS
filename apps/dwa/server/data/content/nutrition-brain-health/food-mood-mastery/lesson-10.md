> **Important Note**: This lesson discusses intermittent fasting and time-restricted eating. These dietary patterns involve periods of not eating and are contraindicated for individuals with active eating disorders, those at risk for eating disorders, pregnant or breastfeeding individuals, children and adolescents, and people with certain medical conditions (diabetes requiring insulin, hypoglycemia disorders). Eating disorder screening is critical before considering any fasting protocol. If you have a history of disordered eating, please approach this content with care and discuss it with your treatment team.

Intermittent fasting (IF) has become one of the most discussed dietary approaches of the past decade. Much of the public conversation has focused on weight management, but a more nuanced and scientifically interesting story is emerging around the relationship between meal timing, circadian rhythms, and mental health. Three significant 2025 studies have substantially advanced our understanding of this relationship.

This lesson examines the current evidence with the same rigor and honesty we applied to the ketogenic diet in Lesson 9. The evidence here is stronger in some respects — we have randomized controlled trial data — but the field is young and nuance matters.

## The 2025 JAMA Network Open RCT

The most significant recent study on intermittent fasting and mental health was published in JAMA Network Open in 2025 — a randomized controlled trial that specifically assessed mood and psychological outcomes alongside metabolic measures.

The trial compared time-restricted eating (TRE) to standard dietary advice for weight management. The key mental health finding was reassuring rather than dramatic: TRE was a viable approach for weight management that produced **no adverse mood effects**. Participants did not experience increased depression, anxiety, irritability, or disordered eating behaviors compared to the control group.

This may seem like a modest finding, but it addresses a legitimate concern. Previous observational studies had raised questions about whether restricting eating windows might increase psychological distress, preoccupation with food, or trigger disordered eating. The JAMA trial provides RCT-level evidence that, for appropriately screened individuals, time-restricted eating does not worsen mood or psychological wellbeing.

The trial did not find that TRE *improved* mental health outcomes beyond what would be expected from general healthy lifestyle changes. This is an important nuance: the current RCT evidence supports TRE as psychologically safe, not as a mental health treatment per se.

## The 2025 Neuroscience Study: Anxiety and Brain Connectivity

A separate 2025 neuroscience study provided much more striking findings about the potential mental health benefits of intermittent fasting. This study used neuroimaging to examine how intermittent fasting affects brain connectivity patterns associated with anxiety.

The headline findings were remarkable:

1. **Intermittent fasting rapidly decreased anxiety** — the effect was detected within the study period, not over months or years
2. **Reduced amygdala-parahippocampal connectivity** — the amygdala is the brain's fear center, and the parahippocampus is involved in contextual memory. Connectivity between these regions is elevated in anxiety disorders, meaning the brain is constantly linking environmental contexts to fear. IF reduced this hyperconnectivity.
3. **Effects maintained for 2+ months** — the anxiety reduction and connectivity changes persisted well beyond the fasting intervention itself
4. **Only 1 dropout in 50 days** — the protocol was remarkably well-tolerated, suggesting feasibility for wider application

The reduction in amygdala-parahippocampal connectivity is particularly interesting because it suggests a specific neural mechanism through which intermittent fasting might affect anxiety — not just a general calming effect, but a measurable change in the brain circuits that generate and maintain anxious states.

However, several caveats apply. This was a relatively small study. The specific IF protocol used may not generalize to all forms of intermittent fasting. The neuroimaging findings need replication. And the relationship between observed brain connectivity changes and clinically meaningful anxiety reduction requires further investigation.

### Gut-Brain Axis Mechanisms

A 2025 systematic review synthesized the evidence for how intermittent fasting affects mental health through the gut-brain axis — a mechanism that connects directly to the earlier courses in this track.

Fasting periods allow the gut to enter a "housekeeping" phase called the migrating motor complex (MMC), which sweeps debris and bacteria from the small intestine. This process is disrupted by constant eating. Regular fasting periods may support a healthier gut microbiome, reduce intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), and decrease systemic inflammation — all of which have been linked to improved mental health outcomes.

The systematic review found consistent evidence across animal and preliminary human studies that intermittent fasting:
- Modifies gut microbiome composition, generally increasing diversity
- Reduces markers of systemic inflammation (CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha)
- Improves intestinal barrier function
- Alters neurotransmitter production by gut bacteria

>

>
> **Finding**: Intermittent fasting rapidly decreased anxiety and reduced amygdala-parahippocampal connectivity (a neural signature of anxiety disorders). These effects were maintained for 2+ months after the intervention. The protocol showed excellent tolerability with only 1 dropout in 50 days.
>
> **Context**: This study provides the first neuroimaging evidence for a specific brain mechanism underlying the anxiolytic effects of intermittent fasting. The amygdala-parahippocampal connectivity finding aligns with established neuroscience of anxiety circuits. However, the sample was small, the study lacked a randomized control group (pre-post design), and the specific IF protocol may not generalize to all fasting approaches. The 2+ month maintenance of effects is promising but longer-term follow-up is needed.
>
> **Practical Application**: These findings are exciting but preliminary. They suggest a plausible brain mechanism for anxiety reduction through intermittent fasting, but they do not yet constitute sufficient evidence to recommend IF as an anxiety treatment. Individuals interested in IF for anxiety should discuss it with their healthcare provider and ensure eating disorder screening first.
>

## Time-Restricted Eating Protocols

For those who are appropriately screened and interested in exploring time-restricted eating, the most studied protocols involve an 8-10 hour eating window:

**8-hour window (16:8)**: Eating between, for example, 10 AM and 6 PM, with a 16-hour fasting period. This is the most commonly studied protocol and the one used in many of the research studies discussed above.

**10-hour window (14:10)**: A more moderate approach with eating between, for example, 8 AM and 6 PM. This may be more sustainable for many people and still provides meaningful fasting periods for gut health and metabolic benefits.

### Circadian Alignment

Emerging research suggests that *when* you eat during your eating window matters as much as the length of the window. Eating in alignment with your circadian rhythm — larger meals earlier in the day when insulin sensitivity is highest, and lighter meals or no eating in the hours before sleep — may optimize both metabolic and mental health outcomes.

**Early time-restricted eating** (eating window earlier in the day, such as 7 AM to 3 PM) has shown some advantages over late time-restricted eating in preliminary studies, including better blood sugar regulation, improved sleep quality, and potentially better mood outcomes. However, this schedule is difficult for many people due to social and work obligations.

**Practical circadian eating principles** (for any schedule):
- Eat your largest meal when you are most alert and active
- Avoid large meals within 2-3 hours of bedtime
- Maintain consistent meal timing day to day (your gut microbiome and metabolic systems thrive on predictability)
- Get morning light exposure to strengthen circadian rhythms (which interact with meal timing)

## Eating Disorder Screening: Critical for Fasting Protocols
This point cannot be overstated: eating disorder screening must precede any fasting protocol. Intermittent fasting involves deliberate periods of not eating, which can:
- Reactivate restriction patterns in individuals with anorexia nervosa history
- Trigger binge-restriction cycles in individuals with bulimia nervosa or binge eating disorder
- Provide a "socially acceptable" framework for food avoidance in individuals with ARFID or other restrictive patterns
- Fuel orthorexic tendencies by adding another "rule" about eating
**Before considering any form of intermittent fasting, complete the SCOFF screening tool (Lesson 8) and honestly reflect on the following questions:**
1. Have you ever been diagnosed with or treated for an eating disorder?
2. Do you experience significant anxiety or guilt around food?
3. Have you engaged in patterns of restrictive eating, binge eating, or purging?
4. Is your interest in fasting primarily about weight loss or food control?
5. Would missing a scheduled meal cause you significant distress beyond normal hunger?
If you answer yes to any of these questions, discuss with an eating disorder-informed therapist before pursuing any fasting approach.
<Callout type="warning" title="Eating Disorder Risk Is Real">Intermittent fasting can provide a socially acceptable framework for food restriction. If you notice increased anxiety around food, preoccupation with eating windows, guilt about "breaking" a fast, or a sense of control or superiority from not eating — these are warning signs, not signs of discipline. Please revisit the SCOFF screening tool in Lesson 8 and speak with a professional.</Callout>

## Practical Application: If Intermittent Fasting Is Appropriate for You
**For individuals who have been appropriately screened and cleared:**
1. **Start gently**: Begin with a 12-hour eating window (such as 7 AM to 7 PM) — most people naturally fast this long overnight. Gradually shorten to 10 hours, then 8 hours over several weeks if it feels sustainable.
2. **Prioritize nutrition quality during eating windows**: The benefits of IF are undermined if the eating window is filled with ultra-processed, nutrient-poor foods. Focus on the Mediterranean dietary pattern during your eating window.
3. **Listen to your body**: If you experience significant irritability, difficulty concentrating, preoccupation with food, or worsening mood, the protocol is not working for you. Flexibility and self-awareness are more important than rigid adherence.
4. **Maintain social eating**: If a fasting schedule consistently prevents you from sharing meals with family, friends, or colleagues, the social and psychological costs may outweigh the metabolic benefits.
5. **Stay hydrated**: Water, herbal tea, and black coffee (if tolerated) are typically consumed during fasting periods. Dehydration can worsen mood, concentration, and anxiety.
6. **Monitor and adjust**: Keep a brief daily log of mood, energy, sleep quality, and anxiety for the first month. Look for patterns. Share this data with your healthcare provider.

## Key Takeaways
- A 2025 JAMA Network Open RCT found that time-restricted eating is psychologically safe — producing no adverse mood effects — addressing concerns about restricted eating windows worsening mental health.
- A 2025 neuroscience study found that intermittent fasting rapidly decreased anxiety and reduced amygdala-parahippocampal brain connectivity (a neural marker of anxiety), with effects maintained for 2+ months and excellent tolerability (only 1 dropout in 50 days).
- A 2025 systematic review supports gut-brain axis mechanisms including improved microbiome diversity, reduced inflammation, and better intestinal barrier function during fasting periods.
- Time-restricted eating protocols of 8-10 hour eating windows are most studied, with early eating windows showing potential advantages for circadian alignment.
- Eating disorder screening is critical before any fasting protocol — intermittent fasting can reactivate restriction patterns, trigger binge-restriction cycles, and fuel orthorexic tendencies.
- The evidence supports IF as psychologically safe for screened individuals, with promising but preliminary findings for anxiety reduction — not yet sufficient for clinical recommendation as a mental health treatment.
## This Week's Practice
**Circadian eating awareness (no fasting required).** This exercise focuses on circadian alignment rather than restriction:
1. **Track your eating times** for five days: Note the time of your first and last bite of food each day. Calculate your natural eating window.
2. **Observe your circadian patterns**: Note your energy levels, mood, and hunger at different times of day. When do you feel most alert? When does your energy dip? When are you genuinely hungry versus eating by habit or schedule?
3. **Assess meal-sleep proximity**: How close to bedtime is your last meal or snack? Note your sleep quality on nights when you eat late versus earlier.
4. **Consistency check**: How consistent are your meal times from day to day? Large variations in eating schedule can disrupt circadian rhythms.
5. **Reflect**: Based on your observations, is there one small circadian alignment change that might support your mental health? (For example: eating breakfast within an hour of waking, finishing eating 2-3 hours before bed, or making lunch your largest meal.) Try this one change for the following week if it feels comfortable.
**Remember**: This exercise is about awareness and gentle alignment, not restriction. If tracking eating times increases food anxiety, stop the exercise and return to the mindful eating practices from Lesson 3.
