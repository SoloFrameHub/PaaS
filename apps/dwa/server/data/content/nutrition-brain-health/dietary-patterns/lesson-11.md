You have spent ten lessons learning the science — how ultra-processed foods increase depression risk, how the Mediterranean diet achieves remission, how inflammation scores predict mood, how fiber feeds the bacteria that protect your brain. Now it is time to turn that knowledge inward. Where does *your* dietary pattern actually stand? Not where you think it stands, not where you wish it stood, but where it genuinely is today.

This lesson introduces three validated assessment tools that researchers use to evaluate dietary patterns. You will use simplified versions of each to score your own eating habits across three dimensions: Mediterranean adherence, ultra-processed food exposure, and dietary inflammatory potential. The goal is not to achieve a perfect score — it is to identify the specific areas where small changes would have the greatest impact on your mental health. Think of this as a diagnostic, not a judgment.

> **Important Note**: If you have a history of disordered eating or find that tracking, scoring, or analyzing your food intake triggers anxiety, obsessive thinking, or shame, please approach this lesson with extra caution — or skip the self-assessment portions entirely. The information about these tools is valuable as general knowledge even if you choose not to apply them to yourself. There is no obligation to complete any of the self-assessment exercises. Your mental health comes first.

## Tool 1: The Mediterranean Diet Adherence Score (MEDAS)

The **MEDAS** is a 14-item questionnaire used in major clinical trials, including the PREDIMED study, to assess how closely someone's eating pattern aligns with the Mediterranean diet. Each item is scored 0 or 1, giving a total score of 0-14.

### Your Simplified MEDAS Assessment

For each of the following questions, give yourself 1 point if the statement describes your eating pattern on most days (more than half the time). Give yourself 0 if it does not.

| # | Question | Your Score (0 or 1) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | I use olive oil as my main cooking fat | |
| 2 | I eat 2 or more servings of vegetables per day | |
| 3 | I eat 3 or more servings of fruit per day | |
| 4 | I eat less than 1 serving of red or processed meat per day | |
| 5 | I eat less than 1 serving of butter or margarine per day | |
| 6 | I drink less than 1 sugary beverage per day | |
| 7 | I eat 3 or more servings of legumes per week | |
| 8 | I eat 3 or more servings of fish or seafood per week | |
| 9 | I eat fewer than 3 servings of commercial sweets or pastries per week | |
| 10 | I eat 3 or more servings of nuts per week (about a handful each) | |
| 11 | I prefer chicken, turkey, or fish over red meat | |
| 12 | I eat dishes made with tomato, garlic, onion, or leeks sauteed in olive oil at least twice per week | |
| 13 | I eat whole grains (brown rice, oats, whole wheat) rather than refined grains most of the time | |
| 14 | I eat at least 2 servings per week of a traditional stew or dish rich in vegetables and legumes | |

### Interpreting Your MEDAS Score

| Score Range | Interpretation | Action |
|------------|---------------|--------|
| **0-4** | Low adherence — significant room for improvement | Focus on the 2-3 easiest items to change first |
| **5-7** | Moderate adherence — a good foundation | Identify specific gaps and target them |
| **8-10** | Good adherence — Mediterranean-leaning pattern | Fine-tune and maintain consistency |
| **11-14** | High adherence — strong Mediterranean pattern | Maintain this pattern; focus on diversity |

Research shows that each 2-point increase in MEDAS score is associated with measurable improvements in inflammatory markers and mental health outcomes. You do not need to reach 14 — even moving from 4 to 6 represents meaningful progress.

<FlipCard front="Myth: The Mediterranean diet only works for people who live in Mediterranean countries." back="Fact: The MEDAS has been validated across multiple cultural settings, not just Mediterranean populations. > **Research Brief:** MEDAS Score and Health Outcomes
>
> **Study**: PREDIMED Trial and subsequent validation studies · Sample: N=7,447 in the original PREDIMED; validated in multiple populations · Design: Randomized controlled trial with observational follow-up
>
> **Finding**: Higher MEDAS scores were associated with lower risk of depression, cardiovascular disease, and cognitive decline in a dose-dependent manner. Participants scoring 9 or above showed the strongest protective effects. The MEDAS was validated as a reliable predictor of both dietary quality and health outcomes across different cultural settings, not just Mediterranean populations.
>
> **Context**: The MEDAS was developed as a quick screening tool — it can be completed in 2-3 minutes. Despite its simplicity, it captures the essential elements of the Mediterranean pattern and predicts health outcomes with reasonable accuracy. Its limitation is that it does not capture food quality within categories (e.g., all olive oils score equally, regardless of extra virgin vs. refined) or portion sizes precisely.
>
> **Practical Application**: Use the MEDAS as a monthly check-in rather than a daily tracker. Scoring yourself once a month can reveal trends in your dietary pattern without the burden of daily food logging. Focus on increasing your score by 2 points at a time.
>
> **Evidence Quality**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Strong
>
> *Source: Research Document, PREDIMED and Mediterranean Adherence Assessment*

### Interpreting Your UPF Estimate

| UPF % of Diet | Interpretation | Mental Health Risk Context |
|---------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **&lt; 20%** | Low UPF — well below average | Below the risk threshold in most studies |
| **20-35%** | Moderate UPF — below Western average | Some room for improvement |

## Tool 3: Dietary Inflammatory Index — Simplified

The full DII scoring system evaluates 45 food parameters and requires specialized software. However, you can estimate your dietary inflammatory direction using a simplified assessment. This will not give you a precise DII score, but it will indicate whether your overall pattern leans pro-inflammatory or anti-inflammatory.

### Your Inflammatory Balance Assessment

**Anti-Inflammatory Foods (score +1 for each you eat regularly — 3+ times per week)**:

| Food | Consumed 3+/week? | Score |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale, arugula) | | |
| Berries (blueberries, strawberries, raspberries) | | |
| Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) | | |
| Extra virgin olive oil (as primary fat) | | |
| Nuts (walnuts, almonds, cashews) | | |
| Legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas) | | |
| Turmeric, ginger, or garlic | | |
| Green tea | | |
| Whole grains (oats, brown rice, quinoa) | | |
| Colorful vegetables (tomatoes, peppers, carrots) | | |
| **Anti-Inflammatory Total** | | **/10** |

**Pro-Inflammatory Foods (score +1 for each you consume regularly — 3+ times per week)**:

| Food | Consumed 3+/week? | Score |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Sugary beverages (soda, sweet tea, energy drinks) | | |
| Processed meats (hot dogs, deli meat, sausage) | | |
| Fried foods (French fries, fried chicken, doughnuts) | | |
| Packaged snacks (chips, cookies, crackers) | | |
| Refined grains (white bread, white rice, regular pasta) | | |
| Added sugar / sweets (candy, pastries, ice cream) | | |
| Excess alcohol (more than 1 drink/day women, 2 men) | | |
| Fast food meals | | |
| Margarine or processed cooking oils | | |
| Heavily processed breakfast cereals | | |
| **Pro-Inflammatory Total** | | **/10** |

### Interpreting Your Inflammatory Balance

**Your Net Score = Anti-Inflammatory Total minus Pro-Inflammatory Total**

| Net Score | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **+6 to +10** | Strongly anti-inflammatory — excellent for mental health |
| **+1 to +5** | Leaning anti-inflammatory — good foundation, room to improve |
| **0** | Neutral — balanced but could shift toward anti-inflammatory |
| **-1 to -5** | Leaning pro-inflammatory — prioritize adding anti-inflammatory foods |
| **-6 to -10** | Strongly pro-inflammatory — significant opportunity for improvement |

> **Research Brief:** Self-Assessment Tools and Dietary Behavior Change
>
> **Study**: Multiple validation studies of dietary screening tools · Sample: Diverse populations across clinical and community settings · Design: Validation and behavior-change studies
>
> **Finding**: Individuals who regularly self-assess their dietary patterns are more likely to make and sustain positive dietary changes compared to those who rely on general impressions. Brief screening tools like the MEDAS are as effective at predicting health outcomes as detailed food diaries while being far less burdensome, increasing long-term adherence to self-monitoring.
>
> **Context**: The effectiveness of self-assessment tools lies not in their precision but in their ability to create structured awareness. People tend to overestimate their intake of healthy foods and underestimate their intake of unhealthy foods. Even simplified scoring systems correct for this bias and reveal actionable gaps. The key is using these tools periodically (monthly) rather than obsessively (daily), which can trigger food anxiety.
>
> **Practical Application**: Score yourself using these three tools once per month. Use the scores not as judgments but as diagnostic information — where are the highest-impact opportunities for change? Track your scores over time to see trends.
>
> **Evidence Quality**: ⭐⭐⭐ Moderate-Strong
>
> *Source: Research Document, Dietary Assessment Tools and Behavior Change*


## Identifying Your Highest-Impact Changes

Now that you have three data points about your dietary pattern, you can identify where change will be most effective. The principle is **highest impact, lowest effort** — start with the changes that will move your scores the most for the least disruption to your daily life.

### The Priority Matrix

| If Your MEDAS Is Low... | Priority Action |
|------------------------|----------------|
| You do not use olive oil | Switch your primary cooking oil — one change, multiple benefits |
| You eat few vegetables | Add one serving to lunch and one to dinner |
| You eat few legumes | Add beans or lentils to two meals per week |
| You eat little fish | Have fish twice per week (canned counts) |

| If Your UPF % Is High... | Priority Action |
|--------------------------|----------------|
| Sugary beverages dominate | Switch to water, sparkling water, or unsweetened tea |
| Packaged snacks are daily | Replace with nuts, fruit, or yogurt |
| Breakfast is ultra-processed | Switch to oats, eggs, or plain yogurt with fruit |
| Most meals are convenience food | Cook one additional meal from scratch per week |

| If Your Inflammatory Score Is Negative... | Priority Action |
|------------------------------------------|----------------|
| Low in anti-inflammatory foods | Add one Tier 1 anti-inflammatory food daily (olive oil, leafy greens, berries) |
| High in pro-inflammatory foods | Reduce the most frequent pro-inflammatory item by half |
| Both | Focus on addition first — add before you subtract |

## Key Takeaways

- **The MEDAS** (14-item Mediterranean Diet Adherence Score) is a validated, quick tool — each 2-point increase is associated with measurable health improvements
- **NOVA-based UPF tracking** does not require counting calories — estimating the percentage of your diet that is ultra-processed reveals your exposure level and guides targeted swaps
- **The simplified Inflammatory Balance assessment** indicates whether your overall pattern leans pro-inflammatory or anti-inflammatory — your net score reveals the direction of dietary influence on your brain
- **Self-assessment tools work** because they create structured awareness and correct for the common tendency to overestimate healthy food intake
- **Monthly scoring** is the recommended frequency — enough to track trends without triggering food anxiety
- **Highest impact, lowest effort** is the guiding principle for change — target the changes that move your scores the most for the least disruption
