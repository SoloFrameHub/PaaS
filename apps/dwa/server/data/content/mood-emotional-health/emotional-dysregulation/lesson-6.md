In Lesson 5, you learned to observe and label your emotions. Now the question becomes: **what do you do with them?** Sometimes the most effective response is simply to let an emotion run its course. But when an emotion does not fit the facts, or when acting on it would cause harm, DBT offers specific, structured strategies for changing your emotional response.

This lesson covers the core emotion regulation change skills: Check the Facts, Opposite Action, and the ABC PLEASE framework for reducing your overall vulnerability to emotional crises.

> **Disclaimer**: This lesson is educational and does not replace professional mental health care. If you are experiencing a mental health crisis or thoughts of self-harm, contact the **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** (call or text **988**) immediately.

## 1. Check the Facts

Before trying to change an emotion, the first step is to determine whether it fits the situation. Emotions are often based on interpretations and assumptions rather than verified facts -- and when the interpretation is inaccurate, the emotion may be disproportionate.

### The Check the Facts Process

1. **Name the emotion** you are experiencing (use your labeling skills from Lesson 5)
2. **Identify the prompting event** -- what happened? Describe it in factual, observable terms (what a video camera would record, not your interpretation)
3. **Identify your interpretation** -- what are you telling yourself about the event? What meaning have you assigned?
4. **Ask: Is my interpretation based on facts or assumptions?**
   - Am I assuming the worst?
   - Am I mind-reading (assuming I know what someone else is thinking)?
   - Am I catastrophizing (jumping to the worst possible outcome)?
5. **Ask: What else could be true?** Generate at least two alternative explanations
6. **Ask: Does the intensity of my emotion match the actual facts?**

Sometimes checking the facts alone is enough to shift the emotion. When it is not, the next skill -- Opposite Action -- provides a more active intervention.

## 2. Opposite Action

Opposite Action is one of the most powerful skills in DBT emotion regulation. The principle is straightforward: **when an emotion does not fit the facts, or when acting on it would be ineffective or harmful, do the opposite of the emotion's action urge.**

### When to Use Opposite Action

Use it when **either** of these conditions is true:

- **The emotion does not fit the facts** (your anxiety is based on a misinterpretation, your anger is disproportionate)
- **The emotion fits the facts, but acting on the urge would make things worse** (you are justifiably angry, but yelling would damage the relationship)

### The Opposite Action Table

<SlideNavigation persistKey="opposite-action-table">
<Slide title="Anger → Gentle Avoidance or Kindness">When anger's urge is to attack, criticize, or lash out — gently avoid the person or situation, be kind or at least civil, unclench your fists, relax your face. Change your body, not just your words.</Slide>
<Slide title="Fear → Approach">When fear's urge is to run away, avoid, or escape — approach what you fear (when it is safe), stay in the situation, do what you have been avoiding. This is the basis of exposure therapy.</Slide>
<Slide title="Sadness → Get Active and Connect">When sadness's urge is to withdraw, isolate, and become inactive — get active, reach out to others, engage in activities even without motivation. Movement and connection counteract the withdrawal cycle.</Slide>
<Slide title="Shame → Be Visible">When shame's urge is to hide, disappear, and keep secrets — be visible, share your experience with someone trustworthy, hold your head up. Shame thrives in secrecy and dies in the light.</Slide>
<Slide title="Guilt → Proportional Response">When guilt fits the facts: make amends proportionally, then stop punishing yourself. When guilt does NOT fit the facts: let it go, do not apologize for something you did not do wrong.</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

### Critical Rule: Do It Wholeheartedly
Opposite Action only works when done **all the way**. This means changing your:
- **Actions**: Do the opposite behavior
- **Body posture**: Adopt the posture of the opposite emotion (relaxed vs. tense, upright vs. collapsed)
- **Facial expression**: Change your expression to match the opposite emotion
- **Thoughts**: Do not ruminate on thoughts that fuel the original emotion; gently redirect attention
Half-hearted Opposite Action -- approaching a feared situation while mentally rehearsing everything that could go wrong -- does not work. It may even make the emotion stronger.

## 3. Accumulate Positive Experiences
Chronic emotional dysregulation often occurs against a backdrop of a life that feels empty, joyless, or meaningless. The "Accumulate Positive Experiences" skill addresses this directly.
### Short-Term: Daily Pleasant Events
Build at least one small pleasant activity into each day. This is not about forcing happiness -- it is about creating the conditions in which positive emotions can naturally occur.
- **Choose activities that align with your values**, not what you think you "should" enjoy
- **Be fully present** during the activity (use mindfulness skills -- do not multitask or ruminate)
- **Do not undermine the experience** with thoughts like "This won't last" or "I don't deserve this"
- **Examples**: A cup of tea enjoyed slowly, a walk in nature, listening to a favorite song, cooking a meal, calling a friend, reading for pleasure
### Long-Term: Values-Based Goals
Short-term pleasant events stabilize daily mood, but lasting change comes from building a life that feels meaningful. This involves:
1. **Identify your values** -- What truly matters to you? (Connection, creativity, growth, service, adventure, integrity)
2. **Set one specific goal** aligned with a core value
3. **Break it into small steps** and take one step this week
4. **Avoid mood-dependent action** -- work toward goals even when you do not feel motivated
## 4. Build Mastery
Build Mastery means doing **one thing each day that gives you a sense of competence or accomplishment**. When emotional dysregulation makes you feel out of control, mastery experiences provide concrete evidence that you can affect your own life.
### Guidelines for Building Mastery
- **Choose tasks that are challenging but achievable** -- too easy provides no sense of mastery; too hard sets you up for failure
- **Adjust difficulty to your current emotional state** -- on hard days, mastering a simple task (making your bed, preparing a meal) counts
- **Notice and acknowledge the accomplishment** -- do not dismiss it with "anyone could do that"
- **Build gradually** -- increase the challenge over time as your capacity grows
## 5. Cope Ahead
Cope Ahead is a proactive skill for situations you know will be emotionally challenging. Rather than walking into a difficult situation unprepared, you mentally rehearse using your skills effectively.
### The Cope Ahead Process
1. **Describe the situation** in specific detail -- what will happen, who will be there, what might trigger you
2. **Identify the emotions** you expect to feel and the action urges that may arise
3. **Choose the skills** you will use (Check the Facts, Opposite Action, TIPP, distress tolerance skills)
4. **Mentally rehearse** the entire scene: imagine yourself in the situation, feeling the emotion, and then using the skill effectively
5. **Rehearse coping with obstacles** -- what if your first skill does not work? What is your backup plan?
6. **Practice relaxation** after the mental rehearsal to associate the scenario with a sense of calm competence

## 6. ABC PLEASE: The Complete Vulnerability-Reduction System
DBT organizes the emotion regulation change skills into a memorable acronym: **ABC PLEASE**. The first three letters represent the skills covered above; the PLEASE skills address the physical foundations of emotional stability.
### A -- Accumulate Positive Experiences
Build pleasant events into daily life and pursue values-based long-term goals (see Section 3).
### B -- Build Mastery
Do one thing each day that creates a sense of competence (see Section 4).
### C -- Cope Ahead
Mentally rehearse managing difficult situations before they occur (see Section 5).
### PLEASE Skills -- Physical Self-Care
The PLEASE skills target the body, because **physical vulnerability amplifies emotional reactivity**. When your body is depleted, every emotion hits harder.
<EnhancedAccordion>
<AccordionItem title="PL — Treat Physical Illness">
Take medications as prescribed, attend medical appointments, address chronic pain. Untreated physical conditions increase emotional vulnerability significantly. This is not optional self-care — it is foundational to emotional stability.
</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="E — Balanced Eating">
Eat regularly, avoid skipping meals, maintain balanced nutrition. Blood sugar instability directly affects mood and irritability. You do not need a perfect diet — you need consistent fuel for your brain and body.
</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="A — Avoid Mood-Altering Substances">
Minimize or eliminate alcohol, recreational drugs, and excessive caffeine. Substances destabilize mood and interfere with emotion regulation. If substance use feels out of control, seek specialized support.
</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="S — Balanced Sleep">
Maintain consistent sleep and wake times, practice good sleep hygiene. Sleep disruption is one of the strongest predictors of emotional dysregulation. Even one night of poor sleep increases emotional reactivity measurably.
</AccordionItem>
<AccordionItem title="E — Exercise">
Engage in regular physical movement appropriate to your fitness level. Exercise has robust evidence for mood regulation and stress reduction. Even a 10-minute walk counts. The key is consistency, not intensity.
</AccordionItem>
</EnhancedAccordion>
**Key insight**: The PLEASE skills are not glamorous, but they are foundational. Many people find that improving even one PLEASE area (especially sleep) meaningfully reduces their emotional reactivity.
## 7. Reducing Vulnerability to Emotion Mind
DBT describes three states of mind:
- **Emotion Mind**: Decisions and actions are controlled entirely by emotions -- intense, urgent, often impulsive
- **Reasonable Mind**: Decisions are based purely on logic and facts -- can feel cold, disconnected
- **Wise Mind**: The integration of emotion and reason -- the calm inner knowing that guides effective action
Emotion dysregulation often means spending too much time in Emotion Mind. The ABC PLEASE skills collectively reduce your vulnerability to being hijacked by Emotion Mind by:
- **Creating a buffer** of positive experiences and mastery so that individual stressors do not tip you over
- **Preparing you in advance** for situations that historically trigger Emotion Mind
- **Maintaining the physical foundation** that keeps your nervous system regulated
### Daily Practice: The ABC PLEASE Check-In
At the end of each day, briefly review:
- **A**: Did I have at least one pleasant experience today?
- **B**: Did I do one thing that gave me a sense of accomplishment?
- **C**: Did I prepare for any upcoming difficult situations?
- **PLEASE**: How am I doing with physical health, eating, substance use, sleep, and exercise?
This is not about perfection. It is about building consistent habits that reduce your baseline emotional vulnerability over time.

## 9. Crisis Resources
If you are experiencing overwhelming emotions, thoughts of self-harm, or suicidal ideation, please reach out for support immediately:
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: Call or text **988** (available 24/7)
- **Crisis Text Line**: Text **HELLO** to **741741**
- **Emergency services**: Call **911** if you or someone else is in immediate danger
Learning emotion regulation skills takes time and practice. Struggling does not mean you are failing -- it means you are working on something genuinely difficult. Professional support can make the process safer and more effective.

## Summary
- **Check the Facts** is the first step: determine whether your emotion fits the situation before trying to change it
- **Opposite Action** means doing the opposite of an emotion's action urge -- wholeheartedly -- when the emotion does not fit the facts or when acting on it would be harmful
- **Accumulate Positive Experiences** builds a foundation of daily pleasant events and long-term values-based goals
- **Build Mastery** means doing one thing daily that gives you a sense of competence, adjusted to your current capacity
- **Cope Ahead** prepares you for difficult situations by mentally rehearsing effective skill use
- **ABC PLEASE** is the complete system: Accumulate positives, Build mastery, Cope ahead, plus physical self-care (treat Physical illness, balanced Eating, Avoid mood-altering substances, balanced Sleep, Exercise)
- **Reducing vulnerability to Emotion Mind** is a daily practice, not a one-time achievement
- The **decision flow** (label, check facts, opposite action or skillful action, review ABC PLEASE) integrates all emotion regulation skills
