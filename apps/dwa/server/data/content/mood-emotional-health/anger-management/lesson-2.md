In the last lesson, you learned the difference between normal anger and problem anger. Now it's time to get specific: what sets your anger off, what your body does when it's rising, and how your typical anger episode unfolds from start to finish.

Mapping your anger pattern is the single most important step in changing it. You can't catch anger early if you don't know what "early" looks like for you.

## 1. Understanding Triggers

A trigger is whatever sets your anger in motion. Triggers can be external (things that happen around you) or internal (things happening inside you).

### External Triggers
- **Disrespect or dismissal** — being talked down to, ignored, or patronized
- **Unfairness** — seeing rules applied unevenly or being taken advantage of
- **Feeling controlled** — being told what to do, micromanaged, or having choices removed
- **Broken expectations** — someone not following through on a promise or agreement
- **Criticism** — feeling attacked, judged, or not good enough
- **Traffic, noise, and hassles** — everyday frustrations that accumulate
- **Other people's behavior** — rudeness, incompetence, or perceived hostility

### Internal Triggers
These are often overlooked, but they dramatically lower your threshold for anger:
- **Physical states** — fatigue, hunger, pain, illness, poor sleep
- **Emotional states** — already feeling stressed, anxious, ashamed, or sad
- **Past associations** — situations that remind you of previous hurt, trauma, or conflict
- **Rumination** — replaying past events or rehearsing future confrontations in your mind
- **Accumulation** — the "last straw" effect where many small frustrations build up

**Key insight:** The same external event can trigger very different reactions depending on your internal state. When you're well-rested, well-fed, and feeling connected, you handle frustrations much more easily. When you're depleted, everything feels like a bigger deal.

## 2. Early Warning Signs

Your body starts reacting before your conscious mind fully registers anger. Learning to read these signals is your early warning system.

<BodyMap title="Where Anger Shows Up in Your Body" prompt="Tap the areas where you first notice anger building. Common areas include the jaw, fists, chest, shoulders, and stomach — but your body is unique." />

### Physical Warning Signs
- Jaw clenching or teeth grinding
- Fists tightening
- Heat rising in your face, chest, or neck
- Heart beating faster or harder
- Muscle tension in shoulders, neck, or back
- Stomach tightening or churning
- Breathing getting faster or shallower
- Restlessness — pacing, tapping, fidgeting

### Thought Warning Signs
- "They always..." / "They never..."
- "This is unbelievable" / "I can't stand this"
- "They're doing this on purpose"
- "They should know better"
- "This isn't fair" (repeated)
- "Who do they think they are?"
- Fantasizing about confrontation or revenge

### Behavioral Warning Signs
- Speaking louder or faster
- Using sarcasm or cutting remarks
- Interrupting
- Withdrawing into silence (cold anger)
- Snapping at people who aren't involved
- Door slamming, heavy sighing, stomping
- Reaching for your phone to fire off an angry text

**The earlier you notice these signs, the more effectively you can intervene.** Most people only become aware of their anger once they're at a 7 or 8 on the intensity scale. By then, the thinking brain is largely offline and behavior becomes more automatic. The goal is to catch yourself at a 3 or 4.

## 3. The Escalation Chain

Anger doesn't happen in an instant — even though it can feel that way. There's actually a chain of events between the trigger and your reaction:

<StepByStep steps={[{"title":"Step 1: Trigger","content":"Something happens (or you remember something that happened). This is the spark — the external event or internal state that starts the process."},{"title":"Step 2: Interpretation","content":"Your brain immediately assigns meaning: 'They're disrespecting me,' 'This shouldn't be happening,' 'They did this on purpose.' This interpretation — not the event itself — determines how angry you get."},{"title":"Step 3: Physiological Arousal","content":"Your body activates the fight response. Heart rate rises, muscles tense, adrenaline flows. This happens fast — within seconds."},{"title":"Step 4: Urge","content":"You feel a strong pull toward action: yelling, confronting, attacking, withdrawing, or retaliating."},{"title":"Step 5: Behavior","content":"You act on the urge (or you don't). This is the part that creates consequences — for better or worse."},{"title":"Step 6: Aftermath","content":"The immediate anger passes (eventually), and you're left with the outcomes: damaged relationships, regret, a temporary sense of relief, or — if you managed it well — a feeling of self-respect."}]} />

**The key intervention points:**
- **Between trigger and interpretation** — can you consider a different explanation?
- **Between interpretation and arousal** — can you catch the thought before your body fully reacts?
- **Between arousal and behavior** — can you use a cool-down technique to buy time?

You don't need to intervene at every point. Even catching yourself at one point changes the outcome.

## 4. Mapping Your Personal Pattern

Think about your last three anger episodes and notice what they have in common:

**Trigger patterns:** Do you react most to feeling disrespected? Controlled? Unfair treatment? Unmet expectations? A specific person?

**Timing patterns:** Is your anger worse at certain times of day, on certain days of the week, or in certain situations (at home vs. work, with certain people)?

**Internal state patterns:** Were you already tired, stressed, hungry, or emotionally depleted before the trigger happened?

**Escalation patterns:** Do you go from calm to rage instantly, or is there a slow build? Do you explode outward (yelling, confrontation) or implode inward (silent fury, withdrawal, cold shoulder)?

**Aftermath patterns:** What typically happens afterward? Do you feel regret? Justified? Both? Do you repair, avoid, or pretend nothing happened?

## 5. The HALT Check

A simple but powerful daily practice: before reacting to something that makes you angry, ask yourself if you're:

- **H**ungry
- **A**ngry (already, about something else)
- **L**onely
- **T**ired

If any of these are true, your anger threshold is lower than normal. This doesn't mean your anger is "wrong" — it means you're more reactive, and you should factor that in before responding.

## 6. Using the Anger Episode Log

The interactive Anger Episode Log below this lesson content helps you track your anger episodes systematically. Each time you notice significant anger, log:

- What triggered it
- What your body felt like
- What thoughts were running
- How intense it was
- What you did
- What happened afterward

After a week or two of logging, patterns will emerge that your memory alone wouldn't catch. You'll see which triggers are most potent, which internal states make you most vulnerable, and where in the escalation chain you have the most room to intervene.

## Summary

- Triggers are both external (events, people, situations) and internal (fatigue, stress, past hurt)
- Your body warns you before your mind fully registers anger — learn to read the signals
- The escalation chain runs: trigger → interpretation → arousal → urge → behavior → aftermath
- Intervention is possible at multiple points in the chain
- Tracking your episodes reveals patterns that memory alone can't capture
- The HALT check (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired) helps you recognize when your threshold is lowered
