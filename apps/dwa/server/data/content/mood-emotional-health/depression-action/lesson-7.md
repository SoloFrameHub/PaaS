Beyond behavioral activation, cognitive skills, and exercise, several lifestyle factors significantly impact depression. This lesson covers nutrition, sleep, and other modifiable factors that can support your recovery.

<Callout type="reflection" title="Meeting You Where You Are">
If you rated yourself low, that is completely understandable. Depression makes basic self-care feel like climbing a mountain. This lesson isn't about perfection — it's about identifying one or two small changes that can support your mood, starting from wherever you are right now.
</Callout>

## 1. Nutrition and Depression

### The Evidence

**Mediterranean Diet Meta-Analysis (2024)**:
- 5 RCTs, 1,507 participants with depression
- Significant reduction in depressive symptoms (SMD = -0.53, moderate effect)
- Benefits seen within weeks of dietary changes

### How Diet Affects Mood

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="🧬" title="Tryptophan">
This amino acid is the precursor to serotonin — your "feel-good" neurotransmitter. Found in proteins like turkey, eggs, and nuts.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🐟" title="Omega-3 Fatty Acids">
Anti-inflammatory and brain-supportive. Found in fatty fish, walnuts, and flaxseed. Low levels are linked to depression.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🥬" title="Folate and B Vitamins">
Directly involved in neurotransmitter production. Found in leafy greens, legumes, and whole grains. Deficiency is associated with depression.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🦠" title="Gut Microbiome">
Your gut bacteria influence brain chemistry through the gut-brain axis. Fiber-rich foods feed beneficial bacteria. Processed foods disrupt the balance.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="📊" title="Blood Sugar">
Stable blood sugar supports stable mood. Refined carbs and sugar cause spikes and crashes that can mimic or worsen depressive symptoms.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🔥" title="Inflammation">
Processed foods increase inflammatory markers linked to depression. Anti-inflammatory foods (fruits, vegetables, fish) help reduce this burden.
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

### Practical Tips

<FlipCard front="Myth: You need a complete diet overhaul to see mood benefits" back="Reality: Small changes add up. <FlipCard front="Myth: When you're depressed, what you eat doesn't matter" back="Reality: Depression often drives us toward comfort foods high in sugar and processed carbs. - **Don't skip meals**: Blood sugar drops worsen mood
- **Add one vegetable** to lunch
- **Replace one processed snack** with nuts or fruit
- **Include fish** twice weekly
- **Stay hydrated**: Dehydration can mimic depression symptoms
- **Small changes add up**: Don't try to overhaul everything at once

## 2. Sleep and Depression

### The Bidirectional Relationship

Sleep and depression have a two-way relationship:
- **80%+** of people with depression experience insomnia
- **Insomnia doubles** the risk of developing depression
- Poor sleep **maintains** depression even when other factors improve

### Sleep Hygiene Fundamentals

<SlideNavigation persistKey="depression-action-lesson7-sleep">
<Slide title="Schedule and Timing">
| Principle | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Consistent wake time** | Anchors your circadian rhythm — this is the single most important sleep habit |
| **Consistent bed time** | Trains your body when to sleep |
| **Avoid weekend catch-up** | "Social jet lag" disrupts your rhythm |
| **Limit naps** | Long naps reduce nighttime sleep drive |
The most powerful change: **set one wake-up time and stick to it every day**, including weekends. Your body will begin to regulate itself around this anchor.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Pre-Sleep Routine">
| Principle | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Wind-down period** (30-60 min) | Signals brain that sleep is coming |
| **Relaxing activities** | Bath, reading, gentle stretching |
| **Dim lights** | Supports melatonin production |
| **Avoid screens** (1 hour before bed) | Blue light suppresses melatonin |
Create a simple routine your body can learn to associate with sleep. It doesn't need to be elaborate — consistency matters more than complexity.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Sleep Environment">
| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Darkness** | Use blackout curtains or sleep mask |
| **Temperature** | Cool (65-68 F / 18-20 C) |
| **Sound** | Quiet or consistent white noise |
| **Bed use** | Sleep and intimacy only — not work or worry |
Your bedroom should signal "sleep" to your brain. If you're using your bed for scrolling, working, or worrying, the association weakens.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Substances and Activities">
| Factor | Guideline |
|--------|-----------|
| **Caffeine** | Avoid 6+ hours before bed |
| **Alcohol** | Disrupts sleep architecture even if it helps you fall asleep |
| **Large meals** | Avoid close to bedtime |
| **Exercise** | Beneficial but not within 3 hours of bed |
Alcohol is particularly deceptive — it makes you feel sleepy but prevents deep, restorative sleep. You may sleep 8 hours and wake exhausted.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

### Managing Racing Thoughts at Night

Depression often comes with nighttime rumination. Try these strategies:

**Worry Time**:
- Schedule 15 minutes earlier in the day to write out worries
- When worries arise at night, remind yourself: "I've already addressed that"

**Thought Parking**:
- Keep notepad by bed
- Jot down any thoughts or tasks that arise
- Tell yourself: "It's captured. I can address it tomorrow."

**Relaxation Techniques**:
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Body scan meditation
- Slow breathing exercises

## 3. Other Lifestyle Factors

<EnhancedAccordion title="Sunlight and Light Exposure">
**Why it matters**: Light exposure affects serotonin production, regulates circadian rhythm, and prevents seasonal depression.
**Recommendations**:
- 15-30 minutes of natural light daily, especially in morning
- Light therapy boxes for winter months
- Get outside even on cloudy days
- Morning light is particularly powerful for resetting your internal clock
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Social Connection">
**Why it matters**: Social isolation is both a symptom and maintaining factor of depression. Social activities predict improved outcomes. Connection provides support and positive experiences.
**Recommendations**:
- At least 2-3 social activities per week
- Quality over quantity — one meaningful connection helps
- Even brief interactions count (texting, calling)
- Scheduled activities help overcome avoidance
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Screen Time and Social Media">
**Why it matters**: Excessive screen time is associated with depression. Social media can increase comparison and isolation. Passive scrolling provides distraction but not restoration.
**Recommendations**:
- Set time limits on social media
- Curate feeds to reduce negative content
- Choose active engagement over passive scrolling
- Replace some screen time with other activities
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Alcohol and Substances">
**Why it matters**: Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant. It may provide temporary relief but worsens depression long-term. It disrupts sleep and can interfere with medication.
**Recommendations**:
- Limit to 1-2 drinks maximum, or none while depressed
- Don't use alcohol to cope with difficult feelings
- Be aware of increasing use as a warning sign
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="Nature and Green Spaces">
**Why it matters**: Time in nature reduces stress hormones. "Green exercise" has additional mood benefits beyond indoor exercise. Nature provides sensory engagement that interrupts rumination.
**Recommendations**:
- Weekly time in parks, forests, or green spaces
- Even urban greenery helps
- Combine with walking for dual benefit
</EnhancedAccordion>

## 6. Key Takeaways

1. **Mediterranean-style eating** shows moderate effects on depression
2. **Sleep and depression** have a bidirectional relationship—improving sleep helps mood
3. **Sleep hygiene** covers schedule, pre-sleep routine, environment, and substances
4. **Light exposure, social connection, and nature** all support mood
5. **Alcohol worsens depression** despite providing temporary relief
6. **Small, sustainable changes** work better than dramatic overhauls
7. **Focus on 1-2 areas** at a time
