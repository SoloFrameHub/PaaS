You've learned powerful self-help strategies throughout this course. These tools work—research shows self-guided interventions are effective for many people with depression.

But self-help has limits. Knowing when and how to seek professional help is an essential skill. This lesson will help you recognize when more support is needed and understand your options.

<Callout type="reflection" title="Whatever You Answered">
There is no wrong answer. Recognizing you need more help is a sign of self-awareness and strength, not failure. And if self-help feels sufficient right now, these tools will be here if that ever changes.
</Callout>

## 1. Immediate Help Needed

Some situations require immediate professional intervention. **If any of the following apply, please reach out now**:

<Callout type="warning" title="Call 988 or Go to Emergency Room If:">
- You have a **specific plan** to end your life
- You have **access to means** (weapons, medications) and thoughts of using them
- You are experiencing **active self-harm** urges
- You are having **psychotic symptoms** (hallucinations, delusions)
- You **cannot care for basic needs** (eating, drinking, hygiene)
- You feel you may **act on suicidal thoughts**
These are medical emergencies. You deserve immediate care.
</Callout>

### Crisis Resources

**988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**
- Call or text: **988**
- Chat: **988lifeline.org**
- Available 24/7, free, confidential
- Veterans: Press 1 after dialing
- Spanish: Press 2 after dialing

**Crisis Text Line**
- Text **HOME** to **741741**
- Free, 24/7 text support

**Emergency Services**
- Call **911** for life-threatening emergencies
- Go to nearest **emergency room**

## 2. Professional Help Recommended

Even without crisis, professional help is recommended in several situations:

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="📅" title="Duration and Severity">
Symptoms lasting more than 2 weeks. Moderate to severe PHQ-9 scores (10+). Self-help strategies aren't helping after 4-6 weeks of consistent effort.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="⚙️" title="Functional Impact">
Unable to work or attend school. Relationships suffering significantly. Self-care declining (hygiene, eating, basic tasks). Responsibilities being neglected.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="⚠️" title="Warning Signs">
Thoughts of death (even without active plan). Substance use increasing to cope. Isolation becoming severe. Hopelessness that won't lift. History of severe depression.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🔄" title="Self-Help Limits">
Self-help works best for mild to moderate symptoms, as an addition to professional treatment, or for maintenance after treatment. For moderate-to-severe symptoms or complex situations, professional support makes a significant difference.
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

## 3. Types of Professional Help

<SlideNavigation persistKey="depression-action-lesson8-treatment">
<Slide title="Psychotherapy (Talk Therapy)">
| Type | What It Involves | Best For |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| **CBT** | Identify and change thought/behavior patterns | Most types of depression |
| **BA** | Structured activity scheduling | Depression with withdrawal |
| **IPT** | Focus on relationships and communication | Relationship-related depression |
| **ACT** | Acceptance, mindfulness, values-based action | Chronic depression |
**What to expect**: Usually 8-16 sessions, weekly (45-60 minutes), with "homework" between sessions. It's an active collaboration, not just talking about your feelings.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Medication">
| Class | Examples | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **SSRIs** | Sertraline (Zoloft), Escitalopram (Lexapro), Fluoxetine (Prozac) | First-line; generally well-tolerated |
| **SNRIs** | Venlafaxine (Effexor), Duloxetine (Cymbalta) | Alternative first-line |
| **Bupropion** | Wellbutrin | Different mechanism; may help with energy/focus |
| **Others** | Mirtazapine, Trazodone | Various uses; discuss with prescriber |
**What to expect**: Takes 4-6 weeks for full effect. Side effects often diminish after first few weeks. May need to try more than one medication. Work with prescriber to find the right fit.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Combination Treatment">
Research shows that **therapy + medication** is often more effective than either alone, especially for:
- Moderate to severe depression
- Chronic or recurrent depression
- When one treatment alone isn't sufficient
Think of it like treating a broken leg: you need both the cast (medication to stabilize brain chemistry) and the physical therapy (therapy to build new skills and patterns).
</Slide>
<Slide title="Intensive Programs">
For more severe depression:
- **Intensive Outpatient (IOP)**: 3+ hours/day, several days/week
- **Partial Hospitalization (PHP)**: Day program, return home at night
- **Inpatient**: 24-hour care for severe/acute situations
These are not signs of failure — they are appropriate levels of care for the severity of the condition.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## 6. What to Expect from Treatment

### First Visit

- Assessment of symptoms, history, functioning
- Discussion of treatment options
- Collaborative treatment planning
- Questions and answers

<Callout type="tip" title="It's Okay to Be Nervous">
Almost everyone feels nervous before a first therapy appointment. That's completely normal. A good therapist will understand this and create a safe, welcoming space. You don't need to have everything figured out before you go — that's what the process is for.
</Callout>

### Ongoing Treatment

**Therapy**:
- Weekly or biweekly sessions
- Active work between sessions
- Gradual skill building
- Typically see benefits within 8-12 sessions

**Medication**:
- Regular check-ins with prescriber
- Monitoring for side effects
- Dosage adjustments as needed
- Full effect takes 4-6 weeks

### Measuring Progress

- PHQ-9 monitoring (every 2-4 weeks)
- Functional improvement (work, relationships, self-care)
- Symptom frequency and intensity
- Quality of life
