Depression doesn't just affect what you do—it affects how you **think**. The depressed mind generates a constant stream of negative thoughts that feel absolutely true but are often distorted.

<Callout type="reflection" title="A Crucial Distinction">
In this lesson, you'll learn to recognize these patterns and develop more balanced thinking. The goal is not to think positively — it's to think **realistically**. Depression pulls your thoughts to one extreme. Balance means seeing the full picture.
</Callout>

## 1. The Thought-Feeling-Behavior Connection

Your thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected:

## 2. Beck's Negative Triad

Psychologist Aaron Beck identified that depression is characterized by negative thinking in three areas:

<InsightGrid>
<InsightItem icon="🪞" title="Negative View of Self">
"I'm worthless." "I'm a failure." "I'm unlovable." "There's something wrong with me." "I'm a burden to everyone." Depression makes you see yourself through the harshest possible lens.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🌍" title="Negative View of the World">
"Nothing ever works out." "People can't be trusted." "The world is unfair." "Everything is harder for me." Depression colors your entire experience, making the world seem hostile or indifferent.
</InsightItem>
<InsightItem icon="🔮" title="Negative View of the Future">
"Things will never get better." "I'll always feel this way." "There's no point in trying." "The future is hopeless." Depression steals your sense of possibility and replaces it with certainty that nothing will change.
</InsightItem>
</InsightGrid>

<Callout type="tip" title="These Are Symptoms, Not Facts">
These thoughts feel like accurate assessments of reality. They are not. They are **symptoms of depression** — as much a part of the illness as fatigue or sleep disruption. Learning to recognize them as symptoms is the first step to loosening their grip.
</Callout>

## 3. Common Cognitive Distortions

Depression uses predictable thinking patterns. Learning to spot them is the first step to challenging them.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="depression-action-lesson5-distortions">
<Slide title="All-or-Nothing Thinking">
**Pattern**: Seeing things in black and white categories
- "If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure"
- "Either they love me completely or they hate me"
- "I either succeed at everything or I'm worthless"
**Reality**: Life exists in shades of gray. Most things fall between the extremes.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Overgeneralization">
**Pattern**: Seeing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern
- "I made one mistake, so I always mess things up"
- "This relationship failed, so I'll never find love"
- "I didn't get this job, so I'll never get hired"
**Reality**: One event doesn't define a pattern. Watch for words like "always" and "never."
</Slide>
<Slide title="Mental Filter & Disqualifying the Positive">
**Mental Filter**: Focusing only on the negative and filtering out the positive. Dwelling on one criticism in a review full of praise.
**Disqualifying the Positive**: Dismissing positive experiences as if they don't count. "They're just being nice." "That success was just luck."
**Reality**: Both positive and negative exist. Depression makes you see only one side.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Mind Reading & Fortune Telling">
**Mind Reading**: Assuming you know what others think (usually negative). "Everyone thinks I'm boring." "My boss thinks I'm incompetent."
**Fortune Telling**: Predicting negative outcomes as certain. "The interview will go badly." "I won't enjoy the party."
**Reality**: You can't read minds, and you can't predict the future.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Catastrophizing & Emotional Reasoning">
**Catastrophizing**: Expecting the worst possible outcome. "If I make a mistake, my life is ruined."
**Emotional Reasoning**: Assuming feelings reflect reality. "I feel hopeless, so things must be hopeless." "I feel like a failure, so I must be a failure."
**Reality**: The worst case rarely happens. Feelings are not facts.
</Slide>
<Slide title="Should Statements & Labeling">
**Should Statements**: Rigid rules about how things must be. "I should be able to handle this." "I shouldn't feel this way."
**Labeling**: Attaching a negative label to yourself. "I'm a loser." "I'm an idiot." "I'm a terrible person."
**Reality**: "Should" creates unnecessary suffering. You are not defined by a single label.
</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## 4. The Thought Challenging Process

When you catch a negative thought, work through these steps:

## 6. Common Challenges

<EnhancedAccordion title="'But my thoughts are TRUE'">
Depression makes negative thoughts feel true. That's what makes them powerful. But feeling true and being true are different things. Examine the evidence objectively — what would a jury say? Often, the "evidence" for the thought is actually other feelings, not facts.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="'Positive thinking feels fake'">
You're not trying to think positively — you're trying to think **realistically**. Balanced thoughts acknowledge the negative AND the positive. "Things are hard right now, AND I have gotten through hard things before" is balanced, not fake.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="'I can't catch my thoughts'">
Start by noticing shifts in mood. When your mood drops, ask: "What just went through my mind?" The thought was there — you just need practice spotting it. Keep a small notebook or use your phone. With time, you'll catch thoughts faster.
</EnhancedAccordion>

<EnhancedAccordion title="'I know this intellectually but don't feel it'">
That's completely normal. Keep practicing. The goal is to weaken the grip of automatic thoughts over time, not to eliminate them immediately. Think of it like physical therapy — each repetition builds strength, even when progress feels invisible.
</EnhancedAccordion>

## 7. Key Takeaways

1. **Depression distorts thinking** in predictable ways (Beck's negative triad)
2. **Thoughts feel like facts** but they're often distorted
3. **Common distortions** include all-or-nothing thinking, overgeneralization, mind reading, and emotional reasoning
4. **Challenge thoughts** by examining evidence for and against
5. **Aim for balanced thinking**, not positive thinking
6. **Practice makes progress**—the more you do this, the more automatic it becomes
