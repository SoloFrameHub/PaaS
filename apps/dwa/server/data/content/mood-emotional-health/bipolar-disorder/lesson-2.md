In Lesson 1, you learned that bipolar disorder follows a chronic, relapsing course -- and that early intervention during warning signs can prevent full episodes. But how do you actually catch a mood shift before it becomes an episode?

The answer is **systematic self-monitoring**. Research consistently shows that people with bipolar disorder who track their mood regularly experience **fewer relapses, better medication adherence, and improved quality of life**.

This lesson will teach you what to track, how to identify your personal early warning signs, and how to build response plans that turn awareness into action.

<Callout type="warning" title="Clinical Guidance Required">
Bipolar disorder requires medication management for most people. This lesson supports your self-awareness and communication with your care team — it is not a substitute for professional assessment, diagnosis, or treatment. All medication and treatment decisions must be made with your prescribing healthcare provider. In crisis, call or text **988**.
</Callout>

## 1. Why Self-Monitoring Matters

### The Evidence

Clinical research on bipolar disorder has demonstrated that structured self-monitoring:

- **Reduces relapse rates** by helping people intervene at early warning stages
- **Improves medication adherence** by making the benefits of treatment visible over time
- **Enhances communication with clinicians** by providing objective data rather than relying on memory alone
- **Increases self-efficacy** -- feeling more in control of your condition
- **Shortens episodes** when they do occur, through earlier detection and response

### The Challenge

Without tracking, most people rely on **retrospective recall** to assess their mood at appointments. Research shows this is unreliable -- we tend to:

- Remember recent mood states more than past ones
- Overestimate severity during depressive episodes
- Underestimate hypomanic symptoms (which can feel "normal" or even good)
- Forget the timeline of symptom changes

**Consistent tracking replaces guesswork with data.**

## 2. Core Variables to Track Daily

Not everything needs to be tracked, but the following variables are the most clinically meaningful for bipolar disorder. Aim to record them at roughly the same time each day (many people choose evening).

### Mood Rating (1-10 Scale)

| Rating | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **1-2** | Severely depressed: unable to function, profound hopelessness |
| **3-4** | Moderately depressed: low energy, withdrawal, difficulty with daily tasks |
| **5** | Baseline/neutral: neither elevated nor depressed |
| **6-7** | Mildly elevated: more energetic, talkative, or optimistic than usual |
| **8-9** | Hypomanic range: significantly elevated mood, reduced need for sleep, racing thoughts |
| **10** | Manic range: euphoria or severe irritability, impaired judgment, reckless behavior |

**Tips**: Rate your overall mood for the day. If your mood varied significantly, note the range (e.g., "3-7") and the predominant state.

<SlideNavigation persistKey="mood-tracking-variables">
<Slide title="Sleep">Track hours slept (actual sleep, not time in bed), sleep quality (1-5), and bed/wake times. Sleep changes are often the **earliest warning sign** of an impending episode — even before mood shifts become obvious.</Slide>
<Slide title="Energy Level">Rate 1-5 daily. 1 = can't get out of bed, 3 = normal energy, 5 = unusually wired or restless. Sudden energy spikes without clear cause can signal hypomania.</Slide>
<Slide title="Irritability">Rate 1-5 daily. Often overlooked, irritability can indicate a depressive mixed state or onset of mania. Track separately from mood — you can be irritable at any point on the mood spectrum.</Slide>
<Slide title="Impulsivity Check">Simple yes/no: Did you engage in unplanned spending, risky driving, uncharacteristic behavior, substance use, or impulsive decisions? Even one "yes" warrants attention.</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## 3. Identifying Your Personal Early Warning Signs
While the core variables apply to most people with bipolar disorder, your specific early warning signs are **unique to you**. Learning them is one of the most valuable things you can do for your stability.
### Common Early Warning Signs of Mania/Hypomania
- Needing less sleep but feeling rested
- Racing thoughts or an "ideas machine" feeling
- Talking faster or more than usual
- Starting multiple new projects simultaneously
- Feeling unusually confident or invincible
- Increased social activity (calling people late at night, making plans constantly)
- Overspending or making impulsive financial decisions
- Increased irritability or impatience with others
- Feeling like thoughts are too fast to capture
- Increased use of alcohol or substances
### Common Early Warning Signs of Depression
- Sleeping more than usual or difficulty waking up
- Social withdrawal (canceling plans, not responding to messages)
- Loss of interest in activities you usually enjoy
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Feeling heavy, slow, or physically weighed down
- Increased negative self-talk ("I'm worthless," "Nothing matters")
- Neglecting personal hygiene or household tasks
- Appetite changes (eating much more or much less)
- Crying spells or emotional numbness
- Thoughts of death or suicide
### Building Your Individualized Warning Sign List
Take time to reflect on your past episodes and identify **your** specific patterns. Consider asking trusted friends, family members, or your therapist -- they often notice signs before you do.
**Questions to guide your reflection:**
1. Think back to your last manic or hypomanic episode. What were the **first** changes you noticed? What did **others** notice first?
2. Think back to your last depressive episode. What changed first -- sleep, energy, mood, or behavior?
3. Are there specific behaviors that reliably appear before an episode (e.g., cleaning the house at 3 AM, binge-watching TV for days)?
4. How many days of warning signs do you typically get before a full episode?
## 4. Building Response Plans
Knowing your warning signs is only useful if you have a plan for what to do when you notice them. A response plan follows a simple structure:
**"If I notice [warning sign] for [number of days], I will [specific action]."**
### Example Response Plans for Elevated Mood Warning Signs
| Warning Sign | Threshold | Response Action |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Sleeping less than 6 hours and feeling rested | 2 consecutive nights | Contact psychiatrist; reinforce sleep hygiene; avoid stimulation after 9 PM |
| Starting 3+ new projects in a week | 1 week | Pause all new projects; discuss with therapist; ask trusted person for reality check |
| Spending more than $100 on unplanned purchases | Single occurrence | Freeze credit cards; give wallet to trusted person; call accountability partner |
| Racing thoughts making it hard to focus | 3 consecutive days | Contact prescriber; reduce caffeine to zero; prioritize sleep above all else |
### Example Response Plans for Depressive Warning Signs
| Warning Sign | Threshold | Response Action |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Sleeping 10+ hours per day | 3 consecutive days | Set alarm and get out of bed; schedule one social activity; contact therapist |
| Canceling plans or avoiding people | 1 week | Text one friend; take a 10-minute walk; contact therapist |
| Difficulty with basic hygiene | 3 consecutive days | Use the "2-minute rule" (just start); contact care team; ask for help |
| Thoughts of death or hopelessness | Single occurrence | Call 988; contact therapist immediately; tell a trusted person |

### Sharing Your Response Plan
Consider sharing your response plan with:
- Your psychiatrist or prescriber
- Your therapist
- A trusted family member or friend
- A partner or spouse
These people can help you follow through when your judgment may be impaired -- particularly during hypomania, when warning signs can feel like "finally feeling good."
## 5. Limits of Self-Management
Self-monitoring is a powerful complement to professional care, but it has important limitations:
- **It does not replace medication.** Mood tracking helps you manage your condition; it does not treat it.
- **Insight may be impaired during episodes.** During full mania, you may not recognize that you are manic. This is why trusted contacts and care team involvement matter.
- **Data is only useful if acted upon.** Tracking without response plans is like having a smoke detector but no fire extinguisher.
- **Self-monitoring can become obsessive.** If tracking starts causing significant anxiety, discuss this with your therapist. The goal is awareness, not hypervigilance.
<Callout type="warning" title="When Self-Monitoring Isn't Enough">
During full manic episodes, your ability to accurately assess your own state is impaired (a phenomenon called **anosognosia**). This is why self-monitoring alone is never sufficient — you need trusted people and a care team who can observe changes you cannot see. If someone you trust tells you they're concerned about your behavior, take it seriously even if you feel fine.
</Callout>

## 6. Privacy and Your Data
Your mood tracking data is personal and sensitive. Important points to understand:
- **Your data belongs to you.** It is stored for your personal self-awareness and is not shared with anyone without your explicit consent.
- **You decide what to share.** You may choose to share your data with your care team, but this is always your choice.
- **Use it as a communication tool.** Bringing tracking data to appointments helps your clinician understand your patterns without relying solely on your recall.
## 7. Tips for Tracking Consistency
The biggest challenge with mood tracking is maintaining it consistently. Here are evidence-based strategies:
<ScenarioCard title="Making Tracking Stick: A Real-World Example">
Sarah had tried mood tracking apps three times before, each time abandoning them within a week. Her therapist suggested a simpler approach: every night after brushing her teeth, she'd rate just two things — mood (1-10) and hours of sleep — in a small notebook by her toothbrush. No paragraphs, no complex categories. Within a month, it became automatic. She later added energy and irritability ratings. At her quarterly psychiatrist appointment, she brought three months of data that revealed a pattern neither she nor her doctor had noticed: her mood consistently dipped on Sundays, correlating with disrupted sleep on Friday and Saturday nights.
</ScenarioCard>
- **Set a daily reminder** at the same time each day (e.g., a phone alarm at 9 PM)
- **Keep it brief** -- 2 minutes is enough for core variables. Don't make it a chore.
- **Link it to an existing habit** (after brushing teeth, after dinner, before bed)
- **Don't aim for perfection** -- missing a day is okay. Resume the next day without guilt.
- **Review your data weekly** -- look for trends rather than individual data points
- **Share your commitment** with your therapist or a trusted person for accountability
- **Start with just mood and sleep** if tracking everything feels overwhelming. You can add more variables once the habit is established.

3. What are your top 3 personal early warning signs for elevated mood?

## 9. Crisis Resources
If you are experiencing suicidal thoughts or are in crisis:
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: Call or text **988** (available 24/7)
- **Crisis Text Line**: Text **HOME** to **741741**
- **Emergency**: Call **911** or go to your nearest emergency room
**Reminder**: Thoughts of death or suicide -- even passive ones like "I wish I wouldn't wake up" -- are always a reason to reach out for support. You do not need to be in immediate danger to call.

## Summary
- **Self-monitoring reduces relapse** by catching mood shifts early and improving communication with your care team
- **Core variables to track daily**: mood (1-10), sleep (hours and quality), energy, irritability, and impulsivity
- **Your early warning signs are unique to you** -- reflect on past episodes and ask trusted people what they noticed first
- **Response plans** turn awareness into action: "If I notice X for Y days, I will do Z"
- **Share your response plan** with your care team and trusted contacts
- **Self-monitoring complements but does not replace** medication and professional care
- **Start simple, stay consistent** -- even tracking just mood and sleep daily is valuable
- **Your data is private** and only shared with your consent
