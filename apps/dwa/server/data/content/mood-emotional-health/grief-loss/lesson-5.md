When you're grieving, self-care can feel impossible — or even irrelevant. How do you care about eating well or sleeping enough when the world has been turned upside down? The answer is that self-care during grief isn't about wellness routines or optimization. It's about the absolute basics: helping your body and mind survive an extraordinarily demanding time.

This lesson offers gentle, realistic self-care invitations — not rules. On some days, just getting through is enough.

## 1. Why Self-Care Matters During Grief

Grief is not just an emotional experience — it's a full-body event. Your nervous system is under extraordinary stress, your brain is working overtime to process the loss, and your body is running on stress hormones. Without basic care, this physical toll compounds the emotional one.

Self-care during grief serves a simple purpose: keeping your body stable enough to grieve, and preventing the kind of physical depletion that makes everything harder.

## 2. Basic Physical Needs

### Eating
Grief often disrupts appetite — either killing it entirely or sending you toward comfort foods you wouldn't normally eat. Both are understandable responses.

**Gentle goals:**
- Aim for small amounts of food throughout the day, even if you're not hungry
- Keep simple, easy foods available (crackers, fruit, nuts, soup, toast)
- Accept help from others — if someone offers to bring food, say yes
- Don't judge yourself for eating patterns that aren't your usual

### Hydration
Dehydration happens easily during grief, especially if you're crying frequently, sleeping irregularly, or neglecting meals. Keep water within reach. A refillable water bottle by your bed, desk, or couch can help.

### Sleep
Sleep disruption is one of the most common grief experiences. You may struggle to fall asleep, wake repeatedly, have vivid or disturbing dreams, or sleep too much as an escape.

**Gentle strategies:**
- Try to keep a rough sleep-wake schedule, even if sleep quality is poor
- Limit caffeine after early afternoon
- Create a calm environment: dark room, comfortable temperature
- If you can't sleep, don't force it — read, listen to something soothing, or simply rest
- If sleep problems are severe and persistent, talk to a healthcare provider

### Medication and Health
If you take prescribed medications, try to continue taking them. Keep up with essential medical appointments if you can. If existing health conditions worsen during grief, let your doctor know — they can help adjust treatment or provide additional support.

## 3. Gentle Movement

Exercise might be the last thing you feel like doing, and that's completely understandable. But gentle movement — nothing ambitious — can help your nervous system regulate.

**Low-pressure options:**
- A short walk around the block
- Gentle stretching for a few minutes
- Standing outside for fresh air
- Slow, aimless movement (wandering through your home, a garden, a store)

The goal is not fitness or calories. It's giving your body something other than stillness and tension. If the most you can manage is walking to the mailbox, that counts.

## 4. The Pacing Principle

Grief is exhausting. A concept from chronic illness management that applies perfectly to grief is **pacing**: doing a little, then resting. Not pushing through. Not expecting consistent productivity. Just alternating between small efforts and intentional rest.

<FlipCard front="What pacing is NOT: Pushing through exhaustion, keeping up normal productivity, saying yes to everything" back="What pacing IS: Do one task, then sit down. Go to part of an event and leave when you need to. Work for an hour, then take a break. Say 'I can do this, but not all of it.' Build rest periods into any day that includes demands." />

Pacing protects you from the crash that follows pushing too hard, and it aligns with the restoration-oriented side of the Dual Process Model — engaging with daily life in sustainable doses.

## 5. Self-Compassion as Self-Care

Self-compassion during grief means treating yourself with the kindness you'd offer a dear friend going through the same thing.

**What self-compassion sounds like:**
- "This is really hard, and I'm doing my best"
- "It's okay that I can't function at my usual level right now"
- "I don't need to justify my grief to anyone"
- "I'm allowed to rest, even if things aren't getting done"
- "I'm carrying something very heavy right now"

**What self-compassion is NOT:**
- Telling yourself to "stay positive"
- Comparing your grief to others' and concluding yours is "less valid"
- Pushing through pain to prove you're "strong"
- Feeling guilty for needing help

Research consistently links self-compassion during bereavement with better adjustment and less self-criticism. It's not a luxury — it's a foundation.

<ScenarioCard title="James and the Art of Doing Less">
James had always been the person who held everything together — at work, at home, in his friend group. When his partner died suddenly, he tried to maintain that role. Slowly, he found that doing less did not make the grief worse — it made space for it to be felt, and for his body to begin recovering enough to carry it.
</ScenarioCard>

## 6. Reducing Demands Where Possible

If you're in a position to reduce demands on yourself — even slightly — now is the time.

**Possibilities to consider:**
- Let non-urgent tasks wait (they'll still be there later)
- Accept help with household tasks, childcare, errands
- Simplify meals and routines
- Take time off work if possible, or communicate with your employer about reduced capacity
- Pause commitments that drain more than they give
- Unsubscribe from noise — social media, news, obligations that don't serve you right now

Not everyone has the privilege of reducing demands — financial pressures, caregiving responsibilities, and other realities don't pause for grief. If that's your situation, self-compassion becomes even more important. Do what you can with what you have, and don't add self-criticism to an already overwhelming load.

## 7. What to Avoid

<Callout type="warning" title="Substance Use as Coping">
Alcohol, cannabis, and other substances may temporarily numb pain, but they disrupt sleep quality, worsen mood over time, delay grief processing, and can quickly become habitual when paired with ongoing emotional pain. If you notice increasing use, be honest with yourself and consider seeking support. There's no shame in this — using substances to cope with pain is understandable, and getting help is wise.
</Callout>

### Isolation Beyond What Helps
Some solitude during grief is healthy and needed. But extended isolation — weeks of avoiding all human contact — can deepen depression and make grief harder to process. Even brief, low-pressure contact (a short phone call, a walk with someone, sitting in the same room as another person) can help.

### Harsh Self-Talk
"What's wrong with me?" "I should be handling this better." "Everyone else seems fine." Grief is hard enough without your inner critic piling on. Notice self-critical thoughts and, when you can, gently replace them with something kinder.

## 8. Daily Self-Care: A Minimum Viable Checklist

The Grief Self-Care Checklist below offers a gentle daily check-in — not a list of requirements, but an invitation to notice whether your basic needs are being met. Even checking off one or two items is meaningful.

The checklist is prefaced with: "If it feels possible, you might consider..." because during grief, anything framed as a "should" adds pressure you don't need.

<StepByStep>
<Step title="Check In With Your Body">
Pause and notice: When did you last eat? Drink water? How did you sleep? Are you in physical pain? This is not a test — it is gentle information gathering.
</Step>
<Step title="Choose One Small Act of Care">
Pick the single easiest thing you can do right now. A glass of water. A piece of toast. A few minutes sitting outside. Lower the bar until it feels possible.
</Step>
<Step title="Do It Without Judgment">
Whatever you manage to do, let it be enough. Do not compare it to what you used to do or what you think you should do. Today, this is enough.
</Step>
<Step title="Rest Afterward">
If doing even one small thing took effort, honor that effort with rest. Sit down. Close your eyes. Let yourself be still.
</Step>
<Step title="Repeat Tomorrow">
Self-care during grief is not a one-time achievement. It is a daily, sometimes hourly, practice of small kindnesses toward yourself. Tomorrow, do it again — whatever "it" looks like that day.
</Step>
</StepByStep>

## Summary

- Self-care during grief is about survival and basic stability, not optimization
- Prioritize food (even small amounts), hydration, sleep, and medication
- Gentle movement — even a short walk — helps your nervous system regulate
- Pacing (doing a little, then resting) prevents crashes and honors your limits
- Self-compassion is not a luxury but a foundation for moving through grief
- Reduce demands where possible; accept help when offered
- Monitor substance use honestly; avoid prolonged isolation and harsh self-talk
