## Where the confusion usually lives

Even when the homepage is acceptable, category confusion often shows up on these pages:

### About page
This page often reveals the “real” identity of the company more than the homepage does. [thecreativemomentum](https://www.thecreativemomentum.com/blog/understanding-information-architecture-for-your-enterprise-website)

If your About page talks heavily about:
- the operating system,
- AI infrastructure,
- transformation architecture,
- GTM frameworks,
- innovation systems,

then a buyer may reinterpret the whole company as a platform or consultancy, even if the homepage leads with digital wellness. [thechannelco](https://www.thechannelco.com/blog/start-with-the-message-the-one-gtm-move-that-changes-everything)

**What to check**
- Does the About page clearly say Digital Wellness Academy is a licensable solution for practices, employers, and platforms?
- Does it explain GTM OS as the enabling foundation, not the primary thing buyers are supposed to understand first?

### Navigation
Navigation often tells the truth faster than homepage copy. [imagexmedia](https://imagexmedia.com/blog/website-information-architecture-tips-improve-ux)

If your nav foregrounds:
- Platform,
- Architecture,
- AI,
- Infrastructure,
- OS,
- Innovation,

before:
- Solutions,
- Course Library,
- For Employers,
- For Practices,
- Licensing,

then the site is signaling “tech platform” first. [webstacks](https://www.webstacks.com/blog/best-b2b-saas-websites)

**What to check**
- Are your top nav items organized around buyer tasks or around internal product architecture?
- Would a clinician or HR leader know exactly where to click next?

### About-the-product pages
Pages like Features, Platform, Technology, Innovation, Ecosystem, or Framework often overpower the commercial story. [uxmatters](https://www.uxmatters.com/mt/archives/2024/09/designing-effective-information-architectures-for-large-scale-web-sites.php)

If those pages are more detailed, more polished, or more persuasive than your buyer-facing solution pages, they can become the dominant narrative of the site. [genesysgrowth](https://genesysgrowth.com/blog/designing-b2b-saas-product-pages)

**What to check**
- Is your Platform page stronger than your Solutions page?
- Does your technical material have better language than your licensing/use-case material?

If yes, then even if the homepage is balanced, the site as a whole may still “feel like GTM OS.”

### Course or library pages
These pages can create a different kind of confusion. [bask](https://bask.health/blog/health-care-web-design)

If the course pages look like:
- consumer self-help offerings,
- certification/training catalog,
- educational library for individuals,

then they may undercut your B2B licensing story. [insivia](https://www.insivia.com/5-must-haves-healthtech-websites-design/)

**What to check**
- Do course pages explain how organizations deploy these courses?
- Do they mention use cases for patients, employees, members, and platforms?
- Do they include licensing CTAs rather than only learner-style CTAs?

### Footer and boilerplate text
This sounds minor, but boilerplate often reveals positioning drift. [b13](https://b13.com/blog/designing-an-effective-information-architecture-best-practices)

If your footer or repeated site text says things like:
- “AI-powered GTM operating system,”
- “platform for innovation,”
- “transformational architecture,”

more often than it says:
- “digital wellness licensing,”
- “branded courses for patients and employees,”
- “enterprise and practice deployment,”

then the deeper brand signal may still be platform-first.

## The real test across pages

Try this on each page:

### Ask: “What business am I in on this page?”
For each page, write the answer in one sentence.

Examples:
- Homepage: “We license digital wellness experiences.”
- For Employers: “We offer digital wellness as an employee benefit.”
- Platform: “We power delivery, analytics, and scale.”
- About: “We built a platform to make digital wellness deployable.”

If a page instead sounds like:
- “We are an AI GTM operating system,”
- “We are a transformation framework,”
- “We are an educational media brand,”

then that page is pulling the site into another category. [theb2bplaybook](https://theb2bplaybook.com/b2b-positioning-strategy)

### Ask: “Would a buyer know the next step?”
Every page should end in the right next action:
- Book a demo
- Explore licensing
- See enterprise deployment
- Review platform capabilities

If technical pages don’t eventually return the visitor to a commercial path, they become narrative dead ends. [kalungi](https://www.kalungi.com/blog/enterprise-buyer-journey-pages-after-homepage)
