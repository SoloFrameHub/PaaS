About SoloFrameHub

The builder behind the runtime.

A semi-technical business and AI strategist, based in Bogotá. Using AI to build what used to require hiring a team.

The platform is proof of its own thesis - it teaches founders to use AI to do what used to require hiring, and it was built the same way.

The Origin

Two stories behind this platform.

One explains why the platform has to teach sales differently. The other explains why it can cost $29.95.

Story 01 · Why sales feels the way it does

The Google rep who built the product thesis.

I was developing this platform on Google Cloud and Firebase. Then a Google Cloud sales rep laid out $12,000/year in committed cloud spend as the price of entry to a "technical demo" - of services I was already working with.

This is the sales rep every founder encounters. They don't diagnose. They don't listen. They run quota-driven scripts at people whose problems they haven't earned the right to hear. It's the reason "sales" has the reputation it does - and the reason most founders flinch when they have to do it themselves.

The platform teaches the opposite. Diagnose before you prescribe. Earn the right to propose. Walk away cleanly when the fit isn't there. The method the Google rep didn't use on me is the method this platform trains founders to run on their own customers.

Story 02 · Why it costs $29.95

Control the cost. Don't rent it.

I wanted to keep the price affordable for pre-revenue founders. That meant picking an infrastructure approach where I could control what things cost directly, instead of paying through abstracted-cloud seat counts and request meters.

So that's what I did. One commodity KVM box, services scheduled together by one orchestrator, so adding the next capability doesn't add the next bill. Cloud spend stays sub-$100/month. Redundant infrastructure is being added, and each product vertical gets its own box once revenue justifies it.

That's the mentality - do the engineering work so founders don't pay the overhead. The price holds because I made that choice, not in spite of it.

The Method

The platform is its own Patient Zero.

GTM OS teaches founders to use AI to do what used to require hiring. It was built by applying that method to itself - three times, in three closed loops.

01

Built with it.

No engineering team. No agency. AI-assisted development produced what would traditionally take a team a year.

02

Stays current with it.

A 5-signal loop feeds the next fine-tune cycle. See a signal Tuesday, ship the fix Thursday. Every lesson, chat, and roleplay improves at once.

03

Supports itself with it.

Customer support runs AI-first with human pickup when the bot can't close. Same pattern taught to founders for their own customers.

Background

Two decades in enterprise tech. Two cultures. One runtime.

I'm Mike Sullivan. Twenty-plus years in enterprise technology and sales leadership, split across North America and Latin America. Long enough to know what enterprise buyers value and what they tolerate - and long enough to know how the rules have rewritten themselves in the last two years.

I'm based in Bogotá. The talent in LatAm is world-class. The GTM playbooks weren't written for it - soft-no patterns, tú/usted register, country-specific objections, a relationship-before-business culture most Silicon Valley frameworks miss entirely. Building a regional LatAm edition of the platform isn't a translation project; it's a recognition project.

I'm a semi-technical business and AI strategist - not a developer who happens to have business ideas. The distinction matters because the platform is designed from the buyer's end first: what does the solo founder actually need to run customer acquisition with discipline? Then the architecture earns its keep serving that answer.

The two closest-analog founders I'd compare the profile to: someone who left BigCo consulting to build something, or someone who spent a career closing enterprise deals and finally wanted to build the tool they'd been forced to pitch workarounds for.

What I Believe

Five things the platform is built on.

01

AI-native, not AI-wrapped.

A platform designed from the AI interaction outward composes differently than one with an LLM bolted onto a legacy workflow. Context-aware coaching only works when context lives at every layer of the runtime - not stapled on later.

02

Retention is the hidden growth engine.

Acquisition rigor matters. But in a world where AI has compressed content production and outreach to near-zero marginal cost, the moat is whether anyone stays. That's why the coaching is context-aware, the pods are DISC-matched, and the curriculum updates monthly instead of quarterly.

03

Founders own their integration keys.

BYOK on third-party integrations is not a feature; it's a political position. When a platform holds your keys to services you already pay for, it rents your leverage back to you at a markup. CRM, email-finding, export, messaging - you supply the credentials, we proxy calls on your behalf. Your contact data stays in your vendor accounts. The platform AI, on the other hand, we manage ourselves - so you don't have to.

04

Judgment calls are most of the work.

A business-minded operator who can orchestrate AI-assisted development can hold their own on work where the hard part is judgment calls, not parallel engineering. The platform exists to make that operational for any founder willing to try.

05

The best investor pitch is a world-class founder experience.

No "Apply for funding" banners. No investor-brief pop-ups. The site is for the founders who will pay for the product. If an investor likes what they see, they'll reach out on their own - that's the signal worth having.

Platform Architecture

The codebase is architected multi-tenant.

A new vertical ships in about a week because the engine doesn't get rebuilt. Course content, prompt templates, integration adapters, and seed data swap. The core engines for coaching, roleplay, pods, and intelligence stay shared.

That's a design claim, not a deployment one. Every vertical that ships gets its own dedicated infrastructure once revenue justifies it. The tenancy is in the code, not in a shared box.
