## COURSE OVERVIEW

### Course Description

Positioning is the space you occupy in your customer's mind—not your logo, not your colors, but the answer to "why should I choose you over alternatives?" This course teaches April Dunford's positioning framework, the StoryBrand messaging structure, and practical brand creation for bootstrap founders. You'll articulate why prospects should choose you over alternatives, translate technical features into business outcomes, and create a Minimum Viable Brand without expensive agencies.

### Why This Course Matters

Technical founders make two positioning mistakes: they either ignore it entirely (looking unprofessional and confusing prospects) or obsess over it prematurely (spending months on logos before validating demand). Both waste time and money.

Research shows design-related first impressions account for 94% of initial brand judgments. For unknown bootstrap founders, positioning is what helps prospects trust you before they've worked with you. But Nathan Barry (ConvertKit) didn't have a logo for the first 2.5 years—he focused on positioning clarity, not visual polish.

The real positioning crisis is this: 65% of customers seek simple, benefit-focused messaging, but technical founders default to feature-first language ("AI-powered algorithms") instead of outcome-first language ("stop waiting for slow databases"). This course fixes that translation problem.

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, founders will be able to:

1. Apply April Dunford's 5-component positioning framework to their product
2. Translate technical features into customer-relevant benefits and outcomes
3. Create a Minimum Viable Brand (value prop, wordmark, colors, fonts, guidelines)
4. Write positioning statements for different contexts (website, pitch, email)
5. Use the StoryBrand framework to structure customer-centric messaging
6. Craft elevator pitches at 30-second, 2-minute, and 5-minute lengths
7. Quantify value in terms prospects care about (time, money, risk)
8. Differentiate from competitors without competitor-bashing

## COURSE STRUCTURE (10 Lessons)

### Lesson 1: What Positioning Actually Means (And Why Features Don't Sell)

**Duration:** 45 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Define positioning as "the space you occupy in your customer's mind"
- Distinguish between positioning, branding, and marketing
- Understand why technical founders default to feature-first messaging
- Recognize the 65% statistic: customers want benefits, not specs

**Content Outline:**

1. **The Positioning Problem**
   - You're not selling features—you're selling a change in the customer's life
   - The curse of knowledge: you know your product too well to explain it simply
   - Why "AI-powered" and "blockchain-based" repel more customers than they attract

2. **Positioning vs. Branding vs. Marketing**
   - Positioning: What you stand for, who you serve, why you're different
   - Branding: How you express that positioning (visual, verbal, experiential)
   - Marketing: How you communicate that positioning to attract customers
   - Order matters: Positioning first, then branding, then marketing

3. **The Technical Founder Trap**
   - Leading with technology instead of outcomes
   - Confusing complexity with value
   - Building for yourself instead of the customer
   - Example: "Uses machine learning algorithms" vs. "Predicts which leads will close"

4. **The Buyer's Mental Model**
   - Prospects slot you into existing categories
   - If they can't categorize you, they ignore you
   - Your job: make the category choice obvious

**Assessment:**  
Find 3 competitor websites. For each, identify: Do they lead with features or outcomes? Can you tell in 5 seconds what they do? What category do they occupy?

### Lesson 2: April Dunford's Positioning Framework

**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Framework Introduction  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Learn the 5 components of Dunford's positioning framework
- Understand why the sequence matters (each builds on the previous)
- Apply the framework to a sample product
- Identify gaps in your current positioning

**Content Outline:**

1. **The 5 Components (In Order)**
   - **Competitive Alternatives:** What would customers do if you didn't exist?
   - **Unique Attributes:** What do you have that alternatives don't?
   - **Differentiated Value:** What value do those attributes enable?
   - **Target Customer Segment:** Who cares most about that value?
   - **Market Category:** What context makes your value obvious?

2. **Why Sequence Matters**
   - You can't define differentiation without knowing alternatives
   - You can't articulate value without knowing your unique attributes
   - You can't target effectively without knowing who values your differentiation
   - You can't choose a category without all of the above

3. **Competitive Alternatives Deep Dive**
   - Not just direct competitors
   - Include: doing nothing, manual workarounds, hiring someone, building in-house
   - The real competition for most startups: "We'll just keep using spreadsheets"

4. **Working Through an Example**
   - Walk through positioning framework for a hypothetical B2B tool
   - Show how each component informs the next
   - Demonstrate the resulting positioning statement

5. **Common Framework Mistakes**
   - Starting with market category (puts cart before horse)
   - Listing features as "unique attributes" instead of capabilities
   - Targeting "everyone" instead of the segment that values you most

**Assessment:**  
Complete the first two components for your product: List 3-5 competitive alternatives (including non-purchase), then identify 2-3 attributes you have that they don't.

### Lesson 3: StoryBrand Messaging Framework

**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Framework Application  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Learn the 7-part StoryBrand framework
- Position the customer as hero, yourself as guide
- Create messaging that follows story structure
- Avoid the "brand as hero" mistake

**Content Outline:**

1. **The StoryBrand 7-Part Framework**
   - **A Character (Customer):** Has a desire
   - **Has a Problem:** Villain, external, internal, philosophical dimensions
   - **Meets a Guide (You):** Who demonstrates empathy AND authority
   - **Who Gives Them a Plan:** 3-4 simple steps
   - **And Calls Them to Action:** Direct CTA
   - **That Helps Them Avoid Failure:** Stakes of not acting
   - **And Ends in Success:** Transformation achieved

2. **Customer as Hero, Not You**
   - Your customer is Luke Skywalker; you're Yoda
   - The brand's job is to help the hero win, not to be the hero
   - Why "Our team has 50 years of combined experience" fails
   - Better: "We've helped 200 founders like you reach $10K MRR"

3. **The Three Levels of Problem**
   - External: The surface problem they want solved
   - Internal: How the problem makes them feel
   - Philosophical: Why it's just wrong that this problem exists
   - Example: External (slow reports), Internal (frustrated, behind), Philosophical (you shouldn't have to waste time on busywork)

4. **Empathy + Authority = Trust**
   - Empathy: "We understand what you're going through"
   - Authority: "And we know how to fix it"
   - Both required—empathy without authority is friendship; authority without empathy is arrogance

5. **The Plan That Removes Confusion**
   - 3-4 steps maximum
   - Make buying feel simple and safe
   - Example: "1. Book a call. 2. We build your system. 3. You get your time back."

**Assessment:**  
Draft your StoryBrand one-liner: [Customer] who struggles with [Problem] can now [Solution] so they can [Transformation].

### Lesson 4: Feature → Benefit → Outcome Translation

**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Practice  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Distinguish between features, benefits, and outcomes
- Translate technical capabilities into customer language
- Use the "so that..." chain to find the real outcome
- Avoid feature-dumping in sales conversations

**Content Outline:**

1. **The Translation Hierarchy**
   - **Feature:** What it IS (technical capability)
   - **Benefit:** What it DOES (functional result)
   - **Outcome:** What it MEANS (emotional/business impact)
   - Customers buy outcomes, tolerate features

2. **The "So That..." Chain**
   - Start with feature: "We have automated reporting"
   - Ask "so that...": "So that you don't build reports manually"
   - Ask again: "So that you save 10 hours per week"
   - Again: "So that you can focus on strategic work"
   - Again: "So that you look competent to your boss"
   - Keep asking until you hit emotional/career impact

3. **Technical Founder Examples**
   - "Real-time sync" → "Never work with stale data" → "Make decisions with confidence"
   - "AI-powered" → "Predicts outcomes automatically" → "Stop guessing, start knowing"
   - "API-first" → "Connects to your existing tools" → "No rip-and-replace; works with what you have"

4. **The Outcome Test**
   - Would a non-technical person understand this?
   - Does it describe their life getting better?
   - Could they explain it to their boss/spouse?
   - If no to any: you're still at feature or benefit level

5. **When Features Matter**
   - Late-stage evaluation with technical buyers
   - Compliance and security requirements
   - Integration compatibility checks
   - But never lead with them

**Assessment:**  
List your top 5 product features. For each, use the "so that..." chain to find the ultimate outcome. Create a feature→benefit→outcome table.

### Lesson 5: Value Quantification

**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Calculation  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Quantify value in terms of time, money, and risk
- Calculate ROI that resonates with economic buyers
- Use discovery questions to gather quantification data
- Present value anchoring in proposals and pricing

**Content Outline:**

1. **The Three Value Currencies**
   - **Time:** Hours saved per week/month/year
   - **Money:** Revenue gained, costs reduced, waste eliminated
   - **Risk:** Problems avoided, compliance maintained, reputation protected
   - Different buyers care about different currencies

2. **Time Value Calculation**
   - Hours saved × hourly rate = dollar value
   - "You mentioned 10 hours/week on this. At $75/hour fully loaded, that's $39,000/year in recovered time."
   - Include: employee time, founder time, opportunity cost

3. **Money Value Calculation**
   - Revenue: "If this increases close rate by 10% on your $500K pipeline, that's $50K additional revenue"
   - Cost reduction: "Replacing [manual process/tool] saves $X/month"
   - Efficiency: "Doing [task] in half the time doubles output"

4. **Risk Value Calculation**
   - "What's the cost if this problem continues?"
   - "What happened last time [bad outcome] occurred?"
   - "What's the compliance penalty for [violation]?"
   - Harder to quantify but often most compelling

5. **Gathering Quantification Data in Discovery**
   - "How much time does your team spend on this per week?"
   - "What's the fully-loaded cost of an hour of your team's time?"
   - "What's this problem costing you monthly?"
   - Build quantification into your discovery framework

6. **Value Anchoring in Pricing**
   - Never present price alone
   - "The investment is $2,500, and based on the $4,000/month this problem costs you, ROI happens in week three."
   - Compare investment to cost of inaction, not to budget

**Assessment:**  
Using your product, create three value statements: one for time saved, one for money impacted, one for risk reduced. Include specific numbers.

### Lesson 6: Competitive Positioning (Without Competitor Bashing)

**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Strategy  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Position against alternatives without naming competitors
- Handle "how are you different from X?" questions
- Use positioning to disqualify poor-fit prospects
- Create comparison content that ranks in search

**Content Outline:**

1. **The Differentiation Principle**
   - You don't need to be better at everything
   - You need to be the obvious choice for YOUR segment
   - Different is better than better

2. **Positioning Against Categories, Not Companies**
   - Instead of "better than Competitor X"
   - Try: "Unlike [category], we [differentiator]"
   - Example: "Unlike traditional CRMs designed for enterprise, we're built specifically for solo founders"

3. **Handling Competitive Questions**
   - "We focus on [segment], while they serve [different segment]"
   - "If [competitor need] is your priority, they're probably a better fit. If [your strength] matters more, we're the choice."
   - Gracious differentiation builds trust

4. **The "Best For" Framework**
   - Define who you're best for (your ICP)
   - Acknowledge who you're not best for
   - Reduces bad-fit deals and support burden
   - "We're best for solo founders doing $10K-$100K. If you have a sales team, [competitor] might be a better fit."

5. **Comparison Content Strategy**
   - "[Your product] vs [Competitor]" pages rank in search
   - Be fair and factual—readers can tell when you're biased
   - Focus on use cases and fit, not feature checkboxes
   - Include: "Choose [competitor] if... Choose us if..."

6. **When to Name Competitors**
   - Prospect explicitly asks
   - Comparison content for SEO
   - Replacement/migration messaging
   - Never in unprompted sales conversations

**Assessment:**  
Write your "Best For" statement: "We're best for [specific customer] who needs [specific outcome]. If you're [different situation], [alternative] might be a better fit."

### Lesson 7: Minimum Viable Brand

**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Creation  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Create a functional brand without expensive agencies
- Develop: value prop, wordmark, colors, fonts, guidelines
- Use free/cheap tools (Canva, Figma, Looka)
- Know when DIY is enough and when to hire

**Content Outline:**

1. **Minimum Viable Brand Components**
   - Clear one-sentence value proposition
   - Simple wordmark logo (company name in clean typeface)
   - 2-3 brand colors (primary, secondary, accent)
   - 1-2 fonts (one for headings, one for body)
   - One-page brand guidelines document

2. **The Nathan Barry Lesson**
   - ConvertKit didn't have a logo for 2.5 years
   - Focus on clarity of message, not visual polish
   - Professional enough to not distract; simple enough to execute

3. **DIY Brand Creation Process**
   - Step 1: Write your one-sentence value prop
   - Step 2: Choose a clean typeface for your wordmark (Google Fonts is free)
   - Step 3: Select colors from a proven palette (Coolors, Adobe Color)
   - Step 4: Create simple assets in Canva
   - Step 5: Document in a one-page guideline

4. **Color Psychology Basics**
   - Blue: Trust, stability, professionalism
   - Green: Growth, health, money
   - Orange: Energy, creativity, friendliness
   - Purple: Premium, creativity, wisdom
   - Match to your positioning, not personal preference

5. **Tools for Non-Designers**
   - **Canva:** Brand Kit feature, templates, Magic Design
   - **Figma:** Free tier for design systems
   - **Looka/Hatchful:** AI logo generation ($20-65)
   - **Coolors:** Color palette generator

6. **When to Hire a Designer**
   - After product-market fit is validated
   - When visual inconsistency is costing you deals
   - For high-stakes assets (investor deck, major launch)
   - Budget: $500-2000 for logo + basic system

**Assessment:**  
Create your Minimum Viable Brand: one-sentence value prop, wordmark (name in chosen font), 3 colors, 2 fonts. Document in a one-page PDF.

### Lesson 8: Brand Voice and Tone

**Duration:** 45 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Definition  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Define your brand voice (consistent personality)
- Understand tone variation (voice adapted to context)
- Create voice guidelines for consistency
- Avoid AI-sounding generic copy

**Content Outline:**

1. **Voice vs. Tone**
   - Voice: Your consistent personality (e.g., friendly, expert, direct)
   - Tone: How voice adapts to context (e.g., celebratory in success emails, empathetic in support)
   - Voice is WHO you are; tone is HOW you speak in the moment

2. **Voice Dimensions**
   - Formal ←→ Casual
   - Serious ←→ Playful
   - Authoritative ←→ Approachable
   - Technical ←→ Simple
   - Pick your position on each spectrum

3. **Finding Your Authentic Voice**
   - How do you naturally explain your product to friends?
   - What voice would your ideal customers trust?
   - What voice can you consistently maintain?
   - Authentic > polished; sustainable > impressive

4. **Voice Guidelines Document**
   - 3-5 adjectives that describe your voice
   - Examples of what you sound like (and don't sound like)
   - Word choices to use and avoid
   - Sample sentences in your voice

5. **Avoiding Generic AI Voice**
   - The tells: "unlock," "leverage," "empower," "game-changer"
   - Why AI-generated copy feels hollow
   - Using AI as starting point, not final output
   - Injecting specific stories, numbers, and opinions

6. **Tone Adaptation Examples**
   - Homepage: Confident, aspirational
   - Error message: Apologetic, helpful
   - Success email: Celebratory, warm
   - Support response: Empathetic, solution-focused
   - Same voice, different tones

**Assessment:**  
Define your brand voice: Pick your position on 4 dimensions. Write 3 adjectives. Create 2 "We sound like..." and 2 "We don't sound like..." examples.

### Lesson 9: Your Positioning Statement

**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Practice + Synthesis  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Synthesize all components into positioning statements
- Create statements for different contexts (website, pitch, email)
- Test statements with the "so what?" filter
- Use Sales Linter to analyze clarity

**Content Outline:**

1. **The Positioning Statement Formula**
   - For [target customer] who [has this problem], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternatives], we [key differentiator].
   - This is internal clarity, not marketing copy

2. **Statement Variations by Context**
   - **Website headline:** Shortest, outcome-focused, 8 words or less
   - **Email subject/opener:** Problem-agitation hook
   - **Pitch introduction:** 30-second version with credibility
   - **Detailed explanation:** 2-minute version with evidence

3. **The "So What?" Test**
   - Read your statement
   - Ask "So what? Why should I care?"
   - If you can ask it, the statement isn't outcome-enough
   - Keep pushing until "so what?" has no answer

4. **Sales Linter Analysis**
   - Input your positioning statement
   - Check for: jargon, passive voice, feature-focus, length
   - Iterate based on feedback
   - Compare multiple versions

5. **Real-World Examples**
   - Weak: "We provide AI-powered analytics solutions for businesses"
   - Better: "We help e-commerce founders find which products to restock before they run out"
   - Test: Can a stranger understand this? Would they care?

6. **Positioning Statement Workshop**
   - Write 3 variations of your positioning statement
   - Test each with the "so what?" filter
   - Run through Sales Linter
   - Select and refine the strongest version

**Assessment:**  
Create 3 positioning statements: website headline (under 10 words), email opener (1-2 sentences), pitch introduction (30 seconds when spoken). Submit to Sales Linter for analysis.

### Lesson 10: Elevator Pitch Workshop

**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Practice + Refinement  
**Learning Objectives:**
- Create elevator pitches at 30-second, 2-minute, and 5-minute lengths
- Structure pitches for different audiences (investor, customer, partner)
- Handle the "what do you do?" question naturally
- Practice and refine through iteration

**Content Outline:**

1. **The 30-Second Pitch (Cocktail Party)**
   - Situation: Someone asks "What do you do?"
   - Goal: Create interest, invite follow-up question
   - Structure: I help [who] [achieve what] by [how]
   - Example: "I help solo founders get their first 100 customers without hiring salespeople. I built a training platform that teaches the complete system."

2. **The 2-Minute Pitch (Networking Event)**
   - Situation: Genuine interest, limited time
   - Goal: Explain value and differentiation
   - Structure: Problem → Solution → Proof → Invitation
   - Include one specific result or customer story

3. **The 5-Minute Pitch (Meeting Introduction)**
   - Situation: Dedicated time to explain
   - Goal: Complete context for deeper conversation
   - Structure: Hook → Problem → Solution → How it works → Proof → Why now → Next step
   - Can include demo offer or discovery question

4. **Audience Adaptation**
   - **Investor:** Market size, traction, team, vision
   - **Customer:** Their problem, your solution, proof, next step
   - **Partner:** Mutual benefit, complementary value, collaboration opportunity
   - Same core message, different emphasis

5. **The Follow-Up Question Test**
   - A good pitch invites: "How does that work?" or "Tell me more"
   - A bad pitch invites: "Oh, that's nice" (conversation ender)
   - Leave something intriguing unsaid

6. **Practice Protocol**
   - Write out each pitch length
   - Practice out loud (reading feels different than speaking)
   - Time yourself (most people go long)
   - Record and review (uncomfortable but effective)
   - Iterate based on actual reactions

**Assessment:**  
Write and practice all three pitch lengths. Record yourself delivering the 30-second version. Note what feels awkward and refine.

1. **Sales Linter as Teaching Tool:** Use Sales Linter analysis of positioning statements as a teaching moment—show common patterns and how to fix them.

2. **Dual Context Examples:** Every framework example should show both B2B SaaS and Creator/Coach application. The frameworks are identical; the specifics differ.
