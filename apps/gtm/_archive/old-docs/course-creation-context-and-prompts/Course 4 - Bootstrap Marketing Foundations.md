## COURSE OVERVIEW

### Course Description

Bootstrap Marketing Foundations transforms marketing from an intimidating black box into a systematic, measurable discipline that technical founders can master. This course teaches the core architecture of modern marketing—the flywheel model, funnel stages, and channel-product-audience fit—using frameworks that resonate with engineering mindsets.

Without a $10K/month ad budget, you need systems that generate leads through time investment rather than cash. This course provides the foundational frameworks that every subsequent marketing course builds upon: the marketing flywheel concept, channel-product-audience fit, TOFU/MOFU/BOFU funnel stages, and AARRR pirate metrics.

Technical founders often default to building more features instead of marketing existing ones. This course reframes marketing as a systematic, measurable discipline that engineers can master through process and iteration.

### Why This Course Matters

**For Technical Founders:**
- Marketing feels like "black magic" because it lacks clear inputs→outputs
- You've been told to "just post on social media" without systematic guidance
- You're uncomfortable with promotion because it feels inauthentic
- You have limited budget but unlimited time to invest in the right channels

**What Changes After This Course:**
- Marketing becomes a debugging exercise: measure, iterate, optimize
- You understand which channels match YOUR product, audience, and constraints
- You can evaluate marketing advice through frameworks, not influencer hype
- You build a sustainable marketing engine, not just run one-off campaigns

**The Bootstrap Reality:**
VC-backed startups can afford $50K/month on paid ads while testing messaging. You can't. This course teaches time-first strategies: content, community, partnerships, and organic channels that compound over months rather than requiring constant cash injection.

## COURSE STRUCTURE (10 Lessons)

### Lesson 1: Why Bootstrap Marketing Is Different
**Duration:** 45 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation (Foundation)  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 80% Teaching, 10% Reflection, 10% Case Study

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Distinguish between VC-backed and bootstrap marketing strategies
2. Understand the cash-vs-time investment trade-off
3. Identify why technical founders resist marketing (and how to overcome it)
4. Recognize the compounding nature of time-based marketing channels

**Content Outline:**

**I. The Two Marketing Paradigms (10 min)**
- **VC-Backed Approach:** Fast growth through paid acquisition
  - Example: Dropbox spent $300M+ on paid ads (2018-2020)
  - Strategy: Buy attention, outspend competitors, optimize for speed
  - Metrics: CAC payback period, LTV/CAC ratio, burn multiple
- **Bootstrap Approach:** Sustainable growth through owned channels
  - Example: Basecamp built $100M+ ARR with zero paid ads
  - Strategy: Earn attention, out-teach competitors, optimize for efficiency
  - Metrics: Organic traffic growth, email list growth, referral rate

**II. Cash vs. Time Investment Trade-Off (12 min)**
- **Cash-Intensive Channels:** Paid ads, sponsorships, PR agencies
  - Pros: Instant results, precise targeting, scalable spend
  - Cons: Requires $5K+ monthly budget, stops when budget stops, competitive
- **Time-Intensive Channels:** Content, SEO, community, partnerships
  - Pros: Compounds over time, zero marginal cost, builds moats
  - Cons: Takes 3-6 months to see results, requires consistent effort
- **The Bootstrap Math:**
  - If you have $500/month → 1-2 weeks of paid ads → then silence
  - If you have 10 hours/week → 12 blog posts → persistent Google ranking
  - **Decision Framework:** "Can I sustain this channel for 12+ months?"

**III. Why Technical Founders Resist Marketing (15 min)**
- **Identity Conflict:** "I'm an engineer, not a marketer"
  - Reframe: Marketing is product distribution, not manipulation
  - Analogy: "Deployment pipeline for customers, not just code"
- **Illegibility:** Marketing feels like guessing, not measuring
  - Reframe: Marketing has metrics, experiments, and optimization loops
  - Analogy: "A/B tests for messaging, not just features"
- **Aversion to Self-Promotion:** "I don't want to be salesy"
  - Reframe: Teaching is marketing; helping is selling
  - Example: Patrick McKenzie (patio11) built Appointment Reminder through blog posts
- **Perfectionism Trap:** "I need a perfect brand/website/content first"
  - Reality: Ship v1.0 marketing just like you ship v1.0 products
  - Framework: Minimum Viable Marketing (MVM)

**IV. The Compounding Advantage (8 min)**
- **Linear Channels:** Paid ads, cold outreach (stop paying → stop getting results)
- **Compounding Channels:** SEO, content, community (results accumulate over time)
- **Real Example:** Nathan Barry (ConvertKit founder)
  - 2013: Started blog, zero traffic
  - 2014: 50 blog posts → 10K monthly visitors
  - 2015: 100 posts → 50K monthly visitors → $500K ARR
  - 2024: 500+ posts → 500K+ monthly visitors → $29M ARR
  - **Key Insight:** Each post continues generating traffic years later

**Assessment:**
**Reflection Exercise (Required):**
"Which marketing paradigm describes YOUR current approach? Are you operating like a VC-backed company without VC resources? Write 3-5 sentences describing how you'll shift your marketing mindset from 'buying attention' to 'earning attention.'"

**Knowledge Check:**
1. What's the primary difference between cash-intensive and time-intensive marketing channels?
2. Why do technical founders often resist marketing?
3. Give an example of a compounding marketing channel and explain why it compounds.

### Lesson 2: The Marketing Flywheel Architecture
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Framework Application  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 70% Teaching, 20% Framework Building, 10% Case Study

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Understand the Attract→Engage→Delight flywheel model
2. Map your customer's journey through flywheel stages
3. Identify activities that accelerate vs. slow the flywheel
4. Design friction-reducing interventions at each stage

**Content Outline:**

**I. From Funnel to Flywheel (12 min)**
- **The Traditional Marketing Funnel Problem:**
  - Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action (AIDA)
  - Treats customers as one-time conversions
  - Ends at purchase, ignoring retention and referrals
  - Creates "leaky bucket" businesses (acquire faster than churn)
- **The Flywheel Model (HubSpot Framework):**
  - **Attract** → **Engage** → **Delight** → (customers become promoters) → **Attract** (loop)
  - Customers become part of the growth engine
  - Momentum compounds with each satisfied customer
  - Energy stored in customer relationships, not just ad spend

**II. Flywheel Stage Breakdown (20 min)**

**Stage 1: ATTRACT**
- **Goal:** Earn attention from strangers who match your ICP
- **Activities:**
  - SEO-optimized content (blog posts, technical guides)
  - Social media presence (Twitter/X, LinkedIn for B2B)
  - Community participation (Reddit, Hacker News, niche forums)
  - Guest posts and podcast appearances
- **Success Metrics:** Website traffic, social followers, brand mentions
- **Common Friction Points:**
  - Generic content that doesn't speak to ICP pain points
  - Inconsistent posting schedule (post once, disappear for months)
  - Wrong channels (B2B SaaS founder posting on Instagram)

**Stage 2: ENGAGE**
- **Goal:** Convert strangers into leads who trust you enough to give contact info
- **Activities:**
  - Lead magnets (templates, checklists, calculators)
  - Email nurture sequences
  - Free tools or freemium products
  - Webinars and live demos
- **Success Metrics:** Email subscribers, free trial signups, demo requests
- **Common Friction Points:**
  - Asking for too much information upfront (long forms)
  - Weak lead magnet (generic PDF that could apply to anyone)
  - No follow-up after opt-in (welcome email → silence)

**Stage 3: DELIGHT**
- **Goal:** Turn customers into promoters who refer others
- **Activities:**
  - Exceptional onboarding experience
  - Proactive customer success check-ins
  - Surprise-and-delight moments (unexpected features, personal touches)
  - Easy referral mechanisms
- **Success Metrics:** NPS score, referral rate, case study participation
- **Common Friction Points:**
  - Onboarding assumes too much technical knowledge
  - No defined success milestones ("What's a good outcome?")
  - Difficult to refer (no referral link, no incentive)

**III. Flywheel Acceleration Principles (10 min)**
- **Reduce Friction:** Make every step easier than the previous
  - Example: ConvertKit's "Import from Mailchimp" button
- **Increase Force:** Do fewer things, but better
  - Example: Basecamp's "Getting Real" ebook became a movement
- **Store Energy:** Build assets that work while you sleep
  - Example: SEO content continues attracting traffic for years

**IV. Case Study: Gumroad's Flywheel (8 min)**
- **Attract:** Sahil Lavingia tweets building in public, writes blog posts
- **Engage:** Creators sign up for free tier, list first product
- **Delight:** Easy payouts, no upfront fees, transparent revenue sharing
- **Loop:** Creators promote their Gumroad stores → new creators see Gumroad → sign up
- **Result:** 100K+ creators, $0 spent on paid ads

**Assessment:**
**Framework Application Exercise:**
"Using the Flywheel Canvas tool, map YOUR product's customer journey:
1. ATTRACT: List 3 ways strangers find you
2. ENGAGE: List 3 ways strangers become leads
3. DELIGHT: List 3 ways customers become promoters

Identify the #1 friction point in each stage and propose one fix."

### Lesson 3: TOFU/MOFU/BOFU - The Funnel Framework
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Content Mapping  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 70% Teaching, 20% Content Mapping, 10% Examples

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Define Top of Funnel (TOFU), Middle of Funnel (MOFU), and Bottom of Funnel (BOFU) stages
2. Match content types to funnel stages based on buyer intent
3. Identify gaps in your current content funnel
4. Create a balanced content calendar across all three stages

**Content Outline:**

**I. The Funnel Stage Framework (15 min)**

**TOFU (Top of Funnel) - Awareness Stage**
- **Buyer Mindset:** "I have a problem, but I don't know solutions exist"
- **Content Goal:** Educate, not sell; build trust through helpful information
- **Content Types:**
  - "How to..." guides (e.g., "How to Reduce Customer Churn")
  - Industry trend analyses (e.g., "State of SaaS Onboarding in 2025")
  - Beginner frameworks (e.g., "5 Types of Product-Market Fit")
  - Infographics and statistics
- **Example:** Blog post "7 Signs Your SaaS Onboarding Is Losing Customers"
  - Doesn't mention your product
  - Solves a problem your ICP has
  - Builds trust by demonstrating expertise

**MOFU (Middle of Funnel) - Consideration Stage**
- **Buyer Mindset:** "I know solutions exist, but which one is right for me?"
- **Content Goal:** Position your approach as superior without being salesy
- **Content Types:**
  - Comparison guides (e.g., "Build vs. Buy for Customer Onboarding")
  - Case studies showing real results
  - Free tools or calculators (e.g., "Churn Rate Calculator")
  - Webinars demonstrating your methodology
- **Example:** Case study "How Acme SaaS Reduced Churn 40% in 60 Days"
  - Shows your product in action
  - Focuses on outcomes, not features
  - Addresses common objections indirectly

**BOFU (Bottom of Funnel) - Decision Stage**
- **Buyer Mindset:** "I'm ready to buy, I just need final validation"
- **Content Goal:** Remove final objections and make buying easy
- **Content Types:**
  - Product comparisons (e.g., "YourProduct vs. Competitor X")
  - ROI calculators
  - Implementation guides (reducing perceived complexity)
  - Customer testimonials and reviews
- **Example:** Landing page "See Why 500+ SaaS Companies Choose [Your Product]"
  - Social proof prominently featured
  - Clear pricing and guarantee
  - Easy trial signup or demo booking

**II. The Content Pyramid (12 min)**
- **Ratio for Bootstrap Founders:** 50% TOFU, 30% MOFU, 20% BOFU
  - Why? TOFU content has longest lifespan and broadest reach
  - TOFU feeds MOFU feeds BOFU (waterfall effect)
- **VC-Backed Ratio:** Often 20% TOFU, 30% MOFU, 50% BOFU
  - Why? Paid ads drive bottom-funnel traffic directly
  - Bootstrap founders lack budget for this approach

**III. Content Mapping Exercise (15 min)**
- **Template: TOFU/MOFU/BOFU Content Matrix**
  - List your existing content (blog posts, videos, guides)
  - Categorize each piece by funnel stage
  - Identify gaps (e.g., "We have 20 BOFU pieces but only 2 TOFU")
- **Common Patterns:**
  - **Too Much BOFU:** Founders jump straight to product pitches
  - **Too Much TOFU:** Content creators avoid selling, never convert
  - **Missing MOFU:** Gap between awareness and purchase decision
- **The Fix:** Create 1-2 pieces per stage per month until balanced

**IV. Intent-Based Keyword Mapping (13 min)**
- **TOFU Keywords:** Informational ("how to reduce churn")
- **MOFU Keywords:** Comparative ("best onboarding tools for SaaS")
- **BOFU Keywords:** Transactional ("buy customer onboarding software")
- **Tool Recommendation:** Use AnswerThePublic (free) or Ahrefs ($99/mo)
- **Bootstrap Approach:** Focus on TOFU + MOFU keywords with lower competition

**Assessment:**
**Content Audit Exercise:**
"Create a TOFU/MOFU/BOFU Content Matrix using the platform tool:
1. List all your existing content
2. Categorize each piece by stage
3. Calculate your current ratio (what % is TOFU vs. MOFU vs. BOFU?)
4. Identify the 3 highest-priority content gaps to fill in the next 30 days"

### Lesson 4: AARRR Pirate Metrics for Solo Founders
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Metrics Setup  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 70% Teaching, 20% Metrics Setup, 10% Benchmarking

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Define the 5 AARRR metrics (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral)
2. Identify your key metric at your current business stage
3. Set up simple tracking for each AARRR stage
4. Benchmark your metrics against industry standards

**Content Outline:**

**I. The AARRR Framework Overview (10 min)**
- **Origin:** Dave McClure (500 Startups) created this framework for lean startups
- **Purpose:** Focus on metrics that matter, not vanity metrics
- **The 5 Stages:**
  - **Acquisition:** How users find you
  - **Activation:** How users experience early value
  - **Retention:** How users come back
  - **Revenue:** How users pay you
  - **Referral:** How users tell others

**II. Deep Dive: Each AARRR Stage (30 min)**

**A - ACQUISITION**
- **Definition:** Visitors arrive at your site/app from various channels
- **Key Metrics:**
  - Traffic by channel (organic, social, direct, referral, paid)
  - Cost per visitor (for paid channels)
  - Channel contribution to conversions
- **What to Track:**
  - Google Analytics: Acquisition → All Traffic → Channels
  - Simple spreadsheet: Weekly/monthly traffic by source
- **Benchmarks:**
  - Bootstrap SaaS: 70% organic, 20% direct, 10% social (ideal)
  - Paid acquisition: Aim for < $5 cost per visitor for B2B
- **Common Mistakes:**
  - Tracking total traffic without segmenting by channel
  - Celebrating traffic spikes from low-quality sources

**A - ACTIVATION**
- **Definition:** New users experience "aha moment" - first meaningful value
- **Key Metrics:**
  - % of visitors who complete signup
  - % of signups who complete onboarding
  - Time to first value (e.g., first project created, first export)
- **What to Track:**
  - Define YOUR activation event (what = "aha moment"?)
  - Track % of new users who reach it within 24 hours
- **Benchmarks:**
  - SaaS: 25-40% of free trials should activate
  - Info products: 20-30% of course buyers should complete lesson 1
- **Common Mistakes:**
  - No defined activation event (guessing at success)
  - Tracking signup as success (signup ≠ activation)

**R - RETENTION**
- **Definition:** Users return after initial activation
- **Key Metrics:**
  - Day 1, Day 7, Day 30 retention rates
  - Churn rate (monthly % of users who leave)
  - Engagement frequency (logins per week)
- **What to Track:**
  - Cohort analysis: "Of users who signed up in January, what % were still active in February?"
  - Simple version: Track monthly active users (MAU) trend
- **Benchmarks:**
  - SaaS: 90%+ retention = good, 95%+ = excellent
  - Info products: 50%+ course completion = good
- **Common Mistakes:**
  - Only tracking growth, ignoring churn
  - Measuring retention for ALL users instead of cohorts

**R - REVENUE**
- **Definition:** Users pay for your product/service
- **Key Metrics:**
  - Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)
  - Average Revenue Per User (ARPU)
  - Conversion rate (free → paid)
  - Lifetime Value (LTV)
- **What to Track:**
  - Stripe/payment processor dashboard for MRR
  - Spreadsheet for ARPU and LTV calculations
- **Benchmarks:**
  - SaaS free→paid conversion: 2-5% (freemium), 20-30% (free trial)
  - LTV should be 3x+ Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
- **Common Mistakes:**
  - Celebrating revenue without tracking profitability
  - Ignoring unit economics (LTV vs. CAC)

**R - REFERRAL**
- **Definition:** Users recommend your product to others
- **Key Metrics:**
  - Net Promoter Score (NPS)
  - Referral rate (% of customers who refer someone)
  - Viral coefficient (new users per existing user)
- **What to Track:**
  - Quarterly NPS survey (scale of 0-10: "How likely to recommend?")
  - Track signups with referral source = "customer referral"
- **Benchmarks:**
  - NPS: 50+ = excellent, 30-50 = good, <30 = needs work
  - Viral coefficient: >1.0 = exponential growth (rare)
- **Common Mistakes:**
  - No formal referral tracking (just guessing)
  - Making referral hard (no shareable links)

**III. Focus on Your Stage (10 min)**
- **Pre-Revenue:** Focus on Acquisition and Activation
- **First Customers:** Focus on Activation and Retention
- **Product-Market Fit:** Focus on Retention and Revenue
- **Scaling:** Focus on Revenue and Referral
- **The Rule:** Improve your weakest metric first

**Assessment:**
**Metrics Dashboard Setup:**
"Using the AARRR Metrics Dashboard tool:
1. Define your activation event (what = "aha moment" for YOUR product?)
2. Set up tracking for your current stage's key metrics
3. Record baseline numbers for each metric
4. Identify your #1 metric to improve in the next 30 days"

### Lesson 5: Channel-Product-Audience Fit
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Channel Evaluation  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 65% Teaching, 25% Channel Scorecard, 10% Case Studies

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Understand why channel selection depends on product type and audience
2. Evaluate marketing channels using the 8-criteria scorecard
3. Identify 2-3 primary channels that fit your constraints
4. Avoid "shiny object syndrome" by focusing on channel fit, not trends

**Content Outline:**

**I. Why Most Channel Advice Fails (12 min)**
- **The Problem with Generic Advice:**
  - "You MUST be on TikTok!" (but your B2B SaaS buyers aren't there)
  - "SEO is dead!" (but it drives 70% of your competitor's traffic)
  - "Community is everything!" (but you have 0 hours for moderation)
- **The Channel-Product-Audience Fit Concept:**
  - Different products succeed on different channels
  - Different audiences congregate in different places
  - Your time/budget constraints eliminate some channels entirely
- **Real Example: Two SaaS Products, Different Channels:**
  - **Figma (B2B Design Tool):** Twitter + Product Hunt + design communities
  - **Mailchimp (Email Marketing):** SEO + content marketing + integrations
  - Same business model, completely different channel strategies

**II. The 8-Criteria Channel Scorecard (25 min)**

Evaluate every channel on these 8 dimensions (scale of 1-5):

**1. Audience Presence**
- **Question:** "Does my ICP actively use this channel?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: Developers on Hacker News
  - 1/5: Developers on Pinterest
- **How to Research:** Check LinkedIn profiles, ask 5 customers "Where do you find tools like ours?"

**2. Channel-Product Fit**
- **Question:** "Can I demonstrate my product's value on this channel?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: Design tools on Instagram (visual medium)
  - 2/5: Complex B2B software on Instagram (hard to explain visually)
- **Framework:** Text-heavy products → blogs/podcasts, Visual products → YouTube/Instagram, Interactive products → live demos/webinars

**3. Competition Level**
- **Question:** "How saturated is this channel in my niche?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: New subreddit with engaged audience, few vendors
  - 1/5: Google Ads for "project management software" (ultra-competitive)
- **How to Check:** Search for your top 3 competitors on the channel

**4. Time Investment**
- **Question:** "How many hours per week does this channel require?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5 (Low): SEO content (front-loaded, then compounds)
  - 2/5 (High): TikTok (requires daily posting)
- **Your Constraint:** Be honest about available hours

**5. Cash Investment**
- **Question:** "What's the minimum effective budget for this channel?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: Twitter, Reddit, Hacker News ($0)
  - 2/5: Google Ads ($500+/month minimum)
- **Your Constraint:** Bootstrap budgets favor $0-150/month channels

**6. Time to Results**
- **Question:** "How long until I see meaningful traction?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: Paid ads (results in days)
  - 3/5: SEO (results in 3-6 months)
- **Your Constraint:** If you need revenue NOW, prioritize faster channels

**7. Skill Match**
- **Question:** "Do I have the skills, or can I learn them quickly?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: Technical founders writing technical content
  - 2/5: Technical founders creating video content (learnable, but slower)
- **Be Honest:** Don't pick video if you hate being on camera

**8. Compounding Potential**
- **Question:** "Do results accumulate over time, or reset each day?"
- **Examples:**
  - 5/5: Blog posts (continue driving traffic for years)
  - 2/5: Social media posts (lifespan of 24 hours)
- **Bootstrap Advantage:** Focus on channels that build assets

**III. Channel Evaluation Matrix (12 min)**

**Channel Archetypes for Technical Founders:**

**High Fit Channels:**
- **Technical Content/SEO:** Blog posts, documentation, tutorials
  - Fits: Developer tools, B2B SaaS, technical products
  - Example: Supabase's blog drives 60% of signups
- **Community Participation:** Reddit, Hacker News, niche forums
  - Fits: Early-stage products seeking feedback
  - Example: Plausible Analytics launched on Hacker News
- **Twitter/X (Technical Community):** Building in public, sharing learnings
  - Fits: Solo founders with interesting build stories
  - Example: Pieter Levels (@levelsio) built $1M+ ARR audience

**Medium Fit Channels:**
- **LinkedIn:** B2B sales content, thought leadership
  - Fits: Enterprise B2B, professional services
  - Requires: Consistent posting, engagement with comments
- **Email Newsletters:** Owned audience, high engagement
  - Fits: Info products, courses, high-touch services
  - Requires: Lead magnet to build list
- **YouTube:** Video tutorials, product demos
  - Fits: Visual products, education-focused businesses
  - Requires: Video editing skills, consistent publishing

**Low Fit Channels (For Most Bootstrap Technical Founders):**
- **Paid Ads:** Google, Facebook, LinkedIn ads
  - Requires: $500+ monthly budget, conversion optimization skills
  - Better for: Scale phase, not early-stage
- **Instagram/TikTok:** Visual-first, personality-driven
  - Works for: Design tools, consumer products, some creator tools
  - Hard for: Complex B2B software, developer tools

**IV. Building Your Channel Portfolio (6 min)**
- **The 1-2-3 Rule:**
  - 1 PRIMARY channel (60% of marketing time)
  - 2 SECONDARY channels (30% of marketing time)
  - 3 EXPERIMENTAL channels (10% of marketing time, test new ideas)
- **Example Portfolio for B2B SaaS (Developer Tools):**
  - Primary: Technical blog + SEO
  - Secondary: Twitter, Hacker News participation
  - Experimental: YouTube tutorials, podcast guesting
- **Rebalance Quarterly:** Move experimental → secondary if working, drop if not

**Assessment:**
**Channel Scorecard Exercise:**
"Using the Marketing Channel Scorecard tool:
1. List 5-7 channels you're considering
2. Score each on the 8 criteria (1-5 scale)
3. Calculate total scores
4. Select your 1 primary, 2 secondary, and 3 experimental channels
5. Write 2-3 sentences justifying your primary channel choice based on product-audience-constraint fit"

### Lesson 6: The 80/20 of Bootstrap Marketing
**Duration:** 45 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Priority Matrix  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 70% Teaching, 20% Activity Prioritization, 10% Case Study

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Identify high-leverage marketing activities that drive 80% of results
2. Distinguish between urgent-seeming and actually important marketing work
3. Eliminate time-sink activities with low ROI
4. Build a weekly marketing routine focused on leverage, not busyness

**Content Outline:**

**I. The Pareto Principle in Marketing (10 min)**
- **The 80/20 Rule:** 80% of your marketing results come from 20% of your activities
- **Why This Matters for Bootstrappers:**
  - You have 10 hours/week max for marketing
  - You can't do everything, so you must choose high-leverage work
  - Busy ≠ productive (you can spend 40 hours on low-ROI tasks)
- **Real Example: Nathan Barry (ConvertKit)**
  - Initially tried: Ads, social media, guest posts, webinars, podcasts
  - 80% of revenue came from: 1 email course, direct sales outreach
  - Doubled down on those two, eliminated the rest

**II. High-Leverage Activities (15 min)**

**Tier 1: Compounding Assets (Build Once, Benefit Forever)**
- **SEO-Optimized Content:**
  - ROI: One blog post can drive traffic for 5+ years
  - Time Investment: 4-6 hours per comprehensive post
  - Example: "The Definitive Guide to [Your ICP's Top Pain Point]"
- **Email List Building:**
  - ROI: Owned audience you can reach anytime, no algorithm
  - Time Investment: 2 hours/week for newsletter
  - Example: Every new subscriber = potential customer for life
- **Case Studies & Testimonials:**
  - ROI: Social proof that sells for you 24/7
  - Time Investment: 3 hours per case study
  - Example: One compelling case study can close 10+ deals

**Tier 2: High-Touch, High-Conversion Activities**
- **Direct Outreach to Dream Customers:**
  - ROI: Personal connection, higher close rates than inbound
  - Time Investment: 5-10 hours/week
  - Example: 20 personalized LinkedIn messages > 1,000 generic cold emails
- **Strategic Partnerships:**
  - ROI: Access to partner's audience, mutual benefit
  - Time Investment: 10 hours to build one partnership
  - Example: Integration partnership with complementary tool
- **Founder-Led Content (Building in Public):**
  - ROI: Authenticity builds trust faster than polished marketing
  - Time Investment: 1 hour/day on Twitter or LinkedIn
  - Example: Sahil Lavingia (Gumroad) tweets progress, gets customers

**III. Low-Leverage Activities to Eliminate (12 min)**

**Time Sinks That Feel Productive:**
- **Obsessing Over Visual Brand:**
  - Reality: Spending 40 hours on logo design won't get you customers
  - Alternative: Use Canva template, spend 2 hours max, move on
- **Posting Generic Social Media Content:**
  - Reality: "Happy Friday!" posts get likes, not customers
  - Alternative: Share specific learnings or ask questions your ICP cares about
- **Attending Every Networking Event:**
  - Reality: 90% of events are low-ROI for your ICP
  - Alternative: Attend 1-2 strategically chosen events per quarter
- **Following Every Marketing Trend:**
  - Reality: Jumping on Clubhouse, BeReal, etc., distracts from core channels
  - Alternative: Master 1-2 channels before exploring new ones
- **Creating Content Without Distribution:**
  - Reality: "Write blog post → publish → hope for traffic" doesn't work
  - Alternative: Spend 50% time creating, 50% distributing (share on relevant communities)

**Delegation vs. Elimination:**
- **Can't Delegate Yet (No Budget):** Eliminate
- **High-Value, Hate Doing It:** Delegate later when revenue supports it
- **Low-Value, Enjoy Doing It:** Eliminate anyway (hobby ≠ business)

**IV. The Weekly Marketing Routine (8 min)**
- **Monday (2 hours): Strategic Planning**
  - Review last week's metrics
  - Prioritize this week's high-leverage tasks
- **Tuesday-Thursday (6 hours): Execution**
  - 3 hours: Content creation (blog post, newsletter, case study)
  - 2 hours: Outreach (LinkedIn, partnerships, community engagement)
  - 1 hour: Distribution (share content on Twitter, Reddit, Hacker News)
- **Friday (2 hours): Learning & Experimentation**
  - 1 hour: Analyze what worked this week
  - 1 hour: Test one new tactic (experimental channel)

### Lesson 7: Lead Magnets and Value Exchanges
**Duration:** 50 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Lead Magnet Design  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 70% Teaching, 20% Lead Magnet Creation, 10% Examples

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Understand the psychology of lead magnets and value exchanges
2. Design lead magnets matched to each funnel stage (TOFU, MOFU, BOFU)
3. Create your first lead magnet using proven templates
4. Set up lead magnet delivery and nurture sequences

**Content Outline:**

**I. The Lead Magnet Concept (12 min)**
- **Definition:** Something valuable you offer in exchange for contact information
- **The Value Exchange:**
  - Stranger gives: Email address (and permission to contact them)
  - You give: Helpful resource, tool, or information
  - Fair trade: Value should feel equal or tilted in their favor
- **Why Lead Magnets Work:**
  - Most visitors aren't ready to buy on first visit
  - Email gives you multiple touchpoints to build trust
  - Owned channel: You control the relationship, not an algorithm
- **The Math:**
  - 2% of website visitors buy immediately
  - 20% might be interested in a valuable free resource
  - Nurturing that 20% can convert 5-10% over 6 months
  - Result: 10x more customers than asking for sale on first visit

**II. Lead Magnet Types by Funnel Stage (18 min)**

**TOFU Lead Magnets (Awareness Stage)**
- **Goal:** Attract strangers who don't know your product exists yet
- **Types:**
  - **Guides & Ebooks:** "The Complete Guide to [ICP Pain Point]"
  - **Checklists:** "10-Point Checklist Before [ICP Activity]"
  - **Templates:** "Plug-and-Play [Resource Type] Template"
  - **Tools:** Free calculators, assessments, or generators
- **Example:** ConvertKit's "Email Marketing Field Guide"
  - 50 pages teaching email marketing
  - No product pitch until page 40
  - Builds trust, positions ConvertKit as expert
- **Success Criteria:** High perceived value, low effort to consume

**MOFU Lead Magnets (Consideration Stage)**
- **Goal:** Help prospects evaluate solutions, including yours
- **Types:**
  - **Case Studies:** "How [Customer] Achieved [Result] in [Timeframe]"
  - **Comparison Guides:** "Build vs. Buy for [Category]"
  - **Webinars:** "Live Workshop: How to [Achieve Outcome]"
  - **Free Trials:** 14-day trial with guided onboarding
- **Example:** Baremetrics' "SaaS Benchmarks Report"
  - Shows industry metrics (MRR, churn, LTV)
  - Subtly demonstrates Baremetrics' analytics capabilities
  - Positions product as the solution for tracking these metrics
- **Success Criteria:** Shows your expertise, hints at product value

**BOFU Lead Magnets (Decision Stage)**
- **Goal:** Remove final objections, make buying easy
- **Types:**
  - **ROI Calculators:** "See How Much You'll Save with [Product]"
  - **Implementation Guides:** "90-Day Rollout Plan for [Product]"
  - **Pilot Programs:** "30-Day Money-Back Guarantee"
  - **Consultations:** "Free 15-Minute Setup Call"
- **Example:** Proposify's "Proposal Template Library"
  - Gives away exact templates their software generates
  - Demonstrates value, but doing it manually is painful
  - Natural upgrade path: "Or let Proposify automate this"
- **Success Criteria:** Lowers perceived risk, shows immediate value

**III. Creating Your First Lead Magnet (15 min)**

**Step 1: Choose the Right Format**
- **Founder's Dilemma:** "Should I make a video, PDF, or tool?"
- **Decision Framework:**
  - Fastest to create: Checklist (2 hours)
  - Most valuable: Comprehensive guide (8-12 hours)
  - Highest conversion: Interactive tool (16-40 hours)
- **Bootstrap Recommendation:** Start with checklist or template, upgrade later

**Step 2: The Lead Magnet Formula**
- **Title Template:** "The [Adjective] Guide to [Outcome] Without [Pain Point]"
  - Example: "The Solo Founder's Guide to Product Launch Without a Marketing Team"
- **Content Structure:**
  - Introduction: Why this matters (1 page)
  - Framework: Your systematic approach (2-3 pages)
  - Implementation: Step-by-step process (3-5 pages)
  - Resources: Tools, templates, next steps (1 page)
- **Length:** 5-10 pages ideal (valuable but not overwhelming)

**Step 3: Delivery Mechanism**
- **Option A: PDF via Email**
  - Tool: ConvertKit, Mailchimp, or Beehiiv (free tiers)
  - Process: User enters email → automated email with download link
- **Option B: Dedicated Landing Page**
  - Tool: Carrd ($19/year), Notion (free), or Tally.so (free)
  - Process: User enters email → redirect to download page
- **Best Practice:** Send email WITH download link (builds email list and delivers instantly)

**Step 4: The Nurture Sequence**
- **Day 0:** Deliver lead magnet immediately
- **Day 1:** "Did you get a chance to read it?" + Quick tip
- **Day 3:** Share related case study or example
- **Day 7:** Introduce your product as the next logical step
- **Day 14:** Offer free trial or demo
- **Length:** 5-7 emails over 2 weeks (MOFU → BOFU journey)

**IV. Case Study: Transistor.fm's Lead Magnet Strategy (5 min)**
- **Lead Magnet:** "Marketing for Podcasters" guide
- **Funnel Stage:** TOFU (awareness)
- **Distribution:** Blog post SEO + social shares
- **Nurture Sequence:** 7 emails teaching podcast marketing
- **Conversion:** Email #6 introduces Transistor as the hosting solution
- **Result:** 15% of lead magnet downloaders became paying customers

**Assessment:**
**Lead Magnet Creation Exercise:**
"Using the Lead Magnet Builder tool:
1. Choose your target funnel stage (TOFU, MOFU, or BOFU)
2. Select a format (checklist, guide, template, tool)
3. Write your lead magnet title using the formula
4. Outline the 5-7 main sections
5. Plan your 5-email nurture sequence"

### Lesson 8: Marketing Stack by Budget Tier
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Teaching Mode:** Explanation + Tool Selection  
**70/20/10 Breakdown:** 70% Teaching, 20% Stack Assembly, 10% Tool Comparisons

**Learning Objectives:**
1. Understand the essential marketing tools vs. nice-to-haves
2. Assemble a marketing stack for $0, $50, or $150/month budgets
3. Avoid tool bloat by focusing on core functions
4. Set up integrations between tools for automation

**Content Outline:**

**I. The Bootstrap Marketing Stack Philosophy (10 min)**
- **The Tool Trap:**
  - Founders see competitors using 15 marketing tools
  - Assume they need the same tools to compete
  - Reality: Tools ≠ results; strategy + execution = results
- **Essential vs. Nice-to-Have:**
  - **Essential:** Tools that enable core marketing activities
  - **Nice-to-Have:** Tools that add 10% efficiency for 10x cost
- **The "Could I Do This Manually?" Test:**
  - If yes, do it manually until it's painful
  - Then buy the tool that eliminates the pain
  - Example: Manually send emails to 20 subscribers → works fine. At 200 subscribers → get email automation tool.

**II. The $0/Month Stack (Fundamentals Only) (15 min)**

**Core Functions:**
1. **Website Hosting:**
   - Tool: GitHub Pages (free) or Netlify (free)
   - Use Case: Simple landing page or blog
   - Limitation: No dynamic features, static HTML only

2. **Email Marketing:**
   - Tool: Mailchimp (free up to 500 subscribers) or Loops (free tier)
   - Use Case: Lead magnet delivery, basic newsletters
   - Limitation: 500 subscriber cap, limited automation

3. **Analytics:**
   - Tool: Google Analytics 4 (free) or Plausible (free for low traffic)
   - Use Case: Track traffic sources, page views, conversions
   - Limitation: Learning curve for GA4

4. **Social Media Management:**
   - Tool: Buffer (free for 3 accounts) or native platforms
   - Use Case: Schedule posts to Twitter, LinkedIn
   - Limitation: 10 scheduled posts at a time

5. **Content Creation:**
   - Tool: Canva (free tier) for graphics, Google Docs for writing
   - Use Case: Create social images, write blog posts
   - Limitation: Watermarks on some Canva templates

6. **Form Builder:**
   - Tool: Tally.so (free) or Google Forms
   - Use Case: Lead capture, surveys, waitlists
   - Limitation: Basic design options

**Total Monthly Cost:** $0  
**What You Can Do:** Publish content, capture leads, send newsletters, track traffic  
**What's Missing:** Advanced automation, A/B testing, CRM features

**III. The $50/Month Stack (Growing Efficiently) (15 min)**

**Upgrade Path:**
1. **Email Marketing → ConvertKit ($29/mo for 1,000 subscribers)**
   - Why Upgrade: Better automation, visual workflow builder, unlimited emails
   - Use Case: Multi-email nurture sequences, segmentation

2. **Website → Carrd ($19/year = ~$2/mo)**
   - Why Upgrade: Custom domain, better templates, multiple pages
   - Use Case: Professional landing pages for lead magnets

3. **Social Media → Hypefury ($15/mo)**
   - Why Upgrade: Automated threading, engagement tracking, post inspiration
   - Use Case: Twitter growth without manual scheduling

4. **Analytics → Keep Google Analytics (free)**
   - Why Not Upgrade: GA4 is powerful enough; invest elsewhere first

5. **Design → Canva Pro ($13/mo)**
   - Why Upgrade: No watermarks, premium templates, brand kit
   - Use Case: Consistent branding across all assets

**Total Monthly Cost:** ~$57/mo  
**What's Better:** Professional email automation, custom domain, better social scheduling  
**What's Still Missing:** CRM, advanced funnel analytics, paid ad tools

**IV. The $150/Month Stack (Scaling with Automation) (15 min)**

**Further Upgrades:**
1. **CRM → HubSpot Free CRM (still free!) or Attio ($29/mo)**
   - Why Upgrade: Track leads through full journey, see deal stages
   - Use Case: B2B SaaS with sales process

2. **Marketing Automation → ConvertKit Creator Pro ($59/mo for 3,000 subscribers)**
   - Why Upgrade: Advanced segmentation, referral tracking, subscriber scoring
   - Use Case: Complex nurture paths based on behavior

3. **SEO Tools → Ahrefs Lite ($99/mo) or Semrush ($129/mo for annual)**
   - Why Upgrade: Keyword research, backlink analysis, competitive intelligence
   - Use Case: SEO-driven content strategy
   - Alternative: AnswerThePublic (free) + Google Search Console (free) gets you 70% there

4. **Analytics → Keep Google Analytics (free) + Hotjar ($32/mo)**
   - Why Add Hotjar: Heatmaps, session recordings, on-site surveys
   - Use Case: See exactly where users drop off

5. **Social Listening → Optional: Mention ($41/mo for brand monitoring)**
   - Use Case: Track brand mentions, competitor activity

**Total Monthly Cost:** ~$150-180/mo  
**What's Better:** Full marketing funnel visibility, SEO competitive edge, behavioral insights  
**What's Still Missing:** Enterprise marketing suite features (but you don't need them yet)

**V. Tool Integration Strategy (10 min)**
- **The Zapier Layer (Optional):**
  - Connect tools together for automation
  - Example: New Tally form submission → Add to ConvertKit → Send Slack notification
  - Cost: Zapier free tier (100 tasks/month) or $20/mo (750 tasks/month)
- **Common Integrations:**
  - ConvertKit + Tally: Lead magnet signup → automated email sequence
  - Google Analytics + HubSpot: Track which content drives leads
  - Twitter + Buffer: Schedule threads, auto-retweet top performers
- **When NOT to Integrate:**
  - Don't connect tools just because you can
  - Only automate workflows you're already doing manually

**Assessment:**
**Stack Assembly Exercise:**

### Lesson 9: Measuring Marketing Without a Data Team

**Learning Objectives:**

**Content Outline:**

**I. The Metrics That Actually Matter (12 min)**

**Vanity Metrics vs. Actionable Metrics:**
- **Vanity Metric:** Total website visitors (feels good, but doesn't indicate business health)
- **Actionable Metric:** Conversion rate of visitors → leads (tells you if marketing is working)

**The 5 Core Marketing Metrics:**
1. **Traffic by Channel** (Where are people finding you?)
2. **Lead Conversion Rate** (What % of visitors become leads?)
3. **Cost Per Lead** (How much does it cost to acquire a lead?)
4. **Lead → Customer Conversion** (What % of leads become paying customers?)
5. **Marketing ROI** (Revenue generated ÷ Marketing spend)

**Why These 5:**
- Cover the full funnel (traffic → lead → customer → revenue)
- Reveal bottlenecks (if lead conversion is 1%, problem is in offer/landing page)
- Enable optimization (test changes, measure impact)

**Step 3: Build Custom Reports**

- **Conversion Funnel Report:**
  - Exploration → Funnel Exploration

**Step 4: Weekly Snapshot Routine**
- Every Monday: Export last week's data

**IV. Interpreting Metrics for Decisions (10 min)**

**Scenario 1: Low Traffic, High Conversion**
- **Metrics:** 100 visitors/week, 10% conversion rate = 10 leads/week
- **Diagnosis:** Your offer is strong, but you need more visibility
- **Action:** Double down on traffic-building (SEO, content, social)

**Scenario 2: High Traffic, Low Conversion**
- **Metrics:** 1,000 visitors/week, 1% conversion rate = 10 leads/week
- **Diagnosis:** Traffic is wrong fit, or offer is weak
- **Action:** Improve ICP targeting, test new lead magnets, simplify forms

**Scenario 3: Uneven Channel Performance**
- **Metrics:** Blog drives 5% conversion, Twitter drives 1% conversion

- **Action:** Shift time from Twitter to blog (double content output)

**Scenario 4: Declining Metrics**
- **Metrics:** Traffic down 20%, conversion rate stable
- **Diagnosis:** Content cadence slowed, or algorithm change
- **Action:** Return to consistent publishing schedule, check SEO rankings

  - Website visitors (Google Analytics)

  - Channel attribution (UTM parameters)
  - Revenue (Stripe)
- **Dashboard:** Simple Google Sheet updated weekly
- **Key Insight:** 80% of trials came from blog posts, not social media
- **Decision:** Stopped Twitter posting, wrote 2 blog posts/week instead
- **Result:** 3x trial signups in 6 months

4. Input last week's data (or dummy data if you don't have real data yet)
5. Identify your #1 metric to improve in the next 30 days and WHY"
