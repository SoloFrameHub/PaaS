# Your Creator Sales Manifesto

Over the past seven lessons, you have dismantled the false narrative that creators cannot sell. You have confronted imposter syndrome, learned to serve before you sell, reframed investment vs. expense, found your authentic selling voice, built a confidence system, and developed rejection resilience.

Now it is time to synthesize everything into a single document: **your Creator Sales Manifesto.**

This is not an abstract exercise. Your manifesto is a working document -- a set of personal rules, principles, and commitments that govern how you show up in every sales interaction. It is your constitution. And like any good constitution, it should be specific enough to guide real decisions and flexible enough to evolve with your growth.

---

## Why a Manifesto Matters

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Power of Written Intentions">Research by Peter Gollwitzer shows that people who write down specific plans ("If X, I will do Y") are 2-3x more likely to follow through than people who merely set goals.</InsightCard>

Research on **implementation intentions** by psychologist Peter Gollwitzer shows that people who write down specific plans ("If X happens, I will do Y") are **2-3x more likely to follow through** than people who merely set goals. A manifesto functions as a collection of implementation intentions for your sales life.

Without a manifesto, you will default to whatever emotional state you happen to be in. Feeling confident? You sell boldly. Feeling anxious? You discount and apologize. Feeling burned out? You ghost prospects and avoid calls. Your manifesto anchors you to a consistent standard that is independent of your mood.

A manifesto also serves as a **decision-making filter.** When you are unsure whether to pursue a lead, lower your price, use a certain tactic, or take on a particular client, you can check it against your manifesto. If the action aligns, proceed. If it does not, decline.

### Section 1: My Sales Identity Statement

This is a one-paragraph declaration of who you are as a creator who sells. It resolves the identity crisis from Lesson 1 and establishes your integrated self -- not "creator or seller" but "creator who sells."

**Template:**
*"I am a [your creative identity] who helps [your audience] achieve [specific outcome]. Selling is not separate from my creative work -- it is the delivery mechanism that connects my expertise to the people who need it. I sell because I believe in the value of what I offer, and I owe it to my audience to make it available."*

**Example:**
*"I am a brand strategist who helps solo founders build positioning that attracts premium clients. When I sell my consulting packages, I am not being pushy -- I am offering a shortcut past the confusion and wasted money that I spent years navigating myself. My sales conversations are an extension of my teaching, and I approach them with the same care and rigor."*

### Section 2: My Non-Negotiable Principles

These are the 5-7 rules you will never break, regardless of circumstances. They define the boundaries of your selling behavior.

**Examples:**

1. **I will never use manufactured scarcity.** If I say "only 5 spots left," there are actually only 5 spots left.
2. **I will never sell to someone I cannot help.** If a prospect is not a good fit, I will tell them honestly, even if I need the revenue.
3. **I will never apologize for my pricing.** My prices reflect the value I deliver and the expertise I have earned.
4. **I will always follow up.** Not following up because I am afraid of being annoying is a disservice to people who need my help.
5. **I will always give value before asking for anything.** Every interaction with a prospect should leave them better informed, even if they never buy.
6. **I will never trash-talk competitors.** If someone else is a better fit, I will refer the prospect to them gracefully.
7. **I will rest before I sell.** I will not take sales calls when I am exhausted, resentful, or desperate.

### Section 3: My Sales Process Standards

This section defines how you will conduct your sales process. It turns the frameworks from Lessons 3-5 into personal commitments.

**Prompts:**

- **Discovery Calls:** "I will spend the first [X minutes] listening and diagnosing before I ever mention my offer. My target talk ratio is [X%] me, [X%] them."
- **Pricing Conversations:** "When I state my price, I will pause and let the prospect respond. I will not preemptively discount."
- **Follow-Up Cadence:** "After a call with no decision, I will follow up [X] times over [X] days/weeks. My follow-up messages will add value, not just ask 'Have you decided?'"
- **Qualification:** "Before accepting a client, I will verify that they meet these criteria: [list your ideal client criteria]."
- **Rejection Protocol:** "When I receive a 'no,' I will process it through the Five-Stage system from Lesson 7 within 24 hours."

### Section 4: My Confidence Commitments

This section codifies the rituals and practices from Lesson 6 that you commit to maintaining.

**Examples:**

- "I will maintain my Evidence File and add to it after every successful client engagement."
- "I will run my 15-minute Pre-Call Ritual before every sales conversation."
- "I will complete a Post-Call Debrief after every sales conversation."
- "I will review my Results Portfolio on the first day of every month."
- "I will not check competitor social media on days when I have sales calls."
- "I will practice saying my price out loud once per week until it requires zero emotional effort."

### Section 5: My Growth Edges

This is the section that makes your manifesto a living document. Here, you name the specific areas where you are still growing and the actions you are taking to improve.

**Template:**
*"I acknowledge that I am still developing in the area of [specific skill]. My current plan to improve is [specific action]. I will revisit this section every [timeframe] to update my progress."*

**Examples:**

- "I acknowledge that I still struggle with follow-up. My current plan is to implement a CRM and commit to a 3-touch follow-up sequence for every qualified prospect."
- "I acknowledge that I undervalue my time in scope negotiations. My plan is to define clear deliverables for each package and practice saying 'That would be outside the scope of this engagement, but I can offer it as an add-on.'"
- "I acknowledge that I still compare myself to competitors before calls. My plan is to implement a 24-hour social media blackout before any sales day."

## The Manifesto Drafting Process

Set aside 60-90 minutes in a quiet place. You will need your notes from all previous lessons.

### Step 1: Review Your Course Work (15 minutes)

Pull up your notes from Lessons 1-7. Review:

### Step 2: Write Each Section (40-60 minutes)

Write freely. Do not edit as you go. Get the ideas down in each of the five sections. Your manifesto will be imperfect on the first pass -- that is expected and fine.

### Step 3: Distill and Sharpen (15 minutes)

Go back through each section and cut anything vague, aspirational, or generic. Every sentence should be specific enough that you could look at it on a Tuesday morning before a sales call and know exactly what to do.

### Step 4: Format and Place (5 minutes)

Format your manifesto as a clean, readable document. Print it and place it near your desk. Save a digital copy on your phone. This document should be accessible within 30 seconds any time you need it.

## Maintaining Your Manifesto

Your manifesto is a living document. Schedule a review every 90 days.

During each review, ask:
- Which principles have I upheld consistently? (Celebrate this.)
- Which principles have I violated? (Diagnose why and recommit or revise.)
- Which growth edges have I improved on? (Update the section.)
- What new insights from recent sales experiences should be added?

Over time, your manifesto will become more refined, more specific, and more authentically yours. It will evolve from something you wrote in a course to something you live by instinct.

## The Bigger Picture

You started this course believing that sales was something that happened *to* you -- an uncomfortable obligation forced upon you by the market. You are finishing this course with a different understanding: sales is something you *design* -- a system of principles, practices, and commitments that you build on your own terms.

You are not pretending to be a salesperson. You are a creator who has decided, deliberately and thoughtfully, to ensure that the work you create reaches the people it can help. Your manifesto is the proof of that decision.

Now go sell something. Your way.
