# Training Sales Help on Your Voice

The single biggest fear creators have about bringing on sales help is this: "They will not sound like me. They will not care the way I care. They will turn my authentic brand into a used-car sales pitch."

This fear is valid. And when it comes true -- which it does, frequently -- it is almost always because the creator failed to train their closer on the one thing that matters most: their voice.

This lesson teaches you how to transfer your unique selling identity to another person without losing what makes your brand trustworthy.

---

## Why Voice Transfer Matters

Your prospects are not buying a product. They are buying a person -- or more precisely, they are buying the transformation that a specific person has promised them through months or years of content. Your Instagram posts, your podcast, your emails, your YouTube videos -- all of it builds an expectation of who they will be talking to when they get on a sales call.

When a closer picks up that call and sounds nothing like the creator they have been following, the disconnect is immediate. It is like calling your favorite local restaurant and getting a corporate call center. Technically the function is the same, but the experience is wrong.

<InsightCard icon="🎭" title="Voice Is Character">
Your voice is not your exact vocabulary. It is your character. And character can be shared.
</InsightCard>

**Voice** in this context is not just tone of voice. It includes:

- **Language patterns**: The specific words, phrases, and metaphors you use
- **Values**: What you stand for and against, and how explicitly you communicate those positions
- **Pace**: How quickly or slowly you move through a conversation
- **Empathy style**: How you acknowledge pain points -- clinical, emotional, direct, gentle
- **Boundaries**: What you are willing and unwilling to say to make a sale

A closer can learn your process in a week. Learning your voice takes deliberate, structured effort.

## Building Your Training Library

Before you can teach someone your voice, you need to document it. Most creators have never done this explicitly because it has always just been "how they talk." Here is how to make it tangible.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Step 1: Record Your Best 15 Calls">

Go through your recent sales call recordings and identify the 15 best examples. These should represent:

- **5 calls that resulted in a yes**: Showcasing your full process from discovery to close
- **5 calls that resulted in a no but felt right**: Demonstrating how you handle rejection without pressure or desperation
- **5 calls with strong objection handling**: Showing how you address price, timing, spouse approval, and other common hesitations

If you have not been recording your calls, start immediately. You need at minimum 4-6 weeks of recordings before you can build a meaningful training library.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 2: Annotate the Key Moments">

For each call, create timestamps and notes for:

- **The opening** (first 2-3 minutes): How do you build rapport? What questions do you open with?
- **The pivot to pain**: The moment you shift from small talk to diagnosing the real problem
- **Your signature framework explanation**: How you present your methodology or approach
- **Objection handling**: The exact language you use when someone says "I need to think about it" or "It is too expensive"
- **The close**: How you ask for the commitment, including your exact phrasing

</Slide>
<Slide title="Step 3: Extract Your Voice Guide">

From these annotated calls, distill a **Voice Guide** -- a document that captures your selling personality. It should include:

**Phrases you always use:**
> "Here is what I hear you saying..."
> "The real question is not whether this works, but whether you are ready to do the work."
> "I want to be honest with you about something..."

**Phrases you never use:**
> "This is a no-brainer" (too salesy)
> "You would be crazy not to do this" (pressure tactic)
> "Limited spots available" (false urgency, unless truly limited)

**Your objection language:**
> When they say "I need to think about it," you say: "I completely respect that. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Sometimes I can address the concern right now, and sometimes thinking about it really is the right move."

**Your values in action:**
> "We never tell someone the program is right for them if we genuinely believe it is not. Disqualifying a bad-fit prospect is a win, not a loss."

This Voice Guide becomes the foundational training document for every closer you hire.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Shadow-Then-Lead Approach

Reading a Voice Guide is not enough. Voice is absorbed through immersion, not instruction. The Shadow-Then-Lead approach is a structured method for transferring your voice through progressive exposure.

### Phase 1: Deep Listening (Week 1)

The closer listens to all 15 annotated calls with the Voice Guide open beside them. After each call, they write a brief analysis:

- What did the creator do in the first 3 minutes that I would not have done?
- How did the creator's language differ from my default sales language?
- What was the emotional arc of this call?

This is not passive listening. It is active study, like a musician learning a new piece by ear before touching the instrument.

### Phase 2: Live Shadowing (Week 2)

The closer sits in on your live calls. They are muted but present. After each call, you debrief together for 15-20 minutes. The two critical questions:

- "What would you have done differently at [specific moment]?"
- "Why do you think I chose to say [specific phrase] instead of [their alternative]?"

This is where the closer begins to understand the *why* behind your voice, not just the *what*.

### Phase 3: Mirrored Calls (Week 3)

The closer takes the lead on calls while you listen. The rule: they should attempt to run the call the way you would, not the way they naturally would. After each call, compare:

- Where did they match your voice? Where did they drift?
- Were there moments where their natural style was actually better? (This happens, and you should acknowledge it.)
- What specific phrases or transitions need work?

### Phase 4: Calibrated Independence (Week 4+)

The closer runs calls independently. You listen to recordings and provide feedback using a simple scoring rubric:

| Dimension | 1-5 Score | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Opening rapport | | |
| Discovery depth | | |
| Voice consistency | | |
| Objection handling | | |
| Close confidence | | |
| Overall brand alignment | | |

Review 2-3 calls per week initially, then 1-2 per week as they stabilize. The goal is not that they become your clone. The goal is that a prospect who has followed your content feels a consistent experience when they get on the call.

## Maintaining Brand Authenticity

Even after training, brand drift happens. Closers bring their own habits, and under pressure, they revert to whatever sales training they learned before you. Here are four systems to prevent drift:

### 1. The Weekly Call Review

Pick one call per week at random and review it together. Not just the outcome, but the experience. Ask: "If this prospect had been following me for six months, would this call have felt consistent with what they expected?"

### 2. The "Would I Say This?" Test

Give your closer a simple filter: before saying anything on a call, ask "Would [Creator Name] say this?" If the answer is no, do not say it. This sounds simplistic, but it is remarkably effective at catching language drift.

### 3. Prospect Feedback Loops

After every call (regardless of outcome), send the prospect a one-question survey: "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate your experience on today's call?" Track this metric over time. A declining score signals voice drift before it shows up in close rates.

### 4. Quarterly Script Refreshes

Your voice evolves. Your offer evolves. Your audience evolves. Every quarter, update the Voice Guide, record 2-3 new example calls, and do a calibration session with your closer. This keeps the training alive rather than letting it calcify around a version of you from six months ago.

<ExampleCard label="Case Study: The Drift That Cost $40K">

A fitness creator hired a closer who had previously worked in high-ticket coaching sales. The closer was excellent at closing — 45% close rate in month one. But by month three, refund requests spiked to 28% (up from the creator's historical 8%).

The issue? The closer had drifted into pressure tactics: "There are only two spots left" (false), "This price goes up next week" (not true), "I can't guarantee you'll get another chance" (manipulative).

Prospects bought, but they bought under pressure — not genuine conviction. The creator lost $40K in refunds and another $60K in referrals that never came because the customer experience felt misaligned.

The fix: A two-week reset. The closer re-listened to the original 15 calls, the creator sat in on every call for a week, and they implemented the "Would I Say This?" test. Close rate dropped to 38%, but refunds returned to 9% and referrals increased 3x.

## The Permission to Evolve

Here is a truth that experienced creators discover after a few months of this process: your closer will sometimes be better than you at certain parts of the sales conversation. Maybe they are more patient with hesitant prospects. Maybe they ask better discovery questions because they approach the call without the curse of expertise.

<FlipCard front="The Goal of Voice Training" back="Not to create a robot that mimics you, but to create a team member who sells with the same values, empathy, and integrity — even if the specific words differ." />

Let them contribute. The goal is not to create a robot that mimics you. The goal is to create a team member who sells with the same values, empathy, and integrity that you do -- even if the specific words differ. Your voice is not your exact vocabulary. It is your character. And character can be shared.

## Lesson Summary

- Voice transfer includes language patterns, values, pace, empathy style, and boundaries -- not just tone
- Build a training library of 15 annotated calls (5 wins, 5 good losses, 5 strong objection handling)
- Create a Voice Guide documenting phrases you use, phrases you avoid, and your values in action
- Use the Shadow-Then-Lead approach: deep listening, live shadowing, mirrored calls, then calibrated independence
- Prevent brand drift with weekly call reviews, the "Would I Say This?" test, prospect feedback, and quarterly refreshes
- Allow your closer to contribute their strengths -- the goal is shared character, not cloning
