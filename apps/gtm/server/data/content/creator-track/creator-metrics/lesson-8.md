# Your Metrics Playbook

You now have the frameworks, formulas, and benchmarks for every metric that matters in a creator business. But knowledge without a system is just trivia. This final lesson is about building the operational ritual that turns metrics tracking from something you "should do" into something you actually do -- every week, without fail.

This is your Metrics Playbook: the weekly review system, the decision frameworks, and the guardrails that keep you focused on what matters.

---

## The 5-Metric Friday Protocol

Every Friday afternoon (or whatever day works for your schedule), you spend 30-45 minutes reviewing five numbers. Not fifteen. Not thirty. Five. Constraint creates clarity.

### How to Choose Your 5 Metrics

Your 5 metrics should cover three categories:

**Input Metrics (2 metrics):** These measure the activities that drive your business. They are leading indicators -- they predict future revenue.
- Examples: daily opt-ins, content published, calls booked, applications received, ad spend

**Process Metrics (2 metrics):** These measure how efficiently your system converts inputs to outputs.
- Examples: show-up rate, close rate, email sequence conversion rate, application completion rate, cart page conversion rate

**Output Metric (1 metric):** This is the revenue or revenue-adjacent number that tells you whether your business is working.
- Examples: weekly revenue, sales per day, revenue per subscriber, LTV:CAC ratio

### Example 5-Metric Friday Dashboards

**For a coach selling $5,000 packages via application funnel:**
1. Applications received this week (Input)
2. Discovery calls booked this week (Input)
3. Call show-up rate (Process)
4. Qualified close rate (Process)
5. Weekly revenue (Output)

**For a course creator running an evergreen funnel:**
1. Daily opt-ins (average this week) (Input)
2. Automated webinar views this week (Input)
3. Webinar-to-sales-page click rate (Process)
4. Sales page conversion rate (Process)
5. Sales per day (Output)

**For a community builder with a membership:**
1. New trial sign-ups this week (Input)
2. Content pieces published this week (Input)
3. Trial-to-paid conversion rate (Process)
4. Monthly churn rate (Process)
5. Monthly Recurring Revenue (Output)

**For a hybrid creator (courses + coaching + community):**
1. New email subscribers this week (Input)
2. Sales conversations (calls + DMs) this week (Input)
3. Email sequence conversion rate (Process)
4. Blended close rate across all offers (Process)
5. Revenue per subscriber -- trailing 90 days (Output)

## The Friday Review Ritual

Here is the exact process. It takes 30-45 minutes once you have it set up:

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Step 1: Record the Numbers (5 min)">

Open your dashboard (spreadsheet, Notion, or whatever tool you use). Enter this week's numbers for all 5 metrics. Do not analyze yet -- just record.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 2: Compare to Last Week (5 min)">

For each metric, note whether it went up, down, or stayed flat compared to last week. Use simple arrows or color coding:
- Green/up arrow: Improved
- Yellow/flat: No meaningful change
- Red/down arrow: Declined

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 3: Compare to 4-Week Average (5 min)">

A single week is noisy data. The 4-week rolling average smooths out variance. Compare each metric to its 4-week average:
- Above average: Something is working. Note what you did differently.
- At average: Steady state. No action needed unless you want to improve.
- Below average: Potential problem. Dig deeper.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 4: Identify the Constraint (10 min)">

Look at the flow of your 5 metrics. Find the bottleneck -- the one metric that, if improved, would have the biggest impact on your output metric.

**Example:** You had 20 applications, 15 calls booked, 12 calls attended (80% show rate), but only 1 close (8.3% close rate). Your bottleneck is close rate. Fixing show-up rate (already at 80%) would only add 1-2 more calls. Fixing close rate from 8% to 20% would triple your revenue.

This is the Theory of Constraints applied to your creator business: **find the bottleneck and fix that before touching anything else.**

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 5: Set One Action (10 min)">

Based on the constraint you identified, choose ONE action for next week. Not three. Not five. One.

Examples of focused actions:
- "I will send a pre-call video to all booked prospects to improve close rate"
- "I will add SMS reminders to improve show-up rate"
- "I will test a new lead magnet to improve daily opt-ins"
- "I will send a dedicated pitch email to my engaged segment to improve sales per day"

Write it down. Put it in your calendar. Next Friday, you will measure whether it worked.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Setting Baselines and Targets

You cannot improve what you have not baselined. Before you can set meaningful targets, you need 4-8 weeks of data for each of your 5 metrics.

### The Baselining Process

**Weeks 1-4:** Track your 5 metrics every Friday. Do not try to optimize anything. Just record the numbers. This is your baseline period.

**Week 5:** Calculate your 4-week average for each metric. This is your baseline.

**Week 6 onward:** Set targets.

### How to Set Realistic Targets

Use the **10% Rule:** Your 90-day target for any metric should be a 10% improvement over your baseline. Not 50%. Not 100%. Ten percent.

<InsightCard icon="📊" title="The Power of 10% Compounding">
Compounding 10% improvements per quarter gives you 46% improvement per year. Small wins build momentum and confidence. Overshooting targets is demoralizing and leads to abandoning the tracking habit.
</InsightCard>

Why only 10%? Because:
1. It is achievable without heroic effort
2. Compounding 10% improvements per quarter gives you 46% improvement per year
3. Small wins build momentum and confidence
4. Overshooting targets is demoralizing and leads to abandoning the tracking habit

**Example:**
- Baseline close rate: 18%
- 90-day target: 19.8% (round to 20%)
- Year-end target if you hit each quarter: 18% -> 20% -> 22% -> 24% -> 26%

Going from 18% to 26% close rate in a year is transformative. On 20 calls per month with a $5,000 offer, that is the difference between $18,000/month and $26,000/month -- an additional $96,000 per year.

## When to Optimize vs When to Pivot

Metrics tell you when something is not working. But they do not automatically tell you whether to fix the current approach or try something entirely different. Here is the framework:

### Optimize When:
- Your metrics are in the "below average but functional" range (close to benchmarks but not hitting them)
- You have identified a specific, fixable bottleneck
- Your audience and offer are aligned (people want what you sell, they just are not buying efficiently)
- Small changes (new email subject lines, better call scripts, faster follow-up) could meaningfully improve the numbers
- You have been running the current system for fewer than 6 months

### Pivot When:
- Your metrics are dramatically below benchmarks and have not improved despite 8+ weeks of optimization
- Multiple metrics are failing simultaneously (this suggests a systemic problem, not a tactical one)
- Your audience does not match your offer (you are attracting the wrong people)
- The market has shifted (your offer was viable 12 months ago but is no longer relevant)
- You have tested 3+ different optimizations on the same metric and none moved the needle

### The 8-Week Rule
Give any strategic change 8 weeks before judging it. Four weeks to implement and stabilize, four weeks to measure results. Judging before 8 weeks is reacting to noise. Waiting longer than 12 weeks to judge is denial.

## The Metrics That Signal It Is Time to Scale

How do you know when to stop optimizing and start scaling? When these conditions are met:

1. **LTV:CAC is above 3:1** -- You can afford to spend more on customer acquisition
2. **Close rate is at or above benchmark** -- Your sales process works
3. **Your system runs without you for 2+ weeks** -- You have SOPs and/or team members handling the daily operations
4. **You have 4+ months of consistent revenue** -- Not just one good month, but a trend
5. **You know your constraint** -- And it is a traffic/volume constraint, not a conversion constraint

## Building Your Metrics Stack

Here are the tools that make tracking feasible for a solo creator:

### Free/Low-Cost Options
- **Google Sheets:** Best for the 5-Metric Friday dashboard. Simple, customizable, shareable.
- **Notion:** Good for combining metrics tracking with project management and content planning.
- **Stripe Dashboard:** Tracks revenue, transactions, and basic customer data automatically.
- **Your email platform's analytics:** ConvertKit, Mailchimp, or ActiveCampaign all provide subscriber and engagement metrics.

### Worth Paying For
- **Deadline Funnel** ($49-99/month): If you run evergreen funnels, this tracks per-person deadlines and conversion timing.
- **Calendly or SavvyCal** ($12-20/month): Tracks booking rates, no-show rates, and call volumes.
- **Hyros or Wicked Reports** ($100+/month): Multi-touch attribution for creators spending $5K+/month on ads.

### Do Not Overcomplicate It
The best metrics system is the one you actually use. A Google Sheet that you update every Friday is infinitely more valuable than a $500/month analytics platform that you log into once and forget.
