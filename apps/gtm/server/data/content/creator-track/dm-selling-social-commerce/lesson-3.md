# Messenger Selling Scripts

Automation gets people into your inbox. Scripts get them to buy. But "scripts" in the context of DM selling do not mean robotic, copy-paste messages that sound like a telemarketer. They mean structured conversation frameworks -- flexible sequences that guide a conversation toward a buying decision while keeping every exchange feeling human and responsive.

This lesson gives you the complete 5-message DM sales framework, teaches you how to qualify through conversation, and shows you when and how to transition from text to a call or direct purchase.

---

## The 5-Message DM Sales Framework

Every successful DM sales conversation follows the same underlying structure, whether it takes 5 messages or 50. The framework has five stages:

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Message 1: Deliver and Open">

This is your first DM -- often triggered by automation but sometimes sent manually. Its job is to **deliver the promised value** and **open a conversation loop.**

**Template:**
> "Hey [Name]! Here is the [resource] I mentioned. [Link or description]. I am curious -- what made you comment? Is [topic] something you are actively working on right now?"

**Why it works:** You lead with value (the resource), then ask a question that invites them to reveal their situation. You are not pitching. You are showing interest.

**Common mistake:** Sending only the link with no question. If you do not open a conversational loop, the person downloads the resource and disappears.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Message 2: Acknowledge and Diagnose">

They replied. Now your job is to **validate their situation** and **dig one level deeper.**

**Template:**
> "That totally makes sense. So you are [restate their situation]. How long have you been dealing with that? And have you tried anything specific to solve it?"

**Why it works:** Restating their problem shows you actually read their message (most people just pitch). The follow-up question helps you understand their urgency and what they have already attempted.

**Key principle:** The person who asks the questions controls the conversation. In DMs, this is even more true because the format naturally rewards short, responsive exchanges.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Message 3: Share Insight and Qualify">

Based on what they have told you, **share a specific insight** that demonstrates your expertise, and **qualify them** for your offer.

**Template:**
> "Based on what you shared, the issue is probably [specific diagnosis]. Most people in your situation try [common mistake], but what actually works is [your approach]. This is actually the core of what I teach in [your offer]. Would it be helpful if I shared how that works?"

**Why it works:** You are demonstrating competence (you can diagnose their problem) and creating curiosity (you have a specific approach). The closing question asks for permission to pitch -- which means when you do pitch, they have already said yes.

**Qualification check:** At this point, assess three things mentally:
1. **Need:** Do they have a real problem your offer solves?
2. **Urgency:** Are they actively trying to solve it, or just casually curious?
3. **Capacity:** Can they realistically invest in a solution? (You do not ask this directly -- you infer it from their language and context.)

If any of these are missing, you can still be helpful, but adjust your approach. Not everyone who enters your DMs is a buyer, and that is fine.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Message 4: Present the Offer">

They said yes to hearing more. Now you **present your offer clearly and concisely.**

**Template:**
> "So here is the quick version. [Offer name] is a [format: course/program/coaching package] where I help [target audience] achieve [specific outcome]. It is [duration] and includes [2-3 key components]. The investment is [price]. Based on what you told me about [their specific situation], I think [specific component] would be especially relevant for you. Want me to send you the details, or would it be easier to hop on a quick 15-minute call?"

**Why it works:** You connect the offer back to their specific situation (personalization), state the price transparently (no bait-and-switch), and give them two easy next steps (details or call).

**Common mistake:** Being vague about the price. If you hide the price, you create anxiety. If someone cannot afford it, it is better to know now than after a 30-minute call.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Message 5: Handle Response or Follow Up">

Three possible responses, three possible scripts:

**If they say yes:**
> "Great, let me send you the link right now. [Link]. If you have any questions during the checkout process, just message me here."

**If they say they need to think about it:**
> "Totally understand. Take your time. One thing that might help -- [specific detail or bonus that addresses their likely hesitation]. I will check in with you in a couple of days. Does that work?"

**If they say no or go silent:**
> "No worries at all. [Resource] should still help you with [their specific situation]. If anything changes or you have questions later, I am always here."

Never beg. Never pressure. The person who is gracious in a "no" gets remembered -- and often gets a referral or a future sale.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Qualification Checkpoint">

## Voice Message Techniques

Text DMs are effective, but voice messages are a secret weapon that most creators underutilize.

### Why Voice Messages Convert Higher

- **They are impossible to fake.** A voice message proves a real human is on the other end.
- **They carry emotion.** Tone, warmth, enthusiasm -- all of these come through in audio but get lost in text.
- **They are harder to ignore.** A voice message bubble in someone's inbox creates curiosity and a sense of personal attention.
- **They take less time to create** than typing a thoughtful paragraph.

### When to Send Voice Messages

- **Message 1:** After the automated opener, follow up with a brief voice message: "Hey [Name], just wanted to say hi properly! I saw your comment and wanted to make sure you got the resource. Let me know if you have any questions."
- **Message 3 (The Insight):** Share your diagnosis and insight as a 30-60 second voice note. It feels more like advice from a friend than a sales pitch.
- **Message 5 (The Follow-Up):** If they went silent, a short voice message ("Hey, just checking in -- no pressure, just wanted to make sure you got what you needed") has a much higher response rate than text.

### Voice Message Best Practices

- Keep them under 60 seconds. Anything longer feels like a monologue.
- Start with their name. "Hey Sarah" immediately signals personalization.
- Smile while you record. It genuinely changes the tone of your voice.
- Do not re-record endlessly. A slightly imperfect voice message feels more authentic than a polished one.

## Transitioning from Chat to Call or Purchase

The DM conversation is a bridge, not a destination. At some point, you need to move the prospect to either a purchase link or a call booking. Here is how to handle each transition.

### Transition to a Call

Use this when your offer is high-ticket ($500+) or complex:

> "I think a quick call would be the best way to figure out if this is the right fit. I have a few slots open this week -- would [Day] at [Time] work? Here is my calendar link if you want to grab a time: [Link]"

**Tips:** Offer a specific time (it increases commitment). Keep the call short -- frame it as 15 minutes, not a "strategy session" (which sounds like a sales pitch). If they resist a call, respect it and offer an alternative: "No problem. Let me send you a short video that explains everything, and you can decide from there."

### Transition to a Direct Purchase

Use this when your offer is lower-ticket (under $500) and straightforward:

> "Here is the link to grab [offer]: [Link]. It is a simple checkout -- takes about 30 seconds. And if you have any issues, just message me right here."

**Tips:** Remove all friction. One link, one click, one purchase. Do not send them to a long sales page from the DM -- they have already been sold through the conversation. A direct checkout link is all they need.

## Scripts by Conversation Type

### The "Just Curious" Person

They commented your keyword but are not sure what they want. Use extra questions in the Diagnose stage to help them clarify their own situation. Often, they become the most loyal buyers because you helped them understand their own problem.

### The "I Have Been Following You for Months" Person

This person has high trust but has been sitting on the fence. They need a slight push. Share a relevant testimonial or case study, and make the next step very easy: "Honestly, you already know the content works. Want me to save you a spot?"

### The "How Much Does It Cost?" Person

They want to skip to the price. Do not dodge the question. Answer it directly, then add context: "It is $497. That includes [component 1], [component 2], and [component 3]. Most of my clients see [specific result] within the first [timeframe]."

### The "I Cannot Afford It" Person

Respect this honestly. If you have a payment plan, mention it. If you have a lower-priced alternative, offer it. If neither applies: "I completely understand. The [free resource] I sent you is a great starting point. When the timing is right, I am here."

## Exercise: Build Your DM Playbook

Create a document (Google Doc, Notion page, or note) with these five sections:

1. **Your Message 1 template** -- customized for your offer and audience
2. **Three diagnostic questions** you can ask in Message 2 to understand someone's situation
3. **Your Message 3 insight** -- what is the key diagnosis or counterintuitive insight you can share that demonstrates expertise?
4. **Your Message 4 offer summary** -- written in 4-5 sentences maximum
5. **Your three response scripts** for yes, maybe, and no

Print this (or pin it in your notes app) and keep it open while you are responding to DMs. It is not about reading the script word-for-word. It is about knowing the structure so you never freeze when a real conversation starts.
