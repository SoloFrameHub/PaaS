# LinkedIn Message Selling

LinkedIn is the only major social platform where sending someone a message about business is not only acceptable -- it is expected. This makes LinkedIn the most powerful DM selling channel for creators, coaches, and consultants who serve a B2B audience or professionals. But there is a paradox: because business messaging is expected on LinkedIn, inboxes are flooded with terrible pitches. The bar for bad outreach is low. The bar for good outreach is high.

This lesson teaches you the connection-to-conversation framework, LinkedIn-specific selling etiquette, and the tactics that separate effective LinkedIn DMs from the spam that everyone deletes.

LinkedIn has a culture that is fundamentally different from Instagram or Facebook. Understanding this culture is not optional -- it is the foundation of everything else.

### Rule 1: Earn the Right to Pitch

On LinkedIn, the connection request itself is a micro-relationship. When someone accepts your connection, they are saying, "I am willing to see your content in my feed." They are not saying, "Please sell me something." If your very first message after connecting is a pitch, you have violated an unspoken social contract. The result: you get blocked, reported, or silently ignored.

### Rule 2: Lead with Value Before You Ask for Anything

The most successful LinkedIn sellers share freely. They post insights, comment thoughtfully on others' content, and provide genuine value in conversations. By the time they make an offer, the recipient already trusts them.

### Rule 3: Reference Context, Not Templates

A generic "Hi [Name], I noticed we have similar interests and I would love to connect" is obviously a template. A specific "Hi [Name], your post about pricing strategy for consultants really resonated -- especially the part about anchoring. I have been testing something similar with my clients" shows that you actually paid attention. The difference in response rate is dramatic: personalized messages get 3-4x higher response rates than templates.

### Rule 4: Respect the Professional Tone

LinkedIn is not Instagram. Excessive emojis, slang, and overly casual language feels out of place. You do not need to be formal, but you should be professional. Write like you are having a conversation with a respected colleague, not texting a friend.

## The Connection-to-Conversation Framework

This is a 4-step process for turning a new LinkedIn connection into a meaningful business conversation.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Step 1: Strategic Connection Request">

Do not mass-connect with everyone in your industry. Be intentional about who you connect with.

**Who to connect with:**
- People who engage with content in your niche (commenters on relevant posts)
- Members of LinkedIn groups related to your expertise
- People who viewed your profile (LinkedIn shows you this in the "Who Viewed Your Profile" section)
- Second-degree connections who work at companies in your target market

**The Connection Note (200 characters max):**
> "Hi [Name] -- enjoyed your recent post on [topic]. I write a lot about [your expertise] and thought it would be great to connect."

This is not a pitch. It is a genuine reason to connect. Keep it under the 200-character limit and reference something specific.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 2: The Welcome Message">

Wait 24-48 hours after they accept, then send a brief, warm message. Do not pitch.

**Template:**
> "Thanks for connecting, [Name]. I have been following your work on [specific topic] -- really good stuff. I am curious, what is the biggest focus for you this quarter?"

**Why it works:** You compliment something specific (not generic flattery), then ask an open-ended question that invites them to share their priorities. Their answer tells you whether and how you might be able to help.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 3: The Value Exchange">

Based on their response, share something genuinely useful. This could be a resource, an insight, an introduction, or a piece of content that is relevant to what they told you.

**Template:**
> "You mentioned you are focused on [their priority]. I actually put together a [resource/framework] on that topic recently. Would it be helpful if I shared it?"

**Key:** This is not your sales pitch disguised as value. This is actual value. A good resource, a relevant article, a connection to someone who can help them. The moment they sense that your "value" is just a Trojan horse for a pitch, trust evaporates.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Step 4: The Transition to Offer">

If the conversation reveals that they have a problem your offer solves, and they have shown genuine interest in solving it, then and only then do you transition to your offer.

**Template:**
> "Based on what you have shared about [their challenge], this is exactly what I help [target audience] with. I run a [format: program/coaching/course] focused on [specific outcome]. Would you be interested in hearing how it works, or would a quick 15-minute call be easier?"

**Important:** If the conversation does not naturally lead to your offer, do not force it. Building a genuine relationship on LinkedIn is an investment that pays off over months and years, not minutes.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## InMail Strategies

InMail is LinkedIn's paid messaging feature that allows you to message people you are not connected with. It is available with Premium, Sales Navigator, or Recruiter subscriptions.

### When InMail Makes Sense

- When someone matches your ideal client profile perfectly but you have no mutual connections
- When you are targeting decision-makers at specific companies
- When you have a time-sensitive offer or event that is relevant to a specific audience

### InMail Best Practices

**Subject line matters.** InMail has a subject line, unlike regular messages. Keep it specific and curiosity-driven:
- Weak: "Quick question"
- Strong: "Your approach to [specific topic] + an idea"

**Keep it short.** InMail that converts is typically 75-100 words. Anything longer gets skimmed or ignored.

**The 3-part InMail structure:**
1. **Specific observation:** What you noticed about them or their company (proves you did research)
2. **Value statement:** What insight, resource, or idea you have that is relevant
3. **Low-commitment ask:** A question or small request (not "Let us schedule a call")

**Template:**
> Subject: "Your [specific content/post] + an idea"
>
> "Hi [Name], I read your [article/post/comment] about [topic] and thought [specific reaction]. I have been working on [related topic] with [type of clients] and noticed something that might be relevant to what you are building. Would you be open to a brief exchange of ideas? Happy to share what I have seen working in [their space]."

**InMail response rates:** Expect 10-25% response rates on well-crafted InMail. If you are below 10%, your messaging needs work. If you are above 25%, you are doing something right.

## Voice Messages on LinkedIn

LinkedIn added voice messaging in 2018, and most users still do not know it exists. This is your advantage.

### How to Send a Voice Message on LinkedIn

Open a message thread with someone. On the mobile app, you will see a microphone icon next to the text input. Press and hold to record. Release to send.

### Why LinkedIn Voice Messages Are Underutilized

Less than 5% of LinkedIn users have ever sent a voice message. That means when you send one, you immediately stand out. The recipient sees a blue audio waveform in their inbox -- it is visually different from every other message, which means it gets noticed and played.

### When to Use Voice on LinkedIn

- **After the welcome message, if they respond warmly.** A voice follow-up feels personal and escalates the relationship.
- **When sharing your insight or resource.** Explaining something verbally is faster and more engaging than typing a paragraph.
- **For the offer transition.** Presenting your offer via voice feels more like a personal recommendation than a sales pitch.

### Voice Message Script for LinkedIn

> "Hey [Name], thanks for the response. I wanted to reply with a voice note because I thought it would be easier to explain. [Share insight or context]. I would love to hear your thoughts on that. And if it makes sense, I have some ideas on how you could apply this to [their specific situation]. No pressure at all -- just let me know."

Keep it under 45 seconds. LinkedIn voice messages that go longer than a minute feel like unsolicited podcasts.

## B2B Creator Selling on LinkedIn

If you are a creator who also serves businesses -- as a consultant, coach, or course creator -- LinkedIn is where your credibility compounds.

### The Content-to-DM Pipeline on LinkedIn

**Post consistently.** Two to three posts per week that demonstrate your expertise. LinkedIn's algorithm heavily favors original content, especially posts that generate comments.

**End posts with engagement prompts.** "What is your experience with this? Drop a comment and I will share my take." Every commenter is a potential DM conversation.

**DM your commenters.** When someone leaves a thoughtful comment on your post, send them a message: "Thanks for the comment on my post -- you made a really good point about [their point]. I would love to hear more about how you are approaching this."

### LinkedIn Newsletter Strategy

LinkedIn newsletters have built-in distribution. When you publish one, every subscriber gets a notification. Use your newsletter to deliver long-form value, and end each issue with a clear DM call-to-action: "If you want to discuss how this applies to your business, send me a message."

### The Social Proof Loop

On LinkedIn, every like and comment from a recognized professional adds to your credibility. When a prospect clicks on your profile and sees that respected people in their industry engage with your content, the trust barrier drops significantly before you ever send a message.
