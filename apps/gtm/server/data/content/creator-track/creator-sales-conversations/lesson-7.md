# Handling "I Need to Think About It"

Of all the responses you will hear on enrollment calls, "I need to think about it" is the most common, the most frustrating, and the most mishandled. It is the number one deal killer in the creator and coaching space, and it is the reason most creators have a close rate under 15%.

Here is the hard truth: "I need to think about it" is almost never a real objection. It is an escape hatch. The prospect does not know how to say no to your face, and they do not feel safe enough to say yes. So they choose the middle ground: a polite exit that gives them time to talk themselves out of it in private.

In this lesson, we are going to break down exactly what is happening when someone says this, how to isolate the real objection hiding behind it, and the specific techniques you can use to either close the deal on the call or set up a follow-up that actually converts.

## Why "Think About It" Is a Dead End

Here is what happens after someone says "I need to think about it" and leaves the call:

1. They feel relieved that the "pressure" is off.
2. They go about their day. Life takes over.
3. Every hour that passes, the emotional urgency they felt on the call fades.
4. They start rationalizing: "I can figure this out myself." "It was too expensive." "Now is not the right time."
5. You send a follow-up email. They see it and think, "I'll respond later."
6. They never respond.

The data backs this up. Industry averages show that when a prospect leaves an enrollment call saying "I need to think about it," the chance of them eventually buying drops to under 10%. Compare that with the 50-70% close rate you can achieve when the decision is made on the call.

This does not mean you should pressure people into decisions. It means you should help them make a decision on the call, whether that decision is yes or no. A clear "no" is better than a "maybe" that wastes both of your time.

## The "What Specifically" Technique

When someone says "I need to think about it," the single most effective response is a simple question:

*"I totally understand. This is a big decision and I want you to feel 100% confident. Can I ask: what specifically do you want to think about?"*

This question does something powerful. It forces the vague, amorphous "I need to think about it" into a specific, addressable concern. And there are really only four things they could be thinking about:

1. **The money.** "I need to figure out if I can afford it."
2. **The timing.** "I need to figure out if now is the right time."
3. **The fit.** "I need to figure out if this is the right program for me."
4. **Trust.** "I need to figure out if I trust you/this enough."

Once they name the specific thing, you can address it directly. You cannot solve "I need to think about it." You can solve "I am not sure I can afford $3,000 right now."

## Handling Each Specific Concern

### If It Is About Money

*"Okay, so the investment is what is giving you pause. Let me ask you this: if the investment were not an issue, if someone handed you the money right now, would you move forward?"*

If they say yes, then the concern is genuinely financial. You have two options:

**Option 1: Present the payment plan.** "We do have a payment plan that breaks it into [X] monthly payments of [Y]. Would that make it more manageable?"

**Option 2: Reframe the cost.** "I hear you. $3,000 is real money. But let me frame it this way: you told me you want to be earning $7,500 a month. If this program gets you even one new client at $2,500, you have nearly paid for the entire thing. The question is not whether you can afford the program. The question is whether you can afford another year of leaving that money on the table."

If they say "Well, even with no money issues, I am not sure," then money is not the real concern. Dig deeper: "Okay, so it is not really about the investment. What else is on your mind?"

### If It Is About Timing

*"You mentioned timing. What specifically about timing is the concern? Are you in the middle of something, or does it just feel like a lot right now?"*

Usually, "bad timing" means they are overwhelmed. The response:

*"I get it. Your plate is already full. But can I challenge that a little bit? I talk to people every week who say 'now is not the right time.' And here is what I have noticed: for most of them, there is never a right time. There is always another project, another launch, another thing on the calendar. The people who actually change their business are the ones who decide to prioritize it even when the timing feels imperfect. If you wait for the 'perfect' time, you will be having this same conversation a year from now."*

### If It Is About Fit

*"What specifically about the program makes you unsure? Is it the format, the content, or something about how it is delivered?"*

This usually reveals a gap in your presentation. Maybe you did not adequately explain something. Maybe they have a specific situation they are not sure the program addresses. Listen to their concern and address it directly. If your program genuinely is not the right fit, tell them that. Integrity is more valuable than a sale.

### If It Is About Trust

This is the hardest one because people will rarely say "I don't trust you" directly. But if they seem interested, the price is manageable, the timing works, and they still hesitate, it is usually a trust issue. Either they do not fully trust you, or they do not trust themselves.

If it is about trusting you: "I want to make sure you feel completely comfortable. Would it help if I connected you with one or two people who have gone through the program so you can hear directly from them?"

If it is about trusting themselves: "I hear something underneath what you are saying, and I want to name it. I think part of you is scared that you will invest in this and not follow through, because that has happened before. Am I off base?" When they confirm, address the self-doubt directly with empathy and a concrete success story of someone who had the same fear.

## The Isolation Technique

Sometimes a prospect will say "I need to think about it" and give you a vague answer when you ask "what specifically?" In this case, use the isolation technique. You systematically eliminate possible concerns until the real one is isolated.

*"Let me ask a few quick questions, and you can just tell me yes or no."*

*"Do you believe this program can help you get to where you want to go?"* (Testing fit.)

*"Do you feel like this is the right approach for your situation?"* (Testing methodology.)

*"Is the investment the part that is giving you pause?"* (Testing money.)

*"Do you feel comfortable with me as the person to guide you through this?"* (Testing trust.)

As they answer, you will find the one question where they hesitate or say "Well, kind of..." That is the real objection. Now you can address it directly.

## When to Let Them Go

Not every "I need to think about it" should be challenged. There are times when the right move is to let them go.

**Let them go when:**

- They are genuinely not the right fit for your program, and you both sense it.
- They have a legitimate external constraint (major life event, genuine financial emergency, health issue).
- You have addressed every concern and they are still unsure. At a certain point, pressing harder destroys trust.
- Your gut tells you they are saying yes out of pressure, not conviction. You do not want clients who were pressured into enrolling. They become refund requests and bad reviews.

**How to let them go gracefully:**

*"I totally respect that. I want you to make the decision that is right for you, not because you felt pressured on a call. Here is what I suggest: take the next 48 hours to sit with it. I am going to send you a summary of everything we talked about, including the investment details and a few success stories from members who were in a similar situation. If you decide this is right for you, the enrollment link will be in that email. And if you have any questions, just reply and I will answer them personally."*

Then follow up exactly when you said you would. One email at 24 hours, one at 48 hours. If they have not responded after two follow-ups, move on. Chasing a cold lead is a waste of your time and their patience.

## The Pre-Close: Preventing "Think About It" Before It Happens

The best way to handle "I need to think about it" is to prevent it. You can do this by setting expectations early in the call.

**At the beginning of the call:**

*"I want to make sure we use this time well. At the end of our conversation, one of three things will happen. You will be excited and ready to get started, and I will help you do that. Or you will decide this is not the right fit, and that is completely fine. Or you might have some questions that we did not get to, and we will set up a quick follow-up to address those. The one thing I would ask is that if you are leaning toward yes or leaning toward no, you let me know on the call rather than saying 'I need to think about it.' That way, I can address any remaining concerns while we are together. Fair?"*

This frame is powerful because it normalizes saying no (which reduces pressure) and gently eliminates "I need to think about it" as an option. When you get to the end of the call, the prospect has already agreed to give you a real answer.

## Tracking Your "Think About It" Rate

Here is a metric you should track: the percentage of enrollment calls that end in "I need to think about it" versus a clear yes or no.

**High performers:** Less than 20% "think about it" rate. Most calls end in a clear decision.

**Average performers:** 40-60% "think about it" rate. This indicates you are not creating enough urgency or safety during the call.

**Underperformers:** 70%+ "think about it" rate. This usually means the call structure is weak, the pain amplification is insufficient, or the prospect is not pre-qualified.

If your "think about it" rate is above 30%, the solution is not better closing techniques. It is a better call structure. Go back to Lessons 2-5 and strengthen the Clarify, Label, and Pain Amplification phases. When those phases are done well, the close handles itself.
