# The C.L.O.S.E.R. Framework

Alex Hormozi popularized a framework for high-ticket sales conversations that has become the gold standard in the creator and coaching world. It is called the C.L.O.S.E.R. framework, and it works because it was designed specifically for the kind of conversations creators and coaches actually have, not the B2B enterprise pipeline conversations we dismantled in Lesson 1.

The acronym stands for:

- **C** - Clarify why they are there
- **L** - Label the problem
- **O** - Overview past experience
- **S** - Sell the vacation (not the plane ride)
- **E** - Explain away concerns
- **R** - Reinforce the decision

Let us walk through each step in depth, with real examples you can use starting today.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="C - Clarify Why They Are There">

This is the opening of your enrollment call, and it accomplishes two critical things: it sets the frame for the conversation, and it gets the prospect to articulate their own motivation out loud.

Most creators start their calls wrong. They say something like, "So, tell me a little about yourself!" This is too open-ended. It leads to rambling, and the prospect never gets to the emotional core of why they booked the call.

Instead, open with a version of this:

*"Hey [Name], thanks for taking the time to chat today. I'm really glad you're here. Before we dive in, I'd love to understand what specifically made you book this call. What's going on in your business or your life right now that made you say, 'I need to do something different'?"*

This question does several things simultaneously. It establishes that something prompted them to take action. It asks them to articulate the pain or desire in their own words. And it creates an emotional anchor you can return to throughout the call.

Listen carefully to their answer. Take notes. The exact words they use here will become the language you mirror back to them later in the conversation. If they say, "I am exhausted from posting content and getting zero clients," you will use those exact words later: "You mentioned you are exhausted from posting content with nothing to show for it."

**Key principle:** The person who is most clear about the problem has the most power in the conversation. By getting them to articulate the problem, you are letting them sell themselves on the need for a solution.

</Slide>

<Slide title="L - Label the Problem">

After they have shared their situation, your job is to name what they are experiencing. This is one of the most powerful moves in any sales conversation because it makes the prospect feel deeply understood.

Labeling is not diagnosing. You are not saying, "Your problem is that you do not have a sales funnel." That is clinical and detached. Instead, you are reflecting back what they told you and giving it a name that resonates.

Here is how this sounds in practice:

*"So if I'm hearing you correctly, what's really going on is that you have all this expertise and you know you can help people, but you're stuck in this cycle where you're creating content, trying to be visible, and yet the people who engage with your stuff never actually become paying clients. It sounds like you have an audience-to-client conversion problem, not a visibility problem. Does that feel right?"*

The phrase "does that feel right?" is essential. You are not telling them what their problem is. You are proposing a label and asking them to confirm it. When they say "Yes, that is exactly it," something shifts. They feel seen. They feel understood. And they start to trust you as someone who actually gets their situation.

**Common labels for creator businesses:**

- "It sounds like you have a monetization gap, not a content gap."
- "What I am hearing is that you are great at attracting attention but you do not have a system for converting that attention into conversations."
- "It seems like you have been treating your business like a hobby instead of an asset, and now you are ready to make the shift."
- "You know what you are good at, but you have not figured out how to package it in a way that people will pay premium prices for."

Each of these labels validates the prospect's experience while subtly reframing their problem in a way that your offer can solve.

</Slide>

<Slide title="O - Overview Past Experience">

This step is where you explore what they have already tried. It accomplishes two things: it builds empathy, and it inoculates against the objection "I have tried something like this before."

Ask something like:

*"So this is not the first time you have tried to solve this, right? Walk me through what you have done so far. Have you taken courses, joined programs, tried to figure this out on your own?"*

Let them talk. Most prospects have a long history of attempts:

- They bought a course that sat untouched in their inbox.
- They joined a group program where they felt lost in the crowd.
- They tried to piece together a strategy from YouTube videos and podcasts.
- They hired a coach who gave them generic advice.

As they share this history, you are building a case. Not against them, but against the approaches they have tried. The key insight you want them to arrive at is: "The methods I have been using are the problem, not me."

After they finish, reflect it back:

*"Okay, so you have invested in [X] and [Y], and you put in real effort, but it did not get you where you wanted to go. Here is what I think happened. Those approaches were not wrong, they just were not designed for where you specifically are. A generic course cannot account for your unique audience, your offer, and your stage of business. That is why it felt like you were spinning your wheels."*

This accomplishes something crucial: it separates the prospect from their past failures. They are not a failure. The approaches failed them. This reframe gives them permission to try again.

</Slide>

<Slide title="S - Sell the Vacation (Not the Plane Ride)">

This is the heart of the framework and the step most creators get wrong. When it is time to present your offer, your instinct will be to describe what is included: the modules, the calls, the templates, the community access.

Stop. Nobody buys a plane ticket because they enjoy sitting in a cramped seat for six hours. They buy the plane ticket because they want to be on the beach.

Your job is to paint a vivid picture of what their life and business look like on the other side of your program. You sell the destination, not the vehicle.

Here is how this sounds:

*"Imagine this: three months from now, you have a clear enrollment process. People are finding you through your content, booking calls with you, and you are confidently walking them through a conversation that ends with them saying yes. You are signing two to three new clients a month at $2,000 each. That is $4,000 to $6,000 in recurring revenue from a system you built once. You are not hustling for every single client. They are coming to you. How would that change things for you?"*

Then you shut up and let them answer. When they describe how that would feel, they are pre-experiencing the outcome. They are living in the future state. This is infinitely more powerful than listing your course modules.

After they have described the destination, you can briefly mention the vehicle:

*"That is exactly what this program is designed to create. The way we get you there is through [brief description of your methodology]. But what matters most is not the how. What matters is that you have a clear path from where you are now to that picture you just described."*

**The ratio should be 80% destination, 20% vehicle.**

</Slide>

<Slide title="E - Explain Away Concerns">

Notice the framework does not say "handle objections." It says "explain away concerns." The framing matters.

After you present the offer and the investment, the prospect will have concerns. This is natural and healthy. Your job is not to "overcome" their resistance. It is to address what is underneath the concern.

The four most common concerns in creator enrollment are:

1. **"It is too expensive."** This almost never means they literally cannot afford it. It means they are not yet convinced the outcome is worth the investment. Return to the vacation: "I understand it is a significant investment. Let me ask you this: if this program delivers what we just talked about, two to three new clients per month at $2,000 each, how quickly does the investment pay for itself?"

2. **"I need to think about it."** This usually means they are scared. (We will do a deep dive on this in Lesson 7.)

3. **"I am not sure it will work for me."** This is a self-belief issue, not a product issue. Share a specific example of someone in a similar situation who succeeded.

4. **"I have been burned before."** This is trust damage from past experiences. Acknowledge it directly: "That makes total sense, and I respect that you are being cautious. Here is how this program is different from what you have tried before..."

We will cover each of these in detail in Lesson 4. For now, the key principle is: every concern has a deeper concern underneath it. Your job is to find the real one.

</Slide>

<Slide title="R - Reinforce the Decision">

This step is often overlooked, but it is critical. The moment someone says yes and gives you their payment information, a wave of buyer's doubt hits them. Their brain starts asking, "Did I just make a mistake? Can I really afford this? What if it does not work?"

Your job is to immediately reinforce their decision. Not with a generic "Congratulations!" but with a specific, genuine acknowledgment.

*"I want you to know something. The fact that you said yes today tells me a lot about you. Most people will stay stuck and keep doing the same thing. You just made a decision to invest in yourself and your business. That takes courage. I am genuinely excited to work with you, and here is exactly what happens next..."*

Then immediately give them a concrete next step: "You will get an email in the next 10 minutes with your onboarding link. I want you to complete the intake form by Friday so we can hit the ground running on our first call."

The combination of emotional reinforcement and a concrete action step reduces buyer's remorse dramatically. They leave the call feeling good about their decision and clear on what to do next.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Putting It All Together

The C.L.O.S.E.R. framework is not a rigid script. It is a conversation arc. In a real enrollment call, you will flow between these phases naturally. Sometimes you will return to "Clarify" in the middle of "Selling the vacation" because the prospect brings up a new angle. That is fine.

What matters is that you hit each phase at some point during the call, and that you spend the majority of your time in the first four letters (C, L, O, S). The concerns and reinforcement phases should be short because if you have done the first four well, the close feels natural.

**Time allocation for a 45-minute call:**
- C (Clarify): 5 minutes
- L (Label): 5 minutes
- O (Overview): 10 minutes
- S (Sell the vacation): 10 minutes
- E (Explain concerns): 10 minutes
- R (Reinforce): 5 minutes

<InsightCard icon="⏱️" title="Time Allocation Reality Check">
Most creators spend 20+ minutes on features and only 2 minutes painting the destination. Flip this. The first 30 minutes should be them talking about their problem and you painting the future. The last 15 minutes is logistics and concerns.
</InsightCard>

## Practice Exercise

Choose a real prospect or a friend who will role-play with you. Run through a full C.L.O.S.E.R. conversation for your current offer. Record the call (with permission). Afterward, listen back and note:

1. How much time did you spend on each phase?
2. Where did you slip into "feature listing" instead of selling the vacation?
3. Did you successfully label the problem in a way that made the other person feel understood?
4. At the close, did the transition feel natural or forced?

This is a skill that improves with repetition. Your first attempt will feel clunky. That is normal. By your tenth enrollment call using this framework, it will feel like a natural conversation.
