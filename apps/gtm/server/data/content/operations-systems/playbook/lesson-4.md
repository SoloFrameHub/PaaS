## You Are the Product

When you sell software, the product is the software. When you sell coaching or consulting, the product is you — your expertise, your frameworks, your way of seeing problems, your track record.

This distinction changes everything about how you acquire customers.

Software buyers evaluate features. They compare pricing tables. They read G2 reviews. They run 30-day trials. The purchase decision is relatively low-emotion.

Coaching and consulting buyers evaluate *fit*, *trust*, and *proof of transformation*. They're asking: "Can this person actually help me? Do I trust them? Have they helped people like me?" The purchase decision is highly personal.

This means your acquisition playbook can't be built on cold outreach volume and email sequences alone. It must be built on content that demonstrates expertise, relationships that build trust, and an enrollment process that filters for genuine fit.

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Trust-Before-Transaction Principle">
In coaching and consulting, trust is the currency. You don't earn trust through a clever pitch — you earn it by demonstrating, repeatedly, that you understand your client's problem better than they understand it themselves. Your content is your proof of expertise before anyone ever pays you.
</InsightCard>

## The Coach/Consultant Context

This playbook is for you if:

- You sell expertise, not software — coaching packages, consulting retainers, done-for-you services, or mastermind memberships
- Your typical deal size is $2,000-$20,000+
- Your clients are buying a transformation, not a transaction
- Relationships and trust heavily influence purchase decisions
- You have expertise that's genuinely valuable and verifiable

## The Content-to-Client Funnel

Understanding the funnel architecture helps you optimize at each stage. Here's how it works for coaches and consultants:

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Stage 1: Content (Awareness)">

Your LinkedIn content does three jobs: attracts your ICP, repels non-ICP, and demonstrates that you understand the problem better than anyone else.

The most effective content for coaches and consultants is **problem-describing content** — not advice, not solutions, but articulate descriptions of the exact pain your ideal client feels. When someone reads your post and thinks "how did they know exactly what I'm experiencing?" — that's the hook.

The second most effective: **transformation stories**. A vivid before/after from a real client (with permission) creates "that could be me" identification.

Avoid: generic advice, motivational quotes, humble-brags about your own results.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Stage 2: Lead Magnet (Capture)">

Your lead magnet converts a content reader into an email subscriber. It must:

1. Solve a specific, acute pain point (not a vague general problem)
2. Deliver quick value (a PDF, a template, a mini-course — something they can use in 20 minutes)
3. Make them want more of what you offer

The best lead magnets for coaches and consultants are diagnostic tools — an assessment, an audit checklist, a "which stage are you in?" framework. Diagnostics create immediate engagement and position you as the authority who can interpret the diagnosis.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Stage 3: Email Nurture (Qualification)">

Your email sequence does two things: builds trust over time and self-selects your best-fit clients.

A 5-7 email welcome sequence over 10-14 days, followed by a weekly or bi-weekly newsletter. Each email deepens the relationship and demonstrates your expertise with specificity.

The goal of nurture isn't conversion — it's building enough trust that when they're ready to buy, they come to you first. Some clients take 3 months from lead magnet to enrollment call. Your email list is how you stay in front of them during that window.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Stage 4: Application (Filter)">

The application form is the most underused tool in the coach/consultant acquisition playbook.

An application form does something a "book a call" button cannot: it requires commitment and filters for seriousness. Someone who fills out a 10-question application is 5-10x more likely to convert than someone who clicks "book a free call."

Your application should include: their current situation (specific), what they've already tried, what outcome they want, when they want to achieve it, what investment they're prepared to make, and why now. This data also prepares you perfectly for the enrollment conversation.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Stage 5: Enrollment Call (Close)">

The enrollment call is not a sales call. It's a collaborative problem-solving conversation where you help them determine if they're a good fit for your program.

The biggest mistake: pitching your program features. The right move: deeply understanding their situation, identifying what's actually blocking them, and showing how your approach addresses that specific block.

Close rates for warm applications: 20-40%. This is dramatically higher than cold outreach close rates because the funnel has pre-qualified, pre-educated, and pre-motivated the buyer before they ever get on the phone.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Enrollment Call Framework

The enrollment call follows a specific structure that maximizes both client fit and conversion:

<ProgressiveReveal title="The 45-Minute Enrollment Call" persistKey="playbook-L4-reveal">

<RevealSection title="Minutes 0-5: Grounding the Conversation">

Thank them for the application. Reference something specific from it — this shows you actually read it and sets a collaborative tone.

"Thank you for the application — what you wrote about [specific thing] really resonated. Before we dive in, I want to share how I run these calls: I'm going to ask you some questions to understand your situation fully, then I'll share exactly what working with me looks like, and we'll both determine if there's a fit. Sound good?"

Setting this frame reduces the pressure they might feel about being "sold to."

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Minutes 5-20: Deep Discovery">

This is where most coaches get it wrong — they rush to present their program. Instead, spend the majority of the call in deep discovery.

Key questions:
- "Walk me through where you are right now. Paint the full picture."
- "What have you tried to solve this? What worked, what didn't?"
- "What does 'success' look like for you in 6 months? What would be different?"
- "What's the cost of staying stuck where you are — in time, money, and quality of life?"

That last question is critical. When they articulate the cost themselves, they're doing the selling for you.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Minutes 20-35: Tailored Presentation">

Now present your program — but tailored precisely to what they just told you.

"Based on what you've shared, here's what I think is happening: [their situation]. Here's why [common solutions] haven't worked for you: [diagnosis]. Here's how we'd approach it in [program name]: [specific modules or methods that address their exact situation]."

Use their words. Reference their specific situation repeatedly. Make them feel like this program was designed for them — because if they're truly your ICP, it effectively was.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Minutes 35-40: Checking for Fit">

Before discussing price or logistics: "Does this feel like it matches what you're looking for? Is there anything that feels off or that you'd want to understand better?"

This is your last chance to address objections before the investment conversation. Address them directly and honestly — including situations where you might not be the right fit. Saying "I don't think I'm the right fit for you right now because [reason]" builds more trust than a hard close.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Minutes 40-45: Investment and Next Steps">

Present the investment clearly and without apology. One price, clear terms, specific deliverables.

"The investment for [Program] is [price], which includes [what they get]. [Payment options if applicable]. If you're ready to move forward, we can start [date]."

Then stop talking. The next person who speaks after you state the price is the client. Silence is your friend.

Clear next steps: application accepted → enrollment form + payment link → onboarding call → start date.

</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>

## Referrals: The Compounding Channel

After 12 months of working with clients, referrals typically become your highest-ROI acquisition channel — 40-60% of new revenue. But referrals don't happen by accident.

The referral request script: "You've made some incredible progress on [specific result]. I'm curious — do you know anyone else in your network who's dealing with [specific plateau]? I'm working with a few clients in similar situations and would love an introduction if there's a good fit."

Simple. Specific. Not transactional.
