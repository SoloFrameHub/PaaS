## The Gap Between Knowing and Doing

There's a painful gap in most founders' sales lives: the space between knowing what to do and actually doing it consistently.

You know you should send 10 cold emails a day. You don't.

You know you should post on LinkedIn three times a week. You don't.

You know you should review your pipeline metrics every Friday. You don't.

This gap isn't a knowledge problem. You already know enough. It's a commitment problem — the absence of a specific, formal promise to yourself (and someone else) about what you'll do every day for the next 90 days.

The 90-Day Commitment Contract closes this gap. It transforms your one-page acquisition system from a *strategy document* into a *behavioral contract* with specific daily activities, explicit boundaries, accountability mechanisms, and review checkpoints.

## Why 90 Days?

The 90-day window is not arbitrary:

<FlipCard
  front="Why not 30 days?"
  back="Thirty days isn't enough to determine if an acquisition channel works. Cold email takes 2-4 weeks to show results. Content takes 8-12 weeks. LinkedIn reach takes 6-8 weeks. A 30-day commitment gives up before the results arrive."
/>

<FlipCard
  front="Why not 180 days?"
  back="Six months without a formal checkpoint allows bad strategies to run too long without correction. Ninety days = 3 monthly review cycles, which is enough data to know if a strategy is working and enough urgency to maintain motivation."
/>

<FlipCard
  front="Why exactly 90?"
  back="At 90 days of consistent daily activity, you will have enough data to evaluate your channel, message, and ICP. You'll have clear evidence of whether to persist, adjust, or pivot. Sixty days is the minimum — 90 gives you the full picture."
/>

<InsightCard icon="📅" title="The 30-60-90 Checkpoint Structure">
**Day 30:** Input check. Are you doing the activities? Is your cadence sustainable? This is about behavior, not results.

**Day 60:** Output check. Are the activities producing the leading indicators you targeted? Reply rates, meetings booked, applications received. This is about early signal.

**Day 90:** Strategy check. Are the leading indicators translating into customers and revenue? Does this strategy warrant continuation, refinement, or a pivot to something new?
</InsightCard>

## The Commitment Contract Structure

The contract has five sections. Each is non-negotiable.

<ProgressiveReveal title="The 5 Sections of the Commitment Contract" persistKey="playbook-L8-reveal">

<RevealSection title="Section 1: I WILL DO (Activity Commitments)">

This section lists your specific daily and weekly acquisition activities with exact numbers. Not ranges ("3-5 emails") but exact numbers ("5 emails"). Not "roughly" but exactly.

The specificity serves a purpose: it makes the choice binary. At the end of each day, you either did the 5 emails or you didn't. There's no partial credit, no "I did 3 which is close enough." Binary commitment creates binary accountability.

Every activity must answer three questions:
- What (specific activity)
- How much (specific quantity)
- When (specific time or day)

"Send 10 cold emails" is incomplete. "Send 10 cold emails before 9:30am, Monday through Friday" is a commitment.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Section 2: I WILL NOT DO (The Distraction Blacklist)">

This section is counterintuitive — and it's where most contracts fail because founders skip it.

The "Will Not Do" list identifies the specific distractions and false-urgent activities you commit to stopping for 90 days. Founders who cut distractions see 20-40% more selling time without working more hours.

Examples of what goes on this list:
- "I will not build new product features during prospecting hours"
- "I will not redesign my website for 90 days"
- "I will not explore new tools or platforms for 90 days"
- "I will not attend networking events that don't directly produce prospect conversations"
- "I will not start a new content platform during this 90 days"

These feel obvious. Yet every founder reading this has violated at least one of them in the last 30 days.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Section 3: I WILL MEASURE (Metrics and Targets)">

Copy your metrics directly from your one-page system. But in the contract, add explicit "minimum viable" thresholds — below which you take corrective action.

For example:
- "Cold email reply rate: target 7%, minimum 5%. If below 5% for 2 consecutive weeks, I will rewrite my subject lines and first lines before sending more."
- "Meetings booked: target 3/week, minimum 2/week. If below 2/week for 2 consecutive weeks, I will review my CTA and meeting conversion approach."

The minimum thresholds turn your metrics from passive observations into active triggers.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Section 4: REVIEW DATES (Checkpoint Calendar)">

Three review dates, pre-scheduled before you sign the contract:

**Day 30 Review:** Block 60 minutes. Answer: Am I doing the activities at the committed level? If no, why? (Only valid answers: genuinely unavoidable life events. 'I got busy' or 'I didn't feel like it' are not valid.) What will I do differently in the next 30 days?

**Day 60 Review:** Block 90 minutes. Answer: Are my activities producing leading indicators at target levels? If not, which single variable will I change?

**Day 90 Review:** Block 2 hours. Answer: Is this working well enough to continue? Do I need a major strategy update (v2.0 of my one-page system)? What did I learn that I didn't know 90 days ago?

These dates go in your calendar right now, before you sign the contract.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Section 5: ACCOUNTABILITY (Who's Holding You to This)">

Name one person who will receive your weekly report. Not a group. Not a community post. One specific named person who will look you in the eye (or send you a message) if you miss your targets.

Your accountability partner should:
- Understand what you're trying to build
- Be willing to call you out honestly, not just encourage you
- Not share your business interests (they won't rationalize your failures away)
- Check in with you at minimum every two weeks

The act of selecting and naming this person is part of the commitment. If you're uncomfortable telling this person "I committed to X and I missed it," that discomfort is the accountability engine.

</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>

## The Signing Ritual

This part sounds dramatic. It's not — it's neuroscience.

Physical signatures increase commitment follow-through by 50%+ (behavioral psychology research). The act of physically signing your name to a document creates a different mental state than clicking "I agree." It's why legal contracts are signed, not checked.

<InsightCard icon="✍️" title="The Signing Protocol">
After completing your contract:
1. Print it. Physical paper.
2. Sign it in pen. Your actual handwriting.
3. Send a photo to your accountability partner.
4. Post it somewhere you see it every day — your desk, your bathroom mirror, wherever you start your mornings.

This sounds trivial. It is not trivial. The act of printing, signing, and posting changes your relationship to the commitment.
</InsightCard>

## The Weekly Accountability Report

Your report to your accountability partner should be brief and specific:

<ExampleCard label="Sample Weekly Accountability Report">
**Week 3 Acquisition Report — [Your Name]**

**This Week's Commitments:**
- Cold emails: 50 target → 47 sent (94% — missed Wednesday due to client emergency)
- LinkedIn DMs: 10 target → 12 sent (120%)
- Discovery calls: 2 minimum → 3 held

**Leading Metrics:**
- Reply rate: 8.3% (target 7% ✓)
- Meetings booked from emails: 2
- Meetings booked from DMs: 1

**What I Learned:**
- The subject line "Quick question about [their tool]" outperformed everything else (12% reply rate)
- One prospect said "not now but Q2 looks interesting" — added to nurture

**Next Week's Focus:**
- Test subject line: "Question about your [role] workflow"
- Fix Wednesday prospecting block — moved to Tuesday to avoid client call conflicts

**What I Need from You:**
- Check in Thursday if you haven't heard from me — that's my "call block" day and I sometimes skip it

---

*This took 8 minutes to write. Your accountability partner will appreciate the specificity and will actually hold you accountable because you gave them something concrete to respond to.*
</ExampleCard>
