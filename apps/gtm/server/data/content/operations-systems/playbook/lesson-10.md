## The Complete Playbook Architecture

Your complete playbook has five sections. Each section links directly to the work you've already done.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Section 1: One-Page Acquisition System">

Your one-page system from Lesson 7 is the executive summary of your entire acquisition strategy. It answers all 5 core questions on a single page.

**Living document rule:** This page gets updated at every version milestone (v1.1 at Day 30, v1.2 at Day 60, v2.0 at Day 90). Each update is timestamped and stored in the Version History.

**Format:** One printed page, signed, dated, version-stamped. This is the page that goes on your wall.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Section 2: Situation-Specific Playbook">

Whichever situation playbook you chose (Lessons 2-6) forms the tactical backbone of your system. It contains your specific templates, scripts, tools, and weekly rhythms.

**Living document rule:** The situation playbook updates less frequently than the one-page system. Major updates happen at Day 90 reviews when you have enough data to revise your approach fundamentally.

**Format:** This is your reference document. Print it or keep it bookmarked. Consult it when you need tactical reminders of how to execute your primary and secondary channels.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Section 3: 90-Day Commitment Contract">

Your commitment contract from Lesson 8 is the behavioral backbone of your system. It specifies exactly what you'll do, what you won't do, how you'll measure it, and who's holding you accountable.

**Living document rule:** Once signed, the contract doesn't change until you complete a formal review checkpoint. Changes to your commitment must be documented in the Version History and shared with your accountability partner.

**Format:** Printed, physically signed, posted visibly. The act of physically seeing your signed commitment every day matters.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Section 4: Decision Log">

Your strategic decision log from Lesson 9 records every pivot, persist, or refinement decision — with the data that drove it and your hypothesis for what comes next.

**Living document rule:** Add an entry every time you make a strategic decision. This is your acquisition system's history file.

**Format:** A running document you add to at each review checkpoint. Not a template to fill in once — a log that grows over time.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Section 5: Version History">

Track every version of your playbook, what changed, when, and why. This becomes your institutional knowledge about what works in your specific market, with your specific ICP, through your specific channels.

**Living document rule:** Update with every version change. v1.0, v1.1, v1.2, v2.0, etc. Each entry records: version number, date, what changed, and why.

**Format:** A running log. Three lines per version: version number + date, what changed, and the data that drove the change.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The 14-Day Execution Sprint

Your playbook is built. Now it's time to run it.

The 14-Day Execution Sprint is how you go from "I have a playbook" to "I am executing my playbook." It's structured to prevent the most common post-planning failure mode: spending Days 1-3 on additional setup and never actually executing.

<ProgressiveReveal title="The 14-Day Sprint Calendar" persistKey="playbook-L10-sprint">

<RevealSection title="Days 1-2: Finalize and Commit">

**Day 1:**

- Print your one-page system and commitment contract
- Sign both documents
- Send photos to your accountability partner
- Add all three review dates (Day 30, Day 60, Day 90) to your calendar
- Post your one-page system where you'll see it every morning

**Day 2:**

- Set up your tracking system (CRM or spreadsheet with your 5 metrics)
- Prepare your first batch of outreach: research prospects, draft messages, set up sequences
- If your channel requires setup (email tool, LinkedIn template), complete it today

**Output:** Playbook signed. Partner notified. Tracking ready. First outreach prepared. You are ready to execute tomorrow.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Days 3-7: Week 1 Execution">

**Every day of Week 1:** Execute your daily commitment exactly as specified.

Cold email: Send your committed daily volume. No exceptions.
LinkedIn: Post, comment, DM as committed.
Community: Participate as committed.
Coaching/consulting: Engage with your application funnel as committed.

**Log your activity each day.** Not as a bureaucratic exercise — as a commitment to honest data. Your Day 8 review is useless without real numbers.

**Do not adjust anything this week.** Week 1 is not the time to optimize. It's the time to execute and build a baseline.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day 8: Week 1 Review">

Block 30 minutes. Answer these questions honestly:

1. What % of my daily commitment did I actually execute?
2. Are there any early-signal surprises? (An unexpectedly high reply rate, an unexpected objection pattern?)
3. What, if anything, physically prevented me from executing? (Not "I forgot" — physical obstacles only)
4. What do I need to change about my _schedule_ (not my strategy) to make Week 2 easier?

Write down your answers. Share them with your accountability partner.

**Note:** Do not make strategic changes at Day 8. You have one week of data — not enough to conclude anything.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Days 9-13: Week 2 Execution">

**Execute Week 2 with any schedule adjustments from Day 8's review.**

If you discovered that your 9am outreach slot doesn't work because of a standing meeting, move it to 8am or post-lunch. Adjust the schedule — not the commitment level.

Continue logging activity daily.

**One potential addition:** If you received your first replies or responses in Week 1, follow up appropriately in Week 2. Your first conversations from your playbook are real signal — treat them accordingly.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day 14: Sprint Retrospective and v1.1 Assessment">

Two-week review. Block 45 minutes.

**Look at your two weeks of data:**

- Total activity (emails sent, posts published, DMs sent, calls held)
- Leading metric actuals vs. targets
- Any positive signals (replies, meeting requests, interest)
- Any negative patterns (consistent objections, low reply rates, wrong people responding)

**Decision:** Are your commitment levels realistic and sustainable? If your committed volume is genuinely impossible to sustain, this is the time to honestly lower it — with your accountability partner's input. If you haven't been hitting your commitment, identify the specific fix.

**Not yet a strategy evaluation.** You have 14 days of data. Strategy evaluation happens at Day 60.

</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>

## The Gap Between Builders and Executors

You are now at the moment that separates most founders.

Builders spend their energy creating strategies, writing plans, building frameworks, and optimizing systems. Executors spend their energy doing the actual activities, every day, regardless of motivation.

The paradox: builders often have better strategies than executors. But executors build better businesses.

Your playbook is built. The gap between you and your revenue goals is now purely execution. Not more courses. Not better tools. Not a smarter strategy. Just the daily activities you committed to, done with specificity and consistency, reviewed honestly, and updated with data.

<ExampleCard label="Two Founders, Same Playbook">
Two founders completed this course on the same day. Both built solid playbooks. Both signed their commitment contracts. Both had accountability partners.

**Founder A:** Executed 78% of her daily commitments for 90 days. Reviewed at Day 30 and adjusted her schedule. Made one single-variable message change at Day 60. By Day 90: 8 new customers, $22K in new MRR.

**Founder B:** Executed 38% of his daily commitments for 90 days. Kept adjusting his strategy based on "feeling like it wasn't working." Changed his channel at Week 4, his ICP at Week 6, and his message at Week 8. By Day 90: 1 new customer, $3K in new MRR.

Same playbook. Same strategy. The only difference was execution consistency and the discipline to not pivot before having data. Founder A ran the playbook. Founder B designed and redesigned it.

Which one will you be?
</ExampleCard>
