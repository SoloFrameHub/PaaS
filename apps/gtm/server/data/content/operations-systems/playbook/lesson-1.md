## The Folder No One Opens

Most founders have a "Sales Strategy" document somewhere. A Google Doc, a Notion page, maybe a Miro board covered in sticky notes. They worked on it over a weekend, felt great about it, and then filed it away.

Six months later, they're still doing the same scattered things that weren't working. Posting on LinkedIn when inspiration strikes. Sending outreach when they "have time." Waiting for warm referrals while quietly hoping inbound will magically appear.

The strategy document is useless because it answers the wrong questions. It describes *aspirations* — revenue targets, market opportunities, competitive advantages. What it doesn't do is tell you what to do tomorrow morning.

A playbook is different. A playbook answers five ruthlessly specific questions. The answers fit on one page. You tape that page to your wall.

And then you do exactly what the page says, every day, for 90 days.

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Playbook-First Principle">
"If it doesn't fit on one page, you lack clarity." Your playbook isn't a business plan — it's the forcing function that strips away everything except what matters. Complexity is where execution goes to die.
</InsightCard>

## The 5 Questions

Every functional customer acquisition system — from a solo founder sending 15 cold emails a day to a VC-backed SaaS company with a 30-person sales team — answers these five questions:

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Question 1: WHO am I selling to?">

This is your Ideal Customer Profile from Course 1 — but stated with enough specificity that you could walk into a room and instantly recognize the person.

**Not specific enough:** "Small business owners"

**Still not specific enough:** "Tech startup founders"

**Getting there:** "B2B SaaS founders with 5-20 employees"

**Specific enough:** "B2B SaaS founders with 5-20 employees, $10K-50K MRR, struggling with unpredictable lead flow, who've tried running ads without results"

The test: Can you name 5 real people who fit your ICP right now? If you can't, your ICP is still too abstract.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Question 2: HOW am I reaching them?">

This is your primary acquisition channel from Course 3. The dangerous word here is "channels" (plural). Most founders spread themselves across 4-5 channels and wonder why none of them work.

**The Channel Rule:** Pick one primary channel (70% of effort) and one secondary channel (30% of effort). No more until your primary is generating consistent results.

Your answer should be specific: not "LinkedIn" but "15 personalized LinkedIn DMs per week + 3 posts per week." Not "cold email" but "50 personalized cold emails per week using Apollo + Instantly."

Vague channel choices produce vague results.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Question 3: WHAT message am I using?">

This draws from your positioning work in Course 2, your cold email frameworks from Course 8, and your LinkedIn strategy from Course 7. Your message must be specific to your ICP and matched to your channel.

A LinkedIn DM message sounds different from a cold email, which sounds different from a community post. Same underlying positioning, different execution.

Your answer should include: the core problem you solve, the transformation you provide, your primary proof point, and your call to action. Write it as a 3-sentence summary.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Question 4: HOW am I measuring success?">

This connects to your analytics work in Course 41. Most founders measure the wrong thing (revenue) and ignore the leading indicators that actually tell them if their system is working.

**Leading metrics** (weekly): outreach volume, reply rate, meetings booked, applications received. These tell you if the engine is running.

**Lagging metrics** (monthly): deals won, revenue, customer acquisition cost, customer lifetime value. These tell you if the engine is pointed in the right direction.

If you only track revenue, you'll discover problems 60-90 days too late to fix them.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Question 5: WHAT commitment am I making?">

This is the question most founders skip entirely — and it's the most important one.

"I'm going to do more outreach" is not a commitment. "I will send 10 personalized cold emails every Monday and Thursday" is a commitment.

The commitment question isn't about goals (lagging outcomes). It's about minimum viable effort (leading activities). Below what daily/weekly activity level can you not expect results? That floor is your commitment.

90 days of consistent execution beats 90 days of excellent strategy with inconsistent execution. Every time.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Data Behind the System

<InsightCard icon="📊" title="Why Documenting Your Process Changes Everything">
Companies with documented sales processes grow **33% faster** than those without — even controlling for team size, funding, and market. A playbook IS a documented process. The one-page version is simply the most honest and actionable form of documentation.
</InsightCard>

Here's what you're working against without a playbook:

- **90% of startups fail due to "premature scaling"** — doing too many things before any one works. The playbook's two-channel rule forces focus.
- **Founders who commit to specific daily activities for 90 days are 4x more likely to find product-market fit** (First Round Capital). Commitment, not strategy, is the differentiator.
- **It takes 60-90 days to determine if a channel is working.** If you haven't given your primary channel 60 days of consistent execution, you don't know if it works. You only know you didn't execute.
