## The Founder Who Hired Too Early

David was 4 months into his B2B SaaS product and exhausted. He'd done 20 demos, closed 8 deals, and convinced himself the reason he wasn't growing faster was that he needed a salesperson. Someone to take the calls, book the meetings, run the outbound. He hired an SDR at $4,500/month.

Three months later, the SDR was gone. Revenue had barely moved.

The problem wasn't the hire. It was the timing.

David had 8 customers — but he couldn't articulate why they bought. He had no written sequences. No objection handling document. No discovery script. He'd handed a new hire a blank page and expected them to figure it out.

**The rule you have to internalize before this course goes any further:** A sales hire can only scale a proven motion. They cannot discover one. That's your job, and yours alone.

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Founder-First Principle">
Only the founder can discover the first sales motion. A hire can optimize and execute a playbook that works. They cannot write the playbook from scratch. If you can't sell it yourself consistently, no one you hire will fix that.
</InsightCard>

## Why Founders Hire Too Early (And Why It Fails Every Time)

Before you can make a good timing decision, you need to be honest about your real motivation for wanting to hire. Most premature hires come from one of three places:

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Motivation 1: I Hate Selling">

You're a builder. Selling feels uncomfortable, awkward, and beneath your pay grade. You think: "If I hire a salesperson, I never have to do this again."

**Why this fails:** If selling feels miserable, it usually means one of two things — either your pitch isn't refined yet (which no hire can fix), or your ICP isn't right (which no hire can fix). You're hiring to escape a problem that exists inside the product and positioning, not inside your schedule.

**The real fix:** Go back to Courses 2 and 14. Refine the message until selling doesn't feel like pulling teeth.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Motivation 2: Someone Will Figure It Out">

You've tried a few approaches, some worked better than others, but you don't really know why customers buy. You think: "A good salesperson will experiment and find what works."

**Why this fails:** Experienced sales hires join companies with proven playbooks. They're trained to execute, not discover. An SDR handed ambiguous instructions will default to generic outreach that doesn't work — and you'll blame the hire.

**The real fix:** Run 50+ meetings yourself until you can articulate why customers buy in exactly two sentences. Then hire.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Motivation 3: I Should Be Building">

Your product roadmap is backed up. Every hour you spend on sales is an hour you're not building. You think: "I need to free up my time to focus on product."

**Why this fails:** This is the only partially valid reason — but only after you've proven the motion. If the motion isn't proven yet, freeing up your time just means nobody is selling. If the motion IS proven, this is actually the right time to hire.

**The test:** Can you hand someone a documented playbook tomorrow and have them run meetings next week? If yes, you might be ready. If not, keep selling.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Revenue Thresholds: When Can You Afford Each Role?

Timing a sales hire isn't just about readiness — it's about math. Here's what the numbers actually look like:

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Fractional Sales Leader: $10K+ MRR">

**Monthly cost:** $2,000–5,000/month for 5–15 hours per week

**What they do:** Set up process, coach you on sales, may do some selling themselves. They've been VP Sales at other companies. You're getting senior guidance without the senior price tag.

**When to choose this:** You have revenue but feel lost on process, pipeline management, or how to structure your motion. You need a thinking partner and coach more than a pair of hands.

**Affordability rule:** Your MRR should be at least 3x their monthly retainer. At $10K MRR, a $3K/month fractional is manageable. At $5K MRR, it's reckless.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Part-Time SDR: $15K+ MRR">

**Monthly cost:** $1,500–3,000/month for 15–25 hours per week

**What they do:** Handle outbound prospecting — research, sequencing, follow-up, meeting booking. You close everything they book.

**When to choose this:** You're good at closing but meetings are your bottleneck. You have proven outbound sequences and need more capacity running them.

**Affordability rule:** Each meeting they book should convert to enough revenue to justify their cost. If a meeting converts to a $2K deal at 30%, one meeting per week covers a $1,800/month PT SDR.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Full-Time SDR: $25K+ MRR">

**Monthly cost:** $4,600–6,250/month fully loaded (base $40–55K/year plus taxes, benefits, tools)

**What they do:** Full outbound ownership — 40 hours per week of prospecting, sequencing, and meeting booking.

**When to choose this:** You have proven outbound that generates consistent pipeline and you need to scale it. You close well, and pipeline is the only constraint.

**Affordability rule:** MRR should be at least 4x the monthly cost. At $25K MRR, a $6K/month fully-loaded SDR leaves room for product, operations, and your own salary.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Full-Time AE: $50K+ MRR">

**Monthly cost:** $7,500–12,000/month fully loaded (OTE $78–120K/year)

**What they do:** Handle the entire sales cycle — discovery, demos, proposals, closing. You exit the sales seat.

**When to choose this:** You want to stop selling entirely and focus on product or operations. Your playbook is mature, your ICP is crisp, and you can hand off a complete motion with training materials.

**Affordability rule:** MRR should be at least 5x the monthly cost. An AE with a $90K OTE needs to generate $270–450K annually to justify their cost at standard 3–5x quota ratios.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Real Cost of Hiring Wrong

The decision tree above tells you when to hire. But here's what it costs when founders ignore it:

<ExampleCard label="Case Study: The $38K Lesson">
Marcus hired an SDR at $4,200/month base when he was at $18K MRR and had closed only 12 deals — but had no written playbook.

The SDR lasted 4.5 months. Here's what it actually cost:

- Salary: $18,900
- Recruiting time (40 hours at $150/hr opportunity cost): $6,000
- Onboarding and management time (5 hrs/week × 18 weeks at $150/hr): $13,500
- Damaged prospect relationships (SDR sent off-brand messages): estimated $5K+ in lost pipeline
- Total: ~$43,400

After the separation, Marcus spent 3 weeks writing the playbook he should have written before hiring. The next SDR, hired 6 months later with a complete playbook, ramped in 8 weeks and hit quota by month 3.

The difference? Documentation and timing.
</ExampleCard>

<InsightCard icon="⚠️" title="The Bad Hire Cost Formula">
A failed sales hire costs 3–6 months of salary in direct costs, plus recruiting time, onboarding time, management time, and opportunity cost of deals that didn't close while you were managing a failing hire. For an SDR at $5K/month, a bad hire costs $25–40K total. Hire slowly.
</InsightCard>

## The Documentation Requirement

Before you post a single job description, you need to have these seven assets ready. A hire without these is set up to fail.

<ProgressiveReveal title="The 7 Documents You Need Before You Hire" persistKey="scale-L1-docs">

<RevealSection title="1. ICP Document">
Who is your ideal customer? Company size, industry, job title, pain points, buying triggers. If this lives only in your head, your hire will prospect everyone and convert nobody. (Covered in Course 1.)
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="2. Positioning and Messaging">
What do you say when someone asks what you do? What's the one-line value proposition? What are the three proof points? Your hire needs to deliver this consistently, which means it must be written. (Covered in Course 2.)
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="3. Outreach Templates and Sequences">
The exact emails, LinkedIn messages, and follow-up cadences that generate replies. Not "write something like what I do" — the actual templates. (Covered in Courses 7, 8, and 24.)
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="4. Discovery Call Script">
The framework your hire will use on every discovery call. The questions to ask, the order to ask them, the transitions between stages. They can adapt it, but they need a starting point. (Covered in Course 14.)
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="5. Objection Handling Database">
The 8–10 objections prospects raise most frequently, with approved responses for each. Your hire will face these on day one. If they're improvising, they'll lose deals. (Covered in Course 17.)
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="6. CRM with Pipeline Data">
A configured CRM with real pipeline data from your own selling. Stage definitions, field standards, and at least 20 active contacts your hire can use as examples. (Covered in Course 40.)
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="7. Sales Playbook">
The master document that ties all of the above together — plus metrics benchmarks, escalation rules, and the "voice" of your company in writing. This is the hire's operating manual. (Covered in Course 44.)
</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>
