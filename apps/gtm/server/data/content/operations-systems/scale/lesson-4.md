## Why Most Sales Interviews Fail to Predict Performance

You've posted the JD. Applications are coming in. Now you have to figure out who can actually sell.

Here's the problem: most founders interview sales candidates the way they'd interview a software engineer — asking questions, listening to answers, and making a judgment based on how confident and articulate the person sounds. Charisma gets mistaken for sales skill. Storytelling gets mistaken for track record. You hire the person who interviewed best, not the person who sells best.

Research is clear: roleplays in interviews improve hire quality by 35–50% compared to behavioral-question-only interviews. The best predictor of whether someone can sell your product is whether they can sell something in a controlled setting right now.

This lesson gives you a 3-stage interview process that takes 3 hours total per finalist and predicts performance far better than any single conversation.

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Core Principle">
Interviews are auditions, not conversations. You're not trying to like the candidate. You're trying to see them perform the actual job. Every stage of the interview should reveal real evidence of sales skill — not just personality.
</InsightCard>

## The 3-Stage Interview Process

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Stage 1: Application Screen + Async Writing Task (30 min)">

**What happens:** You review the resume for relevant experience and evaluate the writing task from the JD application. No call yet.

**What you're looking for on the resume:**
- Prior SDR/AE experience (even 1 year counts)
- Specific metrics (quota attainment %, meetings booked/month, deals closed)
- Startup or small company experience (signals comfort with ambiguity)
- Tools used (HubSpot, Salesforce, Apollo, Outreach — signals they've done this before)

**What you're looking for in the writing task:**
- Did they follow the instructions exactly? (Ability to follow a playbook)
- Is the writing specific to your ICP and product? (Research skills)
- Does it sound like something a human would actually respond to? (Writing instincts)
- Is it personalized or generic? (Did they actually try?)

**Decision:** Advance to Stage 2, decline, or ask for a revision.

<ExampleCard label="Writing Task Assessment">
You asked for a 3-email cold outreach sequence targeting VP Operations at 50–200 person manufacturing firms.

Candidate A sent three emails that mentioned your company by name and cited a specific pain point relevant to manufacturing ops. Each email had a clear, low-friction CTA. The subject lines were short and specific. You would actually open these.

Candidate B sent three generic emails that could have been written for any B2B company. The subject lines were "Quick Question" and "Following Up." Nothing in the emails suggested they read your ICP or understood your product.

Candidate A moves forward. Candidate B doesn't — no matter how good their resume is.
</ExampleCard>

</Slide>
<Slide title="Stage 2: Video Interview + Live Roleplay (45 min)">

**Structure:**
- Minutes 0–5: Intro and warm-up
- Minutes 5–25: Behavioral questions (you ask, they answer)
- Minutes 25–45: Live roleplay (you play the prospect, they sell your product)

**Why the roleplay comes last:** You want them warmed up before you test them under pressure. A cold roleplay in the first five minutes produces worse results than the same roleplay after a normal conversation.

**Setting the roleplay up:**
"For the last part of our interview, I'd like to do a quick roleplay. I'm going to play a prospect — a VP Operations at a 100-person manufacturing company. I'm skeptical but open to hearing more. You've already sent me two emails and this is our first call. Ready?"

Then play a realistic version of your actual prospect. Use real objections you've heard. Be genuinely hard to sell.

</Slide>
<Slide title="Stage 3: Reference Checks + Offer (30 min)">

**When to do it:** After Stage 2, for your top 1–2 finalists only.

**Who to call:** Ask for 2 references — a former manager and a peer. Call both.

**How to get honest information:** Most references are pre-screened and trained to be positive. Break through this with specific questions that require specific answers:

1. "On a scale of 1–10, how likely would you be to hire [name] again if you had an opening?" — Anything below 8 is a red flag. 9–10 is genuine enthusiasm.

2. "What's one thing [name] would need to work on to be truly excellent in their next role?" — Probing for the one honest piece of feedback.

3. "How did [name] respond when they missed their number?" — Tests accountability and coachability under pressure.

**The rule:** If a reference pauses before answering or says "they're a great person" without specifics, probe deeper or treat it as neutral at best.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Behavioral Questions That Actually Predict Performance

Not all behavioral questions are equally useful. These are the ones that reveal the characteristics you care about most: accountability, coachability, resilience, and process.

<ProgressiveReveal title="8 Behavioral Questions + What to Listen For" persistKey="scale-L4-questions">

<RevealSection title="Q1: Tell me about a deal or outreach effort you're most proud of.">
**What you're listening for:** Specificity. A great answer includes a number, a specific situation, and a clear causal chain ("I did X, which led to Y"). A weak answer is vague ("I had a great quarter last year").

**Follow-up:** "What specifically did you do that you think made the difference?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q2: Tell me about a deal you lost and what you learned from it.">
**What you're listening for:** Accountability without self-flagellation. Do they own their part, or do they blame the product, the pricing, the leads? Can they articulate what they'd do differently? A red flag: "The deal was lost because of factors outside my control."

**Follow-up:** "What would you do differently today?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q3: What's your current or most recent outreach process, step by step?">
**What you're listening for:** Process-orientation. Can they describe a repeatable system? Or do they "wing it" every time? SDRs need to run scalable processes, not freestyle every day.

**Follow-up:** "What's your average reply rate on cold email, and what do you attribute it to?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q4: Describe a time a manager gave you feedback that changed how you sell.">
**What you're listening for:** Coachability. Can they name a specific piece of feedback, explain how they implemented it, and show what changed? A red flag: candidates who struggle to name any feedback they've received.

**Follow-up:** "How long did it take to implement that change?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q5: What happened the last time you missed your number?">
**What you're listening for:** Resilience and self-awareness. Did they diagnose the problem or just power through? Did they ask for help or try to fix it alone?

**Follow-up:** "What support did you ask for from your manager?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q6: What does a good week look like for you, in terms of specific activities?">
**What you're listening for:** Whether they think in metrics and activity targets, or vague outcomes. A great SDR should be able to say "I aim to send X emails, make Y calls, and book Z meetings per week."

**Follow-up:** "How do you manage your energy across a week — what's your most productive time of day?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q7: Why do you want to work at a company this early-stage?">
**What you're listening for:** Genuine alignment with startup life, not just desperation. The right answer includes something about ownership, building, or upside. A red flag: "I've always wanted to work at a startup" without specifics.

**Follow-up:** "What's the hardest thing about startup sales environments you've had to adapt to?"
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Q8: What questions do you have for me?">
**What you're listening for:** Curiosity and preparation. Great candidates ask about the ICP, the playbook, quota expectations, and how performance is measured. Weak candidates ask about PTO and benefits. The best sign: a candidate who asks "Can you walk me through the current sequence you're using and what's working?"
</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>

## Running the Live Roleplay

The roleplay is the most valuable 20 minutes of the interview. Here's how to run it effectively.

<InsightCard icon="🎭" title="Roleplay Setup Rules">
1. Brief the candidate clearly: what company they're calling from (yours), who the prospect is (your actual ICP), and where in the sales process this call falls (first call after 2 emails).
2. Play a realistic prospect — skeptical but not hostile. Use actual objections you hear in real life.
3. Take notes in real-time on the scorecard below. Don't try to remember it afterward.
4. After the roleplay, give the candidate a moment to self-assess before you share your scores.
</InsightCard>

## Common Interview Mistakes to Avoid

<PredictionGate
  question="A candidate is charming, articulate, and tells great stories in the behavioral interview. In the roleplay, they jump straight to pitching without asking any discovery questions, but recover when you object. Should you advance them to Stage 3?"
  persistKey="scale-L4-predict"
  type="choice"
  choices={[
    { id: "a", text: "Yes — charisma and recovery skills will make up for the discovery weakness" },
    { id: "b", text: "No — discovery is foundational and can't be ignored just because they're likable" },
    { id: "c", text: "Maybe — ask them why they didn't do discovery and see how they respond" }
  ]}
  correctId="c"
>
Option C is the right call. The roleplay weakness is real and you shouldn't ignore it. But the way to diagnose it is to ask about it directly: "I noticed you went straight to talking about our product before asking me any questions about my situation. Walk me through your thinking there." Their answer tells you everything: if they say "You're right, I jumped ahead — I would normally ask X, Y, Z before pitching," that's coachability. If they say "I thought I had enough context from the email exchange," that's a blind spot they don't see. Score accordingly.
</PredictionGate>

## After the Roleplay: The Self-Assessment

Before you share your scorecard scores, ask the candidate to self-assess. This is one of the most diagnostic moments in the entire interview.

<InsightCard icon="💡" title="The Self-Assessment Prompt">
"Before I share my observations — how do you think that went? What did you do well, and what would you do differently if we did it again?"

A highly coachable candidate will identify the same weaknesses you noted (or find ones you missed). A candidate with poor self-awareness will think they did better than they did. This gap between self-perception and actual performance is the #1 predictor of whether coaching will work.
</InsightCard>
