## The Statistic That Changes How You Think About Onboarding

60% of failed sales hires fail because of poor onboarding — not poor hiring.

That number, from Brandon Hall Group, inverts the way most founders think about sales hiring. They agonize over the hiring decision, then hand the new hire a laptop and a Zoom link on Day 1 and expect them to figure it out.

SDRs who go through structured onboarding ramp 50% faster than those who don't. The first 30 days determine the first 90 days. And the first 90 days determine whether the hire works at all.

This lesson gives you a week-by-week onboarding plan that transfers your sales knowledge — the ICP, voice, playbook, and judgment you've developed through 50+ meetings — to someone who's never sold your product before.

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Onboarding Goal">
By the end of Day 30, your new hire should be able to send outreach sequences, handle the first 10 minutes of a discovery call, and field basic objections — independently, without you on every call. That's the Day 30 bar. Not quota attainment. Not closing deals. Independence on the activities they own.
</InsightCard>

## Why "Just Read the Playbook" Doesn't Work

Before you build your onboarding plan, understand why documentation alone isn't enough.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="The Voice Transfer Problem">

Your sales motion works because of the way you say things, not just what you say. The timing of a pause before answering an objection. The way you phrase a discovery question to sound curious rather than interrogating. The specific words you use when a prospect says "I'm not interested."

None of that lives in a document. It lives in the recordings of your actual calls.

**The fix:** Build a call library of 10 recorded discovery calls and 5 recorded closing calls. Make listening to these the first 3 days of onboarding — before your hire sends a single outreach message.

</Slide>
<Slide title="The Knowledge Transfer Bottleneck">

You know things about your customers that you've never written down. Why the VP Operations at a 100-person manufacturer cares about this problem differently than the CFO. What "we're evaluating options" really means (usually: they've already decided and need to justify it). Why Q1 prospects move faster than Q4 prospects in your industry.

This tacit knowledge takes months to transfer. But you can accelerate it by making it explicit.

**The fix:** Before onboarding starts, write a "Why Customers Buy" document — 3–5 specific patterns you've observed in your closed deals. Not a positioning statement. A real, specific diagnosis: "The CFOs who buy fastest are the ones who've already had a conversation with their board about this metric. The VPs who buy fastest are the ones who missed a quarterly target in the last 6 months because of this exact problem."

</Slide>
<Slide title="The Autonomy Ramp Failure">

Many founders give new hires too much autonomy too fast, assuming competence. Or too little autonomy too long, micromanaging every message. Both kill performance.

The right model is a graduated autonomy ramp: heavy supervision in Weeks 1–2 (reviewing everything), then spot-check in Weeks 3–4 (reviewing 30% randomly), then metrics-only review from Month 2 (checking outcomes, not inputs).

**The fix:** Define explicitly what the hire owns at each phase, and what you still review. Ambiguity about who owns what is a trust killer.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The 30-60-90 Day Framework

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="Day 1–7: Immersion Week">

**Goal:** Understand the business, ICP, product, and playbook deeply enough to start supervised execution next week.

**Day 1 — Company and context:**
- Walkthrough of the company story: why you built this, who you serve, what makes you different
- Read the ICP document (Course 1) — then quiz them: "Describe our ideal customer in 3 sentences"
- Walk through your positioning document — how you'd explain the product to a prospect at a party

**Day 2–3 — Call library:**
- Listen to 5 recorded discovery calls (best ones — successful, where you can point to what worked)
- Listen to 2–3 calls that didn't go well (real learning happens here)
- Debrief each call: "What did you notice? What would you do differently? What questions did I ask that worked?"

**Day 4–5 — Playbook and tools:**
- Read the full sales playbook (Course 44) cover to cover
- Configure their CRM access, email, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, and sequencing tools
- Walk through the outreach sequences: what each email does, why, what a good reply looks like
- Practice: have them send you a "cold email to you as a prospect" — review and give feedback

**Day 6–7 — Product deep dive:**
- Demo of the product from a customer's perspective (not a sales demo)
- Meet the product team or read product documentation
- Goal: they should be able to answer "How does this actually work?" from a prospect without passing to you

**Your time investment:** 5–7 hours this week

</Slide>
<Slide title="Day 8–30: Supervised Execution">

**Goal:** Run your outbound sequences, book their first meetings, with you reviewing everything before it goes out.

**Outreach review — first 2 weeks:**
- They write outreach messages; you review before they send
- Provide specific written feedback on every message: "The opening here is too generic — personalize it to their recent company news" or "Good opener, weak CTA — try: 'Worth a 15-min call this week?'"
- After 2 weeks of review, shift to spot-check: review 30% of messages randomly

**Call shadowing:**
- They join every call you take for Weeks 2–3 — not as participants, just observers
- Debrief each call: "What were the three key moments? What did you notice? What would you try differently?"
- By Week 4: they take their first discovery call with you observing (camera off, listening)

**Activity targets during this phase:**
- Week 2: First outreach sequences sent (small batch — 20–30 contacts)
- Week 3: First replies managed (they draft responses, you review before sending)
- Week 4: First meeting booked from their own outreach

**Your time investment:** 4–5 hours per week

</Slide>
<Slide title="Day 31–60: Independent Execution with Oversight">

**Goal:** Full outreach ownership. Calls with feedback, not oversight.

**Outreach:** They own the sequence execution entirely. You spot-check 20% of messages randomly each week, not in advance.

**Calls:** They run their own discovery calls while you listen to recordings. Provide asynchronous feedback within 24 hours of each call.

**Metrics review:** Weekly 30-minute 1:1 to review activity metrics (emails sent, calls made, meetings booked), outcome metrics (reply rates, show rates, conversion to SQL), and one specific coaching point per week.

**Your time investment:** 3–4 hours per week

</Slide>
<Slide title="Day 61–90: Full Independence">

**Goal:** They own the outbound function. You manage by metrics, not activity.

**What "full independence" means:**
- They set their own daily activity schedule
- They manage their own prospect research and list-building
- You see outcomes in the CRM dashboard — you don't supervise inputs
- Weekly 30-minute 1:1 is for coaching, not status reporting

**What you still do:**
- Listen to 2 call recordings per week (you choose which, they don't know which)
- Provide one specific skill-improvement coaching note per week
- Monthly performance review vs. ramp targets

**Your time investment:** 1–2 hours per week

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Voice Transfer Kit

The hardest thing to transfer isn't the script — it's the tone. Document yours before your hire starts.

<ProgressiveReveal title="Building Your Voice Transfer Kit" persistKey="scale-L6-voice">

<RevealSection title="1. Your Call Recording Library">
Curate 15 recordings before Day 1. Organize them into: Best 5 discovery calls (what to emulate), Worst 3 calls (what to avoid), 5 objection-heavy calls (how to handle pushback), 2 closing calls (what a deal looks like in late stages).

Tag each recording with 3 bullet points: "Listen for X at 4:23," "Notice how Y was handled at 11:40," "This is what a no-show follow-up sounds like."
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="2. Your Messaging Guidelines Document">
One page. Four sections: Approved language (phrases that work, with examples), Banned phrases (never send these), Tone principles (how we sound in writing), CTA guide (what a great call-to-action looks like vs. a weak one).

This is the document your hire reads every time they're unsure whether a message sounds right.
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="3. Your Swipe File">
20+ approved messages, ready to use. Not templates — actual sent messages that got positive responses. Organized by: Cold intro email, Follow-up email 1, Follow-up email 2, LinkedIn connection request, LinkedIn DM after connection, Voicemail script.

Your hire can use these word-for-word or adapt them. Both are fine. The point is they have proven starting points.
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="4. FAQ Document">
The 20 questions prospects ask most often, with approved answers. Not scripts — frameworks for how to respond. "When they say 'how much does it cost?' before the demo, we say X. When they say 'we already use competitor Y,' we say Z."
</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="5. The 'When in Doubt' Protocol">
Three clear rules for escalation: (1) If a prospect asks about pricing and you're not sure how to respond, put the meeting on hold and DM me. (2) If a prospect asks about custom features or enterprise terms, don't answer — forward to me. (3) If you're about to send something you're not sure about, send it to me first.

This prevents off-brand messaging from going out before your hire has the judgment to filter themselves.
</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>

## The Common Onboarding Failure Modes

<PredictionGate
  question="Your new SDR has been onboarded for 3 weeks. They're sending outreach but their reply rates are 1.5% (your sequences get 7%). What's the most likely cause?"
  persistKey="scale-L6-predict"
  type="choice"
  choices={[
    { id: "a", text: "They're not working hard enough — need more activity volume" },
    { id: "b", text: "The sequences aren't customized — they're sending templates without personalization" },
    { id: "c", text: "The ICP targeting is off — they're reaching the wrong prospects" }
  ]}
  correctId="b"
>
In most cases with new hires, the gap between your reply rate and theirs comes from personalization, not volume. They're running the template, but not adding the specific first line or ICP-relevant detail that made your version work. Pull 5 of their actual sent emails and compare them to yours side-by-side. The difference will be obvious. The fix: return to the voice transfer kit and message guidelines. Add a rule: "The first sentence of every cold email must reference something specific about this prospect or company — not a generic opener."
</PredictionGate>
