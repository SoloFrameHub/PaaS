## The Compensation Mistake That Kills Early Sales Hires

Two compensation mistakes destroy more first sales hires than bad hiring decisions.

The first: **underpaying base and over-indexing on commission**, hoping the upside will attract motivated candidates. In reality, a low base signals financial instability and attracts either desperate applicants or people who plan to leave the moment a better base comes along.

The second: **paying too high a base with too little variable**, removing the incentive to perform. A sales hire with a $55K base and $3K variable has almost no financial motivation to grind past their comfort level. The commission should feel meaningful enough to chase.

Great compensation design does three things: it attracts the right candidate, it aligns their incentives with your revenue goals, and it feels fair when they perform well. This lesson gives you the exact numbers and structures to make that happen.

<InsightCard icon="🎯" title="The Incentive Alignment Principle">
The best compensation plans feel generous when your hire performs well and fair when they don't. If your hire hits 100% of quota and feels underpaid, they'll leave. If they miss quota for 3 months and feel hopeless about catching up, they'll stop trying. Design the plan so the numbers make sense at every attainment level.
</InsightCard>

## SDR Compensation: The Full Structure

SDRs are measured on activity and pipeline generation — meetings booked, qualified leads, or SQLs (Sales Qualified Leads). Their variable compensation should pay on the outputs they directly control.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="SDR Base Salary">

**Market rate (2025–2026):** $40,000–55,000/year

**Geographic adjustments:**
- High cost-of-living markets (SF, NYC, Boston, Seattle): add 15–25%
- Mid-cost markets (Austin, Denver, Chicago, Atlanta): standard range
- Low cost-of-living or fully remote: standard range, sometimes 5–10% below

**What to offer as a solo founder:**
- Start in the $42,000–48,000 range for a first hire
- Higher base signals stability; lower base signals risk
- If you're below $42K, be very transparent about why and compensate with higher equity

**The 80/20 rule:** SDR compensation should be roughly 80% base, 20% variable. An SDR at $45K base + $11K variable ($56K OTE) follows this structure.

</Slide>
<Slide title="SDR Variable Compensation">

**Two common structures:**

**Structure 1: Per qualified meeting booked**
- Pay $50–100 per qualified meeting that your SDR books and that you hold
- What counts as "qualified": prospect matches your ICP criteria, confirmed appointment, shows up
- This is the cleanest structure — SDR earns on what they directly control (meetings booked)
- Example: $75 per meeting × 9 meetings/month target = $675/month at target ($8,100/year)

**Structure 2: Per SQL generated**
- Pay per prospect that meets your SQL criteria (typically: ICP match + expressed interest + next step scheduled)
- Slightly more complex to define and track, but better aligned if meeting quality is an issue
- Example: $100 per SQL × 6 SQLs/month = $600/month at target ($7,200/year)

**Which to use:** Per-meeting is simpler and works well early. Switch to per-SQL if you find your SDR is booking meetings that don't convert because the prospect doesn't actually fit your ICP.

</Slide>
<Slide title="SDR Quota and Ramp">

**Setting the quota:**
- Industry benchmark: 8–15 qualified meetings per month for B2B outbound
- For a first hire with a 40-hour week and access to your proven sequences: target 8–10/month
- Don't set quota below 6/month (you could almost do this yourself) or above 15/month (unrealistic without strong tooling)

**The ramp schedule:**
- Month 1: 50% of quota (4–5 meetings) — they're learning the playbook, ICP, and tools
- Month 2: 75% of quota (6–7 meetings) — supervised execution, getting feedback
- Month 3+: 100% of quota (8–10 meetings) — full responsibility

**What ramp means financially:**
- At $75/meeting with an 8-meeting quota, Month 1 targets 4 meetings ($300 variable)
- Month 3+: 8 meetings = $600/month variable
- Your SDR's total compensation in Month 1 is mostly base — plan for this in your cash flow

</Slide>
<Slide title="SDR Accelerator">

**Why accelerators matter:** Without an upside, a high performer who consistently hits 125% of quota has no financial reason to push further. An accelerator rewards the behavior you want.

**Simple accelerator design:**
- At 100% of quota: standard rate ($75/meeting)
- At 101–120% of quota: 1.5x rate ($112.50/meeting)
- At 120%+ of quota: 2x rate ($150/meeting)

**Example calculation:**
- SDR books 12 meetings in a month (quota is 9)
- Meetings 1–9: 9 × $75 = $675
- Meetings 10–12: 3 × $112.50 = $337.50
- Total variable: $1,012.50 (vs. $675 at quota — a 50% bonus for 33% overperformance)

**Note:** Make sure your accelerator is funded by the revenue those extra meetings generate. If 3 extra meetings close at $3K each = $9K revenue, $337.50 in accelerator comp is a great trade.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## AE Compensation: The Full Structure

AE compensation is fundamentally different from SDR compensation. AEs are measured on closed revenue — dollars they bring in. The variable percentage is higher because the output is directly tied to company revenue.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="AE Base Salary">

**Market rate (2025–2026):** $60,000–80,000/year

**The OTE principle:** AE total compensation (base + variable) is called OTE (On-Target Earnings). Standard AE OTE: $78,000–120,000/year. The base/variable split is typically 60/40 or 70/30.

**Solo founder guidance:**
- First AE hire: $65,000–75,000 base is appropriate for a strong candidate with 2–4 years AE experience
- You're asking them to join an early-stage company with higher execution risk — the upside (equity, OTE) compensates for the risk premium
- AEs with 5+ years experience at strong companies typically won't accept below $70K base

</Slide>
<Slide title="AE Variable Compensation">

**Commission structure:** AEs earn a percentage of the revenue they close.

**Standard range:** 8–15% of first-year contract value

**Example:**
- AE base: $70,000
- Commission: 10% of revenue closed
- Annual quota: $300,000 (deals they need to close)
- At 100% of quota: $30,000 in variable comp
- OTE: $100,000/year

**Key principle — the quota-to-OTE ratio:** AE quota should be 3–5x their OTE. At $100K OTE, quota should be $300K–500K annual. This ratio exists so your hire creates real leverage — they generate 3–5x more revenue than they cost.

**What this means for your math:** If you're at $30K MRR ($360K ARR), hiring an AE with a $300K quota means they'd need to roughly double your current ARR in year one. Is that realistic? If yes, an AE makes sense. If not, hire an SDR first and build pipeline to justify the AE hire later.

</Slide>
<Slide title="AE Ramp and Quota">

**AE ramp timeline:**
- Month 1: Onboarding, shadow calls, no quota expectation
- Month 2: 25% of quota — first independent calls, supervised deals
- Month 3: 50% of quota — small pipeline developing
- Month 4: 75% of quota — deals in late stages
- Month 5+: 100% of quota — full responsibility

**Why AE ramp takes longer than SDR:** SDRs have measurable output (meetings) within weeks. AEs work deals that take 30–90 days to close. You won't see full-quota revenue until their Month 3 or 4 pipeline starts closing.

**Financial planning:** Budget for 4 months of mostly-base AE compensation before you see revenue attributed to them. At $70K base, that's $23,333 in salary before meaningful commission is earned.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Equity: The Startup Compensation Advantage

You can't compete with enterprise base salaries. Equity is how you recruit people who would otherwise take jobs paying $10–20K more in base.

<InsightCard icon="💡" title="Equity Ranges for First Sales Hires">
- SDR (first hire): 0.05–0.15% on a 4-year vest with 1-year cliff
- AE (first hire): 0.10–0.30% on a 4-year vest with 1-year cliff
- Fractional sales leader: 0.10–0.50% (smaller % but valuable given their seniority)

The earlier the stage, the higher the grant — because the risk is higher. At pre-seed, 0.25% for a great AE is appropriate. At $500K ARR, 0.10–0.15% is more typical.

When presenting equity, always frame the potential upside: "At our current trajectory, a 0.15% stake could be worth X at a 5x exit" — even rough math makes equity real.
</InsightCard>

## Compensation Documentation

Once you've designed the plan, document it clearly. Comp ambiguity destroys trust.

<FlipCard
  front="What must be in writing before Day 1"
  back="1. Base salary (annual + monthly). 2. Variable rate ($ per meeting or % of revenue). 3. Quota (monthly and annual). 4. Ramp schedule (Month 1, 2, 3+ targets). 5.
