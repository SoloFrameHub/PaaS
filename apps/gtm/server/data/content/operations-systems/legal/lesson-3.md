## The Cash Flow Problem You Created

Here's the scenario: you closed a $15,000 project in January. You put Net 30 terms in the contract because that's what you saw in a template. You delivered the work in full on February 1st.

You get paid on March 15th.

That's 43 days after delivery. 73 days after the project started. You spent January doing the work, February waiting, and most of March still waiting.

**Net 30 means 38 days on average.** Not 30. Because clients pay 8 days past terms on average, according to Fundbox and Dun & Bradstreet data. Plan for it.

The payment terms you set — and enforce — determine your real cash flow. Not your projected cash flow. Not the cash flow you'd have if clients paid on time. Your actual cash flow.

<InsightCard icon="⚖️" title="The Stark Reality">
60% of small businesses have experienced cash flow problems due to late payments. This is a self-inflicted wound for most of them — they set Net 30 without understanding the actual float, skipped deposits, and had no late fee mechanism. You can fix all three today.
</InsightCard>

## Net Terms Explained

<FlipCard front="Net 7" back="Payment due 7 days after invoice date. Standard for coaching, short consulting engagements, and digital products. Most appropriate when you've already delivered the work and want fast turnaround." />

<FlipCard front="Net 14" back="Payment due 14 days after invoice. The recommended default for most solo founder service businesses. Short enough to maintain cash flow, long enough that clients can process internally." />

<FlipCard front="Net 30" back="Payment due 30 days after invoice. Standard for enterprise and government clients. Actual average payment: ~38 days. Only accept Net 30 when required by the client's size or procurement process." />

<FlipCard front="Due on Receipt" back="Payment due immediately upon invoice delivery. Standard for SaaS subscriptions, digital products, and prepaid services. Works best when payment is automated (Stripe, PayPal)." />

<FlipCard front="Net 45 / Net 60" back="Enterprise and government payment terms. Almost never appropriate for solo founders. If a client insists on Net 60, require a larger upfront deposit to offset the float." />

## Which Terms Should You Set?

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="The Payment Terms Matrix">

| Client Type | Recommended Terms | Deposit | Late Fee |
|-------------|------------------|---------|----------|
| Solo / Small startup | Net 14 | 50% upfront | 1.5%/month |
| Mid-market company | Net 14-30 | 25-33% upfront | 1.5%/month |
| Enterprise / Government | Net 30-45 | None (they won't) | Per contract |
| Coaching / Consulting | Net 7 or Due on Receipt | 100% or 50% | 1.5%/month |
| SaaS subscription | Prepaid (monthly/annual) | N/A | Auto-retry + dunning |
| Digital products / Courses | Due on Receipt | 100% | N/A |

**The default rule:** Unless a client specifically requires longer terms, start with Net 14. It's long enough to be professional; short enough to maintain cash flow.

</Slide>

<Slide title="The Deposit Strategy">

50% upfront deposit collection eliminates 80% of non-payment risk. Here's why it works:

1. **Skin in the game** — A client who has paid you $5,000 is invested in seeing the project succeed. They'll respond to your emails. They'll make decisions. They'll show up.

2. **Filters bad clients** — Clients who balk at a deposit are often the ones who dispute invoices later. You've learned something valuable before you start the work.

3. **Cash flow** — You're working with the client's money, not your own. Your personal finances don't fund their project.

**Structure by deal size:**
- Under $10K: 50% upfront, 50% on delivery
- $10-50K: 33% upfront, 33% at midpoint milestone, 34% on delivery
- Over $50K: Monthly milestone payments, due within 14 days of milestone acceptance

</Slide>

<Slide title="Annual Prepay for Recurring Revenue">

If you have clients on monthly retainers, offer an annual prepay option. The formula:

**Standard terms:** $3,000/month × 12 = $36,000/year
**Annual prepay offer:** $36,000 × 0.83 = $29,880 (17% discount)

What you get: $29,880 in January. The client gets: $6,120 in savings.

Annual prepaid contracts have **15-25% lower churn** than monthly contracts (ProfitWell data). You get cash upfront and better retention. This is one of the highest-leverage financial levers for solo founders with recurring revenue.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## The Late Fee Clause: Write It Right

<InsightCard icon="⚖️" title="Enforceability Requires Contract Language">
Late fees are only enforceable if stated in the contract. You cannot charge a late fee you didn't agree to in writing. The time to add the clause is before the project starts — not when the invoice is past due.
</InsightCard>

**The standard late fee clause:**

"Invoices not paid within [NET TERMS] days of the invoice date shall accrue interest at the rate of 1.5% per month (18% per annum) or the maximum rate permitted by applicable law, whichever is less."

This language:
- Specifies the rate (1.5%/month = 18% annual, the most commonly upheld rate in US B2B)
- Includes a safe harbor for jurisdictions with lower caps
- Is clear enough that any judge can apply it without interpretation

**On $10,000 overdue by 30 days:** $150 in late fees. On $50,000 overdue by 60 days: $1,500.

It's not the revenue that matters — it's the leverage. Clients know the clock is running.

## The Collections Escalation Ladder

When an invoice is past due, your response should be systematic and escalating — not emotional, not sporadic.

<ProgressiveReveal title="The Collections Escalation Ladder" persistKey="legal-L3-reveal">

<RevealSection title="Day 0: Invoice Sent">

Send the invoice the moment the trigger event occurs (project delivered, milestone accepted, first of the month for retainers). Don't wait until you "have time." Use automated invoicing in FreshBooks, Wave, or Stripe so it goes out immediately.

**Include on the invoice:** Your late fee terms (reference the contract), the exact due date, and all available payment methods.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day +1 Past Due: Automated Friendly Reminder">

This goes out automatically from your invoicing tool. Tone: warm, assumes it was an oversight.

**Template:**
"Hi [Name], just a friendly reminder that Invoice [#] for $[X] was due [date]. If you've already sent payment, please disregard this notice. Otherwise, you can pay here: [payment link]. Let me know if you have any questions."

**This single step recovers 82% of late payments.** Most late invoices are forgetfulness, not malice.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day +7: Personal Email">

This one comes from you, not your invoicing tool. One sentence. No drama.

**Template:**
"Hi [Name], just checking in on Invoice [#] — wanted to make sure it didn't get lost. Let me know if there's anything holding it up on your end."

This opens the door for the client to tell you there's an issue (approval bottleneck, payment system problem) so you can solve it, rather than letting it fester.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day +14: Direct Phone Call">

Pick up the phone. This is the escalation that separates routine late payments from genuine problems.

**What to say:**
"Hi [Name], I'm calling to follow up on Invoice [#] for $[X]. It's now [X] days past due. I wanted to make sure everything's okay on your end and figure out how we can get this resolved."

Pause. Listen. If there's a legitimate problem, problem-solve. If they're avoiding you, that's also important information.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day +21: Formal Notice with Late Fee Applied">

Send a written notice — email with delivery confirmation. Apply the late fee per your contract.

**Template:**
"Dear [Name], this is a formal notice that Invoice [#] for $[X] remains unpaid as of [date] — [X] days past the agreed payment terms. Pursuant to our contract dated [date], a late fee of 1.5% per month is now accruing. The current balance including late fees is $[X]. Payment is due immediately. Please respond within 5 business days."

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day +30: Pause Work + Final Demand">

If this client has an ongoing engagement, pause work. Send a final demand letter.

"Due to the outstanding balance of $[X] on Invoice [#], all work on [project] is paused effective [date]. Work will resume upon receipt of full payment. I remain available to discuss resolution."

You're not threatening litigation — you're stating the obvious consequence. Make it factual.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Day +45: Decision Point">

Three paths:
1. **Small claims court** — For amounts under $10,000 (threshold varies by state). No attorney needed. File the claim, show up, present your contract and invoice. Usually resolves in your favor when documentation is solid.
2. **Collections agency** — For amounts over $10,000. They take 25-40% of what they collect. Worth it when you've written off the relationship.
3. **Write-off** — For small amounts where the cost of recovery exceeds the value. Document in your accounting system for tax purposes.

</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>
