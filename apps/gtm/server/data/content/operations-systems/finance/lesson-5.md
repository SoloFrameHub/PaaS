## The Feast-or-Famine Trap

March: $28,000 in invoices. You close a major project, two new clients start retainers, and a big proposal converts. Life is good. You invest in new tools, maybe hire a part-time contractor.

April: $8,000 in invoices. One project ends. The two new clients are in their slow onboarding month. The big-proposal client is in their procurement cycle.

You have $800 in your business account by April 28th.

This is not a business failure. This is lumpy revenue — the defining financial characteristic of solo founder businesses. Project-based work creates feast-or-famine cycles by design. Even SaaS revenue is lumpy in early stages when customer counts are small and a single cancellation moves your MRR by 10-20%.

**82% of small businesses fail due to cash flow problems.** Most of those businesses were profitable on paper. The problem wasn't insufficient revenue — it was insufficient visibility into when cash would arrive and when it would leave.

<InsightCard icon="💰" title="The Counter-Intuitive Truth">
Solo businesses with a 13-week cash flow forecast are 30% less likely to run out of cash — not because the forecast magically creates money, but because seeing the problem 6-8 weeks in advance changes your behavior. You sell harder, collect faster, and cut expenses before the crisis hits.
</InsightCard>

## The 2-3 Month Buffer Rule

Before anything else, establish your baseline metric:

**Operating Expense Floor** = Your monthly non-negotiable expenses: tools, subscriptions, contractors, insurance, your personal draw (the minimum you need to pay yourself to meet personal obligations).

**Cash Buffer Target** = 2-3x your Operating Expense Floor, held in a separate savings account that you don't touch for operations.

**Example:**
- Tools and subscriptions: $500/month
- Part-time contractor: $1,200/month
- Personal draw minimum: $4,000/month
- Operating Expense Floor: $5,700/month
- Cash Buffer Target: $11,400-17,100

If your buffer drops below 1x monthly expenses, you're in emergency territory. More on that below.

## The 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast

The 13-week (one quarter) horizon is the standard for solo founder cash management. Long enough to see problems before they arrive; short enough to be accurate.

Build this in Google Sheets. Update it every Friday. It takes 20-30 minutes per week once you've built the template.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="The Forecast Structure">

Each row is one week. Each week has four columns:

**Expected Cash In:** What revenue do you actually expect to receive this week (not invoice, but receive)?
- Signed contracts with known payment dates
- Recurring subscriptions that renew automatically
- Invoices sent with likely payment timing (use your historical average, not the stated terms)

**Expected Cash Out:** What expenses will you pay this week?
- Tool subscriptions (note their billing dates)
- Contractor payments
- Advertising spend
- Personal draw
- Any quarterly or annual payments (insurance, software licenses)

**Net Cash Flow:** Cash In minus Cash Out for the week.

**Running Balance:** Previous week's balance + this week's Net Cash Flow.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Color Coding the Forecast">

Color-code the Running Balance column:
- **Green:** Greater than 3 months of your operating expense floor
- **Yellow:** 1-3 months of operating expenses
- **Red:** Less than 1 month of operating expenses

When you see yellow or red appearing in future weeks, you have time to act. That's the entire point.

**The 6-8 week window:** With a 13-week forecast, you see a cash crunch 6-8 weeks before it hits. That's enough time to close a deal, accelerate collections, cut a discretionary expense, or offer an existing client an annual prepay discount.

</Slide>

<Slide title="Forecast Accuracy Tips">

Revenue forecasting is the hard part. Use these rules:

1. **Only include revenue you're highly confident will arrive.** Signed contracts yes; verbal commitments no; proposals no.
2. **Use historical average collection time.** If your Net 14 clients actually pay in 22 days on average, use 22 days in your forecast.
3. **Assume all SaaS revenue arrives as scheduled.** Subscriptions are the most predictable line item you have.
4. **Add a 10-15% buffer to expense estimates.** Costs almost always end up higher than you plan.

Update the forecast every Friday by removing the completed week and adding the week 13 projection. It stays current.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Revenue Smoothing Strategies

The best cash flow management is prevention: reducing the lumpiness of your revenue before it creates crises.

<ProgressiveReveal title="Revenue Smoothing Playbook" persistKey="finance-L5-reveal">

<RevealSection title="Strategy 1: Convert Project Clients to Retainers">

This is the highest-impact move available to service businesses. A client who pays you $6,000 per project twice a year gives you two lump payments and four months of nothing. A client on a $1,000/month retainer gives you 12 predictable payments.

**How to propose it:** "Based on how much we've worked together, I'd like to offer a monthly retainer arrangement that gives you ongoing priority access and simplifies both of our billing. Instead of project-by-project invoicing, it would be [X]/month for [scope]."

**Ideal retainer scope:** Advisory access, a set number of hours, priority turnaround, and a defined deliverable package.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Strategy 2: Annual Prepay with a Discount">

For any client paying monthly (SaaS or retainer), offer an annual prepay at a 15-20% discount.

**The math:** $1,000/month × 12 = $12,000 annual.
Annual prepay offer: $10,200 (15% discount).
You receive $10,200 in January. The client saves $1,800.

Your cash flow for the year is dramatically smoother. Their retention is higher (annual prepaid SaaS has 15-25% lower churn than monthly). Both parties win.

**Implementation:** Make the offer at contract renewal, at year-end planning conversations, or proactively when your forecast shows a yellow/red period coming.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Strategy 3: Stagger Client Start Dates">

When possible, don't close three major projects in the same week. Stagger project starts — and therefore project end dates — across the month and quarter.

This smooths out both the delivery peaks (when you're most stretched) and the revenue peaks (which mask the valleys that follow).

**In practice:** If you're closing multiple deals, structure one to start immediately and another to start in 3-4 weeks. Most clients can accommodate a reasonable start date.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Strategy 4: The 10-20% Buffer Rule">

During feast periods, don't immediately convert all excess revenue to lifestyle. Set aside 10-20% of all revenue above your operating expense floor into your buffer account until you hit your 3-month target.

**The mindset shift:** "Revenue above my floor is not available to spend until my buffer is full."

Once your buffer is at 3 months, revisit. But until then, treat it as untouchable.

</RevealSection>

<RevealSection title="Strategy 5: Never Stop Prospecting During Feast">

The most common cause of famine is stopping prospecting during feast. When you're slammed with work, outreach stops. 60-90 days later, when current projects end, there's nothing in the pipeline.

**The rule:** Protect 3-5 hours per week for business development — even during your busiest months. These hours are non-negotiable.

</RevealSection>

</ProgressiveReveal>

## The STAR Emergency Protocol

When your cash buffer drops below 1 month — the red zone — execute the STAR protocol immediately. Not "this week." Now.

<SlideNavigation>
<Slide title="S — Slash">

Cut all non-essential expenses immediately. This week.

**What to cut:**
- Any tool you haven't used in the last 30 days
- Trial subscriptions you forgot to cancel
- Advertising spend on channels with high CAC
- Any contractor work that's discretionary
- Premium tier on any tool when a free tier would do

**How to find cuts:** Export your last 3 months of credit card statements. Flag every subscription. For each one: did this generate revenue or reduce your time in the last 30 days? If no, cancel.

**Estimated recovery:** Most solo founders find $500-1,500/month in cuts they didn't know they were making.

</Slide>

<Slide title="T — Tighten">

Accelerate all outstanding collections immediately.

**Actions:**
1. Pull every open invoice — make a list
2. For invoices 1-7 days overdue: send a personal email today, not the automated one
3. For invoices 8-21 days overdue: call today
4. For invoices 22+ days overdue: send formal notice with late fee + pause any ongoing work
5. Consider offering a small early payment incentive: "If you can process this by [date], I'll waive the late fee"

**The psychology:** When you're in a cash crisis, every dollar matters. An invoice that's 10 days late represents real cost to you right now. Don't wait for your automation to catch up.

</Slide>

<Slide title="A — Accelerate">

Accelerate revenue from your existing pipeline.

**Actions:**
1. Offer annual prepay discounts to your best recurring clients: "Prepay for the next 12 months and get 15% off"
2. Call your warmest pipeline prospects — not to pressure, but to ask what's needed to move forward
3. Propose a Phase 1 to stalled projects — a smaller first deliverable that generates immediate revenue
4. Offer existing clients a "priority sprint" at a premium rate if you have capacity

**The mindset:** You're not being desperate — you're being proactive. Clients who have a positive relationship with you often have the budget; they just haven't felt urgency to move.

</Slide>

<Slide title="R — Revenue Bridge">

If Slash, Tighten, and Accelerate don't close the gap fast enough, take a short-term revenue bridge.

**Options:**
- Freelance projects in your skill area outside your normal business
- Contract consulting work for a former employer or adjacent company
- Workshops or training sessions you can sell quickly to your existing audience
- Short-term service work that's below your normal rate but above zero

**The principle:** Revenue bridge is triage, not strategy. Use it to buy time to fix the underlying issue. Don't let it become a permanent distraction from your main business.

</Slide>
</SlideNavigation>

## Client Concentration: The Hidden Risk

<InsightCard icon="💰" title="The 30% Rule">
Revenue concentration risk: if any single client represents more than 30% of your total revenue, you are one email away from a crisis. A single cancellation email from your largest client should not threaten your business survival.
</InsightCard>
