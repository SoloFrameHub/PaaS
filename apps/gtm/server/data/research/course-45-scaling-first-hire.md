## LESSON 1: Prerequisites: What Must Be True Before You Hire (50 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The "Earned the Right" Checklist** — You can hire when: (1) You've personally closed 10+ deals. (2) You've held 50+ meetings. (3) You have documented scripts and sequences that work. (4) You can articulate why customers buy in 2 sentences. (5) Your playbook (Course 44) is written and working.
2. **Why Founders Hire Too Early** — Impatience ("I hate selling"), hope ("a salesperson will figure it out"), or distraction ("I should be building product"). None of these produce a successful hire. If you can't sell it yourself, no one can.
3. **The Founder-First Principle** — Only the founder can discover the first sales motion. Hires can optimize and scale a proven motion. They cannot discover one. Hiring before product-market fit wastes money and burns through talent.
4. **Revenue Thresholds** — Fractional: $10K+/month MRR (you can afford $2-5K/month for part-time help). Full-time SDR: $20-30K+/month MRR (you need $4-6K/month fully loaded). Full-time AE: $50K+/month MRR (you need $8-12K/month fully loaded).
5. **Documentation Requirements** — Before hiring, you must have: (1) ICP document (Course 1). (2) Positioning and messaging (Course 2). (3) Outreach templates and sequences (Courses 7, 8, 24). (4) Discovery call script (Course 14). (5) Objection handling database (Course 17). (6) CRM with pipeline (Course 40). (7) Sales playbook (Course 44).
6. **The Cost of Hiring Wrong** — Bad hire cost: 3-6 months of salary + management time + opportunity cost + damage to prospect relationships. For a $5K/month SDR, a bad hire costs $25-40K total. Hire slowly.

## LESSON 2: Fractional vs Part-Time vs Full-Time: Which First? (55 min)

### Key Topics

1. **Fractional Sales Leader ($2,000-5,000/month)** — Experienced sales leader working 5-15 hours/week. Sets up process, coaches you, may do some selling. Best for: founders who need guidance, not just execution. Typical engagement: 3-6 months.
2. **Part-Time SDR ($1,500-3,000/month)** — Dedicated prospector working 15-25 hours/week. Handles outbound sequences, follow-ups, qualification, meeting booking. Best for: founders who are good at closing but need more pipeline.
3. **Full-Time SDR ($3,500-5,500/month fully loaded)** — Dedicated prospector working 40 hours/week. Full outbound responsibility. Best for: founders with proven outbound motion and enough pipeline to keep an SDR busy.
4. **Full-Time Account Executive ($5,000-10,000/month fully loaded)** — Handles discovery, demos, proposals, closing. Best for: founders who want to step out of sales entirely. Requires very mature playbook and high revenue.
5. **The Progression Path** — Most solo founders should follow: Fractional (3-6 months) → Part-Time SDR (3-6 months) → Full-Time SDR (6-12 months) → Full-Time AE (when ready to leave sales). Skip steps only if revenue supports it.
6. **When to Choose Each** — Fractional: you need process help, not just hands. PT SDR: you need more meetings, not help closing. FT SDR: meetings are your bottleneck, and you close well. FT AE: you want to exit sales and focus on product/operations.

## LESSON 3: Writing the Job Description (45 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The Anti-Job-Description Approach** — Most sales job descriptions are wishlists of impossible requirements. Solo founder JDs should be honest: small company, big upside, scrappy culture, real ownership.
2. **JD Structure** — (1) The hook: why this role is exciting (real equity, founder-level impact, build the team). (2) The role: specific daily activities, not vague responsibilities. (3) Requirements: max 5, prioritized (must-have vs nice-to-have). (4) Compensation: transparent range. (5) How to apply: specific instructions (include a sample email).
3. **SDR Job Description Template** — "We're hiring our first SDR. You'll own outbound prospecting for [company]. Day-to-day: research prospects, run email and LinkedIn sequences, book meetings for the founder. You'll use [tools]. We need someone who has done outbound before and thrives with autonomy."
4. **AE Job Description Template** — "We're hiring our first Account Executive. You'll own the full sales cycle from discovery to close. The founder has built the playbook and closed 50+ deals — now we need someone to scale it. You'll own a quota and have real equity."
5. **Red Flags to Avoid in JDs** — "Rock star," "Guru," "Ninja" (signals immaturity). "Must have 10+ years experience" (over-indexed on seniority). "Must know our exact industry" (too restrictive). "Self-starter" without specifics (everyone says this).
6. **Attracting Startup-Savvy Talent** — Lead with: equity/upside, ownership, direct access to founder, build the team, shape the culture. These matter more than base salary for the right candidates.

### Data Points & Statistics

| Statistic | Source | Context |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Job posts with transparent compensation get 30-50% more qualified applicants | LinkedIn Talent Solutions | Always include a comp range |
| "Requirements" lists longer than 5 items reduce applications by 40% | Indeed research | Keep it to 5 must-haves |
| 60% of sales candidates prioritize autonomy and growth over base salary | RepVue surveys | Startup advantages in recruiting |
| Application instructions (e.g., "include a sample email") filter 40% of low-effort applicants | Hiring best practices | Built-in screening |
| Average time to fill an SDR role: 30-45 days | Recruiting benchmarks | Start early |

### Frameworks & Models

- **JD Template Structure:**

| Section | Content | Length |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Hook | Why this role is exciting (equity, ownership, impact) | 2-3 sentences |
| About Us | What the company does, stage, traction | 3-4 sentences |
| The Role | Daily activities (not responsibilities) | 5-7 bullet points |
| Requirements | Must-haves (3-4) + Nice-to-haves (1-2) | 5 total max |
| Compensation | Base range + variable structure + equity/perks | 3-4 lines |
| How to Apply | Specific instructions with screening task | 2-3 sentences |

## LESSON 4: The Interview Process for Sales Roles (50 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The 3-Stage Interview Process** — Stage 1: Application screen + async task (30 min). Stage 2: Video interview + roleplay (45 min). Stage 3: Reference check + offer. Total time investment: ~3 hours per finalist.
2. **Stage 1: Application Screen + Async Task** — Review resume for relevant experience (prior SDR/AE role, startup experience). Send an async task: "Write a 3-email cold outreach sequence targeting [our ICP]. You have 48 hours." This tests: writing ability, research skills, initiative, and ability to follow instructions.
3. **Stage 2: Video Interview + Live Roleplay** — First 20 min: behavioral questions (tell me about a deal you lost and what you learned; describe your prospecting process; what did you do when you missed quota). Last 25 min: live roleplay — you play the prospect, they sell your product. Score: discovery questions, listening, objection handling, close attempt.
4. **The Roleplay Scoring Rubric** — (1) Did they ask about my situation before pitching? (2) Did they listen and adapt? (3) Did they handle my objection (I'm not interested / too expensive) with LARA? (4) Did they attempt a clear next step? (5) Would I actually take a meeting with this person?
5. **Stage 3: Reference Checks** — Call 2 references (former managers or peers). Ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how likely would you be to hire this person again?" Anything below 8 is a red flag. Ask: "What's one thing they need to improve?"
6. **Common Interview Mistakes** — (1) Only asking theoretical questions (always include roleplay). (2) Hiring based on charisma (charisma ≠ sales skill). (3) Not checking references. (4) Rushing the process (take 2-3 weeks, not 2-3 days).

### Data Points & Statistics

| Statistic | Source | Context |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Roleplays in interviews improve hire quality by 35-50% | Hiring research | Best predictor of actual sales performance |
| Async tasks filter 50-60% of candidates who can't write | Hiring best practices | Writing is a core sales skill |
| References are the most underused and most predictive hiring tool | Laszlo Bock (Google) | Always call references |
| "Would you hire them again?" score of 8+ predicts 80% of successful hires | Reference check research | The single best reference question |
| Time-to-hire for sales roles: 30-45 days average | Recruiting benchmarks | Don't rush quality for speed |

### Frameworks & Models

- **3-Stage Interview Process:**

| Stage | Activity | Time | Purpose |
|-------|----------|------|---------|
| 1 | Application screen + async writing task | 30 min review | Filter for writing ability, research skills |
| 2 | Video interview (20 min behavioral + 25 min roleplay) | 45 min | Assess sales skills, personality, judgment |
| 3 | Reference checks (2 calls) + offer | 30 min | Verify track record |

- **Roleplay Scoring Rubric (1-5 per category):**

| Category | 1 (Poor) | 3 (Average) | 5 (Excellent) |
|----------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Discovery | Jumped straight to pitch | Some questions asked | Thorough discovery before any pitch |
| Listening | Talked over me | Paused occasionally | Active listening, reflected back |
| Objection Handling | Argued or froze | Acknowledged but weak response | LARA framework, empathetic + firm |
| Next Steps | No close attempt | Vague "let me follow up" | Clear, specific next step |
| Overall Impression | Would not take a meeting | Might take a meeting | Would definitely take a meeting |

## LESSON 5: Onboarding: Training on Your ICP, Voice, and Playbook (55 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The 30-60-90 Day Onboarding Plan** — Day 1-30: Learn the business, ICP, product, and playbook. Shadow founder on calls. Day 31-60: Execute playbook with oversight. Run calls while founder observes. Day 61-90: Independent execution with weekly 1:1s.
2. **Week 1: Immersion** — Read the sales playbook (Course 44). Review ICP document (Course 1). Listen to 5-10 recorded calls. Study the CRM and pipeline (Course 40). Meet the product team/read product docs.
3. **Week 2-3: Shadow and Practice** — Shadow founder on 5-10 live calls. Practice discovery calls using AI roleplay (Course 15). Send first outreach sequences (founder reviews every message). Debrief each day: what worked, what didn't.
4. **Week 4-6: Supervised Execution** — Own outreach sequences (founder spot-checks 20%). Take discovery calls (founder listens to recordings and provides feedback). Book meetings from own prospecting. Daily 15-minute check-in with founder.
5. **Week 7-12: Independent Execution** — Full ownership of outreach and calls. Weekly 1:1 with founder (30 min). Performance tracked against ramp targets. Monthly playbook review and adjustment.
6. **The Voice Transfer Problem** — The hardest part of onboarding: transferring the founder's authentic voice and judgment. Tools: recorded call library, messaging guidelines document, "swipe file" of approved language, weekly message review (first 30 days).

## LESSON 6: Compensation Models (Base + Variable) (50 min)

### Key Topics

1. **SDR Compensation** — Base: $40,000-55,000/year. Variable: 10-20% of base ($4,000-11,000), paid on meetings booked or qualified pipeline generated. OTE (on-target earnings): $44,000-66,000/year. Split: 80/20 to 85/15 (base/variable).
2. **AE Compensation** — Base: $60,000-80,000/year. Variable: 30-50% of base ($18,000-40,000), paid on closed revenue. OTE: $78,000-120,000/year. Split: 60/40 to 70/30 (base/variable).
3. **Fractional Compensation** — Monthly retainer: $2,000-5,000/month for 5-15 hours/week. Some fractionals want a small equity stake (0.25-1%). Performance bonus: 5-10% of revenue improvement attributed to their work.
4. **Variable Compensation Design** — SDR: pay per qualified meeting (e.g., $50-100/meeting) or per SQL generated. AE: commission on closed revenue (8-15% of first-year revenue). Accelerators: 1.5-2x rate above 100% of quota.
5. **Quota Setting** — SDR quota: 8-15 qualified meetings/month (varies by market and ACV). AE quota: 3-5x their OTE in annual revenue (e.g., $80K OTE = $240-400K annual quota). Ramp: 50% quota Month 1, 75% Month 2, 100% Month 3+.
6. **Equity and Startup-Specific Compensation** — Equity grants: 0.05-0.5% for early sales hires (SDR lower, AE higher). Vesting: 4-year with 1-year cliff. Other perks: flexible schedule, remote work, learning budget, conference attendance.

## LESSON 7: Managing a Sales Hire as a Solo Founder (50 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The Management Paradox** — You hired to free up time, but now you spend time managing. Expect 3-5 hours/week on management in Month 1-2, decreasing to 1-2 hours/week by Month 3+.
2. **The Weekly 1:1 Structure** — 30 minutes, same time every week. Agenda: (1) Wins this week (2 min). (2) Pipeline review: key deals and next actions (10 min). (3) Metrics vs targets (5 min). (4) Blockers and help needed (5 min). (5) Coaching moment: one skill to improve (5 min). (6) Action items (3 min).
3. **Call Coaching** — Listen to 2-3 call recordings per week. Provide specific feedback: "At 3:47, when they said X, you could have asked Y." Use the roleplay scoring rubric from Course 15 as the coaching framework.
4. **Metrics Management** — Daily: activity metrics (emails sent, calls made, DMs sent). Weekly: outcome metrics (replies, meetings, pipeline). Monthly: revenue metrics (deals closed, quota attainment). Share a live dashboard — no surprises.
5. **Motivation and Accountability** — Celebrate wins (deals closed, meetings booked). Address misses early (don't wait for a quarterly review). Create healthy urgency without panic. Remember: they're building skills, not just hitting numbers.
6. **The 90-Day Management Cadence** — Month 1: daily 15-min check-ins + weekly 1:1 + listen to all calls. Month 2: weekly 1:1 + listen to 50% of calls + spot-check outreach. Month 3: weekly 1:1 + listen to 20% of calls + full independence on outreach.

### Data Points & Statistics

| Statistic | Source | Context |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Sales managers spend 3-7 hours/week on management (per rep) | Sales management surveys | Solo founders should target 2-3 hours |
| Weekly 1:1s improve rep performance by 20-30% | CEB/Gartner | Non-negotiable management practice |
| Call coaching is the #1 driver of SDR/AE improvement | Bridge Group | Listening to calls > any other coaching |
| Reps who receive weekly call feedback ramp 40% faster | Sales training data | Invest in feedback, not just training |
| 70% of rep underperformance is due to management gaps, not talent gaps | Salesforce | Your management quality determines their results |

### Frameworks & Models

- **Weekly 1:1 Agenda Template:**

| Segment | Duration | Content |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Wins | 2 min | What went well this week? |
| Pipeline Review | 10 min | Top 5 deals: stage, next action, blockers |
| Metrics | 5 min | Activity and outcome metrics vs targets |
| Blockers | 5 min | What's preventing progress? How can I help? |
| Coaching | 5 min | One specific skill to improve (with example) |
| Action Items | 3 min | Summarize commitments for next week |

- **Call Coaching Framework:**
  1. Pick 2-3 calls to review per week (prioritize: meetings that didn't convert, deals that stalled)
  2. Note specific timestamps with observations
  3. Frame feedback as: "At [timestamp], you did [X]. Try [Y] instead because [Z]."
  4. Ask the rep to self-assess first: "What do you think went well? What would you change?"

## LESSON 8: When to Fire and Start Over (45 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The 90-Day Evaluation Window** — Give every hire 90 days of supported ramp before making a keep/fire decision. But don't wait 90 days if there are clear red flags (dishonesty, zero effort, attitude problems).
2. **Performance Red Flags** — (1) Activity metrics consistently below 50% of target (not effort, not bad luck — lack of effort). (2) Unwillingness to follow the playbook ("I have my own way"). (3) No improvement in quality after coaching. (4) Attitude problems: blaming the product, the leads, the market.
3. **Performance Yellow Flags** — (1) Meeting targets but poor conversion (activity without quality). (2) Good weeks followed by bad weeks (inconsistency). (3) Resistant to feedback but willing to try. These warrant more coaching, not firing.
4. **The Difficult Conversation** — Be direct: "Based on [specific metrics], we're not hitting the targets we agreed on. Here's what I need to see in the next 30 days: [specific, measurable targets]. If we don't hit these, we'll need to part ways." Document this conversation.
5. **The 30-Day PIP (Performance Improvement Plan)** — Specific targets. Daily check-ins. Written agreement. If targets met: extend employment with new quarterly targets. If not: separation. Keep it clean and fair.
6. **Moving On** — When you fire: be kind, be direct, be quick. Pay any owed compensation immediately. Offer to be a reference (if appropriate). Document what went wrong for the next hire. Most importantly: don't delay the decision.

### Data Points & Statistics

| Statistic | Source | Context |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| 50% of sales hires don't work out | SalesHacker / Bravado | It's normal. Plan for it. |
| Average time to fire a bad sales hire: 6-9 months | Sales management data | Most founders wait too long |
| Optimal decision window: 60-90 days | Hiring best practices | Enough data, not too much sunk cost |
| Cost of delaying a fire by 3 months: $15-30K (SDR) or $30-50K (AE) | Salary + management time + opportunity cost | Act quickly when it's clear |
| 30-day PIPs result in improvement 20-30% of the time | HR data | Worth trying, but be ready to act |

### Frameworks & Models

- **Performance Review Framework:**

| Category | Green (Keep) | Yellow (Coach) | Red (PIP/Fire) |
|----------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|

## LESSON 9: Transitioning from Founder-Led to Team-Led Sales (55 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The Founder-Led Sales Trap** — You're closing deals, but you're the bottleneck. Revenue grows linearly with your time. The transition is from "I sell" to "I build the system that sells."
2. **The 4-Phase Transition** — Phase 1: Founder does everything (0-50 meetings). Phase 2: Founder + SDR (SDR prospects, founder closes). Phase 3: Founder + SDR + AE (SDR prospects, AE closes, founder coaches). Phase 4: Sales team (founder reviews metrics, not calls).
3. **What to Hand Off First** — Prospecting and outbound (Phase 2). Then discovery and demos (Phase 3). Then proposals and closing (Phase 4). Founder keeps: pricing strategy, key account relationships, hiring decisions.
4. **The Knowledge Transfer Bottleneck** — Your intuition about customers, product, and market lives in your head. Transfer it via: recorded calls, written playbook, shared CRM notes, weekly coaching, and ride-alongs.
5. **Metrics-Driven Management** — As you step away from individual deals, manage by metrics: pipeline velocity, conversion rates, activity levels, customer feedback. The dashboard replaces your gut instinct.
6. **Founder's Evolving Role** — Phase 2: Player-coach (selling + managing). Phase 3: Coach (managing + strategy). Phase 4: Architect (strategy + hiring + culture). Most solo founders need 12-24 months to complete this transition.

### Data Points & Statistics

| Statistic | Source | Context |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Founder-led sales ceiling: ~$500K-1M ARR before burnout | SaaS growth studies | You can't scale past this alone |
| Phase 2 (Founder + SDR) unlocks 2-3x pipeline | Hiring impact data | SDR handles prospecting, founder handles closing |
| Phase 3 (Founder + team) unlocks 5-10x revenue potential | Team scaling data | But requires 6-12 months of management investment |
| 60% of founders struggle to let go of sales | First Round Capital | Letting go is the hardest skill |
| Companies that complete the transition grow 3-5x faster than those that don't | Startup growth benchmarks | The transition is the growth strategy |

### Frameworks & Models

- **4-Phase Transition Model:**

| Phase | Team | Founder Role | Revenue Capacity |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Founder only | Everything | $0-500K ARR |
| 2 | Founder + SDR | Closing + coaching | $500K-1M ARR |
| 3 | Founder + SDR + AE | Coaching + strategy | $1-3M ARR |
| 4 | Sales team | Strategy + hiring | $3M+ ARR |

## LESSON 10: Your Hiring Playbook (50 min)

### Key Topics

1. **The Complete Hiring Review** — Walk through all 9 lessons' artifacts and verify completeness
2. **The 14-Day Hiring Sprint** — Day 1-2: Finalize job description and post on Bravado/LinkedIn/Wellfound. Day 3-7: Review applications and send async tasks. Day 8-10: Video interviews with top candidates. Day 11-12: Reference checks. Day 13: Make offer. Day 14: Begin onboarding.
3. **The Hiring Pipeline** — Like a sales pipeline: Applicants (100%) → Screen Pass (30-40%) → Async Task Complete (50-60% of passed) → Interview (30-40% of task completers) → Reference Check (50%) → Offer (1 candidate). Plan for 20-50 applicants to find 1 hire.
4. **Post-Hire Success Metrics** — Month 1: onboarding milestones hit. Month 2: 50% of ramp quota. Month 3: 75-100% of ramp quota. Month 6: full quota attainment. Track these against the 30-60-90 plan.
5. **The Repeatable Hiring System** — Document your hiring process so the next hire is easier. Save: JD templates, interview questions, async tasks, scoring rubrics, onboarding plans. When you hire person #2, the system is ready.
6. **Handoff to Course 48 (Capstone)** — Your hiring playbook is part of the complete acquisition system. Course 48 will integrate all Track 1-7 playbooks, templates, and systems into one comprehensive document.
